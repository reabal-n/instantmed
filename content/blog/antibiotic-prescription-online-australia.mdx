<Callout variant="info">

Some antibiotic prescribing can happen through telehealth in Australia, but suitability depends on the infection, severity, patient risk factors, and whether a physical examination is needed. The prescribing decision still belongs to the registered clinician.

</Callout>

Antibiotics are one of the most scrutinised medication classes in Australian primary care because unnecessary use accelerates antimicrobial resistance. For conditions with clear, symptom-based diagnostic criteria, uncomplicated urinary tract infections being the most established example, remote clinical assessment may be appropriate.

Understanding which conditions may be suitable, how the prescribing decision is made, and why antibiotic stewardship matters helps you recognise when telehealth is reasonable and when it is the wrong first step.

## Which Conditions May Suit Remote Antibiotic Assessment

Not every bacterial infection can be assessed remotely. The conditions commonly assessed and treated via telehealth antibiotic prescribing include:

**Uncomplicated urinary tract infections (UTIs) in adult women:** This is the strongest case for telehealth antibiotic prescribing. The clinical criteria for an uncomplicated UTI - dysuria (pain on urination), frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort without systemic features - are well-validated and can be reliably assessed from a structured symptom history. Australian prescribing guidelines (eTG Antibiotic, published by Therapeutic Guidelines Limited) explicitly support empirical treatment based on clinical diagnosis for this indication.

**Simple skin infections:** Localised cellulitis, infected lacerations, mild impetigo, and early localised skin abscesses can often be assessed from a photograph combined with symptom description. Clear images of the affected area with a size reference allow the doctor to assess extent, depth, and whether systemic spread is occurring.

**Bacterial sinusitis:** Sinusitis lasting more than 10 days with no improvement, or worsening after initial improvement, is more likely bacterial than viral. Specific symptom patterns (unilateral facial pain, purulent nasal discharge, dental pain) support bacterial aetiology. Most acute sinusitis does not require antibiotics, and Australian prescribing guidelines recommend restricting antibiotic use to cases meeting specific criteria.

**Respiratory tract infections where bacterial cause is clinically supported:** Most acute respiratory infections are viral and do not benefit from antibiotics. For cases with features strongly suggesting bacterial infection - prolonged productive cough with fever, specific clinical patterns consistent with a [chest infection](/conditions/chest-infection) - remote assessment may support prescribing, with the understanding that the doctor cannot perform auscultation.

<Callout variant="warning">

Antibiotics do not treat viral infections. A telehealth doctor will not prescribe antibiotics for [colds and flu](/conditions/cold-and-flu), influenza, most [sore throats](/conditions/sore-throat), or most respiratory illnesses, because antibiotics would provide no benefit and carry risks. This is standard clinical practice and reflects Australian antibiotic stewardship guidelines, not a limitation of telehealth.

</Callout>

## The Prescribing Decision: What the Doctor Assesses

When a doctor reviews a possible antibiotic prescription, they are applying the same clinical reasoning as in any medical consultation:

**Does this presentation suggest a bacterial rather than viral infection?** Most infections are viral. Specific features support bacterial diagnosis: purulent discharge, fever above 38.5C with localised infection, failure to improve over the typical viral timeframe (7-10 days for respiratory illness), skin warmth, redness, and tenderness with defined borders in skin infections.

**What is the correct antibiotic for this infection?** Australian antibiotic prescribing follows evidence-based guidance including Therapeutic Guidelines and antimicrobial stewardship standards. The choice of antibiotic, type, dose, and duration depends on the probable causative organism for the specific infection site, local resistance patterns, patient allergies, and any recent antibiotic use that might indicate resistance.

**Does this patient have contraindications?** Allergy history is the most critical factor. Penicillin and cephalosporin allergy affects a significant proportion of the population and substantially limits antibiotic choices. Kidney or liver disease affects dosing. Pregnancy restricts the safe antibiotic options. All of this must be disclosed in the intake form.

**Is this a case that needs in-person assessment first?** Systemic features (high fever, rigors, confusion, hypotension) indicate sepsis and require immediate in-person or emergency care. Rapidly spreading infections, deep tissue infections, infections in immunocompromised patients, and infections in children generally require in-person assessment.

## Why Antibiotic Stewardship Matters to You

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is identified by the World Health Organization as one of the greatest global public health threats. Australia's National Antimicrobial Resistance Strategy 2020-2025 (Australian Government Department of Health and Aged Care) identifies antibiotic stewardship as central to addressing AMR.

Antibiotic stewardship means using antibiotics only when they are needed, using the right antibiotic for the right indication at the right dose for the right duration. This applies whether prescribing happens in person or via telehealth.

For you as a patient, stewardship principles translate to three practical points:

**Take the full course.** Stopping antibiotics early because you feel better is one of the most common causes of treatment failure and a mechanism of antibiotic resistance development at the individual level. Partially resistant bacteria can survive incomplete treatment, multiply, and cause a harder-to-treat recurrence.

**Disclose recent antibiotic use.** If you have taken antibiotics in the past 3 months, particularly for the same type of infection, the doctor needs this information. Recent antibiotic use selects for resistant organisms and changes which antibiotic is most likely to work.

**Do not seek antibiotics for viral infections.** A doctor who declines to prescribe antibiotics for a cold or influenza is practising good medicine, not being unhelpful. Taking antibiotics you do not need disrupts beneficial gut bacteria, creates side effects, and contributes to population-level resistance without providing clinical benefit.

## What the Doctor Needs to Know

The quality of your intake submission directly affects the prescribing decision:

**Symptom details:** What symptoms you have, when they started, how they have progressed, and their severity. For UTIs, specifically: dysuria, frequency, urgency, haematuria (blood in urine), presence or absence of fever, flank pain. For skin infections: the size of the affected area, how quickly it has spread, presence of warmth, redness, swelling, or pus.

**Allergy history:** Any known medication or antibiotic allergies, including the specific reaction (rash, anaphylaxis, gastrointestinal). This is critical - do not skip it.

**Current medications:** Potential interactions between antibiotics and other medications exist and need to be checked. Flucloxacillin and methotrexate; tetracyclines and isotretinoin; metronidazole and alcohol are examples.

**Recent antibiotic use:** Any antibiotics in the past 3 months, including what they were for and which antibiotic was used.

**Photos for skin infections:** Clear images of the affected area in natural light, ideally with a ruler or coin for scale. Include the full extent of any redness or swelling.

**Pregnancy and breastfeeding status:** Substantially affects antibiotic choice. Some antibiotics safe in non-pregnant adults are contraindicated in pregnancy.

## The eScript Process

If the doctor issues an antibiotic prescription:

1. You receive an SMS with a QR code or token number (this is your eScript)
2. Take the SMS to any Australian pharmacy
3. The pharmacist scans the token to access the full prescription details
4. If repeats are authorised, they are linked to the same token
5. The eScript remains active for 12 months from issue (unused repeats expire after this)

You do not need to fill the prescription at a specific pharmacy. Any community pharmacy in Australia can dispense an eScript.

## When a Doctor Will Refer Instead of Prescribe

A responsible telehealth doctor will direct you to in-person care when:

- Fever above 38.5C with systemic symptoms (shaking, confusion, rapid heart rate) - possible sepsis requiring urgent assessment
- Rapidly spreading redness or infection (cellulitis expanding more than 1-2cm/hour), particularly with pain out of proportion to appearance
- Eye or ear infections requiring direct examination
- Deep tissue infection or abscess requiring drainage
- Pregnancy with a urinary tract infection (UTIs in pregnancy require in-person assessment due to higher complication risk)
- Children under 12 with infection (usually require in-person assessment)
- Immunocompromised patients (diabetes, HIV, on immunosuppressants, recent chemotherapy)
- Prior treatment failure with the same condition and first-line antibiotics

Receiving a recommendation to seek in-person care is not a failure. It is the safety boundary working correctly.

## PBS Costs

PBS-listed antibiotics dispensed with a Medicare card:

| Patient type | Co-payment (2025-26) |
|---|---|
| General patient | Up to $31.60 per item |
| Concession card holder | Up to $7.70 per item |
| Safety Net (concessional) | Free after $262.80/year |

Without a Medicare card, antibiotics are dispensed at the full unsubsidised price, which varies from approximately $15 (generic cefalexin) to $30-50+ for newer agents. Source: Services Australia PBS co-payment thresholds 2025-26.
