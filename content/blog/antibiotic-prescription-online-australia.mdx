Antibiotics can sometimes be prescribed after telehealth assessment in Australia. The important word is assessment.

An online form, symptom checklist, or uploaded photo is not the same thing as a guaranteed antibiotic prescription. The prescriber still has to decide whether the illness is likely bacterial, whether an antibiotic is likely to help, which antibiotic is safest, and whether the limits of remote assessment make in-person care the better option.

## The Short Version

Telehealth may be reasonable when the presentation is low risk, the symptom pattern is clear, the patient can provide enough history, and the doctor can make a safe prescribing decision without a physical examination.

Telehealth is a poor fit when the infection could be severe, spreading quickly, affecting a high-risk person, or involving a body site where examination is important.

Antibiotics are not useful for viral illness. Healthdirect and Australian antimicrobial resistance guidance both state that antibiotics do not work for viral infections such as colds, flu, COVID-19, and many sore throats or coughs.

## What "Online Antibiotics" Actually Means

The phrase "get antibiotics online" is misleading. The real flow is:

1. A patient gives a history, symptoms, risks, allergies, and current medicines.
2. A registered prescriber reviews whether the presentation is suitable.
3. The prescriber decides whether an antibiotic is appropriate.
4. If prescribing is appropriate, the prescription may be issued electronically.
5. A pharmacist dispenses and counsels the patient.

The online part changes the communication channel. It does not remove clinical judgement, prescribing law, professional standards, pharmacy checks, or antimicrobial stewardship.

<Callout variant="info">A responsible online service should sometimes say no, ask more questions, recommend testing, or direct you to in-person care. That is a safety feature, not a failure.</Callout>

## When Telehealth May Be Appropriate

Remote assessment can work best when the diagnosis is largely history-based and red flags can be screened clearly.

Examples that may be suitable in some adults include:

- Uncomplicated urinary symptoms where the pattern fits a lower urinary tract infection and there are no kidney infection features
- Mild, localised skin infection where high-quality photos and history are enough for initial assessment
- Selected sinus or respiratory presentations where the duration, pattern, and risk profile support bacterial infection
- Follow-up of a known infection when the patient has already been examined or tested

That does not mean every case in those categories should receive antibiotics. It means the presentation may be assessable remotely if the details are clear enough.

## When In-Person Care Is Safer

Some infection symptoms should bypass online prescribing because the cost of missing severity is too high.

Seek urgent or in-person assessment for:

- Confusion, faintness, severe weakness, rigors, or feeling seriously unwell
- Fever with rapid heart rate, low blood pressure symptoms, or dehydration
- Severe pain, pain out of proportion, or rapidly worsening symptoms
- Rapidly spreading redness, swelling, or warmth
- Shortness of breath, chest pain, blue lips, or trouble speaking full sentences
- Flank pain, fever, vomiting, or pregnancy with urinary symptoms
- Eye infections, severe ear pain, facial swelling, or dental swelling
- Deep wounds, animal bites, suspected abscess needing drainage, or infection around a joint
- Infection in a child, older frail person, immunocompromised person, or someone with poorly controlled diabetes
- Symptoms that are worsening despite recent antibiotics

<Callout variant="warning">If you are deteriorating quickly, do not wait for an online reply. Use urgent care, an emergency department, or Triple Zero (000) depending on severity.</Callout>

## Why Questionnaire-Only Prescribing Is A Risk

The Medical Board of Australia's telehealth guidance expects telehealth to meet the same standard of care as in-person care as far as possible. It also highlights poor-practice concerns where prescribing relies only on text, email, or online questionnaires instead of a face-to-face, video, or telephone consultation.

That matters for antibiotics because infection assessment often depends on:

- How unwell the person sounds or appears
- Whether symptoms are localised or systemic
- Whether there is enough information to distinguish viral and bacterial illness
- Whether a physical examination, vital signs, urine test, swab, imaging, or blood test is needed
- Whether the patient understands the plan and escalation advice

Photos and structured forms can support a consultation. They should not become a rubber stamp.

## What The Prescriber Needs To Know

A safe antibiotic decision needs more than the name of the symptom.

Useful details include:

- Symptom start date and how symptoms changed over time
- Fever, chills, rigors, vomiting, confusion, dizziness, or dehydration
- Exact symptom location, such as throat, chest, skin, urinary tract, sinus, ear, eye, or dental area
- Severity and whether symptoms are improving, stable, or worsening
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- Immune suppression, diabetes, kidney disease, liver disease, or significant heart or lung disease
- Medication allergies, including what happened and how quickly
- Current medicines, including over-the-counter medicines and supplements
- Antibiotics taken in the last 3 months
- Relevant test results, swabs, urine results, or prior diagnoses
- Clear photos for skin issues, with scale and lighting, if requested

Leaving out allergy, pregnancy, kidney function, or recent antibiotic use can change the safest antibiotic choice. It can also mean the safest decision is to pause and seek more information.

## Why Antibiotics Are Often Refused

Refusing antibiotics can be good medicine.

Common reasons include:

- The pattern is more likely viral than bacterial.
- Symptoms are early and may settle without antibiotics.
- The condition usually needs testing or examination first.
- The likely antibiotic risk is higher than the expected benefit.
- The patient has an allergy, pregnancy, interaction, or medical history that changes the choice.
- The infection may need drainage, imaging, hospital care, or another urgent pathway.
- Recent antibiotic use makes resistance or treatment failure more likely.

Australian antimicrobial resistance guidance is blunt about the core point: antibiotics only work against bacteria. Using them for viral infections will not cure the infection, will not stop it spreading, and will not make you feel better faster.

## Antibiotic Stewardship In Plain English

Antimicrobial stewardship means using antibiotics only when they are likely to help, and choosing the right antibiotic, dose, and duration when they are needed.

For patients, this means:

- Do not ask for antibiotics "just in case."
- Do not reuse old antibiotics from a previous illness.
- Do not share antibiotics with another person.
- Do not pressure a clinician to prescribe for colds, flu, or most coughs.
- Take the medicine as directed if it is prescribed.
- Ask what symptoms should trigger review.
- Return unused medicines through a pharmacy disposal program rather than keeping them.

The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care describes antimicrobial stewardship as a way to improve safe and appropriate antimicrobial use, reduce patient harm, and help prevent antimicrobial resistance.

## eScripts: The Dispensing Step

An electronic prescription is a legal prescription sent through secure clinical software. It is commonly received as an SMS or email token and can be presented to a pharmacy that supports electronic prescriptions.

The token is not the clinical assessment. It is the way the prescription is accessed after the prescriber has decided to issue one.

The usual pathway is:

1. The prescriber creates the electronic prescription.
2. The patient receives a token by SMS, email, or another supported method.
3. The pharmacy scans or enters the token.
4. The pharmacist checks the prescription, medicine, interactions, supply rules, and counselling needs.
5. If repeats are issued, repeat tokens may be managed electronically or through an Active Script List.

Do not assume every medicine, repeat, or state-based requirement works the same way. The pharmacist can explain dispensing rules for the specific prescription.

## PBS Costs In 2026

PBS cost rules changed on 1 January 2026. According to Services Australia and the PBS, before reaching the PBS Safety Net:

- General patients pay up to $25 for most PBS medicines.
- Concession card holders pay up to $7.70.
- The 2026 PBS Safety Net thresholds are $1,748.20 for general patients and $277.20 for concession card holders.
- After reaching the threshold, general patients usually pay the concessional rate and concession card holders may receive PBS medicines at no charge.

These figures apply to PBS-listed medicines and eligible patients. Brand premiums, private prescriptions, non-PBS medicines, delivery, after-hours fees, and pharmacy pricing can change the final amount.

## What To Do Before Requesting Antibiotics Online

Before submitting an antibiotic request, gather:

- Your symptom timeline
- Temperature readings if you have them
- Current medicines and allergies
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- Relevant medical conditions
- Recent antibiotic names and dates
- Photos if the issue is skin-related and photos are clinically appropriate
- Any recent pathology, swab, urine, or imaging results

Then be ready for one of four outcomes:

- Antibiotic prescribed because the presentation fits.
- More information requested.
- Testing or in-person assessment recommended.
- Antibiotic declined because it is unlikely to help or not safe remotely.

The fourth outcome can be frustrating, but it is often the one that protects you from unnecessary side effects and protects future treatment options from resistance.
