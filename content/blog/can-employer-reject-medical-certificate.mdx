You have provided a medical certificate from a registered doctor and your employer is questioning it. Can they actually reject it? The short answer is no - not if the certificate is valid and from an AHPRA-registered practitioner. But understanding where the line sits, what employers can legitimately do, and how to respond to pushback will help you navigate this situation without escalating unnecessarily.

## The Legal Position

Under the Fair Work Act 2009 and the National Employment Standards (NES), an employer can request "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of illness. A medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner is the accepted standard for meeting this threshold.

Once that evidence is provided, the employer's obligation is to accept it. They cannot reject a valid certificate because they doubt the illness, because the doctor was not their preferred choice, or because they do not approve of telehealth consultations. Rejecting valid evidence and then withholding sick leave pay or taking disciplinary action may constitute adverse action under Part 3-1 of the Fair Work Act.

<Callout variant="info">

The Fair Work Act does not require a "paper certificate" or an "in-person consultation." It requires evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person. A digitally issued certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor satisfies this, regardless of how the consultation was conducted.

</Callout>

## What Makes a Certificate Valid

For a certificate to be valid under Australian workplace law, it should contain:

- Your full legal name
- The date of the consultation
- A clear statement that you were unfit for work (or unfit for your usual duties)
- The dates covered - the period you are certified as unfit
- The practitioner's name, AHPRA registration number, signature, and contact details

The certificate does **not** need to contain your diagnosis. "Unfit for work due to a medical condition" is legally sufficient and protects your medical privacy under the Privacy Act 1988. A doctor who includes your diagnosis without your explicit consent is breaching their privacy obligations.

If your certificate is missing the AHPRA registration number, ask the issuing doctor to provide an updated version. That is the most common technical reason an employer might have a legitimate concern about a certificate's authenticity.

## Telehealth Certificates: The Same Legal Standing

Some employers have questioned certificates issued via telehealth. This position has no legal basis.

A telehealth consultation with an AHPRA-registered doctor is a legitimate medical consultation. The doctor assesses your symptoms, forms a clinical opinion, and issues a certificate. The consultation format - in person, phone, or online form - does not affect the certificate's legal validity.

The Fair Work Ombudsman has confirmed this explicitly. An employer cannot:

- Refuse a certificate on the basis that the consultation was via telehealth
- Require you to have an in-person consultation for your initial certificate
- Treat telehealth certificates differently from paper ones

If your employer has a written policy requiring in-person consultations, that policy is likely unenforceable to the extent it conflicts with the Fair Work Act's evidence requirements.

## What Employers Can Legitimately Do

There is a difference between rejecting a certificate and having questions about it. Employers can legitimately:

**Ask about the certificate's completeness.** If the certificate is missing required information (like the AHPRA number), they can ask you to obtain a complete version. This is not a rejection - it is a request for the certificate to meet the standard requirements.

**Request an independent medical examination (IME).** For extended absences or when there is a genuine question about fitness for duty, employers can request an IME. Key rules apply: it must be at the employer's expense, it must be requested reasonably (not as harassment), and it cannot be used to replace your initial certificate. The IME assesses your fitness going forward, not whether your original absence was genuine.

**Discuss patterns of absence.** This is separate from questioning a certificate's validity. If you have a pattern of frequent single-day absences - particularly around weekends or public holidays - an employer can raise this as an attendance management matter. This is a legitimate management function, but the discussion must be kept separate from the question of whether individual certificates are valid.

**Verify a doctor's registration.** An employer can check whether the issuing doctor appears on the AHPRA register. This is public information available at ahpra.gov.au. If the doctor's registration is current and the certificate is complete, this verification ends the inquiry.

## What Employers Cannot Do

Regardless of their concerns, employers cannot:

- Demand to know your specific diagnosis
- Contact your doctor without your written consent
- Require you to see a specific doctor for your initial certificate
- Reject a valid certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner on subjective grounds
- Withhold sick leave pay for a legitimate absence supported by valid evidence
- Discipline you solely for taking personal leave you are entitled to

These are not minor technicalities. They reflect the privacy and employment rights that Australian law provides.

## When an Employer May Have Genuine Concerns

There are circumstances where employer concern is more understandable, though the correct response is still process - not rejection:

**The certificate appears incomplete or altered.** If fields are missing, dates do not add up, or there are signs of alteration, the employer can ask you to obtain a new certificate or verify the original. They should not simply reject it without raising the specific concern.

**The issuing practitioner is not on the AHPRA register.** If the doctor's name does not appear on the public AHPRA register with current registration, the certificate's validity is in question. This is rare but does occur with certificates from overseas, expired registrations, or fraudulent documents.

**There are documented inconsistencies.** If an employee is signed off sick but visible on social media at a sporting event the same day, an employer has legitimate grounds to investigate - but this is a misconduct inquiry, not a certificate rejection. The two processes are separate.

## How to Respond to a Rejection

If your employer has rejected or is questioning your certificate:

1. **Ask them to specify the concern in writing.** Vague objections are harder to respond to and harder for them to sustain. "What specific requirement does this certificate fail to meet?" is a reasonable question.

2. **Check the certificate is complete.** Does it have your name, consultation date, unfitness period, and the doctor's AHPRA registration number? If anything is missing, ask the doctor to provide an updated version.

3. **Confirm the doctor's registration at ahpra.gov.au.** If their registration is current and the certificate is complete, the basis for rejection is gone.

4. **Reference the legal framework.** A brief statement that you understand a certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner meets the Fair Work Act evidence requirement, and that you would appreciate the employer specifying their legal basis for rejection, often resolves the situation without escalation.

5. **Escalate to HR.** If your direct manager is the one creating the difficulty, bring HR into the conversation. HR staff are generally better informed about the legal position.

6. **Contact the Fair Work Ombudsman.** If the dispute continues - particularly if pay is being withheld or disciplinary action is threatened - the FWO provides free advice at fairwork.gov.au or on 13 13 94. They can also investigate complaints and issue compliance notices.

<Callout variant="warning">

Keep copies of all certificates and any correspondence with your employer about the dispute. If the matter escalates to a Fair Work Commission claim, documentation of the employer's conduct will be important evidence.

</Callout>

## Adverse Action Claims

If an employer disciplines you, demotes you, or terminates your employment because you took legitimate personal leave supported by valid evidence, this may constitute adverse action under the Fair Work Act. Adverse action claims can be lodged with the Fair Work Commission.

Adverse action is different from attendance management. An employer can legitimately:

- Discuss patterns of frequent absence with you
- Set performance expectations around reliability
- Require follow-up certification for extended absences
- Request an IME for return-to-work planning

What they cannot do is take punitive action specifically because you exercised your right to take personal leave. The distinction matters, and the Fair Work Commission examines the employer's reasoning when assessing adverse action claims.

## A Note on Fraudulent Certificates

The flip side of employer obligations is employee honesty. Using a fraudulent medical certificate - one that misrepresents the consultation, the dates, or the issuing doctor - is serious. Employers who suspect fraud can verify certificates with the issuing clinic. Confirmed fraud can justify termination and, in some circumstances, may be referred to police.

If you are genuinely unwell, there is no reason to use a fraudulent certificate. Legitimate options exist regardless of your circumstances: telehealth, retrospective certificates for short gaps, and statutory declarations for brief absences. None of these options risk your employment or your record.
