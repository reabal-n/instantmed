Telehealth is not a replacement for all healthcare. But for a substantial proportion of common health needs, it is not just adequate - it is often the more practical and equally effective choice. The question is not whether telehealth is good, but whether it is right for a specific clinical situation.

Since the permanent expansion of Medicare-funded telehealth in Australia following the COVID-19 pandemic, telehealth has become part of mainstream healthcare delivery. According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), telehealth accounted for approximately 37% of all GP Medicare-subsidised services in 2021-22, reflecting a permanent shift in how Australians access primary care.

This guide provides a decision framework grounded in what doctors actually assess when choosing between remote and in-person care.

## The Core Clinical Question

Doctors deciding whether a condition can be managed via telehealth ask one question: can I form a reliable clinical opinion from the information available without a physical examination?

For many common presentations, the answer is yes. A diagnosis of an uncomplicated urinary tract infection in an adult woman can be made reliably from a structured symptom history. A prescription renewal for a blood pressure medication the patient has taken for three years requires no examination. A medical certificate for gastroenteritis requires only that the described symptoms are clinically consistent with the claimed inability to work.

For other presentations, the answer is no. A child with ear pain requires otoscopic examination to differentiate otitis media from otitis externa. Suspected pneumonia requires auscultation. Abdominal pain with guarding requires palpation.

This is the foundation of the telehealth decision.

## Conditions Well-Suited to Telehealth

| Condition category | Examples | Why telehealth works |
|---|---|---|
| Common respiratory illness | Cold, flu, COVID-19 management | Symptom-based diagnosis; treatment primarily supportive |
| Urinary tract infections | Adult female UTI (uncomplicated) | Well-validated remote diagnostic criteria |
| Skin conditions | Eczema, contact dermatitis, acne | Photos + symptom description sufficient |
| Mental health | Anxiety, depression check-ins, stress | History-based assessment; scales (PHQ-9, GAD-7) usable remotely |
| Prescription renewals | All stable chronic medications | No new examination needed for established treatment |
| Medical certificates | Most common illnesses | Clinical judgment from symptom description |
| Sexual health | STI testing referrals, contraception | Structured history; referral for testing if needed |
| Chronic disease routine | Diabetes check-in, hypertension review | Labs + history sufficient for stable patients |
| Follow-up consultations | Post-procedure review, medication review | Previous clinical information available |

The common thread across this list: diagnosis and management decisions rely primarily on patient-reported history and symptoms, possibly supplemented by photographs or reported measurements (blood pressure, weight, temperature), not on physical examination findings.

## Conditions Requiring In-Person Assessment

Some clinical situations require the doctor's hands, eyes, and instruments:

**Physical examination required:**
- Chest infection or pneumonia (requires auscultation)
- Ear infections in children (requires otoscopy)
- Abdominal presentations requiring palpation
- Musculoskeletal injuries requiring range-of-motion testing
- New lumps, growths, or skin lesions requiring direct inspection
- Throat infections requiring direct visualisation beyond simple inspection

**Procedures only possible in person:**
- Blood tests and pathology collection
- Vaccinations and injections
- Wound care, suturing, removal of sutures
- Skin biopsy or excision
- ECG, spirometry, or other diagnostic testing
- Physical therapy or manipulation

**Complex clinical situations:**
- New symptoms that are poorly characterised and may require multiple examination findings to interpret
- Conditions with significant red flags requiring urgent physical assessment
- Complex chronic conditions undergoing significant change
- Medication initiations that require baseline examination or monitoring

<Callout variant="emergency">

Chest pain, difficulty breathing, stroke symptoms (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty), severe abdominal pain, uncontrolled bleeding, or thoughts of self-harm or suicide require emergency care. Call 000 or go to your nearest emergency department. Do not use telehealth for emergencies.

</Callout>

## The Grey Areas: Start Online, Escalate If Needed

Many presentations sit in a grey area where telehealth might be appropriate or might not - depending on the specific clinical features. In these situations, a telehealth consultation is a reasonable first step. The doctor will assess whether in-person care is needed and will direct you accordingly.

**Sore throat:** Most sore throats are viral and resolve without antibiotics. A telehealth doctor can assess symptoms and provide a certificate or advice. If bacterial infection (streptococcal pharyngitis) is suspected - high fever, absence of cough, tonsillar exudate, tender cervical lymph nodes - they may recommend a throat swab in person. The initial triage and assessment can happen online.

**Acute lower back pain:** Without neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness in the legs, bladder or bowel changes), acute mechanical back pain can generally be managed with telehealth advice, analgesia guidance, and a certificate. Neurological symptoms, or pain that does not fit the typical acute mechanical pattern, warrant in-person assessment.

**Skin conditions:** Clear photos in good natural light give a doctor substantial information. Common conditions including eczema flares, contact dermatitis, fungal infections, and mild acne are frequently manageable remotely. Changing moles or pigmented lesions, deep or spreading infections, or conditions that appear atypical require in-person assessment.

**Mental health:** Telehealth is well-established for mental health support. Initial assessments, medication reviews, and certificates for mental health leave work well online. Mental Health Treatment Plans (MBS item 2710) can be initiated by GPs via telehealth in many cases. Crisis presentations require in-person assessment or access to crisis support lines (Lifeline: 13 11 14; Beyond Blue: 1300 22 4636).

**Respiratory infections:** Most upper respiratory infections are viral and self-limiting, with telehealth appropriate for management and certification. If lower respiratory symptoms are present - productive cough with colour change, breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain - in-person assessment with auscultation is warranted.

## Using Telehealth as Triage

One underused model is using telehealth as an initial triage and then escalating to in-person care when the clinical picture warrants it. In this model:

1. Submit a telehealth consultation describing your symptoms
2. The doctor either manages the issue fully, or determines that in-person assessment is needed and provides a specific recommendation (go to your GP, get a chest X-ray, present to ED)
3. You have effectively paid for expert triage rather than self-deciding to go to a clinic

This is particularly useful for presentations that are uncertain - the telehealth doctor gives you more information about what you are dealing with than self-triage alone would, and directs you to the right next step.

The RACGP (Royal Australian College of General Practitioners) has endorsed telehealth as a valid first contact for many presentations, provided appropriate clinical governance is in place and the telehealth doctor is willing to escalate when the clinical situation requires it.

## Cost Framework

The cost of a healthcare encounter is not just the consultation fee. Full-cost comparison:

| Factor | Bulk-billed in-person GP | Private GP | Asynchronous telehealth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Consultation fee | $0 | $60-100 | $20-80 |
| Medicare rebate | N/A | ~$41-60 | Varies by service |
| Travel cost | $5-20 | $5-20 | $0 |
| Parking | $5-15 | $5-15 | $0 |
| Time off work | 1-3 hours | 1-3 hours | 0-15 minutes |
| Waiting room | 20-60 min | 15-30 min | None |

For a working person, the productivity cost of a three-hour clinic visit can exceed $100-200 in lost wages or leave. A $40 telehealth consultation that takes 15 minutes total is often the more economical option even when compared to a bulk-billed appointment that takes half a working day.

## What to Look For in a Telehealth Service

Transparency is the clearest signal of clinical quality. Legitimate telehealth services will:

- State clearly what conditions they can and cannot manage
- Confirm that doctors are AHPRA-registered
- Have a documented process for declining requests and referring to in-person care
- Provide a consultation summary you can share with your regular GP
- Be transparent about costs before you commit

A service that promises to approve every request, or that never recommends in-person care, is not exercising genuine clinical judgment. The best telehealth services are those willing to tell you when they cannot help.
