Use telehealth when the clinical question can be answered safely from remote information. Use in-person care when the decision depends on examination, observations, tests, procedures, urgent treatment, or continuity from a clinician who already knows you.

The safest answer is not "telehealth is convenient" or "in-person is always better." The practical question is whether the practitioner has enough reliable information to make the specific decision.

<KeyTakeaway title="The short answer">
- Telehealth may fit focused, low-risk questions where history, photos, readings, records, or follow-up answers are enough.
- In-person care is safer when physical examination, vital signs, procedures, testing, or complex continuity change the decision.
- Urgent or emergency symptoms should bypass routine telehealth.
- A safe telehealth service should be willing to ask more questions, decline, or redirect.
- Cost and convenience matter only after the safety route is clear.
</KeyTakeaway>

## The core decision rule

A practitioner deciding between telehealth and in-person care is usually asking one question:

Can I make a safe, documented clinical decision with the information available remotely?

Remote information can include:

- what happened and when it started
- whether symptoms are improving, stable, or worsening
- current medicines, allergies, pregnancy status, and medical history
- relevant photos or readings, when securely requested
- previous results or discharge summaries
- real-time answers by phone or video
- the ability to arrange follow-up or redirect care

If the answer is yes, telehealth may be suitable. If the answer is no, the next step may be a GP clinic, urgent care clinic, emergency department, pathology collection, imaging, pharmacy advice, or another service.

<EvidenceNote title="Remote care is still clinical care" source="Medical Board of Australia">
The Medical Board's telehealth guidance treats telehealth as a mode of consultation, not a lower standard of care. The practitioner still needs enough information, consent, records, and clinical judgement to decide whether remote care is appropriate.
</EvidenceNote>

## When telehealth may be a good fit

Telehealth is strongest when the question is focused and the safety boundary is clear.

| Situation | Why telehealth may fit | What still matters |
|---|---|---|
| Follow-up after a previous review | The main diagnosis or plan may already be known | New symptoms or worsening signs may change the route |
| Simple administrative evidence | The doctor may assess work or study capacity from a clear history | The certificate or document is not automatic |
| Repeat medicine review | Prior use, dose history, side effects, and safety checks can be reviewed remotely | Some medicines need examination, tests, monitoring, or usual GP involvement |
| Mild, self-limiting symptoms | Advice may depend mainly on history and symptom pattern | Red flags, duration, and deterioration matter |
| Skin or visible concerns | Secure photos may add useful information | Changing, deep, spreading, painful, or atypical findings may need direct examination |
| Results or plan discussion | The question may be explanation, next steps, or monitoring | Serious results may need in-person review or urgent escalation |
| Triage when you are unsure | A clinician may help decide the next route | The service must be willing to redirect rather than force a remote outcome |

The common thread is not the body part or condition name. It is whether remote information is enough for the decision being made.

## When in-person, urgent, or emergency care is safer

Some problems need hands-on assessment or immediate support.

<DecisionBox title="Choose the safest care route">
### May fit telehealth
- The problem is non-urgent, focused, and can be explained clearly.
- Photos, readings, records, or follow-up questions are enough for the likely decision.
- You understand that the clinician may redirect if remote information is not enough.
### Needs in-person care
- Examination, observations, tests, procedures, wound care, injections, imaging, or complex medicine changes may alter care.
- The problem involves a child, pregnancy, frailty, significant comorbidity, or ongoing complex care where continuity matters.
- Symptoms are unclear, unusual, persistent, or worsening despite initial advice.
### Urgent care
- Chest pain, severe breathing difficulty, stroke symptoms, collapse, severe bleeding, severe allergic reaction, suicidal crisis, or rapid deterioration need urgent or emergency help.
- Call triple zero (000) for emergencies.
</DecisionBox>

Telehealth should not be used to delay urgent care. It also should not be used to avoid an examination that is central to the question.

## Grey-area problems depend on the details

Many real situations sit between "obviously remote" and "obviously in person." The same symptom can take different routes depending on severity, timing, risk factors, and what the doctor needs to check.

Examples:

- A sore throat may be simple advice if symptoms are mild, but fever, dehydration, severe pain, breathing difficulty, or concern for complications can change the route.
- A cough may fit remote advice when mild and improving, but shortness of breath, chest pain, low oxygen readings, or clinical concern may need in-person assessment.
- Back pain may fit advice and activity planning when there are no neurological symptoms, but weakness, numbness, bladder or bowel changes, fever, trauma, or cancer history changes the risk.
- Skin rashes may be easier with clear photos, but rapidly spreading infection, severe pain, eye involvement, fever, or atypical lesions may need direct review.
- Mental health support can work well remotely for some planned reviews, but immediate risk of self-harm, psychosis, severe agitation, or inability to stay safe needs urgent help.

The value of telehealth in grey areas is not that it guarantees an online solution. It can help sort the next step when the service is set up to redirect safely.

## Children, pregnancy, and complex conditions need a lower threshold

The threshold for in-person care is often lower when the person is:

- an infant or young child
- pregnant or recently postpartum
- older, frail, or immunocompromised
- living with significant heart, lung, kidney, liver, neurological, or endocrine disease
- on high-risk medicines
- recovering from surgery or hospital admission
- experiencing a new problem on top of complex chronic illness

Telehealth may still have a role for advice, triage, follow-up, or continuity, but the clinician may need observations, examination, local records, usual GP context, or urgent escalation sooner.

<CareBoundary title="Convenience is not the safety test">
A remote consultation can be convenient, but convenience should never override red flags, examination needs, or continuity needs. A safe telehealth outcome can be "please be seen in person."
</CareBoundary>

## Cost and Medicare should come after safety

Cost matters, but it should not decide the care route on its own.

Some telehealth consultations can be Medicare-funded when MBS item rules, provider requirements, patient eligibility, and billing conditions apply. Other telehealth services charge privately. Some in-person GP visits are bulk billed, while others involve a gap fee.

| Cost factor | Telehealth | In-person care |
|---|---|---|
| Consultation fee | May be private, rebated, or bulk billed depending on service and eligibility | May be bulk billed, rebated with a gap, or private |
| Travel and parking | Usually none | Can add time and cost |
| Time away from work or caring duties | Often lower | Can be higher if travel and waiting are needed |
| Tests and procedures | Usually need separate pathology, imaging, pharmacy, or clinic attendance | May be arranged or performed on site depending on clinic |
| Safety cost | Can be high if urgent care is delayed | Can be high if unnecessary ED use replaces suitable primary care |

<PolicyNote title="Billing rules are separate from clinical fit" source="MBS Online">
MBS telehealth arrangements support telephone and video services across many practitioner groups, but eligibility is item-specific. A service can be clinically suitable but privately billed, or Medicare-funded but still unsuitable if examination or urgent care is needed.
</PolicyNote>

## What to check before using a telehealth service

A suitable service should make the boundary clear before you rely on it.

Check for:

- AHPRA-registered practitioners or a clear clinical governance model
- plain-language privacy information
- clear fees and whether Medicare applies
- a process for asking follow-up questions
- a process for declining or redirecting unsafe requests
- a way to receive records, prescriptions, certificates, referrals, or summaries when appropriate
- advice about what to do if symptoms worsen
- no promise that a certificate, prescription, diagnosis, or approval is guaranteed

Be cautious if a site treats every problem as remote-friendly, promises approval before assessment, hides clinician identity or registration pathways, or discourages urgent care when symptoms are concerning.

## How telehealth can support the next step

Telehealth can be useful even when it does not solve the whole problem online.

It may help you:

- understand whether the problem sounds urgent
- prepare for a GP visit with a clearer history
- obtain a referral or investigation request when clinically appropriate
- clarify whether a medicine or symptom needs review
- receive self-care advice while monitoring symptoms
- get a summary to share with your usual GP

After any telehealth consultation, keep the record, follow the escalation advice, and tell your usual GP about changes to medicines, diagnoses, referrals, or ongoing symptoms.
