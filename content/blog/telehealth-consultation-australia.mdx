A telehealth consultation is a healthcare appointment delivered when the patient and practitioner are not in the same room. It may use phone, video, secure messaging, uploaded information, or a structured online form that a practitioner reviews.

The useful way to think about it is simple: telehealth changes the channel, not the clinical responsibility. A safe consultation still needs identity, consent, relevant history, privacy, documentation, and a decision about whether remote care is enough.

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- A telehealth consultation is a clinical review delivered remotely, not an automatic approval process.
- The practitioner needs enough information to decide whether advice, a document, a prescription, a referral, follow-up, or a redirect is appropriate.
- Phone, video, secure forms, and messages can all be legitimate channels when the clinical question fits the format.
- Medicare may cover some telehealth consultations, but private services can also charge clear out-of-pocket fees.
- Urgent symptoms, examination needs, complex diagnosis, or unsafe requests should move to in-person or urgent care.
</KeyTakeaway>

## What happens in a telehealth consultation

A telehealth consultation usually starts before the practitioner gives advice. The service needs enough information to understand who you are, what you are asking for, and whether remote assessment is suitable.

A typical pathway includes:

1. You provide identity and contact details.
2. You explain symptoms, timing, severity, relevant history, medicines, allergies, and current concerns.
3. You consent to the remote process and understand privacy and cost information.
4. The practitioner reviews whether the problem can be assessed remotely.
5. The practitioner may ask follow-up questions, use phone or video, review uploaded information, or request more context.
6. The outcome may be advice, a certificate, an eScript, a referral, a pathology or imaging request, a decline, or a recommendation to seek in-person care.
7. The consultation and decision are documented.

This is why a telehealth consultation should not feel like a vending machine for documents or medicines. The outcome follows the assessment.

<EvidenceNote title="Telehealth is mainstream, but conditional" source="Australian Government Department of Health">
The Department of Health describes telehealth as healthcare delivered by phone or video and says many telehealth services continue in Australia. It also frames telehealth as remote care used when the provider decides a physical examination is not needed.
</EvidenceNote>

## What information the practitioner needs

The most important part of a telehealth consultation is usually the history. Remote care relies on the information that can be gathered without hands-on examination.

Useful information includes:

- the main symptom or request
- when it started and whether it is improving or worsening
- severity and any red flags
- current medicines, allergies, and recent medication changes
- relevant medical conditions
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where relevant
- recent test results, hospital discharge summaries, or specialist letters if you have them
- photos only when the service specifically asks for images and provides a secure upload process
- your usual GP, pharmacy, or treating team details when continuity matters

Short answers can make the consultation less safe. "I need a script" is not the same as a medicine history, allergy check, contraindication screen, and review of whether the medicine is still suitable.

## Phone, video, forms, and messages

Different telehealth formats collect different kinds of information. None is automatically better for every clinical question.

| Format | How it works | Useful when | Main limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Video | Real-time consultation with sight and sound | More interactive discussion, some visual observations, follow-up, mental health, selected skin concerns | Still cannot replace hands-on examination or procedures |
| Phone | Real-time voice consultation | Advice, triage, follow-up, medicine questions, patients with limited internet | No visual cues or physical examination |
| Secure form | Structured symptoms, history, medicines, allergies, and consent submitted for review | Routine, low-risk requests where written information may be enough | Poorly designed or incomplete forms can miss risk |
| Secure message | Written follow-up or clarification | Extra questions, results discussion, administrative follow-up | Not suitable for urgent or rapidly changing symptoms |
| Hybrid | A combination of form, uploaded information, phone, video, or records | Problems that need more than one information source | The practitioner may still need in-person care |

The right format depends on the decision being made. A first presentation of severe symptoms, a stable repeat request, and a follow-up question do not need the same kind of consultation.

## What can and cannot be decided remotely

Telehealth often works best when the decision is mostly history-based, follow-up based, or supported by clear records.

It may fit:

- low-risk symptom advice
- triage about whether GP, pharmacy, urgent care, or emergency care is the next step
- follow-up after a previous consultation
- repeat medicine review where the medicine is established and the history is clear
- some short-illness or carer's leave evidence where remote assessment is enough
- mental health check-ins when risk is low and the person can communicate safely
- clarification about side effects, interactions, or next steps

It may not fit when examination, observations, pathology, imaging, procedures, immunisation, complex medicine changes, or continuity from a usual GP are needed.

<CareBoundary title="Remote review should stop when the information is not enough">
If symptoms are severe, fast-changing, unclear, high risk, or examination-dependent, the safer outcome may be GP clinic, urgent care, emergency care, pathology, imaging, or specialist review. A decline or redirect is a safety feature, not a failure of the consultation.
</CareBoundary>

## Costs, Medicare, and private fees

Telehealth and Medicare are related, but they are not the same thing.

Some telehealth consultations can be billed through Medicare when the consultation type, provider, patient eligibility, and MBS item rules apply. For many GP telehealth items, an established clinical relationship with the practice may be relevant, with limited exceptions.

Private telehealth is different. A private service may charge a listed out-of-pocket fee and not process the consultation as a Medicare item. That does not automatically make it unlawful or lower quality. It means the billing model is different.

<PolicyNote title="Medicare eligibility is conditional" source="MBS Online">
MBS telehealth items are permanent for many services, but eligibility depends on the item rules, provider, patient circumstances, and billing pathway. Patients should not assume every telehealth consultation is bulk billed or rebated.
</PolicyNote>

Before relying on the price, check:

- the upfront fee
- whether a Medicare rebate applies to this specific consultation
- whether the provider bulk bills
- what happens if the request is declined or redirected
- whether there are pharmacy, pathology, imaging, or specialist costs outside the consultation

## Certificates, eScripts, referrals, and follow-up

A telehealth consultation can sometimes lead to a document, eScript, referral, pathology request, imaging request, or written advice. Those outcomes are not separate products. They depend on the clinical review.

For example:

- a certificate needs an assessment and dates that fit the information provided
- an eScript needs medicine history, safety screening, contraindication checks, and suitability
- a referral needs enough clinical context to explain why another service is being asked to review you
- a test request needs a clinical reason and a plan for follow-up
- written advice needs enough information to be useful and safe

Be cautious with any service that frames the outcome as guaranteed before assessment. The safer order is information first, clinical review second, outcome third.

## Privacy and records

Telehealth can involve sensitive information in forms, uploads, messages, images, video, and records. That makes privacy and record handling part of the consultation, not a side issue.

Before sharing health information, check whether the service explains:

- what information it collects
- why the information is needed
- who can access it
- how records, images, prescriptions, and documents are handled
- how to contact the service about privacy concerns
- how to make a complaint
- when information may need to be shared for safety, legal, or care-continuity reasons

Avoid sending health details through ordinary social media messages, workplace accounts, public comments, or unsecured channels when a safer healthcare pathway exists.

## When to use urgent or in-person care

<DecisionBox title="Where a telehealth consultation fits">
### May fit telehealth
- The problem is low risk, the history is clear, no urgent symptoms are present, and the practitioner can get enough information remotely.
- The service can ask follow-up questions, document the assessment, and redirect care if remote review is not enough.
### Needs in-person care
- The decision depends on examination, observations, pathology, imaging, procedures, immunisation, complex medicine changes, or continuity from a usual GP.
- Identity, history, symptoms, medicine details, or the requested document standard are too uncertain for remote review.
### Urgent care
- Chest pain, severe breathing trouble, stroke signs, collapse, severe allergic reaction, uncontrolled bleeding, poisoning, overdose, major injury, or immediate self-harm risk should bypass routine telehealth.
- Call 000 or use local urgent care for emergency symptoms.
</DecisionBox>

## How to prepare

Good preparation makes telehealth safer and more efficient.

Before you start, have ready:

- your main concern in one sentence
- a symptom timeline
- severity and what has changed
- current medicines, allergies, supplements, and recent antibiotics
- relevant medical conditions
- pregnancy or breastfeeding status where relevant
- recent test results or discharge summaries
- clear photos only if requested through a secure process
- your usual GP or pharmacy details if continuity matters
- questions you want answered

Use a quiet private place if possible. If you are using video, check battery, internet, camera, and microphone before the appointment. If symptoms are urgent, do not spend time preparing for routine telehealth; use emergency or urgent care first.

## How to choose a telehealth service

A legitimate service should make the healthcare process understandable before you enter sensitive information or pay.

Look for:

- a real Australian business name and contact pathway
- clear pricing before payment
- privacy, terms, refund, and complaints information
- meaningful questions about symptoms, history, medicines, allergies, pregnancy, risk, and red flags where relevant
- practitioner accountability for clinical decisions
- a way for the practitioner to ask follow-up questions
- plain information that a request may be declined or redirected
- no guarantees about certificates, prescriptions, diagnosis, or approval

Ahpra's public register is the right place to check practitioner registration when a practitioner name or registration number is provided. Registration is a starting point, not the whole quality check.

## Common misconceptions

**"Telehealth is just a quick form."** A form can be part of a real consultation, but the important step is practitioner review.

**"Telehealth is always the same as seeing a doctor in person."** The professional standard still applies, but the tools are different. Remote care cannot perform a physical examination.

**"Medicare means the consultation is free."** Some telehealth is bulk billed, some is rebated with a gap, and some is private fee. Billing rules are separate from clinical suitability.

**"Private telehealth is not real healthcare."** Private services can be legitimate, but they should be transparent about pricing, privacy, practitioner review, and decline pathways.

**"A telehealth doctor must issue what I asked for."** The practitioner may provide a different outcome, ask for more information, decline, or redirect care.
