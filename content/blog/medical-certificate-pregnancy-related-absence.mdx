Pregnancy affects every woman differently. Some continue work with minimal impact. Others experience severe nausea, exhaustion, pelvic pain, or complications that make working unsafe or impossible - sometimes for weeks at a time. Australian workplace law provides clear protections for pregnant employees, including sick leave entitlements, employer safety obligations, and anti-discrimination rights. Here is what you are entitled to and how to access it.

## Pregnancy-Related Illness Is Personal Leave

Under the Fair Work Act 2009 and the National Employment Standards (NES), personal leave covers any illness or injury that prevents an employee from performing their role. Pregnancy-related illness is treated identically. Morning sickness that prevents you getting out of bed is personal leave. Pelvic girdle pain that makes it impossible to sit at a desk is personal leave. Severe fatigue, pregnancy-related anaemia, threatened miscarriage - all personal leave.

Full-time and part-time employees accrue 10 days of paid personal leave per year. This is available for pregnancy-related illness in the same way as any other illness. Using sick leave during pregnancy does not affect your parental leave entitlement - they are completely separate.

<Callout variant="info">Your medical certificate does not need to mention pregnancy. It will state you are unfit for work due to a medical condition. Whether to disclose the cause of your absence is your choice. You are not legally required to inform your employer of your pregnancy until you make a formal parental leave application.</Callout>

## Common Pregnancy-Related Conditions That Warrant Time Off

Pregnancy commonly prevents work due to:

- Nausea and vomiting, particularly in the first trimester
- Hyperemesis gravidarum - severe morning sickness requiring medical treatment
- Extreme fatigue, common in the first and third trimesters
- Pelvic girdle pain (PGP) or symphysis pubis dysfunction (SPD)
- Pregnancy-related anaemia causing fatigue and breathlessness
- Pre-eclampsia or gestational hypertension
- Gestational diabetes with unstable blood sugar levels
- Threatened miscarriage with prescribed rest
- Mental health conditions during pregnancy
- Complications requiring reduced activity or monitoring

For each of these, a medical certificate confirms unfitness for work for the relevant period.

## Hyperemesis Gravidarum: When Morning Sickness Is Serious

Standard morning sickness affects around 70-80% of pregnant women and for most is manageable with adjustments. Hyperemesis gravidarum (HG) affects an estimated 1-3% and is significantly more serious.

HG is characterised by:

- Persistent severe nausea and vomiting multiple times daily
- Inability to keep food or fluids down
- Weight loss exceeding 5% of pre-pregnancy body weight
- Signs of dehydration
- Functional impact that makes working impossible

HG often requires active medical treatment - antiemetic medications, IV fluids, and in severe cases hospitalisation. It is not something to push through. If your morning sickness is severe, a GP assessment is appropriate to determine whether treatment is needed and to provide medical documentation for extended leave.

<Callout variant="warning">If you are unable to keep any fluids down for 24 hours, are experiencing dizziness or confusion, or have not urinated for 8 or more hours, seek urgent medical care rather than telehealth. Severe dehydration in pregnancy requires prompt treatment.</Callout>

## Your Workplace Rights During Pregnancy

### Anti-Discrimination Protections

It is unlawful under the Sex Discrimination Act 1984 and state anti-discrimination legislation to treat an employee less favourably because of pregnancy. This includes:

- Making decisions about employment, promotion, or duties based on pregnancy
- Treating pregnancy-related absence differently from other illness
- Dismissing or threatening to dismiss an employee because of pregnancy or pregnancy-related illness
- Creating a hostile work environment related to pregnancy

If you experience adverse treatment connected to your pregnancy or pregnancy-related illness, this may be unlawful discrimination. The Australian Human Rights Commission and state equal opportunity commissions handle complaints.

### The Safe Jobs Provision

Section 77A of the Fair Work Act includes a Safe Jobs provision. If you are pregnant and your current role poses a risk to your health or safety due to pregnancy, your employer must transfer you to an appropriate safe job at the same pay rate.

If a safe job is not available and your doctor provides a "no safe job" certificate confirming that continuing in your current role or a suitable alternative is not appropriate during pregnancy, you are entitled to no safe job leave:

- Paid leave for employees with 12 or more months of continuous service with the employer
- Unpaid leave for employees with less than 12 months of service
- This applies even if you do not yet qualify for parental leave

Safe job situations arise for roles involving heavy lifting, exposure to hazardous substances, shift work that becomes medically inadvisable, sustained standing, physically demanding work contraindicated in late pregnancy, or any workplace exposure that poses a risk.

### Flexible Work Requests

Under the Fair Work Act, pregnant employees have the right to request flexible working arrangements. Employers can only refuse on reasonable business grounds and must provide reasons in writing. Starting parental leave early, modified hours, or adjusted duties are all options that can be requested formally.

## Getting a Medical Certificate During Pregnancy

For most pregnancy-related absences, the certificate process is straightforward:

- Telehealth is appropriate for morning sickness, fatigue, and other common presentations where physical examination is not required
- For complications involving bleeding, high blood pressure, or pelvic pain requiring examination, in-person assessment is more appropriate
- Your treating obstetrician, midwife, or regular GP may be the most appropriate source of certificates for ongoing complications they are already managing

What to tell the doctor:

- Your current gestational age in weeks
- Your specific symptoms and how they affect your ability to work
- Whether you have been treated previously for this presentation
- Your occupation and specific duties, particularly if they involve physical demands or hazardous exposures

For extended or complex absences, your treating obstetrician is better placed than a new consultation to provide comprehensive documentation that reflects your full clinical picture.

## Sick Leave and Parental Leave: How They Interact

Personal leave and parental leave are separate entitlements. Key points:

- You can use personal leave for pregnancy-related illness right up until your parental leave starts
- Using sick leave does not reduce your parental leave entitlement in any way
- Parental leave begins on the date you choose to nominate, subject to required notice periods
- You cannot be required to commence parental leave because you are unwell

If your pregnancy requires extended time away from work near your due date, this is personal leave, not early parental leave. The distinction matters for your entitlements and should be clearly reflected in your documentation.

## Miscarriage and Early Pregnancy Loss

Miscarriage and early pregnancy loss are covered by personal leave as illness or injury. Additionally, since 2023 amendments to the Fair Work Act, employees are entitled to 2 days of paid compassionate leave when their pregnancy, or their partner's pregnancy, ends other than by birth. This compassionate leave is in addition to, and does not reduce, personal leave.

If you are dealing with pregnancy loss and your employer is creating difficulty with leave entitlements, the Fair Work Ombudsman provides guidance and complaint services.

## When Telehealth Is and Is Not Appropriate During Pregnancy

Telehealth is appropriate for:

- Medical certificates for morning sickness and nausea
- Fatigue assessments
- Mental health support during pregnancy
- Follow-up for previously documented conditions where physical review is not required

Telehealth is not appropriate as the management pathway for:

- Active bleeding at any stage of pregnancy
- Signs of preterm labour
- Reduced or absent foetal movement
- Uncontrolled blood pressure
- Any presentation where physical monitoring of you or your baby is clinically necessary

<Callout variant="emergency">If you experience significant bleeding, severe abdominal pain, significantly reduced foetal movement, or symptoms of preterm labour, contact your hospital's maternity unit or call 000 immediately. These require urgent assessment that cannot be provided remotely.</Callout>
