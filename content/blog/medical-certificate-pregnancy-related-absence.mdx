Pregnancy itself is not a sickness. But pregnancy-related illness, pregnancy complications, injury, severe symptoms, or unsafe work duties can affect whether someone is fit for work. That is where medical evidence matters.

The practical distinction is this:

- If the person is **unfit for work because of illness or injury**, personal leave may be relevant.
- If the person is **fit for work but the usual job is unsafe because of pregnancy-related risk or workplace hazards**, Safe Job or no safe job leave may be relevant.
- If the pregnancy ends or there is a stillbirth, miscarriage, or related loss, separate compassionate leave and parental-leave rules may apply.
- If symptoms suggest a maternity emergency, the priority is urgent clinical review, not workplace paperwork.

This guide is general information for Australian employees and employers. Awards, enterprise agreements, employment contracts, and individual circumstances can change the practical answer.

## Pregnancy-Related Illness and Personal Leave

Fair Work guidance lists sick leave as one of the leave types pregnant employees may be able to access during pregnancy. The usual personal-leave logic still applies: the employee needs to be unable to work because of illness or injury, and the employer can ask for evidence.

Pregnancy-related examples that may affect work capacity include:

- Nausea and vomiting
- Hyperemesis gravidarum
- Severe fatigue
- Pelvic girdle pain, back pain, or mobility limitation
- Pregnancy-related anaemia
- Gestational hypertension or pre-eclampsia concerns
- Gestational diabetes complications
- Threatened miscarriage or bleeding concerns
- Mental health symptoms during pregnancy
- Complications requiring monitoring, reduced duties, or rest

A certificate for personal leave should focus on functional capacity: whether the person was unfit for work, the relevant dates, and any work restrictions if partial capacity is possible.

<Callout variant="info">A routine medical certificate does not usually need to name the diagnosis. It can state that the person is unfit for work due to a medical condition. Disclosure becomes more relevant when the certificate is being used for pregnancy-specific workplace risk, Safe Job, no safe job leave, or parental leave timing.</Callout>

## Evidence Rules: What Employers Can Ask For

Fair Work says an employee has to tell their employer they are taking sick or carer's leave as soon as possible, including the expected length of absence if known.

An employer can ask for evidence that shows the employee was genuinely entitled to the leave. This can be requested for as little as one day or less off work. Medical certificates and statutory declarations are examples of acceptable evidence, but the evidence has to satisfy a reasonable person.

The evidence should usually answer:

- Was the person unfit for work?
- What dates or period does the incapacity cover?
- Are there restrictions on duties, hours, lifting, standing, travel, driving, or exposure?
- Is review needed before returning to usual duties?
- Is the person fit for some work but not their normal role?

Fair Work also says it does not consider it reasonable for an employer to attend an employee's medical appointment unless the employee requests this, or to contact the employee's doctor for further information.

## Common Pregnancy Reasons for Time Away From Work

Pregnancy symptoms vary widely. Some people can continue usual duties with minor adjustments. Others need short absences, modified duties, or extended leave.

### Nausea, Vomiting, and Hyperemesis

Mild nausea may be manageable with workplace adjustments. Severe vomiting is different. Pregnancy Birth and Baby describes hyperemesis gravidarum as severe vomiting during pregnancy and advises speaking to a maternity care provider if severe vomiting occurs.

Work impact can include dehydration, inability to commute safely, inability to stand for long periods, poor sleep, medication side effects, or inability to keep food or fluids down.

### Pelvic Pain, Back Pain, and Mobility Limits

Pelvic girdle pain, back pain, sciatica-like symptoms, and mobility limitation can make some work duties unsafe or unrealistic. The certificate may need to specify restrictions, such as avoiding heavy lifting, prolonged standing, repeated stairs, long driving, or physically demanding tasks.

### Blood Pressure, Headache, Vision, and Swelling

High blood pressure and pre-eclampsia concerns need clinical assessment. Severe headache, blurry vision, sudden swelling, dizziness, or upper abdominal pain should not be treated as routine workplace absence without medical review.

### Mental Health During Pregnancy

Anxiety, depression, panic symptoms, trauma, grief, insomnia, and severe stress can affect work capacity during pregnancy. A certificate can support personal leave where symptoms make the person unfit for work, while ongoing support should come from a treating GP, psychologist, psychiatrist, midwife, or maternity service.

## Safe Job vs Personal Leave

Personal leave and Safe Job evidence solve different problems.

Use personal leave when:

- The person is unfit for work due to illness or injury.
- Symptoms prevent attendance or normal duties.
- The issue is temporary incapacity rather than workplace risk alone.

Use Safe Job evidence when:

- The person is pregnant and fit for work.
- Their current role is not appropriate for a period.
- The issue is pregnancy-related illness, risk, or hazards connected with the position.
- A medical certificate or other evidence is needed to support transfer to safer duties.

Fair Work says all pregnant employees, including casuals, have the right to a safe job. If an appropriate safe job is available, the employer must transfer the employee to it. The safe job must have the same ordinary hours as the current position unless the employee agrees to different hours.

## No Safe Job Leave

If no appropriate safe job is available, Fair Work says the employee can take no safe job leave. This may be paid or unpaid.

Paid no safe job leave applies where the employee:

- Is entitled to unpaid parental leave
- Has complied with the notice and evidence requirements for unpaid parental leave

Unpaid no safe job leave applies where the employee is not entitled to unpaid parental leave.

A useful no-safe-job certificate should be specific enough to help the workplace act on it. It may need to say that the employee is fit for work, but that it is not recommended for them to continue in their usual position for a stated period because of pregnancy-related illness, risk, or workplace hazards.

## The Final 6 Weeks Before the Expected Birth

Different rules can apply close to the expected birth date. Fair Work says if a pregnant employee wants to work in the 6 weeks before the due date, the employer can ask for a medical certificate within 7 days stating whether the employee is fit for work.

If the employee is fit for work, the certificate may also need to state whether it is not recommended for them to continue in their current position because of pregnancy-related illness, pregnancy-related risk, or hazards connected with the job.

If the certificate says the employee is fit but the normal job is unsafe, Safe Job or no safe job leave may apply. If the certificate says the employee is not fit for work, or evidence is not provided within the required timeframe, Fair Work says the employer may be able to direct the employee to start continuous unpaid parental leave.

## Flexible Working Arrangements

Fair Work says pregnant employees can request flexible working arrangements in certain circumstances. Examples include changes to hours, work patterns, or work location.

Flexible work is not the same as sick leave. It is usually relevant when the person can still work but needs changed conditions, such as:

- Later starts due to morning symptoms
- Shorter shifts
- Work from home where the role allows
- Reduced standing or walking
- Fewer night shifts
- Adjusted travel expectations

Employers must follow the rules for responding to flexible work requests, including written responses and reasonable business grounds where a request is refused.

## Pregnancy Loss, Miscarriage, and Compassionate Leave

Pregnancy loss can involve several different leave categories depending on timing and circumstances.

Fair Work guidance says employees may be entitled to 2 days of compassionate leave per occasion for miscarriage before 20 weeks. Compassionate leave can also apply for stillbirth or where a child in the immediate family or household is stillborn.

Unpaid special parental leave may apply if an eligible pregnant employee is not fit for work because the pregnancy ends after 12 weeks other than by birth of a living child, where the child was not stillborn.

Personal leave may also be relevant if the person is unfit for work because of illness, injury, treatment, recovery, or mental health impact.

## Privacy and Discrimination

Fair Work says employees cannot be discriminated against because they are pregnant. This includes being fired, demoted, or treated differently from other employees because of pregnancy.

The Australian Human Rights Commission also identifies pregnancy as a protected attribute in employment discrimination complaints.

For certificates, the privacy principle is simple: give the workplace enough information to support the leave or adjustment being requested, without disclosing unnecessary clinical detail. Diagnosis disclosure may be unnecessary for routine personal leave, but may be needed for pregnancy-specific Safe Job evidence or parental-leave timing rules.

## When Pregnancy Symptoms Should Not Wait

Some symptoms need prompt maternity or emergency review. Better Health Channel advises calling a doctor, midwife, or hospital immediately for concerning symptoms, and calling Triple Zero in an emergency.

Seek urgent advice for:

- Any bleeding during pregnancy, especially heavy bleeding or bleeding with pain
- Severe abdominal or pelvic pain
- Severe vomiting or inability to keep fluids down
- Dizziness, fainting, blackouts, or fits
- Severe headache, blurry vision, or sudden swelling of hands, face, or feet
- Fever or feeling seriously unwell
- Reduced or absent fetal movement
- Waters breaking before labour begins
- Regular, increasingly intense contractions before 37 weeks
- Chest pain, severe breathlessness, or collapse

<Callout variant="emergency">If you think you or the baby may be at risk, contact your doctor, midwife, maternity unit, or hospital immediately. In an emergency, call Triple Zero (000).</Callout>

## Practical Certificate Checklist

Before asking for a certificate, write down:

- Gestational age in weeks
- Symptoms and when they started
- Whether the issue is illness, injury, workplace risk, or a mix
- Specific job duties affected
- Current work hours, commute, lifting, standing, driving, night shifts, or hazards
- Whether modified duties would be possible
- Dates already missed and expected return date
- Whether the certificate is for personal leave, Safe Job, no safe job leave, flexible work evidence, or another purpose

This helps the clinician write evidence that matches the actual workplace issue, instead of producing a vague certificate that leaves the employer guessing.
