Remote work has created a particular kind of pressure around illness. In an office, being sick means being visibly absent - the decision to take a sick day is clear. At home, the logic becomes muddled: you are already there, your laptop is open, maybe you could just get through the morning. This thinking is both wrong and counterproductive. Here is why, and what your actual rights and obligations look like.

## The Core Misunderstanding

Sick leave is not about where your body is. It is about whether you are fit for work.

The Fair Work Act defines the entitlement as leave for when an employee "is not fit for work because of a personal illness or injury." There is no location qualifier. An employee working from home is as entitled to personal leave as an office-based employee. The fact that you are physically in the same building as your workstation is irrelevant.

This seems obvious stated plainly, but the cultural pressure of remote work makes people act as if proximity to a laptop means they should be using it.

## When Working Through Illness From Home Is Acceptable

There is a narrow window where light work while mildly unwell makes practical sense:

- Symptoms are mild and not affecting concentration or judgment
- The work is low-stakes and does not require sustained cognitive effort
- You can genuinely rest between tasks
- You are not setting a precedent with your employer that you are always available when sick

Monitoring an inbox at 20% capacity for a day when you have a mild cold and nothing urgent is due is different from attempting to meet deadlines, attend video meetings, and make decisions while running a fever and taking codeine.

The problem is that most people overestimate their functional capacity when unwell. Cognitive impairment from fever, inflammation, dehydration, and poor sleep is well-documented and often worse than the individual perceives. Work quality suffers more than is apparent in the moment.

## When You Should Take a Full Sick Day

Take a proper sick day - no messages, no checking email, actual rest - when:

- You have a fever (temperature above 37.5-38°C)
- Your concentration or cognitive function is significantly impaired
- You are taking medications with sedative or concentration-affecting side effects
- Physical symptoms are requiring significant management (frequent trips to the bathroom, significant pain, difficulty breathing)
- Your mental state is significantly compromised (acute anxiety, depression episode, severe stress)
- You have a contagious illness (even from home, if you are on video calls)
- Your doctor has certified you as unfit for work

The last point is unambiguous. A medical certificate is a clinical assessment that you are not fit for your duties. "Not fit for work" does not have an asterisk for "unless you work from home."

## The Presenteeism Problem

Presenteeism - being present at work but not functioning properly - is a well-documented problem. At home, it becomes invisible: you look like you are working, your status shows as available, but you are producing substandard output while preventing recovery.

Research consistently shows:

- Cognitive performance on complex tasks drops significantly during fever and acute illness
- Error rates increase during illness
- Total recovery time is longer when people do not rest during acute illness
- Presenteeism costs more in lost productivity than absenteeism in most studies

The business case for rest is solid: a person who rests for two days and returns at full capacity produces more net output than someone who works at 40% capacity for five days. The individual arithmetic is even clearer when you factor in how much worse you feel and how long recovery takes when you do not rest.

## Remote Work and Mental Health Days

Mental health sick days present the same challenge in a remote work context, amplified. In an office, taking a mental health day means you are visibly absent. At home, the expectation to be "around" persists.

A mental health sick day means:

- Not working
- Not checking work messages
- Doing things that support recovery (rest, gentle activity, whatever your mental health requires)
- Not being available for work demands

The Fair Work Ombudsman confirms that mental health conditions are valid grounds for personal leave. This applies regardless of whether you work from home. If your mental state is such that you are not fit to perform your role effectively and safely, you are entitled to take that leave.

Being at home when you have a mental health day does not mean your employer is entitled to contact you about work matters. You are on leave.

## Your Legal Entitlements Are Identical

To be direct about the law:

- Full-time and part-time employees working from home accrue the same 10 days of personal/carer's leave per year as office-based employees
- The evidence requirements are the same (a certificate is required when your employer or award requires it)
- The employer's obligations are the same (cannot require diagnosis disclosure, cannot refuse valid evidence, cannot take adverse action for legitimate absences)
- Location of the work arrangement does not modify any of these entitlements

If your employment contract implies that working from home means you should work through illness, that provision is likely unenforceable to the extent it conflicts with the National Employment Standards.

<Callout variant="info">

The NES sets minimum conditions that cannot be undercut by contract. A contractual term saying "remote workers cannot take sick leave when at home" would be void to the extent it conflicts with the personal leave entitlement under the Fair Work Act. The NES is a floor, not a ceiling.

</Callout>

## How to Notify Your Employer

The notification process is the same as for in-person workers. Notify your employer as soon as reasonably practicable - ideally before your workday starts, or at the start of it.

A message that works:

> "Hi [manager name], I'm unwell and not fit for work today. I'll be taking a personal leave day. I'll let you know if I expect to be out longer."

You do not need to:
- Explain your symptoms in detail
- Justify why being at home does not change your situation
- Offer to "check in" or do light work
- Specify whether your illness is physical or mental

If your employer responds by asking you to work despite the notification, you are entitled to decline. If the pressure continues or becomes a pattern, document the communications and consider raising it with HR or the Fair Work Ombudsman.

## Getting a Certificate When You Work From Home

The same rules apply for certificates: most employers require one for absences of two or more consecutive days, and some may require one for single days in certain circumstances.

Telehealth is particularly practical when you work from home and are sick: you are already in your home environment, you do not need to leave to access a consultation, and the certificate is delivered by email - directly submittable to HR. The irony is that the technology enabling remote work also enables remote healthcare; both are better suited to how people actually live.

## Setting Expectations for Your Team

If you manage other people who work remotely, your own behaviour sets norms. If you send messages while sick, your team learns that being sick does not excuse non-responsiveness. If you take proper sick days and communicate clearly about them, you create permission for your team to do the same.

The sustainable norm for remote teams is identical to the office norm: when someone is sick, they are on leave and not expected to be responsive. The only difference is that it requires more explicit communication to establish, because the physical absence that makes the norm obvious in an office does not exist.
