According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), allergic rhinitis (hay fever) affected approximately 4.6 million Australians in 2017-18, representing around 19% of the population. It is one of the most prevalent chronic conditions in the country, with the Australasian Society of Clinical Immunology and Allergy (ASCIA) noting that prevalence has been rising over recent decades. Despite being widely regarded as a minor inconvenience, hay fever causes significant impairment to sleep, concentration, and quality of life, and is closely associated with asthma in a proportion of sufferers.

## What Is Allergic Rhinitis?

Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction in the nasal mucosa. When a sensitised person inhales an allergen - pollen, dust mite, pet dander, or mould - the immune system releases histamine and other mediators. These cause the classic symptoms: nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, sneezing, and itching.

ASCIA distinguishes two patterns:

- **Seasonal allergic rhinitis**: triggered by pollens, most prominent in spring and summer
- **Perennial allergic rhinitis**: triggered year-round by dust mites, pet dander, or mould

Many Australians have both patterns - seasonal triggers on top of a perennial baseline.

## Triggers: What Causes Hay Fever in Australia

| Trigger | When it peaks | How to reduce exposure |
|---|---|---|
| Grass pollen | September to January (varies by region) | Check AusPollen forecasts, keep windows closed on high-pollen days |
| Tree pollen | Late winter to spring | Avoid outdoor activity in the morning when pollen concentrations peak |
| Weed pollen | Late summer to autumn | Wear wraparound glasses outdoors, shower after being outside |
| Dust mites | Year-round (worse in humid conditions) | Wash bedding weekly at 60C, use dust-mite-proof mattress covers |
| Pet dander | Year-round (cats highest risk) | Keep pets out of bedrooms, use HEPA air filtration |
| Mould spores | Damp or humid conditions | Address moisture sources, run exhaust fans, dehumidify affected rooms |

The AusPollen network (a collaboration between multiple Australian universities and meteorological institutions) provides real-time pollen forecasts at auspollen.com.au. On forecast high-pollen days, pre-medicating with antihistamines before exposure - rather than waiting for symptoms - significantly improves control.

## Symptoms and Diagnosis

Allergic rhinitis symptoms reflect inflammation of the nasal mucosa and, frequently, the conjunctiva:

- Runny or blocked nose
- Sneezing, often in bursts
- Itchy, watery eyes (allergic conjunctivitis, which occurs alongside rhinitis in up to 80% of sufferers)
- Itchy throat, mouth, or ears
- Fatigue from disrupted sleep
- Reduced sense of smell in severe cases

An important distinction from common cold: hay fever does not cause fever, body aches, or productive cough. If those symptoms are present, a viral infection is the more likely diagnosis. ASCIA notes that misidentifying colds as hay fever (and vice versa) is common and leads to inappropriate self-treatment.

Formal diagnosis is typically clinical, based on symptom history and response to antihistamines. Allergy testing (skin prick test or allergen-specific IgE blood testing) confirms sensitisation and is useful when immunotherapy is being considered or when the trigger is unclear.

## Treatment: The ASCIA and RACGP Approach

The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) and ASCIA both endorse a stepwise approach to treatment, starting with the least invasive options and escalating if symptoms are not controlled.

| Treatment | How it works | Best suited for | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-drowsy oral antihistamine | Blocks H1 receptor, reduces sneezing and itch | Mild to moderate symptoms, on-demand use | Works within 30-60 minutes |
| Saline nasal rinse | Mechanically removes pollen and mucus | All severities, safe for daily use | Can be used before other sprays |
| Intranasal corticosteroid spray | Reduces nasal mucosal inflammation | Moderate to severe, particularly nasal congestion | Takes 3-7 days for full effect |
| Antihistamine nasal spray | Local antihistamine effect | Nasal symptoms where tablets are insufficient | Faster onset than steroid sprays |
| Antihistamine eye drops | Targets ocular symptoms directly | Itchy, watery eyes alongside nasal symptoms | Rapid onset |
| Intranasal combination spray | Combines antihistamine and steroid | Moderate-severe rhinitis needing both actions | Rapid antihistamine + slow steroid effect |

**Intranasal corticosteroid sprays** - which include fluticasone propionate, mometasone, and budesonide - are available without prescription in Australia (following TGA scheduling decisions) and are the most effective class for moderate-to-severe symptoms. ASCIA notes that consistent daily use during the allergy season, beginning before symptoms peak, provides substantially better control than using them only when symptoms appear.

**Oral antihistamines**: ASCIA and RACGP recommend second-generation antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) over first-generation antihistamines (promethazine) due to the superior safety profile and absence of significant sedation.

## The Hay Fever-Asthma Connection

ASCIA and Asthma Australia both identify allergic rhinitis as a significant risk factor for asthma development and as a trigger for asthma exacerbations. The AIHW National Health Survey data shows that asthma and hay fever co-occur in approximately 40% of asthma patients. This is described as the "united airway" concept - the upper and lower airways share a mucosal lining and common inflammatory mechanisms.

For people with both conditions, treating hay fever well is part of asthma management. ASCIA recommends that all patients with asthma be assessed for allergic rhinitis, and vice versa.

> **Warning:** Pollen exposure during high-pollen events (thunderstorm asthma) can trigger life-threatening bronchospasm, even in people with no prior asthma diagnosis. If you experience wheezing, chest tightness, or difficulty breathing during hay fever season, seek medical assessment immediately.

## Allergen Immunotherapy

For patients with confirmed allergen sensitisation whose symptoms are not adequately controlled with medications, ASCIA recommends consideration of allergen immunotherapy (AIT). AIT works by gradually desensitising the immune response through repeated, escalating doses of the allergen.

Two forms are available in Australia:

- **Subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT)**: weekly injections administered at a clinic over 3-5 years, followed by a maintenance phase
- **Sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT)**: daily drops or tablets placed under the tongue, taken at home

Both have strong evidence for reducing both rhinitis and asthma symptoms, and for preventing progression of rhinitis to asthma. The TGA has approved several SLIT products for grass pollen, the most common allergen trigger in Australia. AIT requires specialist assessment (allergist or immunologist) and is not appropriate for all patients.

## When to See a Doctor

See your GP or consider telehealth if:

- Over-the-counter treatments are not providing adequate control after 2-4 weeks
- Symptoms are significantly disrupting sleep or daytime function
- You have asthma and hay fever is making it worse
- You are considering allergen immunotherapy (requires specialist referral)
- You want prescription-strength treatment or a management plan

Seek urgent care if you experience:
- Difficulty breathing or wheezing
- Severe facial pain (which may indicate sinusitis requiring antibiotic assessment)
- Vision changes

## Managing the Pollen Season

Practical measures based on ASCIA guidance:

- Monitor AusPollen forecasts (auspollen.com.au) and restrict outdoor exposure on high days
- Stay indoors between 10am and 3pm on high-pollen days when concentrations peak
- Keep car and home windows closed on high-pollen days
- Shower and change clothes after spending time outdoors
- Wear wraparound sunglasses to reduce eye exposure
- Dry laundry indoors on high-pollen days

These measures complement medication but rarely eliminate symptoms alone in moderately sensitive individuals.
