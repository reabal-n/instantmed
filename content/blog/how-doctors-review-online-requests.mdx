When you submit a request to a telehealth service for a medical certificate or prescription, something specific happens on the other side. Understanding that process addresses one of the most common concerns people have about online healthcare: is this a real clinical assessment, or is it automated?

The answer, for legitimate services, is that it is a real clinical assessment made by a real doctor. Here is what that actually looks like.

## The Doctor Receives a Structured Patient Intake

When you submit a request, the doctor receives a compiled view of everything you have provided: your symptoms, how long you have had them, relevant medical history, current medications, and what you are requesting. This is displayed in a format that allows efficient clinical review - not a raw form, but a structured clinical summary.

The doctor's task is to assess whether the request is clinically appropriate based on that information. This is not a tick-box exercise. The Medical Board of Australia's "Good Medical Practice: A Code of Conduct for Doctors in Australia" requires that doctors:

- Take an adequate history relevant to the presenting situation
- Form a clinical opinion based on the history and other available information
- Act in the patient's best interests
- Recognise when in-person assessment is necessary

These standards apply to telehealth consultations exactly as they apply to in-person consultations.

## What Doctors Assess for Medical Certificates

For a medical certificate request, the doctor's assessment focuses on:

**Symptom plausibility:** Does the description of symptoms make clinical sense? Are the symptoms consistent with the condition implied? A person describing severe nausea, vomiting, and inability to eat is presenting clinical features consistent with an inability to work; a vague "feeling unwell" with no elaboration may require follow-up.

**Functional impact:** The certificate is for being unable to work, not for having symptoms. The doctor assesses whether the described symptoms would reasonably render the patient unable to perform their duties. A mild sore throat is less likely to make an office worker unfit for work than a fever of 39C with significant fatigue.

**Duration appropriateness:** Is the number of days requested consistent with the described presentation? A single day for a gastroenteritis episode is appropriate; ten days for a sore throat without other contextualising information would prompt questions.

**Red flags:** Are there any features in the history that suggest the patient needs urgent or in-person care rather than a certificate? Chest pain, neurological symptoms, severe abdominal pain, or other concerning features would result in a recommendation for in-person assessment rather than certification.

**Medical history consistency:** Does the patient's existing medical history provide context for the current presentation? A patient with known inflammatory bowel disease describing a flare has established context; the same symptoms without that history might warrant different advice.

## What Doctors Assess for Prescription Requests

Prescription requests involve additional clinical dimensions:

**Established medication:** Is the patient already taking this medication? Repeat prescription requests are appropriate for established patients; requests for new medications via telehealth are generally not, as they require a full assessment.

**Indication and appropriateness:** Is the medication appropriate for the described condition? Does the dose and formulation requested match the indication?

**Safety screening:** Are there contraindications given the patient's medical history, other medications, or reported allergies? Drug interaction checking and allergy review are part of every prescription review.

**Monitoring requirements:** Some medications require recent pathology results before renewal is appropriate. Levothyroxine without recent thyroid function tests, diabetes medications without recent HbA1c, or medications affecting kidney function without a recent renal panel may prompt requests for information or a recommendation to see a GP in person.

**Controlled substance check:** Under the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) Standard for the Uniform Scheduling of Medicines and Poisons (the Poisons Standard), Schedule 8 (Controlled Drugs) generally require in-person assessment and, in most states, additional prescribing authorities. Telehealth services cannot prescribe these via remote review.

<Callout variant="warning">

A request for a Schedule 8 controlled substance (opioids, benzodiazepines, ADHD stimulants) submitted to a telehealth service will be declined. This is not a failure of the service - it reflects appropriate clinical and regulatory boundaries. If you require a controlled substance, you will need to see a GP in person. In some states, specialist assessment and specific prescribing authority are also required.

</Callout>

## When Follow-Up Questions Are Asked

If the initial submission lacks sufficient information to form a clinical opinion, the doctor will ask follow-up questions before deciding. This might look like:

- "When did you last have a HbA1c test, and what was the result?"
- "How long have you been on this medication, and who first prescribed it?"
- "Can you describe the pain more specifically - where exactly, does it radiate anywhere, any associated symptoms?"

This is the asynchronous equivalent of the doctor's mid-consultation questions. The request sits in a pending state until the patient responds. On receipt of the additional information, the doctor completes their assessment.

Requests where the patient does not respond to follow-up within the service's timeframe may be closed without a decision. Services typically notify the patient by email or SMS if their response is needed.

## Why Requests Are Declined

A declined request is not an error or a glitch. It is a clinical decision. Common reasons include:

**Insufficient information:** The submission does not provide enough clinical detail to allow the doctor to form a confident opinion. The patient did not respond to follow-up questions, or the intake was too vague.

**Clinical inconsistency:** The described symptoms are inconsistent with the condition they are attributed to, or the requested duration is out of proportion to the presentation described.

**In-person assessment required:** The symptoms suggest a condition that cannot be safely assessed remotely. Chest pain, severe abdominal symptoms, a child with high fever and stiff neck, or any presentation where examination would materially change management.

**Controlled substance request:** As above, Schedule 8 and certain other medications cannot be prescribed via asynchronous telehealth.

**Inadequate history for a new prescription:** The patient is requesting a medication they have not previously taken, which requires a full clinical assessment.

**Ethical concern:** Features of the request raise concerns about misuse - for example, patterns suggesting medication-seeking behaviour, or a certificate request with specific inconsistencies.

## Clinical Governance and Accountability

A telehealth doctor issuing a certificate or prescription is exercising clinical judgment and bears professional accountability for that judgment. This accountability is identical to an in-person consultation.

AHPRA's notification process applies. If a patient or other person believes a telehealth doctor's conduct fell below the standard expected of a registered practitioner, they can make a notification to AHPRA. AHPRA investigates and, where warranted, can place conditions on registration, suspend, or cancel registration.

Legitimate telehealth services maintain clinical governance structures: peer review of clinical decisions, audit processes, and clinical supervision of junior or recent practitioners. This is consistent with RACGP (Royal Australian College of General Practitioners) standards for general practice.

## Records and Privacy

The consultation generates clinical records, as any medical consultation does. Under the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles, health information is sensitive information subject to specific protections:

- Health information can only be collected with consent (APP 3)
- It must be kept secure and protected from unauthorised access (APP 11)
- It can only be used for the purpose for which it was collected (APP 6)
- Patients have rights to access and correct their own records (APP 12, 13)

The consultation record - your intake, the doctor's clinical notes, and any documents issued - is retained according to standard medical record retention requirements (minimum seven years for adults, longer for minors, under state-territory health records legislation).

You can request access to your own records from any telehealth service. This right is protected by the Privacy Act regardless of the billing model of the service.

## How to Get the Best Outcome

The doctor can only assess what you provide. Giving thorough, accurate, specific information is both the ethical obligation of the patient and the practical means of getting your request processed efficiently.

Be specific about symptoms (not just "I feel sick"), timing (when exactly did this start), functional impact (what specifically can you not do), and any relevant context (known conditions, recent similar episodes, what you have tried for relief).

Accurate information also protects you: a certificate issued based on accurate clinical information has far stronger standing than one based on a vague or incomplete submission.
