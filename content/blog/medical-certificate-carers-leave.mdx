When your child, parent, partner, or another family member is ill and needs your support, Australian workplace law allows you to take time off. Carer's leave draws from the same personal leave pool as sick leave, and the documentation requirements follow a similar pattern - with one key difference: the certificate is for the person being cared for, not for you. Here is exactly what you are entitled to, what documentation you need, and how to get it without adding to an already difficult day.

## Your Carer's Leave Entitlements Under the Fair Work Act

The Fair Work Act 2009 (sections 97-99) establishes carer's leave as part of personal/carer's leave. Full-time and part-time employees accrue 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year. This pool is shared: the same entitlement covers your own illness and your carer responsibilities.

You can take carer's leave when a member of your immediate family or household requires care or support due to:

- A personal illness or injury
- An unexpected emergency

The leave can be used for medical appointments, recovery support at home, hospitalisation visits, or any situation where the family member genuinely requires your presence and care.

<Callout variant="info">Carer's leave is designed for unexpected or acute situations - a child who wakes up with a fever, a parent taken suddenly ill, a partner incapacitated after an accident. Long-term or planned caring arrangements are managed under different provisions. The key word in the legislation is "unexpected."</Callout>

## Who Counts as Immediate Family or Household

Under the Fair Work Act, "immediate family" for carer's leave includes:

- A spouse or de facto partner
- A child (including step-children and adopted children)
- A parent, grandparent, or grandchild
- A sibling
- These same relationships for your spouse or de facto partner (your partner's parents, children, siblings)

"Household member" means any person who lives with you, regardless of whether they are related to you. This can include a housemate or a person you are supporting in the home.

## What Evidence You Need

Your employer can request evidence that you took carer's leave to care for a family or household member who required it. Acceptable evidence under the Fair Work Act includes:

- A medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor for the person you cared for
- A statutory declaration confirming the person required your care due to illness or an unexpected emergency

The certificate requirement operates the same way as for personal sick leave. Most employers only request documentation for absences of two or more consecutive days, though your specific award, enterprise agreement, or workplace policy may differ.

If you cannot reasonably obtain a medical certificate - the illness was brief and resolved without a doctor visit, or a doctor was not accessible - a statutory declaration is a valid alternative.

## What Needs to Be on the Certificate

This certificate is for the person who was ill, not for you. It should confirm:

- The patient's name (the family member or household member who was unwell)
- That the person has a medical condition
- That the condition required care and support
- The dates covered
- The doctor's name, AHPRA registration, and contact details

The certificate does not need to name you as the carer. It does not need to specify the diagnosis. It simply needs to confirm that the person was genuinely ill and required care during the relevant period.

## Getting a Certificate for a Family Member via Telehealth

### For sick children

A parent or guardian describing a child's symptoms is a standard telehealth scenario. You describe what the child is experiencing - fever level and duration, associated symptoms (rash, ear pain, vomiting, cough), behaviour changes - and the doctor assesses the presentation based on your report.

For many common childhood illnesses (fever, gastroenteritis, upper respiratory infections), telehealth assessment through a parent's description is clinically appropriate and a certificate can be issued for the child. Some conditions in young children require physical examination - checking the ears for otitis media, auscultating the chest for pneumonia, or assessing a rash for severity. If the doctor determines in-person examination is necessary, they will advise you accordingly.

### For elderly parents or other adults

If the person who is ill can participate in a telehealth consultation themselves, they can request a certificate for their condition directly. If they cannot - due to severity of illness, cognitive impairment, or communication difficulties - you can describe their situation to the doctor, though this requires clinical judgement about whether adequate assessment can be performed remotely.

### What to have ready for the consultation

- The patient's name, age, and a description of their symptoms
- When symptoms started and how they have progressed
- Any relevant medical history (chronic conditions, current medications)
- Whether they have seen a doctor recently for this condition

## Emergency Carer's Leave

Separate from the shared 10-day pool, the Fair Work Act provides 2 days of unpaid emergency carer's leave per occasion. This applies in unexpected situations where you must care for a member of your immediate family or household.

Key points about emergency carer's leave:

- It is unpaid, so it does not reduce your paid leave balance
- Both casual and permanent employees are entitled to it
- It applies to unexpected emergencies, not planned situations
- "Per occasion" means you can take it multiple times in a year for separate events
- Evidence is not always required, but your employer can ask for reasonable confirmation

Emergency carer's leave is distinct from compassionate leave (2 days per occasion when a family member has a life-threatening illness or passes away).

## Casual Employees and Carer's Leave

Casual employees do not accrue paid personal/carer's leave. However, they have access to 2 days of unpaid carer's leave per occasion under the NES. Casual employees cannot be penalised or dismissed for taking legitimate unpaid carer's leave, though they have no entitlement to paid leave.

Some casual employment arrangements include paid leave by agreement or under specific enterprise agreements. Check your contract or relevant award to confirm what applies to your situation.

## Statutory Declaration as an Alternative

A statutory declaration is a written statement made before an authorised witness, declaring under legal obligation that the information is true. For carer's leave, a stat dec states that you took leave to care for a named person who was ill or injured and required your care.

Stat decs are appropriate when a medical certificate is not reasonably practicable to obtain. The Fair Work Act does not require a medical certificate in every case. It requires "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person." For brief illnesses that resolved without a doctor visit, a stat dec meets this standard.

Where to get a stat dec witnessed at no cost:

- A Justice of the Peace (available at many courthouses, council offices, and libraries)
- A pharmacist can witness stat decs in most states and territories
- A police officer, accountant, dentist, engineer, or other listed authorised witness

JP finder services are available through each state government website.

## When Your Leave Runs Out

If you have exhausted your 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave:

- 2 days of unpaid carer's leave per occasion still applies
- You can request to take annual leave, subject to employer agreement
- You can apply for leave without pay
- Long service leave may be available if you are eligible

If a family member has an extended serious illness requiring ongoing care, it is worth speaking with your employer and HR about options beyond the standard entitlements. Some enterprise agreements include additional carer's provisions, and reasonable workplace adjustments or flexible work arrangements may be available.

## Your Employer's Obligations

Under the Fair Work Act and anti-discrimination legislation, your employer:

- Cannot refuse to grant carer's leave when you are entitled to it
- Cannot take adverse action against you for taking legitimate carer's leave
- Cannot demand more information than is reasonably necessary to verify the leave
- Cannot require you to use annual leave instead of carer's leave for a genuine caring situation

If you believe your employer is unlawfully refusing or penalising carer's leave, the Fair Work Ombudsman (fairwork.gov.au) provides free advice and complaint services.
