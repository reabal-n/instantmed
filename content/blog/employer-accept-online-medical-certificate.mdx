<Callout variant="info">

For ordinary sick and carer's leave, the core question is not "online or in person?" It is whether the evidence would satisfy a reasonable person that the leave was taken for a valid reason.

</Callout>

Most Australian employers accept medical certificates issued after telehealth without drama. The risk starts when the document is incomplete, the dates do not match the absence, the practitioner cannot be identified, or a workplace manager treats "online" as automatically suspicious.

This guide separates the law, workplace policy, privacy boundaries, and practical steps so the issue stays about evidence quality rather than diagnosis or sales copy.

## The Fair Work Evidence Standard

The Fair Work Act notice and evidence rules require employees to notify their employer as soon as practicable and, if asked, provide evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person.

Fair Work Ombudsman guidance says:

- Employers can ask for evidence for as little as one day or less.
- Medical certificates and statutory declarations are examples of acceptable evidence.
- The evidence has to convince a reasonable person that the employee was genuinely entitled to sick or carer's leave.
- Awards and enterprise agreements can specify evidence requirements, but the type of evidence requested must be reasonable in the circumstances.

That standard is deliberately practical. It does not say the evidence must be on paper. It does not say the healthcare assessment must happen inside a clinic. It asks whether the evidence is credible for the leave being claimed.

## Where Telehealth Fits

Telehealth is regulated healthcare in Australia. The Medical Board of Australia's telehealth guidelines apply to registered medical practitioners who assess patients by video, phone, internet, or other technology.

Those guidelines do not treat telehealth as automatically inferior. They do say telehealth must be clinically appropriate, safe, and held to the same standard as far as possible as in-person care. They also make clear that telehealth is not suitable for every situation.

For workplace evidence, that means a certificate issued after telehealth is strongest when:

- The practitioner is appropriately registered.
- The assessment was genuine, not a rubber-stamp form.
- The condition and absence period could reasonably be assessed remotely.
- The certificate includes enough identifying and work-capacity detail.
- The certificate does not make claims the practitioner could not reasonably support.

If the issue requires a physical examination, workplace injury certificate, fitness-for-duty clearance, drug and alcohol testing, or occupational health assessment, telehealth may not be the right evidence pathway.

## What A Credible Certificate Usually Includes

A useful sick leave certificate usually includes:

- Your full name
- Date of assessment
- Dates or period covered
- Whether you were unfit for work or usual duties
- Practitioner name
- Practitioner profession or registration details
- Practice or service contact details
- Signature, secure digital signature, or other document integrity marker

It usually does not need:

- Diagnosis
- Medication list
- Full clinical history
- Pathology or imaging results
- Private symptoms unrelated to work capacity

If a certificate is questioned, first check the basics: spelling, dates, covered period, practitioner details, and whether the document actually says you were unfit for work.

## What Employers Can Reasonably Question

An employer is not automatically unreasonable just because they ask a question. They may need to check whether the evidence supports paid leave, whether a workplace policy has been followed, or whether the document looks incomplete.

Reasonable questions may include:

| Concern | Practical response |
|---|---|
| Name or date mismatch | Ask the issuer for a corrected certificate |
| Missing period of absence | Request a corrected document that states dates clearly |
| Practitioner cannot be identified | Check the AHPRA public register or ask the issuer for identifying details |
| Certificate does not cover the rostered shift | Clarify the actual absence period with payroll or HR |
| Extended absence | Ask what additional evidence the policy or agreement requires |
| Safety-sensitive duties | Separate ordinary sick leave evidence from fitness-for-duty clearance |

The weak objection is rejecting a complete certificate only because the assessment was online. That objection focuses on format rather than whether the evidence would satisfy a reasonable person.

## What Employers Usually Should Not Demand

For routine sick leave, employers should be careful about overreaching.

They usually should not demand:

- A diagnosis for an ordinary sick day
- Access to medical records
- Permission to speak directly to the practitioner
- Attendance at the medical appointment
- A certificate from a specific doctor if the policy only requires reasonable evidence
- A more intrusive document than the absence reasonably requires

Fair Work guidance says it is generally not reasonable for an employer to attend an employee's medical appointment or contact the employee's doctor for further information. If the certificate has a genuine defect, the cleaner path is usually to request a corrected document through the employee.

## If Your Employer Says "We Do Not Accept Online Certificates"

Treat this as a process issue first, not a fight.

1. Ask for the policy wording in writing.
2. Check your award, enterprise agreement, contract, and leave policy.
3. Confirm whether the objection is the consultation method or a defect in the document.
4. If the document is incomplete, ask the issuer to correct it.
5. If the practitioner identity is questioned, use the AHPRA public register where relevant.
6. Keep the response factual: the evidence was issued after assessment by a registered practitioner and supports the dates claimed.
7. Seek Fair Work, union, HR, or legal advice if pay is withheld or discipline is threatened.

Do not overshare your diagnosis just to make the problem go away. Once sensitive health information is disclosed at work, it is hard to pull back.

## Online Does Not Mean Automatically Good

Some online certificate services create legitimate evidence. Others create weak evidence.

Red flags include:

- Certificate guaranteed before any assessment
- No identifiable practitioner
- No registration or practice details
- Dates supplied without clinical review
- Document says almost nothing about work capacity
- Service encourages false or exaggerated symptoms
- Same certificate wording for every patient

The best test is simple: if an HR manager, payroll officer, or Fair Work adviser looked at the document, would it appear to be credible evidence for the specific absence?

## Special Cases Where More Is Needed

Routine sick leave is different from higher-stakes workplace processes.

| Situation | Why ordinary evidence may not be enough |
|---|---|
| Workers' compensation | Each state and territory has specific certificate and capacity processes |
| Return to safety-critical work | The employer may need fitness-for-duty or occupational health clearance |
| Long or repeated absence | Policy, enterprise agreement, or case management may require more detail |
| Modified duties | The certificate may need restrictions, capacity, or review dates |
| Pre-employment medical | Physical examination or testing may be part of the requirement |
| Drug and alcohol testing | Cannot be replaced by a routine telehealth certificate |
| Family and domestic violence leave | Separate confidentiality and evidence rules may apply |

The common mistake is treating every workplace health document as a "medical certificate". The correct document depends on the purpose.

## Privacy And Diagnosis Boundaries

For ordinary sick leave, the evidence normally confirms capacity, not the medical story behind it.

Appropriate wording often looks like:

- Unfit for work due to a medical condition
- Unfit for usual duties from one date to another
- Fit to return on a stated date
- Fit for modified duties with listed restrictions

More detail may be appropriate for workers' compensation, insurance, prolonged absence management, or occupational health. That is a different evidentiary setting from a short illness absence.

## Employer And Employee Checklist

For employees:

- Notify your employer as soon as practicable.
- Submit evidence promptly if asked.
- Check that the dates match the actual absence.
- Keep diagnosis private unless a specific process requires it.
- Keep written records if the certificate is questioned.

For employers:

- Apply the same evidence standard consistently.
- Focus on whether the document supports the leave period.
- Avoid blanket rejection of telehealth evidence.
- Do not ask for diagnosis unless there is a specific lawful reason.
- Separate sick leave evidence from return-to-work and safety processes.

## The Bottom Line

Australian workplace evidence rules are built around reasonableness, not paper format. A complete certificate issued after an appropriate telehealth assessment by a registered practitioner will usually be strong evidence for ordinary sick or carer's leave.

The employer can question incomplete or suspicious evidence. The employee can ask for the exact concern, correct defects, verify the practitioner where relevant, and keep the conversation focused on dates and work capacity rather than private diagnosis.
