Yes, employers are required to accept online medical certificates in Australia. A certificate issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor through a telehealth consultation is legally indistinguishable from one issued in a clinic. The law does not specify where the doctor must be located relative to the patient -- it specifies who the doctor must be.

Knowing the legal position and navigating a sceptical manager are two different things, however. This article covers the framework, the common objections, and the practical reality of using online medical certificates across different Australian workplace contexts.

## The Legal Framework

The Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) is the starting point. Section 107 sets out what constitutes valid evidence for personal/carer's leave. The provision is broader than most people assume.

Under section 107, an employee who takes personal leave can provide either a medical certificate or a statutory declaration -- not both, just one or the other. The Act defines a medical certificate as a certificate given by a "registered health practitioner." It does not specify that the consultation must occur in person, face to face, or in any particular physical setting.

A "registered health practitioner" under the Act is any person registered with AHPRA -- the Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency -- under the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law. This includes medical practitioners (doctors) who conduct consultations via telehealth. Telehealth consultations are governed by the same AHPRA standards and Medical Board of Australia's Good Medical Practice code as in-person consultations.

> **Key legislation**: Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), section 107 -- notice and evidence requirements for personal/carer's leave. A "medical certificate" from a "registered health practitioner" is explicitly valid evidence. The consultation method is not a qualifying criterion.

## What Makes a Medical Certificate Valid?

Whether issued online or in person, a valid medical certificate must contain:

- The patient's full name
- The date the assessment was conducted
- The practitioner's professional opinion that the patient was unfit for work
- The period of unfitness (specific dates or duration)
- The practitioner's name and AHPRA registration number
- The issuing clinic or practice name and contact details
- The practitioner's signature (or digital equivalent under Australian electronic signature law)

Notice what is not on that list: a diagnosis. Under the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles, health information is sensitive information. Your employer is entitled to know that a qualified, registered doctor assessed you and determined you were unfit for work. They are not entitled to know why. "Medical condition" as the stated reason is entirely appropriate, and any employer demanding a specific diagnosis is overstepping their legal position.

## Common Employer Objections and How to Respond

Most employers accept online medical certificates without issue. Resistance, when it occurs, usually comes from managers unfamiliar with how healthcare delivery has evolved rather than from deliberate bad faith.

### "We don't accept telehealth certificates"

This is not a legally enforceable policy. Under section 107 of the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), any certificate from a registered health practitioner is valid evidence. A workplace policy cannot override federal legislation. A calm, factual reference to section 107 resolves this in the vast majority of cases.

### "How do we know the doctor actually examined you?"

A telehealth assessment is a clinical consultation. The doctor reviews your symptoms, medical history, and clinical picture to form a professional opinion. Not every condition requires physical examination -- a [cold](/conditions/cold-and-flu), gastroenteritis, or [migraine](/conditions/migraine) does not require a stethoscope. The doctor's AHPRA registration number appears on the certificate and is publicly verifiable at ahpra.gov.au. This tends to settle the question.

### "Anyone could fill out an online form and get a certificate"

This misunderstands how responsible telehealth operates. A patient submits a form; a doctor decides whether to issue a certificate. Requests are declined regularly based on inadequate clinical information, inconsistent symptom presentation, or patterns suggesting misuse. The doctor exercises the same clinical judgment as in any consultation. The form is the intake, not the assessment.

### "Our policy requires a certificate from your regular GP"

No workplace policy can require an employee to see a specific doctor or type of practice. The Fair Work Act refers to "a registered health practitioner" generically, not to any particular practitioner or practice model. Employees have the right to choose their healthcare provider.

## What to Do If Your Employer Pushes Back

A measured approach works better than a confrontational one, even when you are legally in the right.

1. Provide the certificate promptly. Do not give your employer a procedural reason to object.
2. If questioned, calmly note that the certificate is from an AHPRA-registered doctor and meets the requirements of the Fair Work Act 2009, section 107.
3. Offer to show the AHPRA registration check if they question the doctor's credentials. The search is free and takes under a minute at ahpra.gov.au.
4. Put your response in writing (email) so there is a record of the exchange.
5. If the issue persists, contact the Fair Work Ombudsman on 13 13 94 for free, impartial advice on your specific situation.
6. Consider speaking with your union delegate if you are a member.

> **Tip**: The goal is resolution, not vindication. Most employer pushback comes from unfamiliarity rather than bad faith. A brief, factual explanation resolves the vast majority of cases without escalation.

## Industry Context: How Different Sectors Respond

Acceptance rates vary across industries, though the legal position is identical across all of them.

**Healthcare and education**: large institutional employers with experienced HR teams tend to accept telehealth certificates without issue. Policies are typically aligned with the Fair Work Act.

**Mining and resources**: many mining operators require a certificate from day one of any absence. The certificate's validity under the Fair Work Act applies here as for any employer -- but note that fitness-for-duty assessments (when returning after an injury or extended illness) are a separate process under site-specific safety protocols, often requiring an occupational physician.

**Hospitality and retail**: casual workers are common in these sectors. Under section 107A of the Fair Work Act 2009, casual employees are entitled to 2 days unpaid carer's leave per occasion. Documentation requirements depend on the relevant modern award or enterprise agreement.

**Public sector (Commonwealth)**: Commonwealth employees are covered by the Fair Work Act 2009. The Australian Public Service Commission has guidance consistent with accepting certificates from registered practitioners, including those practising via telehealth.

## When In-Person Certificates Are Actually Required

There are specific contexts where an in-person medical assessment is genuinely necessary -- distinct from routine personal leave:

- Pre-employment medicals: typically require physical measurements, tests, and documentation specified by the employer's occupational health provider
- Workers' compensation injury claims: require physical examination and specific documentation under the relevant state or territory scheme (SafeWork in NSW, WorkSafe in VIC/WA, ReturnToWorkSA in SA, etc.)
- Fitness-for-duty assessments in safety-critical roles: aviation medical certificates (CASA requirements), heavy vehicle driving (NHVR health standards), high-risk mining roles
- Return-to-work assessments after extended illness: many employers require clearance via their nominated occupational health physician, particularly in safety-critical environments
- Drug and alcohol testing: cannot be conducted remotely

For routine personal leave -- you woke up unwell and need one to three days off -- an [online medical certificate](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate") is legally appropriate and practically accepted in the overwhelming majority of Australian workplaces.

## The Broader Picture: Telehealth Is Regulated Healthcare

Telehealth is not a regulatory grey area. Doctors providing telehealth services hold current AHPRA registration and are bound by the Medical Board of Australia's Good Medical Practice code -- the same standards that apply to any GP in a clinic.

The Australian Government has permanently integrated a wide range of telehealth items into the Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS), a clear signal that telehealth is a mainstream healthcare delivery model. The Fair Work Ombudsman has confirmed in published guidance that a medical certificate from a registered practitioner is valid regardless of the consultation method.

The workplace that rejects a telehealth certificate today is the one that looks legally exposed tomorrow.
