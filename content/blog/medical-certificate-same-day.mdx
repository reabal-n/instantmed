You woke up unwell and your employer needs a medical certificate for today's absence. Your regular GP is fully booked. You need to act now. The right pathway depends on your clinical situation, time of day, and what your employer's requirements actually are.

The fastest option in most circumstances is asynchronous telehealth - submitting a request online and receiving a certificate within hours, without leaving home. A telehealth certificate carries the same legal standing as one issued by a GP you see in person, under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).

## Legal Validity: What Makes a Certificate Acceptable

Under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), section 107(3), an employee can be required to provide evidence that would satisfy a "reasonable person" that the leave was taken because the employee was not fit for work due to a personal illness or injury. The Act specifies that a medical certificate from a "medical practitioner" (defined as a registered medical practitioner under Australian law - that is, AHPRA registration) satisfies this requirement.

The Fair Work Ombudsman (FWO) has confirmed that medical certificates issued via telehealth by AHPRA-registered doctors satisfy the section 107 evidence standard. The FWO guidance does not require the certificate to have been issued following an in-person consultation.

What the certificate must contain to be legally valid:
- The practitioner's name and professional registration details (AHPRA number)
- That the patient was unfit for work (the specific diagnosis does not need to be disclosed - just the period of unfitness)
- The dates covered
- The practitioner's signature or digital attestation

What an employer cannot do: reject a certificate solely because it was issued via telehealth, demand disclosure of the specific diagnosis, or refuse a certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner without other legitimate grounds.

## How Asynchronous Telehealth Works for Same-Day Certificates

The process from submission to receiving your certificate:

1. Complete the online intake form describing your symptoms, when they started, and how long you expect to need time off. Be specific - "fever of 38.5C since yesterday afternoon, body aches, unable to concentrate, can't get out of bed" is far more useful than "feel sick."
2. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your submission. This is a genuine clinical assessment, not automatic approval.
3. If the clinical picture supports it, the doctor approves the certificate. If follow-up information is needed, you receive a question to answer. If the presentation requires in-person assessment, the doctor will tell you.
4. Your certificate is emailed as a PDF. Forward it to your employer, or upload it to your workplace's leave management system.

Typical review times:
| Time of submission | Expected review time |
|---|---|
| Weekday business hours (8am-12pm) | Often 30-90 minutes |
| Weekday afternoon (12pm-6pm) | 1-3 hours |
| Evening (6pm-10pm) | 1-4 hours |
| Late night or early morning | Variable - may carry into next morning |

## What to Prepare Before Submitting

The quality and completeness of your submission directly affects how quickly you receive a decision:

- **Symptom description**: What are your symptoms exactly? When did they start? Have they been getting better or worse? Measured temperature if you have a thermometer.
- **Duration of absence**: How many days do you expect to be unable to work? One day, two days, or does it depend on how you recover?
- **Your full legal name**: The certificate should match your name as it appears on your employer's records.
- **Contact details**: Email address for certificate delivery, and phone number in case the doctor needs to reach you.
- **Any relevant medical history**: Relevant for prescriptions, less critical for certificates, but conditions like asthma (worsened by respiratory illness) or diabetes (affected by infections) provide useful clinical context.

## Conditions Well-Suited to Telehealth Certificates

Telehealth is appropriate for medical certificates when the condition does not require physical examination to assess:

- Cold and flu symptoms (fever, body aches, fatigue, congestion, sore throat)
- Gastroenteritis (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea)
- Mental health days (anxiety, depression, acute stress)
- Migraine
- COVID-19 with mild to moderate symptoms
- Exacerbation of a known condition (established asthma, eczema flare, IBS flare)

Physical examination may be required for:
- Suspected ear or throat infection requiring direct inspection
- Chest symptoms requiring auscultation
- Abdominal pain requiring palpation
- Any presentation where clinical uncertainty requires physical assessment

If a telehealth doctor determines that in-person examination is needed to issue a certificate safely, they will say so. This is the system working correctly, not failing.

## After You Receive Your Certificate

- Forward the PDF to your employer promptly. Most employers accept email delivery.
- Check your employer's specific policy for where to submit - many organisations have an HR portal, leave management system, or direct email address for documentation.
- Keep a copy of the certificate for your records.
- If you are still unwell when the certificate expires, a further certificate may be appropriate. Submit another request or speak to your GP.

## Multi-Day Certificates

If you expect to be unwell for more than one day, say so in your submission. The reviewing doctor will consider the clinical appropriateness of the duration requested. A longer absence requires stronger clinical justification - a certificate covering three days requires a clearer clinical picture than one covering a single day.

For absences expected to exceed 5 days, or for conditions with complex clinical needs, seeing your regular GP in person may be preferable. Telehealth certificates are most efficient for typical acute illnesses with predictable recovery timelines.

## Employer Rejection: What to Do

If an employer refuses to accept a telehealth certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor, the FWO guidance is clear: telehealth certificates satisfy the section 107 evidence requirement. An employer is entitled to ask clarifying questions (was the certificate issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor?) but is not entitled to categorically reject all telehealth-issued certificates.

If you believe your certificate has been improperly rejected, you can contact the Fair Work Ombudsman at fairwork.gov.au or on 13 13 94 for guidance on your rights and options.
