Conjunctivitis, often called pink eye or sticky eye, is inflammation of the conjunctiva: the clear tissue covering the white of the eye and the inside of the eyelid. It is common, but the important safety point is that not every red eye is simple conjunctivitis.

The practical first split is:

- **Likely routine conjunctivitis:** red or pink eye with watery, sticky, itchy, or gritty symptoms but normal vision and no significant pain.
- **Not routine until assessed:** pain, light sensitivity, sudden vision change, corneal haze, injury, contact lens use, newborn eye discharge, or symptoms that are worsening.

Healthdirect notes that conjunctivitis can be caused by bacteria, viruses, allergies, or irritants such as dust or chemicals. The cause matters because antibiotic drops do not help viral, allergic, or irritant conjunctivitis.

## The Four Common Patterns

### Viral Conjunctivitis

Viral conjunctivitis is common in adults and often follows a cold or upper respiratory infection.

Typical pattern:

- Watery or clear discharge
- Gritty, burning, or foreign-body sensation
- One eye may start first, then the other becomes involved
- Puffy lids or mild light sensitivity can occur
- Highly spreadable through eye secretions, hands, towels, tissues, and surfaces

Antibiotic drops do not treat viral conjunctivitis. Care is usually supportive: hand hygiene, gentle cleaning, avoiding shared towels, and waiting for the infection to settle.

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Bacterial conjunctivitis is more common in children than adults, although adults can still get it.

Typical pattern:

- Yellow, white, or green sticky discharge
- Eyelids stuck together on waking
- Gritty discomfort
- One eye may start first, with spread to the other
- More pus-like discharge than viral or allergic conjunctivitis

Bacterial conjunctivitis may require antibiotic eye drops or ointment. The decision depends on severity, age, risk factors, contact lens use, and whether symptoms could represent something more serious.

### Allergic Conjunctivitis

Allergic conjunctivitis is not contagious. It is triggered by allergens such as pollen, dust mites, animal dander, or mould.

Typical pattern:

- Itch is the dominant symptom
- Usually affects both eyes
- Clear watery discharge
- Often occurs with sneezing, runny nose, itchy nose, or hay fever
- Symptoms may be seasonal or linked to a specific exposure

Treatment usually focuses on allergen reduction, lubricating drops, antihistamine eye drops or tablets, and managing associated hay fever symptoms.

### Irritant Conjunctivitis

Irritant conjunctivitis can happen after exposure to smoke, dust, chlorine, cosmetics, chemicals, or foreign material.

Typical pattern:

- Burning or watering after a clear exposure
- Both eyes may be affected if the exposure was environmental
- Symptoms improve when the irritant is removed
- Chemical exposure, strong pain, or reduced vision is not routine

Chemical splashes and significant foreign-body injuries need urgent assessment, especially if pain, vision change, or ongoing irritation persists after rinsing.

## Symptoms That Help Separate the Cause

Discharge and itch are the fastest practical clues:

- **Watery, clear discharge:** more consistent with viral, allergic, or irritant causes
- **Thick yellow or green discharge:** more consistent with bacterial infection
- **Intense itch in both eyes:** strongly suggests allergic conjunctivitis
- **Pain rather than discomfort:** not typical and needs assessment
- **Light sensitivity:** can indicate corneal or internal-eye inflammation
- **Vision change:** not routine conjunctivitis

These patterns are useful, but they are not perfect. Viral and bacterial conjunctivitis can look similar, especially early.

## What Helps at Home

Self-care can reduce symptoms and spread:

1. Wash hands before and after touching the eyes.
2. Clean discharge gently with a clean cotton pad or cloth.
3. Wipe from the inner corner near the nose outward.
4. Use a fresh pad for each eye.
5. Do not share towels, face washers, pillowcases, tissues, or eye drops.
6. Avoid eye makeup until symptoms resolve.
7. Throw out eye makeup or eyelash products used just before or during infection.
8. Avoid rubbing the eyes.

Cool compresses can help itchy or irritated eyes. Warm water can help soften crusted discharge. Do not put steroid drops, leftover antibiotic drops, breast milk, tea, colloidal silver, or home remedies into the eye unless a clinician has specifically advised it.

## Contact Lens Wearers

Contact lens wearers need a stricter safety boundary. A red eye in a contact lens wearer can be conjunctivitis, but it can also be microbial keratitis, a corneal infection that can threaten vision.

If you wear contact lenses:

- Stop wearing lenses immediately.
- Switch to glasses while symptomatic.
- Do not restart lenses while the eye is red, painful, light-sensitive, or blurry.
- Discard disposable lenses worn around the time symptoms started.
- Replace or disinfect the lens case according to optometrist advice.
- Seek prompt assessment, especially if there is pain, light sensitivity, blurred vision, or one-sided marked redness.

RACGP guidance on red eye in contact lens wearers highlights that common sore-eye drops may not cover the organisms that cause contact lens-related corneal infection. This is why contact lens red eye should not be treated casually.

## Children, School, and Childcare

NHMRC Staying Healthy guidance for education and care settings recommends excluding conjunctivitis until eye discharge has stopped, unless a doctor has diagnosed non-infectious conjunctivitis. Contacts without symptoms are not excluded.

For children, keep the focus practical:

- Keep the child home while infectious eye discharge is present.
- Wash hands and clean surfaces.
- Avoid sharing towels, pillows, face washers, and dress-up eye items.
- Ask the school or childcare service whether they apply additional local requirements.
- Seek assessment for newborns, infants, significant swelling, pain, light sensitivity, or worsening symptoms.

Starting drops is not always the same as being ready to return. The NHMRC exclusion point is discharge stopping, unless a non-infectious cause has been diagnosed.

## When Antibiotics Are and Are Not Useful

Antibiotic drops or ointments are for bacterial causes. They do not treat viral conjunctivitis, allergic conjunctivitis, or irritant conjunctivitis.

Antibiotics are more likely to be considered when there is:

- Thick purulent discharge
- Eyelids stuck together on waking
- Bacterial conjunctivitis suspected by a clinician or pharmacist
- High-risk features where bacterial infection must not be missed

Antibiotics are not a shortcut for:

- Viral watery red eye after a cold
- Allergic itchy eyes
- Chemical irritation
- Contact lens-related pain or blurred vision
- Red eye with vision change or light sensitivity

If antibiotics are prescribed or supplied, use them exactly as directed and avoid contaminating the bottle tip.

## Red Flags: When Red Eye Is Not Routine

Seek prompt medical or optometry assessment if there is:

- Sudden vision change
- Eye pain
- Light sensitivity
- A cloudy, hazy, or irregular-looking cornea
- Recent eye injury or chemical exposure
- Contact lens use with red eye
- Newborn eye discharge
- Severe eyelid swelling
- Symptoms not improving after 1-2 days
- Symptoms worsening despite treatment
- Recurrent episodes in the same eye
- Possible sexually transmitted infection exposure with eye symptoms

<Callout variant="emergency">Severe pain, sudden vision loss, chemical splash, penetrating injury, or a contact lens-related painful red eye should be treated as urgent.</Callout>

## Prevention

To reduce spread:

- Wash hands often.
- Avoid touching or rubbing eyes.
- Use separate towels and face washers.
- Change pillowcases regularly while discharge is present.
- Clean high-touch surfaces.
- Do not share eye makeup, eye drops, contact lenses, or lens cases.
- Keep children home from education and care settings until discharge has stopped, unless non-infectious conjunctivitis has been diagnosed.

For allergic conjunctivitis, prevention is different: reduce allergen exposure, manage hay fever, avoid known triggers, and consider preventive allergy treatment before high-pollen periods if this has been recommended for you.
