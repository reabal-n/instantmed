Conjunctivitis - inflammation of the conjunctiva, the clear tissue covering the white of the eye and the inside of the eyelid - is one of the most common eye presentations in Australian primary care. The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) notes that conjunctivitis accounts for a significant proportion of GP eye consultations, and most cases are managed without referral to an ophthalmologist.

Understanding the type of conjunctivitis you have matters because the treatment differs substantially. Viral conjunctivitis does not respond to antibiotics; bacterial conjunctivitis usually does; allergic conjunctivitis needs antihistamines rather than antibiotics. Starting the right treatment promptly reduces the duration and transmission risk.

## The Three Main Types

| Type | Discharge | Both eyes? | Contagious? | Typical duration | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Viral | Watery, clear or slightly mucousy | Often both (starts one side) | Highly contagious for 10-14 days | 1-2 weeks | Supportive care only |
| Bacterial | Thick, yellow-green; lids stuck together on waking | Usually one, can spread to both | Contagious until 24-48h after antibiotics | 5-10 days | Antibiotic eye drops |
| Allergic | Watery with intense itching; stringy mucus possible | Always both | Not contagious | As long as allergen exposure continues | Antihistamine drops; allergen avoidance |

A fourth type - neonatal conjunctivitis (ophthalmia neonatorum) - occurs in newborns and requires urgent medical assessment regardless of cause. This is not addressed here.

## Viral Conjunctivitis

Viral conjunctivitis is the most common form. Adenoviruses are responsible for the majority of cases; herpes simplex virus (HSV) is a less common but more serious cause.

**Characteristics:**
- Watery or slightly mucousy discharge (not thick or purulent)
- Gritty or foreign-body sensation in the eye
- Often starts in one eye and spreads to the other within a day or two
- Frequently associated with recent upper respiratory tract infection
- Pre-auricular lymph node swelling (in front of the ear) is characteristic of adenoviral conjunctivitis

**Important:** Viral conjunctivitis does not require antibiotic eye drops. Antibiotics are ineffective against viruses and their unnecessary use contributes to antimicrobial resistance. The Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Ophthalmologists (RANZCO) guidelines align with this position, noting that topical antibiotics provide no benefit for viral conjunctivitis in otherwise healthy adults.

**Contagion management:** Adenoviral conjunctivitis is highly contagious via hand-to-eye contact and shared items. Wash hands frequently, avoid touching your eyes, do not share towels or pillowcases, and stay home from work if your role involves close contact with others - particularly in healthcare, childcare, or food handling.

## Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Bacterial conjunctivitis is characterised by purulent discharge that causes the eyelids to stick together, particularly overnight. Common causative organisms include Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Haemophilus influenzae. In sexually active adults, Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae can cause conjunctivitis and require specific antibiotic treatment.

**Characteristics:**
- Thick, yellow or green discharge
- Eyelids stuck together on waking
- Usually starts in one eye
- Less watering than viral
- Eyelid swelling is common

**Treatment:** Topical antibiotic eye drops (commonly chloramphenicol or framycetin in Australia) are the standard treatment. Most pharmacies can supply chloramphenicol drops after a pharmacist consultation in mild cases, or your GP can prescribe them. RACGP guidelines note that bacterial conjunctivitis often resolves within 5-7 days without antibiotics, but antibiotics reduce the duration and contagion period. Treatment is therefore recommended in most cases.

Chloramphenicol eye drops are Schedule 3 (pharmacist-only) in Australia for adults, meaning you can obtain them directly from a pharmacy without a prescription in many cases. A doctor's consultation is required for suspected chlamydial or gonococcal conjunctivitis, contact lens wearers, and children.

## Allergic Conjunctivitis

Allergic conjunctivitis is driven by the same IgE-mediated mechanism as hay fever and frequently coexists with allergic rhinitis. According to ASCIA (the Australasian Society of Clinical Immunology and Allergy), allergic conjunctivitis affects a large proportion of hay fever sufferers.

**Characteristics:**
- Intense, persistent itching (the defining symptom - viral and bacterial conjunctivitis cause less itch)
- Watery, stringy, or mucousy discharge
- Always affects both eyes simultaneously
- Associated nasal symptoms in most cases
- Worsens during high-pollen periods

**Treatment:** Topical antihistamine eye drops (ketotifen, olopatadine) provide rapid symptomatic relief. For persistent allergic conjunctivitis, mast cell stabiliser drops (sodium cromoglicate) can be used preventively. Treating the underlying allergic rhinitis with an intranasal corticosteroid spray also reduces ocular symptoms in many patients.

## Self-Care for All Types

Regardless of type, the following measures provide relief and prevent spread:

1. Apply a cool compress (for allergic or viral) or warm compress (for bacterial) to closed eyelids for 5-10 minutes several times per day.
2. Clean discharge gently using a clean cloth or cotton pad dampened with cooled boiled water, wiping from the inner corner outward. Use a fresh pad for each eye.
3. Wash hands thoroughly before and after touching your eyes or applying drops.
4. Avoid wearing contact lenses until all symptoms have fully resolved and you have been off any antibiotic drops for at least 24 hours.
5. Replace all eye makeup (mascara, eyeliner) used in the days before and during the infection - these can reinfect the eye.
6. Do not share pillowcases, towels, or washcloths with others while symptomatic.

## Contact Lens Wearers

Contact lens wearers require extra caution with any conjunctivitis:

- Stop wearing lenses immediately at onset of symptoms
- Do not resume lens wear until all symptoms have cleared completely
- Discard disposable lenses worn during the infection
- Clean and disinfect reusable lenses before wearing again
- If you wear extended-wear or overnight lenses, see an optometrist or GP before resuming

RANZCO guidelines note that contact lens wearers are at higher risk of serious complications including microbial keratitis (corneal infection). Any red eye in a contact lens wearer should receive prompt assessment.

## When to See a Doctor

Most conjunctivitis is self-limiting or responds to straightforward treatment. However, seek medical assessment for:

- Moderate to severe eye pain (discomfort is expected; pain is not)
- Blurred or reduced vision
- Light sensitivity (photophobia)
- Symptoms not improving after 7-10 days
- Any conjunctivitis in a contact lens wearer
- Suspected chlamydial or gonococcal cause (sexual exposure history)
- Newborns with eye discharge of any kind
- Suspected HSV conjunctivitis (history of cold sores, unilateral vesicular rash)

Seek same-day or urgent care for:
- Severe pain
- Significant vision changes
- Corneal haziness or irregularity visible in the mirror
- Recent eye injury

## Return to Work and School

RACGP guidance aligns with national public health recommendations:

- **Viral conjunctivitis**: exclusion until discharge has resolved or for the duration of the infectious period (typically 5-7 days after onset), depending on workplace or school policy
- **Bacterial conjunctivitis**: can return to school or work 24 hours after starting antibiotic drops and once significant discharge has resolved
- **Allergic conjunctivitis**: no exclusion required (not contagious)

Healthcare workers, childcare workers, and food handlers should follow their workplace infection control policies, which may be more restrictive.

## Telehealth for Conjunctivitis

Straightforward viral or bacterial conjunctivitis in adults without contact lenses can often be assessed via telehealth. An AHPRA-registered doctor can assess your symptoms, advise on treatment (or issue a prescription for antibiotic drops if indicated), and provide a medical certificate if you need time off work.

In-person assessment is preferable for contact lens wearers, children, cases with vision changes or significant pain, or when the diagnosis is unclear.
