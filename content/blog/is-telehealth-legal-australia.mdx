<Callout variant="info">Yes, telehealth is fully legal in Australia. Online consultations, prescriptions, and medical certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors carry the same legal standing as services provided in a clinic or GP surgery. The medium of delivery does not change the regulatory framework.</Callout>

## The Legal Framework for Telehealth in Australia

Telehealth in Australia is not a regulatory grey area. It operates within the same statutory framework that governs all medical practice:

- The Health Practitioner Regulation National Law, which establishes AHPRA and the Medical Board of Australia
- The National Health Act 1953, which governs PBS prescriptions and eScripts
- State and territory medicines and poisons legislation, which controls prescribing authority
- The Privacy Act 1988, which governs patient data
- The Fair Work Act 2009, which governs the use of medical certificates for leave purposes

There is no separate "telehealth act" or telehealth-specific registration. A doctor authorised to practise medicine is authorised to practise via telehealth, provided they do so within accepted clinical standards. The Medical Board of Australia's Good Medical Practice guidelines explicitly address telehealth and confirm the same obligations apply regardless of consultation mode.

## AHPRA Registration: The Core Requirement

Every doctor providing telehealth services in Australia must hold current registration with AHPRA as a Medical Practitioner. This registration:

- Requires a recognised medical qualification (bachelor's degree or equivalent, 6+ years)
- Requires completion of supervised internship and postgraduate training
- Requires ongoing continuing professional development
- Is subject to conditions, suspensions, or cancellation for unprofessional conduct
- Is publicly searchable at ahpra.gov.au

You can verify any doctor's current AHPRA registration status, see any conditions placed on their practice, and check any disciplinary findings. A doctor practising without current registration commits a criminal offence.

Legitimate telehealth services display their doctors' AHPRA registration numbers on all documents including certificates and consultation summaries. If you cannot find this information, treat the service as unverified.

## Are Online Medical Certificates Legal?

This is one of the most common questions about telehealth, and the answer is unambiguous: yes.

A [medical certificate](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate online") is a document issued by a registered medical practitioner attesting to a patient's health status. The Fair Work Act 2009 requires an employee who takes personal leave to provide "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of their illness or injury if requested by their employer. Under the Act, a certificate from a registered medical practitioner satisfies this requirement.

The Fair Work Act does not specify in-person consultation. The Medical Board of Australia's telehealth guidelines confirm that issuing a medical certificate via telehealth is within the scope of legitimate medical practice, provided:

- The doctor has gathered sufficient clinical information to make an assessment
- The doctor is satisfied the patient is genuinely unfit for work
- The certificate accurately reflects the doctor's clinical assessment

An employer who refuses a valid medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered telehealth doctor on the basis that it was "done online" is acting outside the legal framework. The Fair Work Ombudsman's position is clear: evidence from a registered practitioner cannot be rejected based on the consultation method. Employees in this situation can seek advice from the Fair Work Ombudsman.

<Callout variant="tip">A telehealth medical certificate carries the same legal weight as one issued at your GP clinic. Both must come from a registered doctor. The consultation medium is legally irrelevant.</Callout>

## Are Online Prescriptions (eScripts) Legal?

Yes. Electronic prescriptions - eScripts - are governed by Commonwealth and state legislation:

- The National Health (Pharmaceutical Benefits) Regulations 2017 (amended to include electronic prescriptions)
- State Medicines and Poisons Acts, which have been updated in all jurisdictions to recognise electronic prescriptions
- The Australian Digital Health Agency standards for eScript format and transmission

An eScript issued by a telehealth doctor is accepted at every Australian pharmacy. It attracts the same PBS subsidies as a paper prescription. The pharmacist dispenses based on the digital token number sent to the patient's phone, which links to the full prescription details in the prescription exchange system.

## Prescribing Restrictions in Telehealth

Most medications can be prescribed via telehealth by AHPRA-registered doctors. Some categories have restrictions:

- **Schedule 8 controlled drugs** (strong opioids, stimulants, certain benzodiazepines): these have significant telehealth prescribing restrictions under state and territory medicines legislation. Requirements vary by state, but most jurisdictions require an in-person examination before initiating Schedule 8 medications via telehealth.
- **Medications requiring specialist authority**: some PBS medications require an Authority Prescription from a specialist. A GP (including a telehealth GP) can refer for specialist assessment, but the Authority prescription must come from the appropriate specialist.
- **Medications requiring examination**: a telehealth doctor who cannot adequately assess whether a medication is clinically appropriate via remote consultation should not prescribe it. This is a clinical rather than purely legal limitation.

Medications not subject to these restrictions - which includes the vast majority of commonly prescribed medications including antibiotics, antihypertensives, contraceptives, [cholesterol medications](/conditions/high-cholesterol "High cholesterol treatment"), and many [mental health](/conditions/depression "Depression treatment") medications - can be prescribed via telehealth when clinically appropriate.

## Patient Rights and Protections

Your rights as a patient are identical whether you receive care in person or via telehealth:

- The right to a second opinion
- The right to decline treatment or medication
- The right to access your medical records
- The right to privacy under the Privacy Act
- The right to lodge complaints with AHPRA or health complaints bodies
- Protection against unlawful discrimination
- Consumer protection rights under Australian Consumer Law

Telehealth providers are not exempt from healthcare regulation because they operate online. The location of the consultation (your home vs a clinic) does not change any of these rights.

## How to Verify a Telehealth Service is Legitimate

Australian law defines what a legitimate healthcare service looks like. Check:

- AHPRA registration of treating doctors (searchable at ahpra.gov.au)
- Australian Business Number (ABN) registered with the ATO
- Physical Australian address and contact details
- Transparent pricing with no hidden fees
- A complaints process
- No guarantees of specific clinical outcomes before assessment
- Clinical questions that reflect genuine assessment (not a rubber stamp)

A service that cannot demonstrate all of these is not operating as a legitimate Australian healthcare provider, regardless of what its website claims.

## Medicare and Telehealth Rebates

Not all telehealth consultations are Medicare-eligible. Medicare telehealth items have specific requirements including an existing patient relationship with the GP in many cases. Private telehealth services that do not claim Medicare operate legally - they simply charge a private fee rather than billing through the Medicare Benefits Schedule.

The legality of a telehealth service is independent of whether it participates in Medicare. Many legitimate private telehealth services operate entirely outside Medicare, just as many legitimate in-person specialists and allied health providers operate privately.

## When to Be Cautious

While telehealth itself is legal and legitimate, some services operating online are not:

- Services that offer to prescribe Schedule 8 controlled drugs without proper assessment or state-mandated requirements
- Services based offshore claiming to serve Australian patients (they operate outside AHPRA's jurisdiction)
- Services that guarantee specific outcomes before clinical review
- Services with no identifiable Australian doctors or ABN

These are not "telehealth" in any regulatory sense. They are unregistered healthcare providers operating illegally.
