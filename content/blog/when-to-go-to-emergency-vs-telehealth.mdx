About 40% of emergency department presentations in Australia are classified as semi-urgent or non-urgent by triage nurses. That means a large proportion of ED visits are for conditions that could be safely managed by a GP, an after-hours clinic, or telehealth. Understanding the difference is not about making you feel guilty for attending ED - it is about making sure you get the right care, from the right place, at the right time.

Getting this wrong in either direction has consequences. Attending ED unnecessarily means hours in a waiting room, exposure to other sick patients, and using resources meant for people in genuine danger. Staying home when you need emergency care can be fatal.

## Level 1: Call 000 Immediately

Some symptoms require ambulance attendance or immediate emergency care. Do not drive yourself. Do not wait to see how things develop. Call 000.

**Call 000 for:**

- Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or pain radiating to the arm or jaw
- Difficulty breathing or sudden severe shortness of breath
- Signs of stroke: face drooping, arm weakness, speech problems (FAST: Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
- Sudden loss of consciousness or collapse
- Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): throat tightening, facial swelling, difficulty swallowing or breathing following exposure to an allergen
- Heavy bleeding that does not slow with direct pressure
- Serious injury or trauma from a car accident, fall from height, or assault
- Overdose or suspected poisoning (also call the Poisons Information Centre: 13 11 26)
- Suspected spinal injury - do not move the person
- Seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or a first seizure with no prior history
- Child who is not breathing, is unconscious, or is blue around the lips

<Callout variant="emergency">

If you are unsure whether symptoms warrant 000, call anyway. Ambulance staff and emergency medicine is designed to handle false alarms. What it is not designed to handle is someone who waited too long. Call and describe what you are seeing - the operator will advise you.

</Callout>

## Level 2: Go to Emergency Department

ED is appropriate for serious conditions that require investigation, equipment, or specialist care not available elsewhere - but where you can safely get there yourself or have someone drive you.

**Go to ED for:**

- Severe or rapidly worsening chest pain (even if you called 000 and were told to drive yourself)
- Sudden severe headache described as the worst of your life - may indicate a ruptured brain aneurysm
- High fever accompanied by severe neck stiffness, light sensitivity, and a non-blanching rash - possible meningitis
- Severe abdominal pain, especially with rigidity or tenderness that comes on suddenly
- Suspected broken bones or dislocation, particularly involving the spine, pelvis, or femur
- Deep cuts or lacerations requiring stitches, particularly on the face or over joints
- Significant burns covering more than a small area, or burns to the face, hands, or genitals
- Eye injuries or sudden changes in vision
- Possible stroke symptoms even if they have resolved (transient ischaemic attack)
- Signs of a serious infection: high fever, rigors (uncontrollable shaking), confusion, or rapid breathing
- Severe dehydration where oral rehydration is not working (particularly in infants, elderly, or immunocompromised patients)
- Psychiatric emergency where there is immediate risk of harm to yourself or others

A useful guide: if the symptom has come on suddenly, is getting worse rapidly, or involves vital functions (breathing, heartbeat, consciousness, vision), ED is the right place.

## Level 3: After-Hours GP Clinic or Urgent Care

Not every serious-feeling condition requires ED. After-hours GP clinics and urgent care centres handle conditions that need same-day assessment but not emergency resources.

**Suitable for after-hours GP or urgent care:**

- Suspected urinary tract infection with fever
- Ear pain with fever or discharge (particularly in children)
- Minor fractures or sprains requiring imaging
- Skin infections showing signs of spreading (cellulitis)
- Moderate pain not controlled by over-the-counter medications
- Symptoms suggesting a [chest infection](/conditions/chest-infection "Chest infection") or pneumonia without severe breathing difficulty
- Acute mental health symptoms that need same-day assessment but do not involve immediate risk

After-hours GP clinics typically bulk-bill Medicare patients and are available in most urban areas. They handle conditions that genuinely need in-person examination and possibly imaging, but that can safely wait a few hours.

## Level 4: Telehealth or GP Appointment

Most illness and injury falls into this category. These are conditions that need medical assessment and potentially a prescription or certificate, but do not require physical examination in most cases.

**Telehealth is appropriate for:**

- [Cold and flu](/conditions/cold-and-flu "Cold and flu") symptoms in adults
- [Gastroenteritis](/conditions/food-poisoning "Food poisoning") that does not require IV fluids or urgent investigation
- Urinary tract infections in otherwise healthy adults without fever
- Skin rashes that can be described and photographed
- Mild to moderate respiratory infections
- Medical certificates for work or other purposes
- Repeat prescription requests for ongoing medications
- Mental health check-ins and certificate requests
- Medication queries and reviews
- Chronic disease management (blood pressure, diabetes, cholesterol) follow-up

The common thread: conditions where a doctor's assessment of your described symptoms can guide treatment without a physical examination.

## When Telehealth Should Refer You to In-Person Care

A responsible telehealth doctor will not attempt to manage conditions outside telehealth's scope. You should expect to be referred for in-person assessment when:

- Your symptoms suggest something that requires physical examination (a possible abscess, a joint that may need aspiration, symptoms suggesting a serious infection)
- Your symptoms are severe or deteriorating during the consultation
- You require investigations that cannot be ordered without prior examination
- The situation involves a young child with an unclear presentation
- A mental health situation involves immediate safety risk

If telehealth refers you to a GP, walk-in clinic, or ED, that is the service working as intended. It means the doctor has assessed that your situation needs more than remote management. Follow the advice.

## Children: A Special Note

Children can deteriorate quickly and often present symptoms differently from adults. Some specific guidance:

- **Under 3 months**: Any fever (temperature above 38°C) requires same-day in-person assessment. Do not use telehealth as a first step.
- **Under 12 months**: A high fever, poor feeding, unusual crying, or signs of difficulty breathing warrant in-person assessment.
- **Any age**: Breathing that is fast, laboured, or involves visible chest retractions (the skin pulling in between or below ribs with each breath) is an ED presentation.
- **Febrile convulsions**: Call 000 if this is a first seizure or if it lasts more than 5 minutes.

Telehealth is appropriate for mild conditions in children who are alert, feeding, and not in distress. When in doubt about a child, in-person assessment is the safer default.

## Chronic Condition Flares

If you have a diagnosed chronic condition (asthma, diabetes, heart disease, epilepsy, inflammatory bowel disease), you already know that "bad day" is different from "emergency." The challenge is knowing when a flare has crossed a threshold.

General guidance:

- If the flare is within the range of what you have experienced before and your management plan covers it, telehealth or your regular GP is appropriate
- If the flare is more severe than usual, involves new symptoms, or your usual management is not working after the expected time, escalate to GP or urgent care
- If there is any respiratory compromise (asthma or COPD that is not responding to your reliever), go to ED

Many people with chronic conditions have a written action plan from their specialist. That plan should define when to seek emergency care. If yours does not, ask for one at your next appointment.

## Mental Health: A Different Decision Framework

Mental health emergencies are real emergencies. The decision framework is slightly different:

**Call 000 or go to ED for:**

- Active suicidal ideation with a plan or intent
- Psychotic episode involving danger to self or others
- Severe self-harm requiring medical treatment

**Call a crisis line (not ED) for:**

- Suicidal thoughts without immediate intent or plan - Lifeline: 13 11 14, Beyond Blue: 1300 22 4636
- Mental health distress that feels urgent but is not physically dangerous

**Telehealth or GP for:**

- Mental health check-ins, certificate requests, medication reviews
- [Anxiety](/conditions/panic-attacks "Anxiety and panic attacks"), [depression](/conditions/depression "Depression"), burnout, or stress affecting work capacity
- Referral to psychology or psychiatry

Crisis support lines are staffed 24/7 and are often more appropriate than ED for mental health distress that does not involve immediate physical risk. ED environments can be distressing for people in mental health crisis, and wait times are long.

Lifeline: 13 11 14 (24/7). Beyond Blue: 1300 22 4636 (24/7). If you believe someone is in immediate danger, call 000.

</Callout>

## When You Are Unsure

If you genuinely cannot categorise your symptoms using the framework above, two resources exist specifically for this:

**Healthdirect Helpline: 1800 022 222** - Free, 24/7, staffed by registered nurses. Describe your symptoms and they will advise you on the appropriate level of care. This is a government-funded service that exists precisely for "I do not know if this is an emergency" situations.

**Nurse on Call (Victoria): 1300 60 60 24** - Similar service for Victorian residents.

Both services are non-emergency calls - they are advice lines that help you triage yourself. Use them. They are more reliable than searching your symptoms online, and they know the difference between "this can wait" and "you need to leave now."

## The Real Cost of Using ED for Non-Emergencies

ED triage is based on clinical urgency, not arrival time. A non-urgent condition will wait - often for 4-8 hours - while genuinely critical patients are prioritised. In the meantime, you are:

- Sitting in a waiting room full of sick people
- Using nursing and administrative capacity that is genuinely needed elsewhere
- Adding to an already stretched system

This is not about making you feel guilty for a good-faith misjudgement. It is about the fact that once you understand the framework, you will make better decisions - and so will everyone around you.

Telehealth, after-hours GP clinics, and urgent care centres exist to fill the gap between "this will be fine with advice and a prescription" and "this needs emergency care." Using them correctly makes the whole system work better.
