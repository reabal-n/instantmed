Use emergency care when the situation is serious and urgent. Use telehealth only when the problem can be assessed safely from history, images, video, records, or conversation, and when waiting for remote review will not delay urgent treatment.

The safest rule is simple: danger signs bypass routine telehealth.

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- Call triple zero (000) when symptoms are serious and urgent, or someone needs urgent medical help.
- Go to an emergency department for serious illness or injury that needs hospital assessment, monitoring, imaging, procedures, or emergency treatment.
- Use urgent care, after-hours GP, pharmacy, or your regular GP for problems that need same-day or in-person care but are not immediately life-threatening.
- Call healthdirect on 1800 022 222 if the problem is not obviously an emergency but you are unsure what to do.
- Telehealth may fit low-risk issues where remote information is enough and the clinician can redirect if the risk changes.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Start with danger signs

Do not start with convenience, wait time, cost, or whether you can get an online appointment. Start with whether the symptom could be serious or time-critical.

| Symptom pattern | Safer first route | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Chest pain, chest tightness, severe breathing difficulty, collapse, seizure, stroke signs, severe bleeding, poisoning, overdose, major injury | Call 000 | Ambulance and emergency care may be needed now |
| Serious but able to travel safely, such as severe pain, serious injury, fever in an infant, loss of consciousness, severe mental health concern | Emergency department | Hospital triage and urgent assessment may be needed |
| Same-day but not immediately life-threatening, such as worsening infection, moderate injury, dehydration concern, or pain needing examination | Urgent care, after-hours GP, or GP clinic | Examination, observations, tests, or treatment may be needed |
| Mild, stable, low-risk issue with clear history | Telehealth may fit | Remote review may be enough if no urgent features are present |
| Unsure and not clearly life-threatening | healthdirect 1800 022 222 | A registered nurse can help choose the care route |

<CareBoundary title="Call 000 for serious and urgent symptoms">
Healthdirect says triple zero (000) is for ambulance, police, or fire services in an emergency, and should be used only if the situation is serious and urgent. It lists medical problems such as chest pain, stroke signs, breathing problems, sudden collapse, seizure, and uncontrollable bleeding as ambulance reasons.
</CareBoundary>

## What emergency departments are for

Emergency departments are built for urgent medical assessment, triage, treatment, and escalation. They can monitor people, run urgent tests, perform procedures, manage deterioration, and involve hospital teams.

Healthdirect says emergency departments are open 24 hours for serious medical conditions and that a triage nurse assesses how urgently each person needs treatment. People are not seen in arrival order when someone else is more urgent.

<EvidenceNote title="ED care is triaged by urgency" source="Healthdirect">
Healthdirect explains that emergency departments use a triage system so the people who need help most urgently are treated first, and that less urgent problems may be better suited to urgent care, a GP, pharmacist, or healthdirect.
</EvidenceNote>

Examples that often fit ED or 000 include:

- chest pain, chest pressure, or symptoms that could be a heart attack
- stroke signs, such as face droop, arm weakness, speech trouble, sudden vision change, or sudden balance problems
- severe breathing difficulty
- collapse, loss of consciousness, first seizure, or prolonged seizure
- uncontrollable bleeding, major injury, serious fall, car accident, assault, or severe burn
- poisoning or overdose
- severe abdominal pain, severe headache unlike usual, or rapidly worsening severe pain
- fever in very young infants or a child who is difficult to wake, not drinking, not breathing normally, or looks very unwell
- immediate self-harm risk, risk of harm to others, severe psychosis, extreme agitation, or a mental health crisis with immediate danger

## When urgent care or a GP may be safer than telehealth

Some problems are not emergencies but still need hands-on assessment. They may be better suited to urgent care, an after-hours GP clinic, a usual GP, or a local clinic than routine telehealth.

| Problem | Why in-person may fit |
|---|---|
| Injury, suspected fracture, deep cut, burn, or wound | Examination, imaging, wound care, dressing, or procedure may be needed |
| Fever with worsening pain, dehydration, confusion, or a very unwell appearance | Observations and same-day review may change the plan |
| Ear pain in a young child | The eardrum may need to be examined |
| Abdominal, pelvic, testicular, chest, or severe back pain | Examination and urgent tests may be needed |
| Pregnancy-related pain, bleeding, severe vomiting, or reduced fetal movement | Pregnancy assessment, ultrasound, pathology, or hospital care may be needed |
| Mental health symptoms without immediate danger but needing same-day support | GP, crisis line, mental health triage, or urgent mental health service may fit better |

If the route is unclear but not immediately life-threatening, healthdirect can help. If it may be life-threatening, call 000 first.

## Where telehealth fits

Telehealth may fit when symptoms are low risk, stable, clearly described, and the clinician can make a safe decision remotely.

Examples can include:

- advice for mild cold or flu symptoms without breathing difficulty, dehydration, confusion, severe pain, or high-risk features
- low-risk certificate or work-capacity questions when no urgent symptoms are present
- repeat medicine review where the medicine history and safety context are clear
- follow-up after a recent GP or hospital review when the plan is already known
- triage about whether the next step should be GP, urgent care, pharmacy, pathology, imaging, or ED

Telehealth is not a way to avoid emergency care. It is a care route for problems where remote assessment gives enough information.

<PolicyNote title="Remote care must keep checking suitability" source="Medical Board of Australia">
The Medical Board says telehealth is not appropriate for all consultations and should not be treated as a routine substitute for in-person care. During a telehealth consultation, doctors should continuously assess whether telehealth remains appropriate and arrange in-person care if needed.
</PolicyNote>

## Use healthdirect when the route is unclear

The healthdirect helpline exists for the middle zone: not obviously an emergency, but not obviously safe to wait either.

The Australian Government describes the healthdirect helpline as a service where you can speak to a registered nurse about health concerns. It can help if you are not sure whether to see a doctor or manage the condition at home, and it is open 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

| Situation | healthdirect may help with |
|---|---|
| You are unsure whether symptoms can wait | Choosing home care, GP, urgent care, ED, or 000 |
| You are away from your usual GP | Finding the safest local route |
| Symptoms are uncomfortable but not clearly dangerous | Deciding whether same-day care is needed |
| A child or older person is unwell and you are unsure | Triage advice about urgency |
| You need after-hours advice | 24/7 nurse advice |

If the nurse thinks emergency care is needed, follow that advice.

## Children, older adults, pregnancy, and frailty lower the threshold

Some people can become seriously unwell faster or show less typical symptoms. Telehealth can still be useful in selected low-risk situations, but the threshold for in-person or urgent care is lower.

Be cautious with:

- babies under 3 months with fever
- any child with breathing difficulty, poor feeding, drowsiness, dehydration, blue or grey colour, seizure, non-fading rash, or a parent who says the child is not acting normally
- pregnancy symptoms such as pain, bleeding, fever, severe vomiting, or reduced fetal movement
- older, frail, immunocompromised, or medically complex people
- people who cannot explain symptoms clearly or are confused, intoxicated, alone, unsafe, or unsupported

| Person or context | Safer bias |
|---|---|
| Baby or young child with fever or breathing concern | In-person or urgent care |
| Older or frail person with sudden decline | In-person or urgent care |
| Pregnancy-related concerning symptoms | In-person, maternity, urgent, or emergency pathway |
| Immunocompromised person with fever or worsening symptoms | Same-day medical advice |
| Immediate danger to self or others | 000 |

## Mental health crises are emergencies too

Mental health crisis symptoms can require emergency care. Healthdirect says if someone is having suicidal thoughts and is in immediate danger, call triple zero (000), and lists Lifeline, Beyond Blue, and Suicide Call Back Service as national crisis supports.

| Mental health situation | Safer route |
|---|---|
| Immediate risk of self-harm, harm to others, severe agitation, hallucinations, delusions, or unsafe behaviour | Call 000 or emergency care |
| Suicidal thoughts without immediate intent or plan, or intense distress where safety is maintained | Lifeline 13 11 14, Beyond Blue 1300 22 4636, Suicide Call Back Service 1300 659 467, GP or local crisis service |
| Ongoing anxiety, depression, burnout, medication review, certificate, or referral needs without immediate danger | GP or telehealth may fit |

If you are supporting someone in crisis, do not put yourself at risk. If there is immediate danger, call 000.

## What each care route can do

| Route | Can do | Cannot safely replace |
|---|---|---|
| 000 ambulance | Emergency dispatch, urgent advice, paramedic care, transport | Routine advice or non-urgent medical review |
| Emergency department | Triage, monitoring, urgent tests, procedures, specialist escalation | Routine certificates, repeat scripts, low-risk advice |
| Urgent care or after-hours GP | Same-day examination and many non-life-threatening urgent problems | Major emergencies or hospital-level deterioration |
| Regular GP | Continuity, examination, chronic care, certificates, referrals, medicines | Immediate ambulance-level emergencies |
| healthdirect | Registered nurse triage and route advice | Emergency dispatch if danger is immediate; call 000 first |
| Telehealth | Remote assessment for suitable low-risk issues and triage | Emergencies, examination-heavy problems, procedures, urgent monitoring |

## If telehealth redirects you, follow it

A redirect from telehealth to ED, urgent care, GP, pathology, imaging, or 000 is not a failure. It means the clinician has judged that the remote channel does not provide enough safety for the symptoms.

Follow the advice, especially when:

- symptoms are worsening during the consultation
- the clinician says examination or observations are needed
- a child, pregnant person, older person, or medically complex patient is involved
- mental health safety becomes uncertain
- the advice includes a timeframe such as "today", "now", or "call 000"

## Choose the right route

<DecisionBox title="Emergency, in-person care, or telehealth">
### May fit telehealth
- Symptoms are low risk, stable, clearly described, and no danger signs are present.
- The clinician can ask follow-up questions and redirect if remote assessment is not enough.
### Needs in-person care
- Examination, observations, tests, imaging, procedures, pregnancy assessment, child assessment, or same-day review may change the plan.
- The patient is very young, older, frail, immunocompromised, pregnant with concerning symptoms, confused, unsupported, or medically complex.
### Urgent care
- Chest pain, stroke signs, severe breathing difficulty, collapse, seizure, severe bleeding, poisoning, overdose, major injury, immediate self-harm risk, or rapid worsening should bypass routine telehealth.
- Call 000 for serious and urgent symptoms.
</DecisionBox>

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. Waiting for routine telehealth when symptoms are severe or getting worse.
2. Driving yourself when ambulance care is needed.
3. Assuming ED works by arrival order rather than triage urgency.
4. Using ED for routine documents or low-risk issues when safer alternatives exist.
5. Treating a telehealth redirect as optional.
6. Underestimating risk in babies, pregnancy, frailty, immunocompromise, or mental health crisis.
