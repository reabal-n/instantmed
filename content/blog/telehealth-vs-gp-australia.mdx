Telehealth is not better than seeing a GP in person. A GP clinic is not automatically better than telehealth. They answer different clinical questions.

The useful comparison is this: does the doctor have enough information to make a safe decision remotely, or does the problem need examination, tests, procedures, monitoring, continuity, or urgent care?

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- Telehealth can suit low-risk issues where history, records, images, phone, or video give the doctor enough information.
- A GP clinic is usually better when physical examination, observations, tests, procedures, immunisations, or long-term continuity matter.
- Medicare funding can differ. Some GP telehealth services are MBS-funded when eligibility rules are met, while private telehealth may be out-of-pocket.
- Telehealth should not be used for emergencies or as a workaround when in-person care has been recommended.
- The strongest model is often hybrid: regular GP for ongoing care, telehealth for suitable remote needs, urgent care when symptoms are serious.
</KeyTakeaway>

## The practical difference

Telehealth changes the channel. It does not remove the doctor's responsibility to assess suitability, protect privacy, keep records, prescribe safely, and redirect when remote care is not enough.

In-person GP care gives the clinician more tools in the room: examination, observations, procedures, vaccination, pathology collection in some clinics, and a better chance of building long-term context.

Telehealth gives access and speed when the question is well suited to remote review. It is especially useful when the issue is clear, low risk, time-sensitive, or follow-up based.

<EvidenceNote title="Telehealth is mainstream, but conditional" source="Australian Government Department of Health">
The Department of Health says telehealth allows phone or video consultation and that many expanded services have been retained permanently. It also describes telehealth as remote care used when the provider has determined a physical examination is not needed.
</EvidenceNote>

## Head-to-head comparison

| Factor | Telehealth may fit | GP clinic may fit better |
|---|---|---|
| Main strength | Access, convenience, follow-up, low-risk administrative or history-based care | Examination, continuity, procedures, complex decision-making |
| Clinical information | History, uploaded images, records, phone, video, medication list | Hands-on examination, observations, physical signs, clinic records |
| Common use cases | Repeat medicine review, simple certificates, advice, follow-up, low-risk triage | New or unclear symptoms, chronic disease, procedures, immunisations, injuries |
| Safety limit | Remote review may miss findings that need touch, instruments, or observations | Still needs escalation when symptoms are urgent or beyond GP scope |
| Relationship | Can be episodic or linked to a usual care plan | Best for longitudinal care and whole-person context |
| Medicare | Some MBS-funded telehealth is available when eligibility rules are met | Standard GP Medicare arrangements may apply, depending on billing and eligibility |
| Cost | Private telehealth may charge a transparent out-of-pocket fee | Bulk billing, mixed billing, or private billing can vary by clinic |
| Best result | Used for suitable remote problems and redirects when needed | Used for examination, continuity, investigations, and complex care |

## What telehealth does well

Telehealth works best when the clinical decision mostly depends on history, context, records, images, or a conversation.

Examples can include:

- repeat prescription review where the medicine is established and the history is clear
- routine medical certificates where a short illness or carer's leave can be assessed remotely
- follow-up questions after a previous review
- low-risk advice where urgent symptoms are absent
- medication side-effect questions or interaction checks
- review of non-identifying photos when a service is set up to handle images safely
- mental health check-ins where the patient is safe, able to communicate, and already has an appropriate care plan
- triage to decide whether the next step should be GP, urgent care, pharmacy, pathology, or specialist review

The common feature is not convenience. It is information sufficiency. The doctor must have enough to decide.

## What GP clinics do better

GP clinics are strongest when the problem needs examination, equipment, procedures, direct observation, or continuity.

Examples include:

- abdominal pain where tenderness, guarding, swelling, or severity matters
- chest symptoms where oxygen level, lung examination, or same-day escalation may be needed
- ear pain in young children where the eardrum needs to be seen
- injuries where movement, strength, sensation, circulation, or fracture risk must be assessed
- suspicious lumps, breast changes, changing skin lesions, or new growths
- immunisations, injections, wound care, stitches, dressings, and procedures
- chronic disease reviews needing trend data, pathology, physical checks, and whole-person context
- new mental health care planning, complex risk, or crisis presentations
- pregnancy symptoms, young babies, frailty, or complex disability support where remote assessment may be incomplete

<PolicyNote title="Remote care must stay clinically appropriate" source="RACGP">
RACGP guidance says telephone or video consultations should generally not be used when a physical or internal examination is required or cannot be deferred to support clinical decision-making.
</PolicyNote>

## Medicare and cost are not the same question

Cost comparisons can be misleading because there are several systems operating at once.

In Australia:

- some GP telehealth services may be funded through Medicare when eligibility rules are met
- the Department of Health says GP telehealth eligibility generally requires a face-to-face consultation with that GP or another GP in the same practice in the previous 12 months, with limited exceptions
- MBS Online says ongoing telehealth arrangements remain in place for a wide range of phone and video services by qualified health practitioners
- private telehealth services may operate outside Medicare and charge their own fees
- in-person GP clinics may bulk bill, mixed bill, or privately bill depending on the clinic, patient eligibility, and appointment type

The practical question for patients is: what will this specific service cost me, and is this the right care route for the problem?

## Safety is the deciding factor

Telehealth is safe when the problem fits remote assessment and the service can redirect when it does not. It becomes unsafe when convenience overrides clinical need.

<CareBoundary title="Do not use telehealth for emergency symptoms">
Chest pain, stroke signs, severe breathing trouble, collapse, seizure, severe allergic reaction, uncontrolled bleeding, major injury, poisoning, overdose, or immediate self-harm risk should bypass routine telehealth. Call 000 for serious and urgent symptoms.
</CareBoundary>

For non-emergency but concerning symptoms, a GP clinic, urgent care clinic, virtual urgent care service, or Healthdirect triage may be a better first step.

Healthdirect says virtual care is only offered when safe and appropriate, and that the clinician may help arrange in-person or emergency care when virtual care is not suitable.

## Quality depends on matching the channel to the problem

The question "is telehealth as good as a GP?" is too broad. For a suitable low-risk issue, telehealth can be an efficient way to reach a doctor. For a problem that needs examination or continuity, the clinic is better because it has the right tools.

Quality depends on:

- whether the patient can explain the issue clearly
- whether identity, consent, privacy, and records are handled properly
- whether the doctor can ask follow-up questions
- whether the request has enough clinical information
- whether the service is willing to decline or redirect
- whether urgent symptoms have been excluded
- whether the care plan links back to the usual GP when needed

Ahpra's updated telehealth guidance highlights practitioner accountability, necessary history, informed consent, patient identity, and the need to tell patients when telehealth may not meet their care needs.

## The hybrid model is usually strongest

For many Australians, the best care pattern is not telehealth or GP. It is telehealth plus GP.

Use your regular GP for:

- chronic disease management
- preventive care and screening
- immunisations and procedures
- new or recurring symptoms that need examination
- complex medicine changes
- mental health care planning and high-risk mental health care
- long-term coordination across specialists, hospitals, and allied health

Use telehealth when:

- the issue is low risk and remote-suitable
- you need timely advice or a document that can be assessed from history
- you need a repeat medicine review and the medicine history is clear
- you are away from home and need triage about the next step
- access, travel, mobility, illness isolation, or work constraints make in-person care harder

## Choosing the first step

| Situation | Better first step | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Severe or rapidly worsening symptoms | 000 or emergency care | Treatment and monitoring may be time-critical |
| Injury, severe pain, or possible fracture | GP, urgent care, or emergency depending on severity | Examination and imaging may be needed |
| Stable repeat medicine request | Telehealth may fit | The doctor can review history, safety, and current use remotely |
| New medicine for unclear symptoms | GP clinic may fit better | Diagnosis, examination, or tests may be needed first |
| Routine short illness certificate | Telehealth may fit | The decision may be history-based if no red flags are present |
| Chronic disease review | Usual GP | Trends, monitoring, examination, and continuity matter |
| Mental health check-in with existing plan | Telehealth may fit | Conversation-based care can work when risk is low and support exists |
| Mental health crisis or immediate self-harm risk | 000 or crisis support | Safety and urgent support come before routine consultation |
| Unsure where to go | Healthdirect 1800 022 222 or Symptom Checker | Triage can help choose GP, urgent care, pharmacy, telehealth, or emergency |

## Where each option fits

<DecisionBox title="Where each option fits">
### May fit telehealth
- The issue is low risk, the history is clear, no urgent symptoms are present, and examination is unlikely to change the decision.
- The doctor can ask follow-up questions, document the assessment, and redirect you if remote review is not enough.
### Needs in-person care
- The problem needs examination, observations, pathology, imaging, procedures, immunisation, complex medicine changes, or continuity from a usual GP.
- The patient is very young, frail, pregnant with concerning symptoms, unable to communicate safely, or the information is incomplete.
### Urgent care
- Serious, sudden, or worsening symptoms should bypass routine telehealth and ordinary GP booking.
- Use 000 for emergencies, or local urgent care when same-day assessment is needed but it is not immediately life-threatening.
</DecisionBox>
