Getting a [medical certificate](/medical-certificate) online is one of the most practical applications of telehealth. If you are genuinely unwell and unable to attend a clinic, the irony of having to go out sick to prove you are sick is not lost on most Australians. Asynchronous telehealth resolves this: you describe your symptoms from home, a doctor assesses your case, and the certificate arrives by email - typically within hours.

This guide covers everything you need to know: the legal standing of online certificates, how the process works, what the doctor is actually assessing, how to submit effectively, and how to use your certificate once you have it.

## Are Online Medical Certificates Legally Valid?

Yes. The legal standard in Australia for sick leave evidence is set by the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).

Under section 107(3), a medical certificate satisfies the evidence requirement for personal leave when it is issued by a person "registered or licensed to practise in a medical profession." The Act does not specify that the consultation must be in person, that it must occur at a clinic, or that it must involve a physical examination.

AHPRA-registered medical practitioners - whether they practise in person or via telehealth - are registered medical practitioners for the purposes of the Fair Work Act. A certificate issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor following a telehealth consultation meets the section 107(3) standard.

The Fair Work Ombudsman has published guidance confirming this: telehealth medical certificates are accepted as valid evidence under the Fair Work Act. Employers who reject telehealth certificates solely on the basis of how they were obtained - rather than any substantive issue with the certificate content - are not acting consistently with this guidance.

## What Information You Need to Provide

The quality of your submission directly determines how efficiently your request is processed. Before submitting, gather:

**Your symptoms:** Be specific. Not "I feel sick" but: what symptoms (nausea, fever, headache, [back pain](/conditions/back-pain), cough - be specific), when they started, how they developed, their current severity, and how they are affecting your ability to work.

**Temperature if measured:** If you have a thermometer, take your temperature and include the reading. A measured fever of 38.4C is more clinically informative than "I feel feverish."

**Medical history relevant to current symptoms:** Known conditions that provide context (e.g., known [migraine](/conditions/migraine) sufferer describing a migraine episode; known IBS patient describing a flare). This makes the clinical picture coherent.

**Current medications:** Particularly anything taken for the current symptoms.

**How long you need:** Be honest about the number of days you need. A single day is appropriate for most acute presentations; request more if the condition genuinely warrants it.

**Certificate purpose:** Standard workplace sick leave. If you need the certificate for a different purpose (Centrelink, university, insurance), mention this as requirements may differ.

## The Clinical Review Process

When you submit your request, it enters a review queue. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews every submission individually. There is no automated approval.

The doctor assesses:

- Whether your described symptoms are consistent with an inability to perform your work duties
- Whether the number of days requested is proportionate to the described presentation
- Whether there are any clinical concerns suggesting you need in-person assessment rather than a certificate
- Whether the information provided is internally consistent

If the doctor has questions, they send a follow-up message requesting clarification. If the request is clinically appropriate, the certificate is issued. If it is not appropriate, the request is declined with an explanation.

The Medical Board of Australia's "Good Medical Practice" code applies to telehealth consultations exactly as to in-person consultations. A doctor who issues a certificate for a presentation they would not certify in person is not practising according to the Board's standards - which means a legitimate telehealth service has the same clinical standards as your regular GP.

## What the Certificate Contains

A valid medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered telehealth doctor includes:

- Patient's full name and date of birth
- Date(s) the certificate covers
- Statement that the patient is/was unfit for work for the specified period
- Date of the consultation
- Issuing doctor's name and AHPRA registration number
- The service or practice name
- Contact information for the practice

The certificate does not typically include the diagnosis unless you request it or the context makes it relevant. "Suffering from a medical condition" is the standard wording. Your diagnosis is your private medical information and you are not obligated to disclose it to your employer.

## How to Submit Your Certificate to Your Employer

The certificate is delivered as a PDF by email. Standard process for submission:

- Forward the email (or the attached PDF) directly to your manager or HR
- If your workplace uses an HR system, upload the PDF as an attachment to your leave request
- For Centrelink or other purposes, attach the PDF to the relevant form or upload via the relevant portal

Your employer cannot require a wet signature or an original paper document for a standard sick leave certificate. A PDF from a registered practitioner satisfies the documentary requirement.

If your employer has a specific form they want completed (rather than accepting a standard certificate), forward that form to the telehealth service and ask whether they can complete it. Most services can accommodate specific form requirements within clinical boundaries.

## Certificate Timing and Same-Day Requests

You can submit a request at any time a service is operating. Asynchronous services typically operate 8am-10pm AEST, seven days a week. If you submit at 7am before work, you will typically have a certificate before midday. If you submit at 9pm, you will have it by the morning.

You can also request a certificate for an illness that began the same day. If you woke up unwell, submit as early in the day as possible. The certificate will cover the current day.

For illness that started the previous day or earlier and you did not seek care at the time, retrospective certification is available. Describe when the illness started and why you were not able to seek care earlier. The doctor will assess whether retrospective certification is clinically appropriate for your described timeline.

## When Online Certification Is Not Appropriate

A telehealth service will decline a certificate request (and recommend in-person care) when:

- The described symptoms suggest an emergency or urgent condition requiring immediate assessment
- The clinical features suggest a condition that cannot be adequately assessed without physical examination
- The described situation does not constitute genuine illness preventing work
- Information provided is insufficient to form a clinical opinion despite follow-up

If your request is declined, follow the recommended next step. If the recommendation is to [see your GP](/consult) or attend a clinic, do so. The fact that an online request was declined does not mean you cannot get a certificate - it means the doctor determined that in-person assessment is the appropriate pathway for your specific presentation.

## Certificates for Specific Purposes

**University and education:** Most universities accept certificates from any registered medical practitioner. Some require their own forms or specific wording for academic consideration or exam-related application purposes. Check with your specific institution before submitting a general certificate for academic purposes.

**Centrelink:** Standard medical certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors satisfy most Centrelink requirements. Specific Centrelink forms may require additional clinical information. If you need a Centrelink-specific medical statement, mention this when submitting your request.

**Travel insurance:** Travel insurance policies generally require documentation of illness from a treating practitioner. A telehealth certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor is generally accepted, though policies vary.

**Workers' compensation:** If your absence relates to a workplace injury, the Certificate of Capacity system (different from a standard sick leave certificate) applies. This requires a specific form and a treating practitioner who can assess your work capacity in relation to the specific injury. A standard sick leave certificate is not appropriate for workers' compensation absences.
