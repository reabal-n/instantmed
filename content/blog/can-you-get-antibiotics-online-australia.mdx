<Callout variant="info">

**Short answer:** You can't buy antibiotics over the counter in Australia, and you shouldn't expect them on demand. But an appropriately registered prescriber can issue an antibiotic after a proper telehealth assessment when one is clinically appropriate — and a responsible service will say no when the infection is probably viral, unclear, severe, or unsafe to assess remotely.

</Callout>

If you searched "can I get antibiotics online," you probably want a fast, legitimate way to treat an infection without sitting in a waiting room. Here is the honest version: there is a legal online pathway, it runs through a real clinical decision, and it works well for some infections and not others.

Antibiotics are powerful medicines. They can be essential for bacterial infections, but they also cause side effects, allergic reactions, drug interactions, and antimicrobial resistance when used unnecessarily. That is why access is controlled — and why "access" and "appropriateness" are two different things.

## Can You Buy Antibiotics Over the Counter in Australia?

No. The antibiotics most people are looking for — tablets or capsules for a chest, throat, sinus, urinary, dental, or skin infection — are **Schedule 4 (Prescription Only) medicines**. A pharmacy cannot sell them simply because you recognise the box, used them before, or think this illness is the same as last time.

A few narrow exceptions exist, and they cause most of the confusion:

- **Some topical antiseptics and wound-care products** are available off the shelf, but these are not the same as a prescribed antibiotic.
- **A small number of pharmacist-only (Schedule 2/3) products** can be supplied after a pharmacist conversation — for example, certain antibiotic eye drops for uncomplicated adult conjunctivitis in some circumstances.
- **Pharmacist-supplied antibiotics for an uncomplicated UTI** are now available in many cases (more on this below), but that is a structured assessment, not over-the-counter self-selection.

For everything else, the safe question is not "where can I buy it?" It is "what assessment do I need, and is an antibiotic actually the right treatment?"

## How Online Antibiotic Prescribing Actually Works

"Get antibiotics online" makes it sound like a shopping cart. The real flow is a clinical one:

1. You provide a history: symptoms, timeline, severity, allergies, current medicines, pregnancy or breastfeeding status, and relevant medical conditions.
2. A registered prescriber reviews whether your presentation is suitable for remote assessment.
3. The prescriber decides whether an antibiotic is appropriate at all — and if so, which one, at what dose, for how long.
4. If prescribing is appropriate, the prescription is usually issued as an **electronic prescription (eScript)** token.
5. A pharmacist dispenses the medicine and counsels you on safe use.

The online part changes how you communicate with the prescriber. It does not remove clinical judgement, prescribing law, professional standards, pharmacy checks, or antimicrobial stewardship.

<Callout variant="info">

A responsible online service should sometimes say no, ask more questions, recommend testing, or direct you to in-person care. That is a safety feature, not a failure.

</Callout>

## Antibiotics Are Prescription-Only: What Schedule 4 Means

In Australia, medicines are regulated through the Poisons Standard. The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) places medicines into schedules according to the level of control needed to protect public health. **Schedule 4 means Prescription Only Medicine** — a prescriber must assess whether the medicine is appropriate before a pharmacy can dispense it.

For common systemic antibiotics, that means:

- A prescriber needs to assess whether an antibiotic is appropriate.
- The prescription must be valid.
- The pharmacy dispenses only after checking the prescription.
- A previous prescription does not authorise future use.

This applies whether the assessment happens in a GP clinic, by phone, by video, or in a hospital. The consultation method does not make antibiotics over the counter.

## Can a Pharmacist Prescribe Antibiotics?

For one specific situation, increasingly yes. Pharmacist prescribing for **uncomplicated urinary tract infections** has expanded from state pilots to coverage across Australia, under each state and territory's own legislation and training requirements.

It is deliberately narrow. A community pharmacist can usually only supply UTI antibiotics for **eligible people** — generally a non-pregnant adult with a clear, uncomplicated lower urinary tract infection and no red flags. Pharmacist UTI prescribing typically **excludes**:

- Men and children
- Pregnant or breastfeeding people
- Recurrent UTIs or recent treatment failure
- Fever, flank or back pain, vomiting (possible kidney involvement)
- Significant other medical conditions

For everything beyond uncomplicated UTI — chest, throat, skin, sinus, dental, and ear infections — pharmacists can advise on symptom relief and when to seek care, but they cannot supply a prescription-only antibiotic without a valid prescription. If your symptoms sound bacterial, a pharmacist may recommend medical assessment rather than supply.

If you think you have a UTI, our guide on [getting UTI treatment in Australia](/blog/uti-antibiotics-online-australia) explains the uncomplicated-versus-complicated distinction in detail.

## What Telehealth Can Assess Well — and What It Can't

"Can I get antibiotics online?" is too broad a question, because each infection site needs different evidence. A safe assessment starts by asking what is being treated, then whether remote history is enough to separate likely bacterial infection from viral illness, inflammation, allergy, injury, or a condition needing examination.

| Presentation | What remote review may clarify | When remote-only care is weak |
|---|---|---|
| Urinary symptoms | Burning, frequency, urgency, absence of fever, pregnancy status, kidney-risk screen | Flank pain, fever, vomiting, pregnancy, recurrent infections, male urinary symptoms, or kidney disease |
| Sinus symptoms | Duration, double-worsening pattern, severity, allergy pattern, facial pressure | Eye swelling, vision change, severe headache, confusion, or symptoms early in a typical cold |
| Skin infection | Size, spread, photos, pain, fever, wound or bite history | Abscess, rapidly spreading redness, diabetic foot concern, deep wound, bite, burn, or systemic illness |
| Throat symptoms | Fever, absence of cough, swollen glands, exposure, testing need | Trouble breathing or swallowing, drooling, neck swelling, severe dehydration, or possible glandular fever |
| Chest symptoms | Cough duration, fever, sputum, wheeze, risk factors, deterioration pattern | Breathlessness, chest pain, oxygen concern, suspected pneumonia, frailty, pregnancy, COPD, or immune risk |

This is why a responsible service should not use one generic antibiotic intake for every infection. A lower-UTI screen, a skin-infection screen, and a chest-symptom screen need different safety questions. Remote assessment works best when the diagnosis is largely history-based and red flags can be screened clearly — for example, [uncomplicated urinary symptoms](/blog/uti-antibiotics-online-australia), a mild localised skin infection with clear photos, or follow-up of an infection that was already examined.

## When In-Person or Urgent Care Is Safer

Telehealth is not a substitute for examination when examination is the point. Seek in-person or urgent care for:

- Severe illness, confusion, fainting, or rapidly worsening symptoms
- High fever with shaking (rigors), severe weakness, or any concern for sepsis
- Chest pain, shortness of breath, or severe headache
- Eye pain, vision change, facial swelling, or significant ear pain
- Deep wounds, animal bites, burns, abscesses, or rapidly spreading skin infection
- Flank pain, fever, or vomiting with urinary symptoms
- Infection symptoms during pregnancy or with a weakened immune system
- Significant infection symptoms in a young child or a frail older person
- Symptoms that are worsening despite recent antibiotics

<Callout variant="warning">

If symptoms are severe or rapidly worsening, do not wait for an online antibiotic decision. Use urgent in-person care, or call **000** if there are emergency symptoms such as difficulty breathing, chest pain, or confusion.

</Callout>

## Why Antibiotics Are Often Not Prescribed

Most respiratory infections are viral, and antibiotics do not treat viruses. Australian antimicrobial resistance guidance is blunt: using antibiotics for viral infections will not cure them, will not stop them spreading, and will not make you feel better faster. Antibiotics are usually not helpful for:

- Colds and influenza
- COVID-19
- Most acute coughs and many sore throats
- Most early sinus symptoms
- Viral gastroenteritis

In these cases, antibiotics can still cause harm — diarrhoea, thrush, rashes, allergic reactions, interactions, and selection for resistant bacteria. The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care promotes **antimicrobial stewardship**: using antibiotics only when they are likely to help, and choosing the right antibiotic, dose, and duration when they are needed. A doctor declining antibiotics is often the correct clinical outcome, not poor service. Our guide on [when antibiotics are actually appropriate](/blog/antibiotics-when-appropriate) goes deeper on this.

## Why "Questionnaire-Only" Prescribing Is a Risk

The Medical Board of Australia's telehealth guidance expects online care to meet the same standard as in-person care as far as possible, and it specifically flags concerns about prescribing that relies only on a text-based questionnaire instead of a real-time consultation by video or phone.

That matters for antibiotics because infection assessment often depends on how unwell someone sounds or looks, whether symptoms are localised or systemic, and whether enough information exists to separate viral from bacterial illness. Photos and structured forms can support a consultation — they should not become a rubber stamp. If a service guarantees antibiotics from a form alone with no way to ask follow-up questions, treat that as a red flag rather than a convenience.

## What Antibiotics Cost in Australia (2026)

Many commonly used antibiotics are PBS-listed when prescribed for eligible indications. Under the *National Health Amendment (Cheaper Medicines) Act 2025*, from **1 January 2026**, before the PBS Safety Net:

- **General patients** pay up to **$25** for most PBS medicines.
- **Concession card holders** pay up to **$7.70**.
- The **2026 PBS Safety Net thresholds** are **$1,748.20** (general) and **$277.20** (concession).
- After reaching the threshold, general patients generally pay the concessional rate, and concession card holders may receive eligible PBS medicines at no charge for the rest of the calendar year.

These are PBS limits, not a guarantee for every supply. The consultation fee is separate, and private (non-PBS) prescriptions, brand premiums, delivery, and pharmacy pricing can change the final amount.

## What Happens If Antibiotics Are Prescribed

If antibiotics are appropriate, the prescription is usually issued as an **electronic prescription**. You receive a token by SMS or email and present it to any Australian pharmacy that supports eScripts. The pharmacist scans the token, checks the prescription, medicine, interactions, and supply rules, and counsels you on use. If repeats are issued, they may be managed electronically or through an Active Script List. (For the full mechanics, see [how eScripts work](/blog/how-escripts-work).)

The token is not the clinical assessment — it is simply how a prescription is accessed after a prescriber has decided to issue one. The final cost depends on the antibiotic, its PBS listing, your eligibility, and pharmacy pricing.

Do not share antibiotics, keep leftovers for later, or use an old antibiotic for a new illness. The right medicine depends on the infection site, the likely organism, resistance patterns, allergies, and your individual risks.

## How to Prepare Before Requesting Antibiotics Online

A better-prepared request leads to a faster, safer decision. Before you start, gather:

- Your symptom timeline and how things have changed
- Any temperature readings
- Current medicines, including over-the-counter products and supplements
- Allergies — especially penicillin or cephalosporin reactions, and what happened
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding status
- Relevant conditions (kidney or liver disease, diabetes, immune suppression)
- Names and dates of any antibiotics taken in the last three months
- Clear, well-lit photos if the issue is skin-related
- Any recent urine, swab, or pathology results

Then be ready for one of four outcomes: an antibiotic is prescribed; more information is requested; testing or in-person assessment is recommended; or an antibiotic is declined because it is unlikely to help or unsafe to issue remotely. The last outcome can be frustrating, but it often protects you from unnecessary side effects and protects future treatment from resistance.

## How to Use Antibiotics Safely

If antibiotics are prescribed:

- Follow the directions exactly, and ask what to do if you miss a dose.
- Check whether food, alcohol, sun exposure, or other medicines matter.
- Seek help for rash, facial or throat swelling, breathing difficulty, severe diarrhoea, or worsening illness.
- Do not stop early unless your prescriber tells you to.
- Arrange review if symptoms are not improving in the expected timeframe.

If you are unsure why a particular antibiotic was chosen, ask. The answer should make sense for your infection, risk factors, and safety profile.

## How to Spot an Unsafe Online Antibiotic Seller

Avoid any website or seller that:

- Offers antibiotics without a prescription
- Lets you choose the antibiotic yourself
- Guarantees antibiotics before any assessment
- Ships medicines from unclear overseas sources
- Hides the prescriber's identity
- Uses fake urgency or "no doctor needed" claims
- Does not ask about allergies, pregnancy, kidney function, or current medicines

The risk is not only legal. Counterfeit, inappropriate, expired, or poorly selected antibiotics can delay proper care and increase harm.

## The Bottom Line

You can get antibiotics online in Australia when a registered prescriber decides they are appropriate after assessment — and, for an uncomplicated UTI, sometimes directly from a trained pharmacist. You cannot safely or legally treat antibiotics like a product you add to a cart.

The best online antibiotic pathway is not the fastest one. It is the one that asks enough questions, redirects unsafe symptoms, avoids viral prescribing, and only issues an eScript when the benefit is likely to outweigh the risk.
