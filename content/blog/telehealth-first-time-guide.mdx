Over 100 million telehealth services were delivered in Australia between 2020 and 2024, according to the Australian Department of Health and Aged Care. What began as a pandemic accommodation has become a permanent feature of how Australians access healthcare. If you have not used it yet, you are not behind - you are about to understand why the adoption rate has been so high.

This guide covers everything for a first-time user: how to choose a service, how to verify it is legitimate, what to prepare, what actually happens during the consultation, and what to do afterwards.

## Two Models of Telehealth: Understanding the Difference

The word "telehealth" covers two distinctly different service models, and understanding which you are using shapes your expectations.

**Asynchronous (form-based) telehealth:** You complete a detailed health questionnaire at a time that suits you. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your information and responds - approving your request, asking follow-up questions, or declining and recommending alternative care. There is no scheduled appointment, no video call, and no real-time interaction. You can submit from bed at any hour.

This model works well for: [medical certificates](/medical-certificate) for sick leave, [repeat prescriptions](/prescriptions) for established medications, specific condition assessments with clear diagnostic criteria (UTI, some skin conditions), and documented requests where a written response is appropriate.

**Synchronous (video or phone) telehealth:** You book a time and speak with a doctor directly in real time, either via video or phone. The clinical interaction mirrors an in-person consultation except that the doctor cannot physically examine you.

This model works better for: complex or nuanced clinical presentations, mental health support requiring rapport-building, detailed discussions about changing a treatment plan, or situations where the doctor needs to see you and ask rapid follow-up questions.

Some services offer both, escalating from asynchronous to a call when the clinical situation warrants it.

## How to Verify a Service Is Legitimate

Not all online health services are equal, and some that appear to be healthcare services are not providing genuine clinical assessment. Before using any telehealth service for the first time, verify:

**AHPRA-registered doctors:** The service should state explicitly that its doctors are AHPRA-registered. You can independently verify any specific practitioner's registration at ahpra.gov.au by searching their name under Medical Practitioners. A legitimate service will not object to you doing this - in fact, the practitioner's AHPRA number should appear on any documents they issue.

**Privacy policy:** Read it, or at least check that it exists and is specific. It should address how health information is collected, stored, used, and how you exercise your rights under the Privacy Act 1988. Generic corporate privacy policies copied from non-healthcare businesses are a red flag.

**LegitScript certification:** LegitScript is an independent international certification body for online healthcare services. Australian telehealth services can apply for LegitScript certification, which involves verification of regulatory compliance. Not all legitimate services have it, but having it is a positive indicator.

**Transparent pricing:** The cost of the service should be clearly displayed before you commit to the consultation. Hidden fees or ambiguous pricing structures are a red flag.

**Refund or decline policy:** A reputable service has a clear policy for situations where the doctor cannot fulfill the request (declining to issue a certificate or prescription after clinical review). This should include a refund process.

<Callout variant="tip">

The fastest way to verify a telehealth service is legitimate: look for the specific AHPRA registration number of the reviewing practitioner on any document they issue. A certificate or prescription from a legitimate service will include the doctor's name and AHPRA registration number. Search that number on ahpra.gov.au and confirm the registration is current.

## What to Prepare Before Your First Consultation

Preparation significantly improves the quality of the clinical assessment you receive. The doctor can only work with what you provide.

**Your Medicare card number:** Required for prescription services that allow PBS pricing at the pharmacy. Not always required for medical certificates. Have it available even if unsure.

**Medications list:** Write down every medication you take - name, dose, and frequency. Include prescription medications, over-the-counter products, and vitamins or supplements. This is essential for any prescription request (interaction checking) and useful for any consultation.

**Allergy information:** Any known drug allergies, what the specific drug was, and what the reaction was (rash, anaphylaxis, gastrointestinal reaction). This affects prescribing decisions.

**Symptom summary:** When your symptoms started, what they are specifically, how they have changed, and how they are affecting you. Use specific language: "fever of 38.6C since Tuesday morning, productive [cough](/conditions/chest-infection) with yellow sputum, significant fatigue, unable to concentrate" is far more useful than "sick and tired."

**Relevant medical history:** Known diagnoses that provide context for your current symptoms. A history of irritable bowel syndrome is relevant if you are describing gastrointestinal symptoms; a history of [anxiety](/conditions/panic-attacks) is relevant if you are describing a mental health day.

**Previous test results:** Recent pathology results (blood tests, urine tests) are particularly valuable for chronic condition prescription renewals. Most Australian pathology providers (Sonic Healthcare, Australian Clinical Labs, Sullivan Nicolaides Pathology) now provide digital patient portals where you can access your own results.

## The Intake Form: Making It Work for You

For asynchronous consultations, the intake form is the consultation. The quality of your assessment is directly proportional to the quality of what you submit.

Do not rush the form. Set aside 5-10 minutes and be thorough. In free-text fields:

- Use the clinical history structure: what is happening, where, when it started, severity, what changes it, associated symptoms
- Include specifics: measured temperature (not just "I have a fever"), exact duration ("since Saturday afternoon, approximately 60 hours")
- Note what you have already tried (medications taken for current symptoms, whether they helped)
- Describe the functional impact on your work capacity - this is particularly important for medical certificate requests

Accurate information protects you as well as enabling better care. A certificate issued based on accurate clinical information has stronger standing than one based on vague or incomplete information.

## During a Video or Phone Consultation

For real-time consultations:

- **Start with your main concern:** "I have had a high fever and cough for four days and I need to know if I need antibiotics and whether I need a certificate."
- **Answer questions fully:** The doctor will guide the consultation. Give complete answers, not just yes/no.
- **Ask your questions:** Prepare them in advance. "What are the warning signs that I should seek emergency care?" "How long should I expect to be unwell?" "What should I do if this antibiotic doesn't work?"
- **Confirm the plan:** Before ending the call, confirm what is being prescribed or issued, any follow-up needed, and what to do if your condition changes.

## What Happens After

**If a prescription was issued:** Take the eScript SMS to any pharmacy. You do not need to go to a specific pharmacy. Show the pharmacist the SMS and they will dispense the medication.

**If a certificate was issued:** Forward the PDF to your employer or HR. Or upload it to your workplace's leave management system.

**If follow-up was recommended:** Book an appointment with your regular GP. Do this promptly, not when symptoms worsen.

**In all cases:** Share the consultation summary with your regular GP at your next appointment. This is best practice for continuity of care. According to RACGP guidelines, telehealth consultations are most effectively integrated when they are part of an ongoing patient-practitioner relationship rather than isolated encounters. Your regular GP benefits from knowing what was assessed and what was prescribed via telehealth.

**Keep a record:** Save the consultation summary, certificate, and any prescription records in a place you can access easily. Most telehealth services provide a patient portal or email records that you should retain.

## Medicare Rebates for Your First Telehealth Consultation

For asynchronous services (form-based), Medicare rebates do not apply. These are private services with flat fees.

For real-time video or phone consultations with a Medicare-registered practitioner, Medicare rebates are available through the Medicare Benefits Schedule telehealth items. The rebate covers a portion of the consultation fee; the service may charge above the rebate (a gap), or may bulk bill (no gap).

To claim a Medicare rebate for an eligible telehealth consultation:
- Ask the service for a receipt with the MBS item number
- Claim through the Medicare app, Services Australia website, or at a Medicare service centre

Medical certificates and prescriptions via asynchronous services are private fees. The PBS price at the pharmacy remains the same regardless of how the prescription was obtained.

## Connecting Telehealth to Ongoing Care

Telehealth works best as a complement to, not a replacement for, an ongoing GP relationship. A telehealth service handles the acute or administrative needs - the same-day certificate, the repeat prescription, the after-hours question. Your regular GP handles the longitudinal care: the annual health check, the chronic disease management plan, the specialist referrals that require clinical coordination.

If you do not have a regular GP, finding one is worth the effort. Many GPs now offer telehealth alongside in-person appointments, which means you can use telehealth for straightforward needs and see your GP in person when examination or complex management is needed - all within the same practice, with continuity of records.
