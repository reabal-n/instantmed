Most Australians store at least some medications at home - a course of antibiotics, regular chronic disease medications, pain relief for headaches, antihistamines for hay fever. Few think much about where or how they store them. But medication storage affects both safety and effectiveness, and some common storage habits actively degrade medications or create risks, particularly for households with children.

The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) requires that medication packaging include storage instructions as part of the Consumer Medicine Information (CMI) and product labelling requirements. Following those instructions is not overly cautious - it maintains the medication's effectiveness and safety profile across its shelf life.

## Why Storage Conditions Matter

Medications are chemically complex. Their active ingredients can degrade through exposure to:

**Heat:** Elevated temperatures accelerate chemical reactions, including the breakdown of active pharmaceutical ingredients. The Australian summer, and in particular the inside of a car, can reach temperatures exceeding 60 degrees Celsius - far beyond what most medications are designed to withstand.

**Humidity and moisture:** Water penetrates tablet coatings, dissolves sugar coatings that protect the active ingredient, and can trigger chemical reactions in the tablet matrix. Extended humidity exposure also promotes microbial growth in liquid preparations.

**Light:** Many medications are photosensitive - ultraviolet and visible light energy breaks chemical bonds in the active ingredient. This is why many medications come in amber glass or opaque bottles, and why the packaging says "store away from light."

**Oxygen:** Oxidation degrades some compounds. This is why some medications come in sealed blister packs rather than loose in a bottle.

When storage conditions are outside the approved range, the active ingredient concentration in the medication can fall below its labelled amount, the medication can become inactive, or in some cases breakdown products can form that are pharmacologically different from the intended compound.

## TGA Storage Guidance: Temperature Categories

The TGA aligns with international pharmaceutical storage standards. Most Consumer Medicine Information documents specify one of these storage categories:

| Storage label | Temperature range | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Store below 25 degrees Celsius | Under 25C | Most tablets and capsules |
| Store below 30 degrees Celsius | Under 30C | Some solid dose forms |
| Store in a cool place | 8-15 degrees Celsius | Some liquids |
| Refrigerate | 2-8 degrees Celsius | Insulin, some eye drops, some biologics |
| Do not freeze | Above 0 degrees Celsius | Most refrigerated medications |
| Freeze | Below 0 degrees Celsius | Some vaccines (varicella, some mRNA products) |

"Room temperature" in pharmaceutical terms means 15-25 degrees Celsius. An Australian living room in summer, or a car in any month, does not qualify as room temperature by this definition.

## Best and Worst Storage Locations at Home

| Location | Suitability | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Cool, dry bedroom drawer | Best option | Stable temperature, dark, low humidity |
| Kitchen cupboard (away from stove) | Good | Typically cool and dry; avoid near oven or dishwasher |
| Fridge (if required) | Required for some | Use main body, not door; see refrigeration guidance below |
| Bathroom cabinet | Poor | Heat and humidity from showers and baths accelerates degradation |
| Kitchen counter near stove | Poor | Heat from cooking; also potential for moisture |
| Car glove box | Poor to dangerous | Australian summers can cause glove box temperatures to exceed 60C |
| Windowsill | Never | Direct sunlight rapidly breaks down most medications |
| Garage | Poor | Temperature swings and potential moisture |

<Callout variant="tip">

The "bathroom medicine cabinet" is one of the most common and worst medication storage locations. Bathrooms generate significant heat and humidity from daily showers - conditions that accelerate medication degradation. A small, lidded container in a bedroom drawer costs nothing and is almost always a better option.

</Callout>

## Refrigerated Medications

Some medications must be refrigerated to maintain their effectiveness. The most common include:

- **Insulin:** All formulations require refrigeration until opened; once opened (pen cartridge or vial in use), can be stored at room temperature for 28-30 days (check the specific product)
- **Biologics and injectable medications:** Adalimumab (Humira), etanercept (Enbrel), insulin analogues, and similar products
- **Some eye drops:** Latanoprost and some other glaucoma eye drops require refrigeration
- **Live attenuated vaccines:** Varicella, some influenza formulations (always handled by healthcare providers)
- **Some oral liquids:** Amoxicillin suspension after reconstitution, some antibiotic syrups

For refrigerated medications:

**Store in the main body of the fridge, not the door.** The door is the least thermally stable location - temperature fluctuates with every opening. The back of the middle shelf maintains the most consistent 2-8C range.

**Do not place near the freezer section.** Accidental freezing can permanently damage some medications, including insulin. Frozen insulin should not be used.

**Keep in original packaging.** The packaging protects against light and provides moisture barrier protection.

**After power outages:** If you are uncertain whether refrigerated medications remained below 8C, contact your pharmacist before using them. Insulin that has been exposed to elevated temperatures for extended periods should be replaced.

## Child Safety: Beyond the Child-Resistant Cap

Child-resistant packaging is required in Australia under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989 and state-territory poisons legislation for medications that are "potentially lethal" in small doses. This includes most opioids, many cardiac medications, iron supplements above certain doses, and various other substances.

However, "child-resistant" is not "child-proof." Standard testing requires only that 80% of children aged 42-51 months cannot open the container within a set time. A determined or older child may be able to open a child-resistant container.

Safe storage for households with children:

- Store all medications out of sight and out of reach - ideally above adult shoulder height
- A locked cabinet is the gold standard
- Do not leave medications on countertops, bedside tables, or tables after doses
- Do not refer to medications as "treats" or "sweets" to children
- Be aware of medications in visitors' bags and handbags - these are a common source of paediatric medication poisoning

The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care identifies accidental medication ingestion by children under five as a preventable safety priority. Paracetamol (acetaminophen) is the most commonly ingested medication in paediatric poisonings in Australia, partly because it is perceived as harmless and partly because it is stored accessibly.

**If a child ingests any medication:** Call the Poisons Information Centre on 13 11 26 (available 24/7) immediately. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically directed to by the Poisons Centre.

## Expiry Dates

Expiry dates on medications are not arbitrary - they represent the date through which the manufacturer guarantees the labelled potency and safety under the specified storage conditions. After this date, the active ingredient concentration may have fallen below the therapeutic level.

**Practical guidance:**

Check expiry dates every six months. A useful cue is to check when daylight saving changes in autumn and spring. Dispose of anything expired.

Certain medication forms are higher priority to dispose of when expired:

- **Eye drops:** After opening, use within the period stated on the packaging (typically 4 weeks for preserved drops, 24 hours for preservative-free single-dose vials). Expired eye drops risk contamination and infection.
- **Liquid medications:** Including suspensions, syrups, and reconstituted antibiotics. These degrade faster than solid forms.
- **Injectable medications:** Including insulin. Expired injectables should never be used.
- **Vaginal creams, topical preparations:** Can develop altered pH or microbial growth after expiry.

Solid tablets and capsules kept in proper storage conditions generally have a longer shelf life than their labelled expiry date, but the expiry date is the manufacturer's guarantee. Use it as the practical cutoff.

## Returning Medications for Safe Disposal

Australia's Return Unwanted Medicines (RUM) project is a federally-funded program enabling the return of unwanted and expired household medications to pharmacies for safe disposal. It is free, available at participating pharmacies across Australia, and accepts most types of household medications.

What to do:
1. Sort medications out of original packaging if desired, or return in original containers
2. Remove personal details from labels if privacy is a concern
3. Take to any participating pharmacy - most community pharmacies in Australia participate
4. Hand to pharmacy staff - no need to explain what you are returning

RUM project medications are incinerated at high temperatures, preventing environmental contamination from pharmaceuticals entering water supplies or landfill.

Do not flush medications down the toilet or sink. Pharmaceutical compounds including antibiotics, hormones, and analgesics are not fully removed by standard water treatment and have been detected in waterways and drinking water systems.

Do not put medications in household rubbish, where they can be accessed by children, pets, or people seeking substances to misuse.

## Travelling with Medications

For domestic travel within Australia, medications in original labelled containers do not require additional documentation.

For international travel, requirements vary by destination. The TGA recommends:

- Carry medications in original, labelled packaging
- Bring a letter from your prescribing doctor for controlled substances or injectable medications
- Check the destination country's import regulations for your specific medications before travel
- Carry enough supply for the trip plus a buffer in case of delays
- Keep medications in carry-on luggage, not checked baggage, to avoid temperature extremes in cargo holds and the risk of delayed baggage

Some Schedule 8 controlled substances are prohibited or restricted in countries including the UAE, Japan, Singapore, Indonesia, and others. Australia's TGA maintains guidance on travelling with prescription medications.
