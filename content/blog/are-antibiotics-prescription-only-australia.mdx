Yes. Common oral and systemic antibiotics used for human infections in Australia are prescription-only medicines. A pharmacy cannot simply sell them over the counter because you have used them before, recognise the box, or think the symptoms are the same as last time.

That rule is not red tape for its own sake. Antibiotics need a diagnosis, a safety check, and a stewardship decision. The wrong antibiotic can fail, cause side effects, hide a serious infection, or increase antimicrobial resistance.

## The Direct Answer

In Australia, prescription medicines are regulated through the Poisons Standard. The TGA explains that medicines are placed into schedules according to the level of control needed to protect public health and safety. Schedule 4 means Prescription Only Medicine.

For common systemic antibiotics, that means:

- A prescriber needs to assess whether an antibiotic is appropriate.
- The prescription needs to be valid.
- The pharmacy dispenses the medicine after checking the prescription.
- Over-the-counter self-supply is not the pathway.
- A previous prescription does not authorise future use.

<Callout variant="info">If you need an antibiotic, the safe question is not "where can I buy it?" It is "what assessment do I need, and is an antibiotic actually the right treatment?"</Callout>

## Why Antibiotics Sit Behind A Prescribing Checkpoint

Antibiotics are controlled because the benefit depends on the details.

The same symptom can come from:

- A viral infection
- A bacterial infection
- Allergy or inflammation
- Asthma or another respiratory condition
- Dental disease needing source control
- A wound needing drainage, cleaning, or in-person review
- A serious infection needing urgent care

Antibiotics only treat susceptible bacteria. They do not treat viral infections such as colds, influenza, COVID-19, and many sore throats or coughs.

## What The Prescriber Checks

Before prescribing, a clinician usually needs to consider:

- The likely infection site
- Whether the pattern is bacterial or viral
- Severity and red flags
- Whether examination, swab, urine test, imaging, or blood test is needed
- Allergy history, especially penicillin or cephalosporin reactions
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Kidney or liver disease
- Immune suppression or diabetes
- Current medicines and interactions
- Recent antibiotic use
- Local resistance patterns
- The narrowest effective option

This is why antibiotics should not be treated like a commodity. The correct decision may be no antibiotic, a different antibiotic, urgent care, testing first, or review if symptoms change.

## What A Pharmacist Can And Cannot Do

Pharmacists are often the best first stop for minor symptoms. They can advise on:

- Pain or fever relief
- Oral rehydration
- Saline sprays or rinses
- Minor wound cleaning
- Non-antibiotic antiseptic products where appropriate
- Whether symptoms need GP, urgent care, or emergency assessment
- How to take a prescribed antibiotic safely

They cannot dispense prescription-only antibiotics without a valid prescription.

If symptoms sound like a bacterial infection, the pharmacist may recommend medical assessment rather than supply an antibiotic directly.

## Telehealth Can Be Legitimate, But It Is Still Prescribing

Telehealth does not make antibiotics over the counter. It is still medical assessment.

A telehealth prescriber may be able to prescribe antibiotics when:

- The condition is suitable for remote assessment.
- The history is clear enough.
- Red flags have been screened.
- The patient can provide allergy, pregnancy, medication, and risk information.
- The prescriber can give safety-net advice.
- In-person examination or testing is not needed first.

Telehealth is a poor fit when there is severe illness, diagnostic uncertainty, pregnancy with infection symptoms, immune risk, a young child, rapidly spreading infection, chest symptoms needing examination, or a possible abscess or wound needing drainage.

## Why Leftovers Are Unsafe

Leftover antibiotics are a common shortcut, and a bad one.

They may be:

- The wrong antibiotic for the current infection
- An incomplete course
- Expired or poorly stored
- Unsafe with a new medicine or health condition
- Unsuitable during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Able to partially suppress symptoms while delaying correct care

They also bypass the main safety check: confirming whether an antibiotic is needed at all.

Return unused medicines to a pharmacy disposal program rather than keeping them for next time.

## Why Offshore Sellers Are A Risk

Unregulated online antibiotic sellers create several risks:

- No proper diagnosis
- No allergy or interaction check
- No assurance the product is genuine
- Possible counterfeit, contaminated, degraded, or substandard medicines
- Delayed care for a serious infection
- More resistance pressure

Some overseas websites make antibiotics look like ordinary consumer products. They are not. If the pathway avoids a valid Australian prescription and pharmacy supply process, it removes the clinical safety layer.

## PBS Costs In 2026

Many commonly used antibiotics are PBS-listed when prescribed for eligible indications.

From 1 January 2026, before the PBS Safety Net:

- General patients pay up to $25 for most PBS medicines.
- Concession card holders pay up to $7.70.
- The 2026 PBS Safety Net thresholds are $1,748.20 for general patients and $277.20 for concession card holders.
- After reaching the threshold, general patients generally pay the concessional rate and concession card holders may receive eligible PBS medicines at no charge.

Those are PBS limits, not a guarantee for every antibiotic supply. Private prescriptions, non-PBS medicines, brand premiums, delivery, and pharmacy-specific charges can change the final amount.

## What Prescription-Only Does Not Mean

Prescription-only does not mean antibiotics are rare, restricted only to hospitals, or unavailable through telehealth. It means there must be a proper prescribing decision before supply.

It also does not mean every infection needs a doctor immediately. Many minor viral illnesses can be managed with pharmacist advice, symptom relief, fluids, rest, and clear review triggers. The point is to match the pathway to the risk.

Prescription-only status also does not guarantee that an antibiotic is the right medicine once a prescription is requested. A prescriber may still decide:

- No antibiotic is needed because the illness is likely viral.
- Testing should happen first.
- A different treatment is safer.
- In-person examination is needed.
- The infection may need drainage, dental treatment, imaging, or hospital care.
- The safest plan is review if symptoms persist or worsen.

This is the part online sellers and leftover antibiotics remove. They skip the distinction between access and appropriateness. Easy access to the wrong antibiotic is not better care.

## Claims To Be Careful With

Be cautious with any service, seller, or message that suggests:

- "No prescription needed"
- "Same antibiotic as last time"
- "Choose your own antibiotic"
- "No doctor review required"
- "Antibiotics for colds and flu"
- "Bulk antibiotic packs"
- "International pharmacy, no questions asked"

Those claims treat antibiotics like consumer goods. In practice, the safety value sits in the questions: what is the likely diagnosis, what are the risks, what is the right medicine, and what should happen if symptoms worsen?

## When To Seek Urgent Care Instead

Do not try to solve severe infection symptoms with an online antibiotic search.

Seek urgent in-person care for:

- Confusion, fainting, severe weakness, or drowsiness
- Shortness of breath, chest pain, or blue lips
- Fever with rigors, dehydration, or feeling seriously unwell
- Severe pain or rapidly worsening symptoms
- Rapidly spreading redness, swelling, or warmth
- Flank pain, vomiting, or fever with urinary symptoms
- Eye pain, vision change, facial swelling, or severe ear symptoms
- Infection symptoms during pregnancy or immune suppression
- Symptoms in a young child, frail older person, or someone with major chronic disease

<Callout variant="warning">If symptoms are severe or rapidly worsening, the safer pathway is urgent assessment, not trying to obtain antibiotics faster.</Callout>

## Practical Summary

Antibiotics are prescription-only in Australia because they require clinical judgement. The rule protects diagnosis, allergy safety, medicine interactions, and antibiotic effectiveness.

The right pathway is assessment by an authorised prescriber, then pharmacy dispensing if antibiotics are appropriate. The wrong pathway is leftovers, sharing, or unregulated offshore sellers.
