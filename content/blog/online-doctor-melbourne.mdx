Melbourne is Australia's second-largest city, home to over 5 million residents according to the ABS 2021 Census, spread across an urban area that extends from Craigieburn in the north to Frankston in the south and Werribee in the west. The scale of Melbourne's footprint creates genuine access challenges: a resident of Cranbourne seeking a same-day GP appointment faces a very different situation from someone in Carlton, and someone working in the CBD without access to nearby affordable primary care faces a different set of constraints again.

According to AIHW data, Victoria's bulk-billing rates sit below the national average in many Melbourne metropolitan areas, with inner-suburban areas having particularly high GP fees. Melbourne has also experienced rapid population growth in outer corridors - the southeast growth area, the northwest (Sunbury, Melton, Caroline Springs), and the Mornington Peninsula - where healthcare infrastructure has not always kept pace with residential development.

Telehealth provides consistent, postcode-neutral access to non-emergency healthcare needs.

## What Telehealth Can Provide

An AHPRA-registered telehealth doctor can issue:

- **Medical certificates**: Under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) section 107, a certificate from any AHPRA-registered doctor satisfies the legal evidence standard for personal leave. Victorian employers cannot refuse a valid telehealth certificate.
- **Repeat prescriptions**: For established, stable medications. Delivered as eScripts (SMS QR codes) fillable at any Victorian pharmacy.
- **General consultations**: For non-urgent conditions including respiratory symptoms, skin concerns (with photos), UTIs, mental health support, and medication management.
- **Referrals and pathology requests**: Issued digitally and taken to any specialist or collection centre (Dorevitch Pathology, Melbourne Pathology, Sonic Healthcare all operate across Melbourne).

## Melbourne by Region

**Inner city (CBD, Carlton, Fitzroy, Collingwood, Richmond)**: High concentration of GP clinics but also very high demand. Bulk-billing is uncommon in inner Melbourne. Gap fees of $50-100 are typical at many practices.

**Inner South and Bayside (South Yarra, Prahran, St Kilda, Brighton, Bayside)**: Predominantly private practices with high out-of-pocket costs. Well-serviced but expensive.

**Eastern suburbs (Box Hill, Doncaster, Glen Waverley, Knox)**: Good coverage in established suburbs. Monash Health provides public emergency services in the southeast.

**Western suburbs (Footscray, Sunshine, Werribee, Hoppers Crossing)**: Lower GP density per capita in outer western areas. The Western Health network serves the public hospital needs but primary care access is more variable.

**Northern suburbs (Preston, Reservoir, Coburg, Craigieburn)**: Mixed. Established inner-northern suburbs have reasonable access; Craigieburn and outer northern growth areas have less.

**South-eastern suburbs (Dandenong, Springvale, Frankston, Cranbourne, Berwick)**: Monash Health provides public hospital services but GP access in outer southeast can be limited, particularly for same-day appointments.

**Mornington Peninsula**: More limited GP capacity for a large population. Telehealth is particularly practical for Peninsula residents who are unwell and cannot comfortably make the drive to Frankston or Berwick.

## Victoria-Specific Healthcare Context

**SafeScript**: Victoria operates SafeScript, the state's real-time prescription monitoring system for Schedule 8 (controlled) medications. SafeScript monitors all dispensing of opioids, benzodiazepines, and other Schedule 8 drugs across Victorian prescribers and pharmacies. Schedule 8 medications cannot be prescribed via asynchronous telehealth - they require in-person assessment. SafeScript does not affect prescribing of the vast majority of medications used for chronic disease management.

**Victorian public sector employment**: Victorian public servants employed under the Public Administration Act 2004 and relevant Victorian Government enterprise agreements are required to provide medical evidence for personal leave in accordance with their enterprise agreement. Certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors - including telehealth certificates - meet this requirement.

**WorkSafe Victoria**: Workers' compensation claims in Victoria are managed by WorkSafe Victoria. For work-related illness or injury, claims require specific documentation. A standard telehealth certificate can initiate a claim, but WorkSafe often requires completion of their Certificate of Capacity form by the treating practitioner.

## University Students in Melbourne

Melbourne has one of the highest concentrations of university students in Australia. Major institutions include the University of Melbourne, Monash University (Clayton, Caulfield, Peninsula campuses), RMIT University, La Trobe University, Deakin University (Burwood, Geelong), Swinburne University, ACU, and several other institutions.

Most Melbourne universities accept medical certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors for special consideration and academic adjustment applications. Key points:

- The University of Melbourne and Monash University both accept telehealth-issued certificates for special consideration applications
- Ensure the certificate is dated during the period of illness affecting the assessment
- Check whether your faculty's specific form is required, or whether a standard certificate is sufficient
- All major Melbourne universities have student health services - these remain the best option for complex health concerns, ongoing management, or when physical examination is needed

## After-Hours Access

Melbourne's after-hours primary care options include:

- **National Home Doctor Service**: operates across Greater Melbourne after hours
- **Priority Primary Care Centres**: introduced by the Victorian Government in 2022-23, providing alternative care for non-emergency presentations to reduce ED pressure
- **Various after-hours clinics**: located throughout Melbourne's suburbs

**Major Melbourne emergency hospitals:**
- The Alfred, Prahran
- Royal Melbourne Hospital, Parkville
- St Vincent's Hospital, Fitzroy
- Austin Hospital, Heidelberg
- Monash Medical Centre, Clayton
- Western Health (Footscray Hospital), Footscray
- **Call 000** for any life-threatening emergency

Telehealth operates 8am to 10pm AEST daily - useful for evening needs where the choice would otherwise be a limited after-hours clinic or an emergency department.

## Pharmacy Access and eScripts

Melbourne has extensive pharmacy coverage across all suburbs. All pharmacies in Victoria participate in the national Prescription Exchange Service and accept eScripts. Present the QR code SMS to the pharmacist - no additional steps required.

PBS co-payments: general patients pay up to $31.60 per item; concession card holders pay up to $7.70 (Services Australia, 2025-26). The PBS price is the same regardless of how the prescription was issued.

## When to Present In Person

Telehealth is not appropriate for:
- Conditions requiring physical examination (ear infections, abdominal assessment, chest auscultation)
- Injuries requiring wound management or imaging
- Conditions where physical examination is necessary for a safe clinical decision

If a telehealth doctor determines in-person assessment is needed, they will advise the appropriate care pathway. This is the system working correctly.
