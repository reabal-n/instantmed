Male pattern hair loss (androgenetic alopecia) affects roughly 50% of Australian men by age 50, according to published epidemiological data, with a strong hereditary component driven by sensitivity to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). Of all the products marketed for hair loss, only two medications have robust clinical evidence to support their use: finasteride (oral, prescription-only) and minoxidil (topical, over-the-counter). They work by completely different mechanisms, have different side-effect profiles, and can be used alone or together.

## How Finasteride Works

Finasteride is a 5-alpha-reductase type 2 inhibitor. It blocks the enzyme that converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). DHT is the androgen primarily responsible for miniaturising hair follicles in genetically susceptible men. By reducing scalp and serum DHT levels by approximately 70% (per phase III clinical trial pharmacokinetic data), finasteride slows follicle miniaturisation and, in many cases, allows partially miniaturised follicles to recover.

**Key clinical facts:**

- Dose: 1mg daily (oral tablet), taken at the same time each day
- Onset: visible results typically 3-6 months; full effect assessed at 12 months
- Efficacy: 83-90% of men maintain or increase hair count over 2 years, based on the pivotal trials published by Kaufman et al. (1998) and Whiting et al. (2003) using standardised vertex hair counts
- Schedule: S4 (prescription-only) in Australia under the Therapeutic Goods Administration's Poisons Standard
- PBS listing: not listed for hair loss; private prescription only for this indication
- TGA approval: finasteride 1mg has TGA registration for androgenetic alopecia in adult men

## How Minoxidil Works

Minoxidil is a vasodilator originally developed for hypertension. Its mechanism in hair growth is not fully characterised, but it appears to prolong the anagen (growth) phase of the hair cycle, increase follicle size, and stimulate blood flow to the follicle. It works independently of the androgen/DHT pathway, which is why it can complement finasteride without pharmacological overlap.

**Key clinical facts:**

- Dose: topical 5% solution or foam, applied twice daily (once daily for some foam formulations)
- Onset: visible results typically 3-4 months; full effect assessed at 6-12 months
- Efficacy: approximately 40% of men show moderate to dense regrowth; approximately 60% show some improvement compared to placebo
- Schedule: S3 (pharmacist-only) in Australia -- no prescription required, but the pharmacist must assess appropriateness before supply
- Oral minoxidil (2.5-5mg daily, off-label at low dose) is increasingly prescribed by dermatologists for patients who cannot tolerate or do not respond to topical formulations

## Effectiveness: Head-to-Head

Direct comparison trials show finasteride is generally more effective than topical minoxidil for vertex (crown) hair loss. A 2004 randomised trial by Arca et al. comparing finasteride 1mg to topical minoxidil 5% over 12 months found superior standardised hair count improvements with finasteride at the vertex.

| Area | Finasteride | Minoxidil |
|---|---|---|
| Vertex (crown) | Superior in head-to-head trials | Moderate effectiveness |
| Frontal hairline | Modest efficacy | Modest efficacy; some evidence for advantage at hairline in subgroups |
| Preventing further loss | Superior (addresses DHT mechanism) | Effective while in use |
| Recovering miniaturised follicles | Yes, in partially miniaturised follicles | Yes, in partially miniaturised follicles |

Neither drug can recover terminally miniaturised (dead) follicles. Both treatments work best when started early in the hair loss process.

## Side Effects

### Finasteride side effects

Sexual side effects (decreased libido, erectile difficulty, reduced ejaculate volume) are reported by 2-4% of men in controlled clinical trials. These effects resolve on discontinuation in the vast majority of cases. Post-finasteride syndrome -- persistent sexual side effects after stopping -- is reported anecdotally, and the TGA and FDA have added post-marketing warnings based on consumer reports; however, causation has not been confirmed in controlled studies.

Other rare effects include breast tenderness or gynaecomastia (less than 1%) and mood changes (anecdotal).

The nocebo effect is well-documented with finasteride. A 2007 randomised study by Mondaini et al. found men who were informed about the possibility of sexual side effects were significantly more likely to report them than men receiving the same drug without that specific information. Discuss this with your doctor to put the risk in context before starting treatment.

### Minoxidil side effects (topical)

- Scalp irritation, dryness, or itching -- most common with the alcohol-based solution
- Initial shedding in weeks 2-8 (a normal sign the treatment is working -- miniaturised hairs shed before thicker follicles emerge)
- Unwanted facial hair growth if solution drips onto the face
- Systemic side effects (dizziness, palpitations) are rare with topical application at standard doses; more relevant with oral minoxidil
- The foam formulation generally causes less irritation than the ethanol-based solution

> **Important**: Finasteride is contraindicated in women of childbearing potential. It is classified as Category X in pregnancy -- exposure during pregnancy can cause genital abnormalities in a male foetus. Women should not handle crushed or broken finasteride tablets. If your partner is pregnant or may become pregnant, take precautions with tablet handling.

## Combining Finasteride and Minoxidil

Because finasteride and minoxidil work by different mechanisms (androgen suppression vs direct follicle stimulation), combination therapy is a well-established clinical approach. Published studies show combination therapy produces greater hair count improvements than either treatment alone, particularly for men with moderate vertex or diffuse hair loss who want maximum regrowth potential.

The Australian Therapeutic Guidelines (eTG) and the approach used by most Australian dermatologists support combining both treatments when monotherapy has produced a partial response or when the patient has moderate-to-severe hair loss at initial presentation.

## PBS Status and Cost in Australia

Neither finasteride 1mg nor minoxidil is PBS-listed for androgenetic alopecia. Both are purchased as private prescriptions or over-the-counter at full cost.

| Product | Approximate cost (2026) |
|---|---|
| Finasteride 1mg generic (30 tablets, 1 month) | $20-$40 |
| Minoxidil 5% topical solution (1 month supply) | $25-$50 |
| Minoxidil 5% foam, branded (e.g., Regaine, 1 month) | $40-$70 |

Generic finasteride 1mg is bioequivalent to branded Propecia per TGA bioequivalence requirements and costs significantly less. Ask your pharmacist for the generic option. Generic topical minoxidil solutions are also available and are substantially cheaper than branded products.

## When to See a Dermatologist

Consider specialist referral if:

- You are losing hair rapidly or in patches -- this may indicate alopecia areata, telogen effluvium, or another diagnosis rather than androgenetic alopecia
- You are female -- female pattern hair loss has different hormonal drivers and treatment approaches (finasteride is not used in women of childbearing potential, and evaluation for [iron deficiency](/conditions/iron-deficiency), [thyroid disease](/conditions/thyroid-issues), and androgen excess is important)
- Standard treatments have been ineffective after 12 months of consistent use
- You have scalp inflammation, scarring, or unusual features -- these suggest a different diagnosis
- You want to discuss advanced options such as low-dose oral minoxidil, dutasteride off-label, or hair transplant assessment

## Getting Treatment in Australia

Minoxidil (topical 5%) is available from any pharmacy as a Schedule 3 (pharmacist-only) product -- no prescription required, but the pharmacist must supply it and assess appropriateness. Finasteride 1mg requires a prescription from an AHPRA-registered doctor.

Telehealth consultations are appropriate for hair loss assessment and finasteride prescribing when the doctor can review your medical history, current medications, and hair loss pattern. A structured hair loss assessment via telehealth can determine whether finasteride is appropriate for you and issue an eScript sent directly to your phone.
