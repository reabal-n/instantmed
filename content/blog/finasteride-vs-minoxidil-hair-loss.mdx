<Callout variant="info">

Finasteride and minoxidil are not interchangeable. Finasteride acts on the androgen pathway; topical minoxidil acts more on the hair-growth cycle. The right comparison starts with the diagnosis.

</Callout>

Male pattern hair loss, also called androgenetic alopecia, usually causes gradual thinning at the temples, hairline, mid-scalp, or crown. It is common, genetic, and chronic. Healthdirect notes it affects almost half of males over 40 years of age.

Finasteride and topical minoxidil are the two medicines most people compare because they have the strongest mainstream evidence and are widely used in Australia. They can help, but neither is a cure, neither works for everyone, and both require realistic expectations.

## First: Confirm The Pattern

Not every shedding episode is male pattern hair loss.

Before comparing medicines, check whether the pattern fits androgenetic alopecia:

- Gradual recession at the temples or hairline
- Thinning through the crown or mid-scalp
- Family history of similar pattern loss
- No obvious scalp rash, scaling, scarring, or pain
- No sudden diffuse shedding after illness, surgery, crash dieting, medication change, or major stress

Other causes may need different assessment, such as alopecia areata, telogen effluvium, iron deficiency, thyroid disease, fungal infection, traction alopecia, medication effects, inflammatory scalp disease, or scarring alopecia.

## Quick Comparison

| Feature | Finasteride | Topical minoxidil |
|---|---|---|
| Main action | Reduces conversion of testosterone to DHT | Supports hair growth activity and follicle cycling |
| Usual form | Oral 1 mg tablet | 5% foam or solution applied to scalp |
| Australian access | Schedule 4 prescription medicine | Schedule 2 Pharmacy Medicine for topical products |
| Best fit | Men with androgen-driven pattern loss who accept systemic risks | People wanting non-prescription topical treatment or growth support |
| Common limitation | Sexual, mood, breast, pregnancy-handling, and PSA considerations | Irritation, messiness, shedding, adherence, unwanted facial hair |
| Stopping | Benefit is lost over time after stopping | Regrown hair often sheds within months of stopping |
| Off-label variants | Topical finasteride or dutasteride may be specialist-led | Oral minoxidil is prescription-only and off-label for hair loss |

## How Finasteride Works

Finasteride inhibits type II 5-alpha-reductase, an enzyme involved in converting testosterone to dihydrotestosterone, or DHT.

DHT is a major driver of follicle miniaturisation in genetically susceptible men. Miniaturised follicles produce shorter, finer hairs over time. By lowering DHT activity, finasteride can slow progression and help some partly miniaturised follicles recover.

Australian product information for finasteride 1 mg states that it is indicated for male pattern hair loss in men 18 years or older, with efficacy not demonstrated in men over 41 years in the listed product information. It is not indicated for women or children.

Useful expectations:

- Daily use is usually needed.
- Early visible change can take several months.
- A 6 to 12 month photo review is more useful than judging week to week.
- Benefit depends on continued use.
- It is usually more about slowing loss than restoring a teenage hairline.

## Finasteride Safety Tradeoffs

Finasteride is prescription-only for a reason. The main decision is not whether it "works"; it is whether the expected benefit is worth the risk for the individual person.

Potential issues to discuss include:

- Reduced libido, erectile difficulty, ejaculation changes, or other sexual side effects
- Mood symptoms, including low mood or anxiety in some people
- Breast tenderness, breast enlargement, lumps, or nipple discharge
- Testicular pain or fertility concerns
- PSA reduction, which can affect prostate cancer screening interpretation
- Pregnancy exposure risk if tablets are crushed or broken
- Whether the person has a history that changes the risk-benefit discussion

Product information includes post-marketing reports of sexual dysfunction continuing after discontinuation. Post-marketing reports cannot always prove frequency or causality, but they are important enough to discuss before starting.

## How Topical Minoxidil Works

Topical minoxidil is applied to areas of scalp affected by hair loss. Its exact hair-growth mechanism is not fully understood, but it appears to support follicle activity, prolong the growth phase, and increase hair calibre in some responders.

In Australia, common topical minoxidil hair-regrowth products are Pharmacy Medicines. A pharmacist can help check whether the product is suitable and how to use it.

Useful expectations:

- It needs consistent application.
- It can take at least 4 months to see useful change.
- Some people get early shedding in the first weeks.
- Stopping usually leads to loss of gained hair over the following months.
- It works best when there are still miniaturised follicles, not completely bald shiny scalp.

## Minoxidil Safety Tradeoffs

Topical minoxidil is often perceived as "low risk" because it is non-prescription, but it still has side effects and misuse problems.

Common or important issues include:

- Scalp irritation, dryness, flaking, itch, or dermatitis
- Greasy texture or residue, depending on solution or foam
- Initial shedding that can be alarming
- Unwanted facial hair growth if product runs or transfers
- Dizziness, palpitations, swelling, or blood-pressure symptoms if too much is absorbed
- Higher absorption risk on irritated, inflamed, sunburnt, or broken scalp skin

Oral minoxidil is a different medicine-use decision. It is prescription-only, used off-label for hair loss, and needs a clinician to consider blood pressure, fluid retention, heart symptoms, interactions, and dose.

## Results Timeline

Hair growth is slow. A bad comparison happens when someone judges both medicines after a few weeks.

| Time | What may happen |
|---|---|
| First month | No visible improvement; minoxidil shedding may start |
| 2 to 4 months | Shedding may settle; early stabilisation or subtle thickening may appear |
| 4 to 6 months | Some responders notice visible improvement |
| 6 to 12 months | Better window for comparing photos and deciding whether treatment is helping |
| After stopping | Gains usually fade because the underlying pattern continues |

Take consistent photos in the same light, angle, hair length, and wet/dry state. Hair looks dramatically different under bathroom lighting, after styling product, or at a different haircut length.

## Monotherapy Or Combination?

Starting one treatment at a time has a practical advantage: you can tell what helped and what caused side effects.

Combination therapy may make sense when:

- The diagnosis is clear male pattern hair loss.
- Hair loss is moderate or progressing.
- Monotherapy has partly helped but not enough.
- The person accepts the combined burden of daily tablet plus topical routine.
- Side effects can be tracked clearly.

Combination therapy may be less useful when:

- The diagnosis is uncertain.
- The person is anxious about side effects and wants a clean trial.
- Adherence is unlikely.
- Scalp irritation makes topical use difficult.
- The hair loss is patchy, sudden, scarring, or inflammatory.

## When The Comparison Changes

Finasteride vs minoxidil is not the right frame for everyone.

| Situation | Why the plan changes |
|---|---|
| Female pattern hair loss | Different causes, pregnancy considerations, and treatment options |
| Sudden shedding | Consider telogen effluvium, illness, medications, nutrition, thyroid, iron, or stress |
| Patchy circular hair loss | Alopecia areata is a different diagnosis |
| Pain, scale, redness, pustules, or scarring | Needs scalp assessment before cosmetic treatment |
| Hair loss under 18 | Specialist or GP assessment is more important than self-treatment |
| Desire for transplant | Medical stabilisation is usually discussed before surgery |
| Significant distress | Mental health support can be part of care, not an afterthought |

## Australian Access And Cost Reality

Finasteride 1 mg for hair loss is a Schedule 4 prescription medicine and is generally a private cost for this indication. Topical minoxidil products are Pharmacy Medicines and are usually bought from pharmacies without a prescription.

Costs vary by brand, pharmacy, quantity, and formulation. Generic medicines can reduce cost, but the lowest-cost option is not always the best practical option if it causes irritation, is hard to apply, or is not used consistently.

For many people, the real cost is long-term adherence. A medicine that works only while used becomes an ongoing routine, not a one-off purchase.

## The Bottom Line

Finasteride is usually the stronger DHT-pathway option for male pattern hair loss, but it carries systemic risk and needs a prescription. Topical minoxidil is more accessible and can support regrowth, but it requires consistent application and can irritate the scalp.

The best plan starts with a correct diagnosis, realistic photos, a clear safety discussion, and a willingness to reassess after months rather than days.
