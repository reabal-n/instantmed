<Callout variant="info">

Finasteride and minoxidil are not interchangeable. Finasteride works through the androgen pathway. Topical minoxidil works more through scalp-level growth-cycle support. The useful comparison starts with confirming that the hair loss pattern actually fits androgenetic alopecia.

</Callout>

Male pattern hair loss is also called androgenetic alopecia. It usually causes gradual thinning at the temples, hairline, mid-scalp, or crown. It is common, genetic, and chronic. Healthdirect notes that male pattern baldness affects almost half of males over 40 years of age.

Finasteride and topical minoxidil are the two medicine options many Australians compare first because they are widely used and have clinical evidence. They can help the right person, but neither is a cure, neither works for everyone, and neither should be judged after only a few weeks.

The short version is simple: finasteride is a prescription medicine that reduces DHT activity; topical minoxidil is a pharmacy medicine applied to the scalp to support hair growth activity. The safer decision is rarely "which is stronger?" It is usually "what diagnosis do I have, what risks matter for me, and what can I use consistently for months?"

## First Confirm The Pattern

Not every shedding episode is male pattern hair loss.

Before comparing medicines, check whether the pattern fits androgenetic alopecia. Common clues include:

- Gradual recession at the temples or front hairline
- Thinning through the crown or mid-scalp
- Family history of a similar pattern
- Slow change over months or years
- No painful, red, scaly, pustular, or scarred scalp
- No sudden diffuse shedding after illness, surgery, crash dieting, medication change, childbirth, or major stress

Other causes can look similar at first. Alopecia areata, telogen effluvium, iron deficiency, thyroid disease, fungal infection, traction alopecia, medicine effects, inflammatory scalp disease, and scarring alopecia can all require a different assessment.

Australian Prescriber highlights several warning patterns that should change the plan, including rapid or extensive hair loss, scalp pain, itching, burning, or signs of scarring. If the diagnosis is unclear, the comparison between finasteride and minoxidil is premature.

## The Quick Answer

Finasteride and topical minoxidil are different tools.

Finasteride:

- Targets the DHT pathway involved in follicle miniaturisation
- Is usually taken as a prescription tablet for male pattern hair loss
- Requires a clinician to weigh benefit against systemic risks
- May affect sexual function, mood, breast symptoms, fertility discussions, and PSA interpretation
- Is not suitable for women who are pregnant or may become pregnant, and is not indicated for children

Topical minoxidil:

- Is applied to the scalp rather than taken for the DHT pathway
- Is available in common Australian hair-regrowth products as a Schedule 2 Pharmacy Medicine
- Requires consistent use and patience
- Can cause scalp irritation, unwanted hair growth, and early shedding
- Is different from oral minoxidil, which is prescription-only and used off-label for hair loss

The comparison becomes more nuanced when someone has advanced hair loss, poor adherence, side effect anxiety, uncertain diagnosis, other medicines, fertility plans, prostate screening, scalp irritation, or cardiac and blood-pressure concerns.

## How Finasteride Works

Finasteride inhibits type II 5-alpha-reductase, an enzyme involved in converting testosterone to dihydrotestosterone, or DHT.

DHT is a major driver of follicle miniaturisation in genetically susceptible men. Miniaturised follicles produce shorter, finer hairs over time. By lowering DHT activity, finasteride can slow progression and may help some partly miniaturised follicles recover.

Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods listings include finasteride 1 mg products for male pattern hair loss. Healthdirect lists finasteride among medicines used to manage male pattern baldness, and RACGP guidance describes finasteride as one of the established options for men with androgenetic alopecia.

Useful expectations:

- Daily use is usually needed.
- Visible change takes months, not days.
- Stabilising loss can be a meaningful result, even before visible thickening.
- A 6 to 12 month photo review is more useful than judging week to week.
- Benefit depends on continued use.
- It is usually more realistic to aim for slowing progression and modest thickening than restoring a teenage hairline.

Finasteride is not a general hair-loss medicine for every shedding pattern. If the loss is patchy, sudden, inflammatory, scarring, or caused by a non-androgen issue, lowering DHT may miss the real problem.

## Finasteride Safety Tradeoffs

Finasteride is prescription-only for a reason. The central decision is not just whether it can help hair. It is whether the expected benefit is worth the safety tradeoffs for the individual person.

Issues to discuss before use include:

- Reduced libido, erectile difficulty, ejaculation changes, or other sexual side effects
- Mood symptoms, including low mood or anxiety in some people
- Breast tenderness, breast enlargement, breast lumps, or nipple discharge
- Testicular pain or fertility concerns
- PSA reduction, which can affect prostate cancer screening interpretation
- Pregnancy exposure risk if tablets are crushed or broken
- Current medicines, medical history, mental health history, and personal risk tolerance

RACGP guidance notes that 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors can lower prostate-specific antigen by about 50%, which matters if PSA is being used for prostate cancer screening. Australian Prescriber also flags sexual dysfunction, breast symptoms, and PSA interpretation as part of the counselling discussion.

Some medicine information includes post-marketing reports of sexual dysfunction continuing after stopping. Post-marketing reports do not always prove frequency or causality, but they are important enough to be raised before treatment starts, especially for someone who would find that risk unacceptable.

## How Topical Minoxidil Works

Topical minoxidil is applied to areas of scalp affected by hair loss. Its exact hair-growth mechanism is not fully understood, but it appears to support follicle activity, prolong the growth phase, and increase hair calibre in some responders.

Healthdirect lists minoxidil solution or foam among medicines used for male pattern baldness. TGA public information for common 5% topical minoxidil products lists the active ingredient as minoxidil and the route as topical use.

Useful expectations:

- It needs consistent application according to the product directions.
- It can take at least 4 months to see useful change.
- Early shedding can happen and can be alarming.
- Stopping usually leads to loss of gained hair over the following months.
- It works best when there are still miniaturised follicles, not completely bald shiny scalp.

Topical minoxidil can suit someone who wants a non-prescription topical option, wants to avoid systemic DHT-pathway treatment, or wants growth-cycle support as part of a broader plan. The practical limitation is adherence. A topical medicine that is messy, irritating, or awkward may not be used consistently enough to help.

## Minoxidil Safety Tradeoffs

Topical minoxidil is often perceived as low risk because it is not prescription-only. That framing can be too casual.

Common or important issues include:

- Scalp irritation, dryness, flaking, itch, or dermatitis
- Greasy texture or residue, depending on formulation
- Initial shedding that can feel like the problem is worsening
- Unwanted facial hair growth if product runs or transfers
- Dizziness, palpitations, swelling, or blood-pressure symptoms if too much is absorbed
- Higher absorption risk on irritated, inflamed, sunburnt, or broken scalp skin

The scalp itself matters. If the skin is red, painful, infected, broken, scaly, or inflamed, applying a hair-growth product without assessment can delay the right diagnosis and may increase irritation or absorption.

Oral minoxidil is a separate decision. It is prescription-only and used off-label for hair loss in selected patients. Australian Prescriber notes that systemic minoxidil can cause effects such as postural hypotension and fluid retention, and that caution is needed in people with cardiovascular disease.

## Timeline: What To Expect

Hair growth is slow. A poor comparison happens when someone judges both medicines after a few weeks.

In the first month, there may be no visible improvement. With minoxidil, shedding can start early because hair cycling is changing. That does not automatically mean failure, but severe or worrying shedding should still be discussed with a clinician or pharmacist.

Between 2 and 4 months, shedding may settle and early stabilisation can appear. Healthdirect notes that improvement is usually noticed after about 4 months with finasteride or minoxidil.

Between 4 and 6 months, some responders notice visible thickening or reduced shedding. Others only see stabilisation. A stable hairline can still be a useful outcome if the previous pattern was steady worsening.

Between 6 and 12 months, photos become more useful for judging whether the plan is helping. Australian Prescriber recommends setting expectations that improvement takes at least 6 months and using objective monitoring such as serial photography.

After stopping, gains usually fade because the underlying pattern continues. Healthdirect notes that hair loss usually returns when these medicines are stopped.

## What Counts As A Useful Result

Hair-loss treatment is often judged too harshly or too generously because the goal is vague. "Regrowth" can mean several different things.

A useful result may be:

- Slower recession at the temples or hairline
- Less visible thinning at the crown
- Reduced shedding compared with the previous pattern
- Thicker calibre in partly miniaturised hair
- Better styling coverage without a dramatic new hairline
- Enough stabilisation to make future transplant planning more predictable

The opposite can also be true. A medicine can feel like it is doing something because the routine is active and reassuring, while photos show continued loss. That is why a pre-treatment baseline matters.

Expectations should also account for stage. Early pattern hair loss with many miniaturised follicles is a different situation from long-standing smooth bald scalp. Medicines generally work by preserving or improving follicles that are still biologically active. They do not reliably recreate hair in areas where follicles are no longer producing visible hair.

There is also a difference between cosmetic satisfaction and clinical response. A person might have measurable stabilisation but still feel distressed because the hairline has already changed. Another person might be happy with modest crown thickening. This is not trivial: Healthdirect notes that hair loss can affect confidence and mental health, so distress is part of the care conversation, not vanity.

The practical review question is: compared with baseline photos and symptoms, is the plan helping enough to justify ongoing cost, effort, and risk?

## How To Track Progress Without Fooling Yourself

Hair is easy to misread. Lighting, hair length, wet hair, styling product, a fresh haircut, camera angle, and anxiety can all distort the comparison.

Use a simple tracking routine:

- Take photos before starting treatment.
- Use the same room, lighting, distance, and angle each time.
- Photograph the front hairline, both temples, crown, and mid-scalp.
- Keep hair length and wet/dry state as consistent as possible.
- Track shedding, itch, irritation, sexual symptoms, mood changes, dizziness, swelling, and adherence.
- Review after months, not days.

If you change several things at once, interpretation becomes harder. A new supplement, shampoo, medicine, topical product, haircut, and prescription started together can create confusion about what helped or what caused a side effect.

## One Medicine Or Combination Treatment?

Starting one treatment at a time has a practical advantage: you can tell what helped and what caused side effects.

Combination therapy may make sense when:

- The diagnosis is clear male pattern hair loss.
- Hair loss is moderate or progressing.
- One option has partly helped but not enough.
- The person understands the safety tradeoffs.
- The routine is realistic enough to maintain.
- Side effects can be tracked clearly.

Combination therapy may be less useful when:

- The diagnosis is uncertain.
- Hair loss is sudden, patchy, painful, inflammatory, or scarring.
- The person is anxious about side effects and wants a clean trial.
- Scalp irritation makes topical use difficult.
- Adherence is unlikely.
- A fertility, pregnancy exposure, prostate screening, cardiac, or blood-pressure issue changes the risk discussion.

Australian Prescriber notes that oral finasteride with topical minoxidil is a commonly used combination because the mechanisms differ. That does not mean everyone should start both at once. It means the combination can be reasonable when the diagnosis, risks, and monitoring plan fit.

## When This Is The Wrong Comparison

Finasteride versus minoxidil is not the right frame for everyone.

The plan changes when hair loss is:

- Sudden and diffuse, especially after illness, surgery, childbirth, crash dieting, or major stress
- Patchy or circular, which can suggest alopecia areata
- Painful, itchy, burning, pustular, scaly, or associated with scalp redness
- Associated with scarring, shiny skin, loss of follicle openings, or permanent-looking patches
- Occurring in someone under 18
- Occurring in a woman who is pregnant, may become pregnant, or is breastfeeding
- Linked to a new medicine or medical condition
- Severe enough to cause significant distress, avoidance, or mood symptoms

Healthdirect notes that diagnosis usually involves history and examination, and sometimes tests such as blood tests or biopsy. Australian Prescriber recommends referral to a dermatologist when the diagnosis is unclear, scarring alopecia is suspected, there is no response after 6 months, or there is significant psychological distress or other symptoms.

## Australian Access And Scheduling

In Australia, access is not the same for the two medicines.

Finasteride 1 mg for male pattern hair loss is a prescription medicine. TGA ARTG listings identify finasteride 1 mg tablet products as registered medicines, and prescribing requires a clinician to decide whether it is appropriate for the person.

Topical minoxidil products for hair regrowth are available through pharmacies. TGA public information for 5% topical minoxidil products lists them as Schedule 2 Pharmacy Medicines, which means pharmacy advice is part of access.

Oral minoxidil is prescription-only and is not the same as topical minoxidil. Its use for hair loss is off-label and requires clinical judgement because systemic effects matter.

Cost also differs. RACGP guidance notes that androgenetic alopecia medicines are not covered by the PBS for that indication. The practical cost depends on medicine, brand, quantity, pharmacy, consultation requirements, and how long the treatment is continued.

## Questions To Ask Before Choosing

Useful questions are concrete:

- Does my pattern clearly fit androgenetic alopecia?
- Are there warning signs that need scalp examination or tests first?
- What is the realistic goal: slowing loss, thickening, reducing shedding, or preparing for transplant?
- What side effects would make me stop?
- Do I need PSA testing now or in future?
- Am I trying to conceive, or is there any pregnancy exposure risk in my household?
- Do I have scalp dermatitis, irritation, or sensitivity that might make topical treatment hard?
- Do I have blood-pressure, heart, swelling, or dizziness issues that matter for minoxidil decisions?
- Can I honestly maintain this routine for 6 to 12 months?
- How will I measure progress without relying on memory?

These questions are not paperwork. They are how a hair-loss plan stays realistic, safe, and reviewable.

## The Bottom Line

Finasteride targets the androgen pathway that drives follicle miniaturisation in many men with male pattern hair loss. Topical minoxidil supports scalp-level hair growth activity and is more accessible, but it still needs consistent use and can cause irritation or shedding.

The better option depends on diagnosis, safety, expectations, and adherence. A sensible plan starts with confirming the pattern, checking red flags, choosing a realistic review window, and tracking both benefit and side effects over months.
