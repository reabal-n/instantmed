Not all Centrelink payments use the same medical evidence. A standard medical certificate - the kind you get from a GP or telehealth service - works for some situations and is completely inadequate for others. Understanding what Centrelink actually needs for your specific circumstance is the first step to getting documentation right the first time, rather than cycling through rejected submissions.

## Standard Certificates vs Centrelink-Specific Forms

For Centrelink purposes, medical evidence falls into two categories.

**Standard medical certificates** from any AHPRA-registered doctor are accepted for:

- Short-term illness preventing you from meeting mutual obligations (JobSeeker, Youth Allowance)
- Brief temporary incapacity where evidence is requested
- Being unable to attend a scheduled Centrelink appointment due to sickness
- Follow-up periods for already-documented conditions

**Centrelink-specific forms** are required for:

- Formal Exemptions from Mutual Obligations (EMO)
- Disability Support Pension (DSP) applications
- Carer Payment and Carer Allowance applications
- Job Capacity Assessments (JCA) and Employment Services Assessments (ESAt)
- Situations where Centrelink needs to formally assess your functional capacity

The distinction comes down to duration and purpose. Short-term incapacity for a specific obligation can usually be covered by a standard certificate. Longer or more complex situations require the forms Centrelink uses to formally assess capacity and eligibility.

## JobSeeker and Mutual Obligations

If you receive JobSeeker and are temporarily unable to meet your mutual obligations - job search requirements, appointments with employment services providers, or Centrelink reporting - you need to notify Centrelink and provide evidence of your incapacity.

**For short periods (1-14 days):** A standard medical certificate from any registered doctor is generally sufficient. The certificate should state you were unfit for your mutual obligation activities during the specified period.

**For longer periods or formal exemption:** An Exemption from Mutual Obligations (EMO) is a formal arrangement where Centrelink suspends your requirements for a defined period. To obtain an EMO, Centrelink typically requires the SU415 Medical Certificate form, which your doctor must complete in full.

Notify Centrelink as soon as possible when you cannot meet your obligations - ideally before missing the activity. Late notification can affect your payment.

<Callout variant="info">If you miss an obligation due to illness and are worried about payment impacts, notify Centrelink online through myGov as soon as you are able. Centrelink can apply a "reasonable excuse" if you provide evidence of your illness promptly.</Callout>

## The SU415: Centrelink's Medical Certificate Form

The SU415 is Centrelink's own medical certificate form. When a standard certificate is insufficient, Centrelink will request this form be completed by your doctor. It asks for:

- Your diagnosis or medical condition
- How the condition affects your functional capacity (what you can and cannot do)
- The expected duration of incapacity
- Whether the condition is temporary or permanent
- Whether further review is expected and when

The form must be completed by your doctor and returned to Centrelink. A standard medical certificate does not substitute for the SU415 when Centrelink specifically requests it.

If you need an SU415 completed, book with your regular GP rather than a first-time telehealth consultation. Completing this form well requires knowledge of your history, functional limitations, and prognosis. Some telehealth services can complete Centrelink forms for established patients - ask when booking.

## Youth Allowance

Youth Allowance (student and jobseeker categories) has similar mutual obligation requirements to JobSeeker. Temporary incapacity provisions follow the same pattern: standard certificates for short periods, SU415 and potentially a formal capacity assessment for longer or complex situations.

For Youth Allowance students, being temporarily unable to study due to illness may also affect your study load requirements. Notify both Centrelink and your education provider with medical documentation to protect your payment and enrolment status.

## Parenting Payment

Parenting Payment recipients have mutual obligations depending on the age of their youngest child. If illness prevents you from meeting these obligations, standard medical certificates apply for short-term incapacity. For longer periods, similar provisions to JobSeeker apply.

## Disability Support Pension: What Evidence Is Actually Required

DSP has the most demanding medical evidence requirements of any Centrelink payment. A standard medical certificate - including a comprehensive one - is not sufficient for a DSP application. What Centrelink needs includes:

**Treating Doctor's Report (TDR):** A detailed report from your primary treating doctor covering your diagnosis, treatment history, functional limitations, and prognosis. This is completed on a specific Centrelink form (SU684 or equivalent). It must come from your treating doctor, not a new consultation.

**Specialist reports:** For conditions involving specialists, Centrelink expects evidence from the treating specialists. A GP report alone is often insufficient.

**Impairment Table assessment:** Centrelink assesses functional limitations against the DSP Impairment Tables, which quantify impairment across body systems. The medical evidence must be sufficient to support this formal assessment.

**Condition requirements:** Conditions must be fully diagnosed, treated, and stabilised before assessment. Evidence of treatment history over time is required, not just a current snapshot.

<Callout variant="warning">Do not attempt to build a DSP application from a single telehealth consultation with a doctor who has never seen you. DSP requires a documented history of treating relationships with GPs and specialists. Applications supported only by recent or limited medical evidence are routinely rejected.</Callout>

DSP applications are frequently rejected on first application due to insufficient evidence. Working with your regular GP over time - building documentation with specialist input - produces better outcomes than assembling evidence at the point of application.

## Carer Payment and Carer Allowance

These payments support people providing ongoing care to someone with a severe disability or medical condition. Evidence requirements focus on the person being cared for:

- Treating Doctor's Report from the care recipient's doctor, confirming the condition and level of care required
- Child Disability Assessment Tool (CDAT) for children under 16 with a disability or medical condition
- Medical evidence from treating specialists for adults receiving care

As with DSP, this documentation must come from established treating relationships, not a new consultation.

## When a Standard Online Certificate Works

Despite the complexity of the above, standard telehealth certificates are appropriate and accepted for:

- Short-term illness preventing you from meeting JobSeeker or Youth Allowance obligations for a few days
- Being unable to attend a scheduled Centrelink appointment due to genuine illness
- Brief temporary incapacity where Centrelink requests evidence
- Follow-up certificates for ongoing conditions already documented with Centrelink

For these situations, a telehealth consultation can produce what you need quickly. The certificate should clearly state the specific dates you were incapacitated and that you were unfit to participate in your obligations during that period.

## Tips for Getting Documentation Right First Time

- Ask Centrelink directly what form or evidence they need before booking a doctor. This avoids providing the wrong document and having to repeat the process.
- If Centrelink requests the SU415, book with your regular GP rather than attempting a first-time telehealth consultation.
- Keep copies of every certificate, report, and form you submit. Centrelink can lose documents and having your own records protects you if disputes arise.
- For DSP: start building your evidence base over time with your treating team, rather than trying to assemble comprehensive documentation at the point of application.
- If Centrelink rejects your documentation, request a written explanation of exactly what they need. This tells you precisely what gap to fill.
- If your payment is incorrectly affected and you believe the documentation was sufficient, you can apply for an internal review and ultimately appeal to the Administrative Review Tribunal.

## What If the Certificate Is Rejected?

If Centrelink rejects your medical evidence:

- Request specific written advice on what is missing or insufficient
- Contact your doctor to provide whatever additional information Centrelink has specified
- Ask whether a different form (the SU415 rather than a standard certificate) is required
- For complex situations, consider asking your regular GP to write a detailed letter addressing the specific gaps Centrelink has identified

Centrelink decisions are reviewable. If you believe the rejection was wrong, the Social Services Appeals process starts with an Authorised Review Officer and can escalate from there.
