<Callout variant="info">Short answer: Yes, telehealth is safe - when you use a legitimate service. Australian telehealth is regulated under the same framework as in-person medicine. The doctors are real, the privacy protections are real, and the clinical standards are real. This guide explains how to verify all of that.</Callout>

## Are Telehealth Doctors Real Doctors?

Yes. In Australia, every doctor who provides telehealth must be registered with the Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency ([AHPRA](https://www.ahpra.gov.au "Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency")). There is no separate "online doctor licence" - the same registration required to practise at a hospital or GP clinic is required to practise online. AHPRA registration requires a recognised medical degree, supervised clinical training, and ongoing compliance with the Medical Board of Australia's standards.

What this means for you: the doctor who reviews your telehealth request completed the same 6-year medical degree as your regular GP. They have the same legal and ethical obligations. They carry the same professional indemnity. If they make a negligent clinical decision, they face the same consequences as any other registered doctor.

You can verify any doctor's registration yourself at ahpra.gov.au. Search by name or registration number. The register shows registration status, any conditions on practice, and any disciplinary findings.

<Callout variant="tip">Any legitimate telehealth service will tell you the name of your treating doctor and display their AHPRA registration number on every document they issue. If a service won't identify its doctors, that is a significant red flag.</Callout>

## LegitScript Certification: An Independent Safety Standard

Beyond AHPRA registration, some telehealth services hold LegitScript certification. LegitScript is an independent verification organisation that monitors online healthcare services for compliance with healthcare laws and ethical standards. It was originally developed to address the problem of illegal online pharmacies but now covers telehealth platforms globally.

LegitScript-certified services have demonstrated to an independent auditor that they:

- Use licensed healthcare practitioners appropriate to the jurisdiction
- Do not dispense or prescribe medications in violation of applicable laws
- Maintain transparent business practices and clear pricing
- Have proper patient safety protocols
- Do not operate as a front for illegal pharmaceutical sales

LegitScript certification is voluntary - not all reputable services hold it - but it is a useful indicator when evaluating whether a service has been independently verified. It is particularly valuable for patients who want assurance beyond the baseline AHPRA compliance.

## Data Security and Privacy Protections

Your health information is among the most sensitive data that exists. Australian law treats it accordingly.

### What the Privacy Act Requires

Under the Privacy Act 1988 and the 13 Australian Privacy Principles, health information is classified as "sensitive information" - the highest protection category. A telehealth service that holds your health records must:

- Store data securely with appropriate access controls
- Not share your information with third parties without your consent (with narrow exceptions)
- Not use your health information for marketing or advertising purposes
- Respond to your requests to access or correct your records
- Notify you of any data breach that may cause serious harm
- Destroy records when they are no longer needed for a legitimate purpose

The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) enforces these obligations and accepts complaints about privacy breaches.

### Technical Security Standards

Reputable Australian telehealth services use:

- Encryption in transit (TLS/HTTPS) and at rest (AES-256 or equivalent)
- Australian-based data storage to maintain data sovereignty
- Role-based access controls so only treating doctors can access your records
- Audit logging of all data access events
- Regular penetration testing and security reviews

Your consultation data - symptoms, history, treatment decisions - should never leave the service's secure systems except to communicate with you directly (email or SMS) or with your explicit consent.

### Doctor-Patient Confidentiality

Standard doctor-patient confidentiality applies in telehealth. A doctor cannot disclose your health information to your employer, your family members (without your consent), or any other third party. The exceptions are the same as in any medical context: mandatory reporting requirements for certain communicable diseases, child protection notifications, and court orders.

## Clinical Quality and Safety Standards

The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care has published detailed standards for telehealth, recognising it as a legitimate and safe mode of healthcare delivery. Telehealth doctors are held to the same clinical governance requirements as in-person practitioners:

- They must assess clinical appropriateness before proceeding
- They must document consultations to the same standard as in-person records
- They must follow evidence-based prescribing guidelines
- They must decline to treat conditions beyond the scope of telehealth
- They must refer patients to in-person care when required

The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) has published telehealth guidelines confirming that the standard of care expectation does not change based on the consultation mode.

## When Telehealth Refers You to In-Person Care

A key safety feature of good telehealth is knowing its limits. Responsible services will decline to treat conditions that require physical examination, and this is the correct outcome. Common reasons for referral include:

- Symptoms suggesting a serious or complex condition requiring investigation
- Conditions requiring physical examination for diagnosis (certain ear, eye, or chest presentations)
- Mental health situations requiring crisis intervention or specialist assessment
- Requests for medications that require monitoring or examination before prescribing
- Paediatric presentations in young children where physical assessment is important
- Any situation where the doctor cannot obtain sufficient clinical information remotely

If a telehealth service refers you to in-person care, that is the safety system working correctly. A service that is never willing to refer you - that will treat everything remotely regardless of clinical appropriateness - is the dangerous one.

## How to Identify a Scam or Unsafe Telehealth Service

Not every service operating online meets Australian standards. These are the concrete warning signs:

- **No named doctors**: The service will not identify which doctor reviewed your case. AHPRA-registered doctors are legally required to identify themselves.
- **No AHPRA numbers on documents**: Certificates and prescriptions from registered doctors always carry the doctor's AHPRA registration number.
- **Guaranteed outcomes**: No legitimate medical service guarantees a specific prescription or certificate outcome before assessment. Clinical decisions are made after review, not before.
- **Suspiciously low prices**: Very low pricing can indicate either unregistered practitioners or a business model that does not include genuine clinical assessment.
- **No safety screening**: A legitimate service asks about your medical history, allergies, and current medications. A service that skips this is not performing adequate clinical assessment.
- **Offers controlled substances or Schedule 8 medications**: Legitimate telehealth services in Australia do not prescribe Schedule 8 controlled drugs. Any service offering these online is likely operating illegally.
- **No complaints process**: Every legitimate healthcare service has a complaints process and is obligated to respond to concerns.
- **No Australian ABN or physical address**: Australian businesses providing healthcare must be registered and identifiable.

## What If Something Goes Wrong?

The same regulatory machinery that oversees in-person medicine oversees telehealth. If you have concerns:

- **About a doctor's clinical conduct**: Lodge a complaint with AHPRA at ahpra.gov.au. AHPRA has powers to investigate, impose conditions, suspend, or deregister practitioners.
- **About a health service**: Contact the Health Care Complaints Commissioner (or equivalent body) in your state. NSW: HCCC. Victoria: Health Complaints Commissioner. Queensland: OHO. Western Australia: Health and Disability Services Complaints Office.
- **About a business practice**: The Australian Consumer Law covers misleading conduct by healthcare businesses. Report to the ACCC.
- **About a data breach**: The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner handles privacy complaints.

Telehealth services are not exempt from any of these oversight mechanisms. An online business model does not create regulatory immunity.

## The Research on Telehealth Safety

The evidence base for telehealth safety has grown substantially since 2020. Key findings from Australian and international research:

- Patient outcomes for telehealth-appropriate conditions are comparable to in-person care
- Patient satisfaction with telehealth is consistently high, particularly for convenience and accessibility
- Diagnostic accuracy for primary care presentations assessed via telehealth is similar to face-to-face consultation for conditions where physical examination is not required
- Telehealth significantly improves healthcare access for rural and remote Australians, reducing health disparities

The safety literature also consistently shows the same finding: telehealth is safe when used for appropriate conditions and unsafe when used for conditions requiring physical examination. The responsibility of a good telehealth service is to know the difference and act on it.
