## What Is an Uncomplicated UTI?

A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection of the urinary system - most commonly the bladder (cystitis) or urethra. UTIs are among the most common bacterial infections in Australia, affecting roughly half of all women at least once in their lifetime.

An "uncomplicated" UTI means:

- The infection is limited to the lower urinary tract (bladder and urethra, not the kidneys)
- The patient is an otherwise healthy adult woman, not pregnant
- There are no signs of systemic illness (no significant fever, no confusion, no back pain suggesting kidney involvement)
- There is no known structural abnormality of the urinary tract
- The patient is not immunocompromised

This specific combination is why uncomplicated UTIs are the most common condition treated via telehealth antibiotic assessment. The symptom profile is well-characterised, the evidence base for first-line treatment is strong, and physical examination rarely adds diagnostic value for straightforward cases.

## Symptoms That Suggest a UTI

Classic symptoms of a lower urinary tract infection:

- Burning or stinging sensation during urination (dysuria)
- Needing to urinate more frequently than normal
- Urgency - a sudden, strong urge to urinate that is difficult to delay
- Lower abdominal or pelvic discomfort or pressure
- Urine that looks cloudy, dark, or has an unusually strong odour
- Passing only small amounts of urine despite strong urge
- General feeling of being unwell

These symptoms, occurring together in an adult woman without other significant health concerns, form a clinical picture that doctors recognise immediately.

<Callout variant="warning">Symptoms that suggest the infection has spread to the kidneys (pyelonephritis) include fever above 38 degrees, pain or tenderness in the back or flank (the area between your ribs and hip), nausea, or vomiting. These require in-person assessment, potentially including urine culture and more intensive treatment. Do not try to treat these symptoms online.</Callout>

## How to Get an Antibiotic Prescription for a UTI Online

The process is straightforward for eligible patients:

- Complete the online [consultation form](/consult "Start a UTI consultation"). Describe your symptoms, when they started, any previous UTIs and how they were treated, current medications, and known allergies.
- An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case. For a clear presentation of uncomplicated UTI symptoms with no complicating factors, this is typically reviewed within 1 to 2 hours during operating hours.
- If treatment is appropriate, the doctor issues an antibiotic prescription as an eScript. The digital token is sent to your phone via SMS.
- Take the SMS to any Australian pharmacy to collect your medication. Your Medicare card applies for PBS pricing.

If the doctor identifies complicating factors from your history, you will receive advice on why in-person assessment is more appropriate and what that should involve.

## What the Doctor Needs to Know

To assess your UTI safely, the doctor needs:

- **Symptom description**: which symptoms you have, how long they have been present, and whether they are getting better or worse
- **Severity**: is this mild, moderate, or severe? Severe symptoms may indicate complications.
- **Previous UTIs**: how recently, how many, and what you were treated with previously. Frequent recurrence changes the clinical picture.
- **Pregnancy status**: non-negotiable safety check. UTIs in pregnancy require different management.
- **Allergies**: particularly to antibiotics. Penicillin allergy, for example, does not affect standard UTI treatment, but sulphonamide allergy does.
- **Current medications**: some medications interact with UTI antibiotics
- **Fever or [back pain](/conditions/back-pain "Back pain information")**: absence of these confirms lower tract involvement; presence indicates referral

Accurate answers lead to appropriate treatment. If you are unsure about any of these, note that uncertainty rather than guessing.

## When Telehealth Is Not Appropriate for a UTI

Telehealth is appropriate for uncomplicated UTIs in healthy adult women. It is not appropriate when:

- **You have a fever above 38 degrees** - this suggests kidney involvement or sepsis
- **You have back or loin pain** - possible pyelonephritis requires in-person assessment and often urine culture
- **You are pregnant** - UTIs in pregnancy require different antibiotic selection and closer monitoring
- **You are male** - UTIs in men are considered complicated by definition and need in-person assessment
- **The patient is a child** - paediatric UTIs require in-person assessment
- **You are immunocompromised** - diabetes, HIV, chemotherapy, or immunosuppressive medications change the risk profile
- **This is a recurrent UTI (more than 3 in 12 months)** - recurrent UTIs need investigation to rule out an underlying cause
- **Your symptoms are not improving on current antibiotics** - treatment failure needs urine culture
- **You have a known urinary tract abnormality** - structural issues require specialist assessment

## PBS-Subsidised UTI Antibiotics

Most antibiotics used for UTI treatment are listed on the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS). With a valid Medicare card and a [prescription](/prescriptions "Request a prescription online"), you pay the standard co-payment:

- General patients: up to $31.60 per script
- Concession card holders: up to $7.70 per script

The PBS subsidy applies whether your prescription came from a telehealth service or an in-person GP. The eScript is accepted at all Australian pharmacies.

Without Medicare, you pay the full price. Common UTI antibiotics typically cost $15 to $35 without subsidy, varying by pharmacy and specific medication.

## Completing Your Course of UTI Antibiotics

Complete the full prescribed course, even if symptoms improve after the first day. UTI symptoms often begin to improve within 24 to 48 hours of starting antibiotics, but stopping early allows surviving bacteria to persist and potentially develop resistance.

If your symptoms have not begun to improve within 48 hours of starting antibiotics, contact your prescribing doctor. Failure to respond to treatment may indicate:

- The bacteria causing your infection is resistant to the prescribed antibiotic
- The diagnosis may be incorrect (other conditions can mimic UTI symptoms)
- The infection may have spread beyond the bladder

Urine culture - which requires an in-person visit and laboratory testing - is the definitive way to identify the causative bacteria and its antibiotic sensitivity. This is recommended for recurrent or treatment-resistant UTIs.

## Preventing Recurrent UTIs

If you are experiencing UTIs repeatedly, consider:

- Drinking adequate fluid throughout the day to promote regular urination
- Urinating after sexual intercourse, which reduces bacterial colonisation of the urethra
- Wiping front to back to reduce cross-contamination
- Avoiding prolonged use of spermicidal products, which can disrupt normal vaginal flora
- Discussing preventive antibiotic options with your GP if you have three or more UTIs per year

Cranberry products have mixed evidence. They may reduce recurrence slightly in some people but are not a substitute for antibiotic treatment of an active infection.

## Connection to the Broader Antibiotics Framework

UTI treatment is part of Australia's broader approach to responsible antibiotic prescribing. First-line antibiotics are chosen based on local resistance patterns, which the Therapeutic Guidelines (eTG) update regularly. This matters because overuse of certain antibiotics creates resistance that makes future UTI treatment harder for everyone.

A doctor prescribing for your UTI is following these guidelines - not arbitrarily choosing a drug. If the antibiotic prescribed is not what you expected or have had before, there may be a good clinical reason. Ask the doctor if you are uncertain.
