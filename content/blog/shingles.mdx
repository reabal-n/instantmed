Shingles - medically known as herpes zoster - is a reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), the same virus responsible for chickenpox. Following a primary chickenpox infection, VZV establishes lifelong latency in sensory nerve ganglia throughout the body. In some individuals, particularly as immune function declines with age or immunosuppression, the virus reactivates and travels down the sensory nerve to the skin, producing the characteristic dermatomal rash.

According to the AIHW, an estimated 120,000 Australians are diagnosed with shingles each year, and approximately 1 in 3 people will experience at least one episode during their lifetime. The lifetime risk increases substantially with age: over 50% of those who reach 80 will have had shingles.

## Who Gets Shingles?

Risk factors for shingles reactivation include:

- **Age**: The most significant risk factor. Risk rises steeply after age 50 as immune surveillance of VZV-infected ganglia weakens.
- **Immunosuppression**: HIV, haematological malignancies, solid organ transplantation, and immunosuppressive medications substantially increase risk.
- **Psychological stress**: Significant life stress has been associated with shingles reactivation in epidemiological studies, likely via stress-related immune modulation.
- **Prior chickenpox infection**: Essentially all Australians born before widespread varicella vaccination were infected with wild-type VZV.

People who received the varicella vaccine (which uses live attenuated VZV) rather than having wild-type chickenpox can also develop shingles, though the risk appears lower than with wild-type infection.

## Recognising Shingles: The Stages

Shingles progresses through distinct phases. Early recognition is critical because antivirals are most effective if started within 72 hours of rash onset.

### Prodrome Phase (1-5 days before rash)

Many patients experience symptoms before the rash appears:
- Localised pain, burning, or tingling in the area where the rash will appear
- Heightened skin sensitivity (allodynia - even light touch is painful)
- Itching or unusual skin sensations
- Headache, fatigue, low-grade fever
- Occasionally: photophobia, malaise

The prodrome without rash can be diagnostically challenging. Pain in the chest or abdomen in this phase is sometimes mistaken for cardiac, pulmonary, or gastrointestinal conditions.

### Active Rash Phase (days 2-14)

- Red base with clusters of clear, fluid-filled vesicles appearing on the red areas
- Rash distributed in a dermatomal pattern - a band or strip following the path of the affected nerve
- **Always unilateral**: shingles does not cross the midline in its initial presentation
- Vesicles progress: initially clear, then cloudy, then crusting
- Pain is usually significant during this phase
- Most commonly affects the thoracic dermatomes (chest/back/ribs), but can affect face, neck, or limbs

The dermatome most frequently affected is T3-L2 (thoracic and lumbar). Facial involvement via the trigeminal nerve is less common but more consequential.

### Crusting and Resolution (weeks 2-4)

Vesicles crust over approximately 7-10 days after onset and heal over the following weeks. Most rashes resolve completely within 3-4 weeks. Scarring and pigmentation changes can persist.

## Why the 72-Hour Window Matters

Antiviral therapy is the cornerstone of shingles treatment - but timing is critical. RACGP and Therapeutic Guidelines (eTG) guidance identifies 72 hours from rash onset as the therapeutic window within which antivirals provide the greatest benefit.

**Why 72 hours?** The antivirals (valaciclovir, aciclovir, famciclovir) suppress viral replication. In the early phase, active viral replication is occurring in the sensory neurons and skin. Suppressing replication at this stage:
- Reduces the severity and duration of the acute rash
- Reduces acute pain
- Most importantly, reduces the risk of postherpetic neuralgia (PHN), the most common serious complication

After 72 hours, the replication phase is winding down and antivirals provide diminishing benefit for most patients. Exceptions where antivirals are initiated beyond 72 hours: ophthalmic zoster (regardless of timing), immunocompromised patients, and severe or complicated presentations.

**Australian prescribing**: Valaciclovir 1000mg three times daily for 7 days is the standard first-line regimen in Australian practice (eTG recommendation). Aciclovir and famciclovir are alternatives.

> **Warning:** If you suspect shingles - particularly if you have the typical unilateral burning pain or early rash - seek medical assessment that day, not the following week. The 72-hour window closes quickly.

## Shingles in Special Locations

### Ophthalmic Zoster (Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus)

When the ophthalmic branch of the trigeminal nerve is affected, the rash involves the forehead, periorbital area, and tip of the nose (Hutchinson's sign - rash on the nose tip predicts eye involvement). Ophthalmic zoster can affect the cornea, uvea, retina, and optic nerve.

**This requires urgent ophthalmological review** regardless of the duration of symptoms. Antiviral therapy should be started immediately, and eye involvement must be assessed and treated to prevent permanent vision damage.

### Ramsay Hunt Syndrome

Reactivation in the geniculate ganglion of the facial nerve causes:
- Vesicular rash in the ear canal or on the eardrum
- Ipsilateral facial palsy (weakness or paralysis of the face on the affected side)
- Ear pain
- Vertigo, tinnitus, and hearing loss in some cases

Ramsay Hunt syndrome requires urgent assessment and combined antiviral plus corticosteroid treatment. Facial nerve recovery is better when treatment is initiated early.

## Complications

### Postherpetic Neuralgia (PHN)

PHN is pain persisting in the affected area beyond 90 days after the rash, caused by nerve damage from viral inflammation. It is the most common serious complication of shingles.

PHN affects approximately 10-15% of shingles patients overall, rising to 25-50% in those over 70. The pain can be severe, constant, and debilitating - described as burning, stabbing, or electric. Allodynia (pain from light touch like clothing on the skin) is characteristic.

Treatment options for PHN include tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline), anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin), topical capsaicin, topical lidocaine patches, and opioid analgesia for refractory cases. Management of established PHN is difficult; prevention via antiviral therapy and vaccination is the priority.

## Contagiousness

Shingles is not the same as chickenpox - you cannot "catch" shingles from a person with shingles. However, a person with active shingles vesicles can transmit VZV to someone who has not previously had chickenpox or received the varicella vaccine, in whom it would cause chickenpox (not shingles).

Shingles is contagious from vesicle onset until all vesicles have crusted over. During this period, avoid contact with:
- Infants too young for varicella vaccination (before 18 months)
- Immunocompromised individuals
- Pregnant women who have not had chickenpox or been vaccinated

Once crusted over, the virus is no longer transmissible.

## Prevention: Shingles Vaccination

Two vaccines have been used in Australia for shingles prevention:

**Zostavax (live attenuated)**: A single-dose live vaccine. Previously on the National Immunisation Program (NIP) as a free vaccine for adults aged 70 (catch-up to age 79). Efficacy approximately 51% for shingles and 67% for PHN. Not for use in immunocompromised individuals.

**Shingrix (recombinant subunit)**: A two-dose non-live vaccine given 2-6 months apart. Highly efficacious (over 90% against shingles, over 89% against PHN in adults over 50). Safe in immunocompromised individuals. Added to the NIP in November 2023, replacing Zostavax.

**Current NIP schedule (from November 2023)**:
- Free for adults aged 65 and over
- Free for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander adults aged 50 and over
- Free for immunocompromised adults aged 18 and over

Services Australia administers the NIP. Check eligibility at the Department of Health and Aged Care website or with your GP. Private vaccination with Shingrix is available for adults outside the funded age groups.

The vaccines do not treat existing shingles - they are preventive. Even if you have had shingles before, vaccination is still recommended to reduce the risk of recurrence.

## Telehealth for Shingles

Early-stage shingles without ophthalmic or ear involvement, in an immunocompetent adult, can often be assessed via telehealth. A doctor can assess your symptom description and visible rash (via photo or video), prescribe antivirals if clinically appropriate, and arrange a medical certificate if you need time off.

In-person assessment is recommended for:
- Any suspicion of ophthalmic or facial nerve involvement
- Immunocompromised individuals
- Uncertainty about diagnosis
- Significant systemic unwellness
