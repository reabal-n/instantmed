Shingles, also called herpes zoster, is a reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus: the same virus that causes chickenpox. After chickenpox, the virus can stay dormant in sensory nerves for years. If it reactivates, it can travel along a nerve to the skin and cause a painful rash.

The Australian Immunisation Handbook describes shingles as a painful, self-limiting vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution. In plain English: it often appears as grouped blisters in a strip or band on one side of the body.

Most shingles settles, but the timing and location matter. Early assessment can reduce missed treatment opportunities, and shingles near the eye, ear, face, or in people with immune risk can be more serious.

## What Shingles Usually Looks Like

Shingles often follows a sequence.

### Before the Rash

Some people feel symptoms before there is anything obvious to see:

- Burning, tingling, itching, or stabbing pain
- Skin sensitivity, where light touch feels painful
- Localised discomfort in a stripe or patch
- Headache, tiredness, or feeling generally unwell

This stage can be confusing because pain may appear before the rash. Chest, back, or abdominal shingles pain can be mistaken for muscle strain or other conditions.

### Rash and Blister Stage

The rash usually:

- Appears on one side of the body
- Follows a band, strip, or patch corresponding to a nerve area
- Starts as red or inflamed skin
- Develops small fluid-filled blisters
- Becomes painful, itchy, sensitive, or tender
- Crusts over as it heals

Shingles can occur on the chest, back, abdomen, pelvis, neck, face, scalp, arm, or leg. A rash that crosses widely across both sides of the body, is not painful, or looks unlike grouped blisters may need a different diagnosis considered.

## Why Early Assessment Matters

Antiviral treatment works by reducing varicella-zoster virus replication. It is generally most useful when started early after the rash appears.

The practical window people hear about is **within 72 hours of rash onset**. This is not because shingles becomes harmless after 72 hours. It means the clearest benefit for uncomplicated shingles is usually earlier in the course.

Assessment can still be important after 72 hours if:

- New blisters are still appearing
- Pain is severe
- The rash is near the eye, forehead, nose, ear, or face
- There is facial weakness, hearing change, dizziness, or ear pain
- The person is pregnant
- The person is immunocompromised
- The rash is widespread or severe
- There are signs of bacterial infection

<Callout variant="warning">If shingles is suspected, same-day advice is usually sensible, especially when the rash is new, painful, on the face, near the eye or ear, widespread, or the person has immune risk.</Callout>

## Eye, Face, and Ear Shingles

Shingles in certain locations needs faster assessment because complications can affect vision, hearing, facial movement, or deeper structures.

### Eye or Forehead

Shingles around the eye area can cause temporary or permanent vision problems. Healthdirect notes that shingles in the eye area may require referral to an ophthalmologist.

Seek urgent assessment for:

- Rash on the forehead
- Rash around the eyelid
- Eye redness or pain
- Vision change
- Light sensitivity
- Rash on the tip or side of the nose

### Ear or Facial Weakness

Shingles can also be associated with Ramsay Hunt syndrome. Healthdirect lists features including rash around the ear, earache, hearing loss, dizziness, and difficulty moving facial muscles.

Seek urgent assessment for:

- Ear pain with a shingles-like rash
- Blisters in or around the ear
- Facial droop or weakness
- New dizziness or vertigo
- Hearing changes
- Ringing in the ear

## Who Is at Higher Risk

Risk rises with age and with reduced immune function. The Australian Immunisation Handbook says the risk and severity of shingles and complications increases with age, and that people who are immunocompromised have higher risk.

Higher-risk groups include:

- Adults over 50
- Adults over 65
- People with moderate or severe immunocompromise
- People receiving chemotherapy or certain immune-suppressing medicines
- People with haematological malignancy or after transplant
- People with HIV or other conditions affecting immunity
- People who are pregnant and develop a shingles-like rash

People with immune risk should not manage shingles casually because complications, recurrence, and disseminated disease are more likely.

## Is Shingles Contagious?

Shingles itself is not caught from another person in the way a cold is. You cannot catch shingles from someone with shingles.

The contagious issue is the blister fluid. If someone who has never had chickenpox and has not been vaccinated comes into direct contact with active shingles blisters, they can catch varicella-zoster virus and develop chickenpox.

To reduce spread while blisters are active:

- Keep the rash covered where practical.
- Avoid scratching or touching blisters.
- Wash hands after touching the rash or dressings.
- Do not share towels or clothing that contact the rash.
- Avoid direct blister contact with people who may be vulnerable.
- Continue precautions until blisters have crusted.

Vulnerable contacts include infants, pregnant people without immunity, and immunocompromised people.

## Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

The most common longer-term complication is post-herpetic neuralgia: nerve pain that continues after the rash has healed.

Healthdirect says around 1 in 10 people have shingles pain and tingling that lasts for months or years, and that it is more common in people over 50. The Australian Immunisation Handbook notes that post-herpetic neuralgia becomes more common with age.

Pain may feel:

- Burning
- Stabbing
- Electric
- Aching
- Sensitive to clothing or light touch

Persistent pain after shingles should be reviewed. It may require a different pain-management approach from ordinary skin pain.

## Treatment: What Usually Helps

Treatment depends on timing, severity, location, age, pregnancy status, immune status, and complications.

Common parts of care include:

- Early clinical assessment when shingles is suspected
- Antiviral medicine when indicated
- Pain relief
- Keeping the rash clean and covered
- Watching for bacterial infection
- Avoiding direct blister contact with vulnerable people
- Review if pain persists after the rash heals

Do not use leftover antiviral tablets, antibiotics, steroid creams, or home remedies without clinical advice. Shingles can look like other rashes, and high-risk locations need specific assessment.

## Vaccination in Australia

Vaccination is the best prevention strategy for shingles and its complications.

The Australian Immunisation Handbook says Shingrix is the only shingles vaccine currently available in Australia for prevention of herpes zoster and associated complications. It is a 2-dose vaccine.

In 2026, a 2-dose Shingrix course is funded under the National Immunisation Program for:

- Adults aged 65 years and over
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people aged 50 years and over
- Selected people aged 18 years and over with moderate or severe immunocompromise

The schedule is:

- **Immunocompetent people:** 2 doses, 2-6 months apart
- **Immunocompromised people:** 2 doses, 1-2 months apart

The vaccine prevents shingles; it does not treat an active shingles episode. People who have had shingles before can still receive zoster vaccine at the recommended age. The Immunisation Handbook suggests immunocompetent people wait at least 12 months after an episode before vaccination, while immunocompromised people can receive Shingrix from 3 months after acute illness, with individual timing advice.

If someone previously received a free Zostavax vaccine under the NIP, Department of Health guidance says they are not eligible for free Shingrix through the NIP until 5 years after the Zostavax dose.

## Practical Shingles Checklist

If shingles is possible, write down:

- Date and time the rash first appeared
- Whether pain, tingling, or sensitivity started before the rash
- Location of the rash
- Whether it is on one side only
- Whether the eye, forehead, nose, ear, or face is involved
- Whether new blisters are still appearing
- Current medicines and immune-suppressing treatments
- Pregnancy status, if relevant
- Whether there is fever, spreading redness, pus, or worsening pain

This makes assessment faster and reduces the chance of missing high-risk features.
