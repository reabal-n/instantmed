Most people taking multiple medications do not think about interactions until something goes wrong. But medication interactions are common, clinically significant, and often preventable with good information-sharing. Understanding the types of interactions, the high-risk combinations, and what to tell your healthcare providers can protect your health and your treatment outcomes.

## What a Medication Interaction Is

A medication interaction occurs when one substance (a drug, food, or supplement) affects how another medication works in your body. The result can be:

- **Reduced efficacy**: The medication does not work as well as expected
- **Increased efficacy**: The medication has a stronger effect than intended - which can lead to toxicity
- **New side effects**: The combination causes effects that neither substance causes alone
- **Toxicity**: In serious cases, a dangerous level of the drug accumulates

Interactions happen through several mechanisms. The most clinically significant involve cytochrome P450 (CYP450) enzymes in the liver - the primary system for metabolising drugs. If one drug inhibits or induces these enzymes, it can dramatically alter the blood concentration of other medications.

## Drug-Drug Interactions

### High-Risk Combinations to Know

**Anticoagulants (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban)**: Warfarin has one of the most interaction-prone profiles of any common medication. It interacts with aspirin, many antibiotics, NSAIDs, and numerous other drugs. The newer direct oral anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban) have fewer interactions but still interact with some antibiotics and antifungals.

**SSRIs and other serotonergic medications**: Combining two medications that increase serotonin levels can cause serotonin syndrome - a potentially serious condition involving agitation, high fever, and rapid heart rate. Combinations to be careful of: SSRIs with tramadol, SSRIs with MAOIs (a contraindicated combination), SSRIs with other antidepressants used to treat [depression](/conditions/depression).

**NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) with multiple medications**: NSAIDs interact with blood pressure medications (can reduce efficacy), warfarin (increased bleeding risk), SSRIs (increased bleeding risk), and ACE inhibitors (can affect kidney function). This is relevant because NSAIDs are available over the counter and people often do not mention them to their prescribers.

**Statins and some antibiotics/antifungals**: Clarithromycin and some azole antifungals inhibit the enzyme that metabolises many statins, causing statin blood levels to rise and increasing the risk of myopathy (muscle damage). Some statin-antibiotic combinations require temporary statin dose reduction.

**Metformin and contrast agents**: If you take metformin for [type 2 diabetes](/conditions/type-2-diabetes) and are having a scan with contrast dye (CT scan with contrast), you may need to temporarily stop metformin. The combination can affect kidney function. Your doctor or radiologist will advise you.

**Combined oral contraceptives and antibiotics/anticonvulsants**: Some medications induce liver enzymes that speed up contraceptive hormone breakdown, reducing contraceptive efficacy. Rifampicin (used for tuberculosis) is a well-established example. For most common antibiotics, the interaction is less clear-cut, but discussing with your doctor is warranted.

<Callout variant="warning">

If you are prescribed a new medication and you take warfarin, anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, or anti-epileptic medications, always ask the prescriber to check for interactions with your existing medications before starting. These drug classes have narrow therapeutic windows and significant interaction potential.

</Callout>

## Food and Medication Interactions

### Grapefruit

Grapefruit and grapefruit juice contain compounds that inhibit CYP3A4, a key enzyme involved in metabolising many medications. Inhibiting this enzyme means the drug is broken down more slowly, leading to higher blood concentrations - sometimes 2-5 times higher than intended.

Medications affected by grapefruit include:
- Some statins (simvastatin, lovastatin, atorvastatin to a lesser extent)
- Some calcium channel blockers (felodipine, nifedipine, amlodipine)
- Cyclosporin and tacrolimus (transplant medications)
- Some antiarrhythmics
- Some antiretrovirals

The amount of grapefruit that matters varies. For high-risk medications, even one glass of grapefruit juice can produce clinically significant effects. Check the consumer medicine information (CMI) for your medication - grapefruit interactions are usually listed.

Other citrus fruits (oranges, lemons, limes) do not have the same effect. This is specific to grapefruit and pummelo.

### Dairy and Tetracycline Antibiotics

Calcium in dairy products (milk, yoghurt, cheese) can bind to tetracycline-class antibiotics (doxycycline, minocycline) and some fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin), forming insoluble complexes that cannot be absorbed. The practical impact: take these antibiotics at least 2 hours before or after dairy products, calcium supplements, or antacids.

### Vitamin K and Warfarin

Warfarin works by blocking vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. Green leafy vegetables (broccoli, spinach, kale, brussel sprouts) are high in vitamin K. Eating unusually large amounts of these foods can affect warfarin efficacy. The key is consistency - sudden large changes in vitamin K intake affect INR more than a stable diet that includes vegetables.

### Alcohol

Alcohol interacts with many medications:
- CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, opioids, some antihistamines, sleep medications): additive sedation, increased risk of respiratory depression
- Metformin: rare but serious risk of lactic acidosis with heavy alcohol use
- Metronidazole and tinidazole (antibiotics): disulfiram-like reaction (flushing, nausea, rapid heart rate) - avoid alcohol entirely while taking these
- NSAIDs: increased risk of gastrointestinal bleeding
- Paracetamol: regular heavy alcohol use increases the risk of paracetamol hepatotoxicity

## Supplement and Herbal Interactions

### St John's Wort

St John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) is one of the most clinically significant herbal supplement interactions. It is a potent inducer of CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein, substantially reducing blood levels of many medications including:

- Combined oral contraceptives (reduced efficacy, potential breakthrough bleeding and pregnancy)
- SSRIs and SNRIs (serotonin syndrome risk)
- Antiretrovirals (reduced efficacy in HIV treatment)
- Warfarin (reduced anticoagulant effect, clotting risk)
- Cyclosporin (transplant rejection risk)
- Some anticonvulsants

St John's Wort is widely available over the counter and many people do not consider herbal supplements as "real medications" that need to be disclosed. This is a dangerous assumption.

### Fish Oil and Other Omega-3 Supplements

At high doses (more than 3g per day), omega-3 fatty acids can affect platelet function and bleeding time. This is clinically relevant for patients taking warfarin, antiplatelet drugs (aspirin, clopidogrel), or NSAIDs. At standard supplement doses (1g/day), the interaction is usually not clinically significant.

### Magnesium, Calcium, Iron, and Zinc

These minerals can reduce the absorption of certain medications by binding to them in the gut. Medications commonly affected include:
- Bisphosphonates (osteoporosis medications) - take on empty stomach, no minerals for 30-60 minutes after
- Levothyroxine ([thyroid issues](/conditions/thyroid-issues)) - take 4 hours away from minerals
- Quinolone and tetracycline antibiotics
- Some antifungals

### Vitamin E

High-dose vitamin E supplements can affect blood clotting and may interact with anticoagulants.

## Telling Your Healthcare Providers

The most effective prevention is complete disclosure. Every prescriber who writes you a new medication needs to know:

- All prescription medications (including those from other doctors)
- Over-the-counter medications (ibuprofen, antihistamines, antacids, laxatives)
- Vitamins and minerals (doses matter, not just the supplement)
- Herbal preparations (St John's Wort, echinacea, valerian, kava, etc.)
- Recreational substances (alcohol quantity, cannabis, others)

Bring a written list or show your prescriber the contents of your medicine cabinet on your phone. Do not assume they know what other doctors have prescribed.

For telehealth consultations, provide your complete medication list as part of your intake form. A telehealth doctor prescribing without a full medication list cannot safely check for interactions.

## Free Tools for Checking Interactions

**Your pharmacist**: The most accessible expert. When you fill any new prescription, ask: "Can you check this against my other medications?" They have access to dispensing records and interaction databases.

**NPS MedicineWise (nps.org.au)**: Free consumer tool with a Medicine Finder and information on individual drugs.

**Medsafe NZ**: Useful for quick CMI (Consumer Medicine Information) lookups.

**Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (TGA ARTG)**: For complete Australian prescribing information.

**Electronic Medicines Information (eMIMs)**: Used by clinicians but also available to consumers for detailed interaction information.

<Callout variant="info">

If you are on multiple medications and see different doctors or use both in-person and telehealth services, consider asking your regular GP for a medication review. This is a funded service under Medicare (MBS item 903 for residential aged care; similar reviews available for complex patients) where a pharmacist conducts a structured review of all your medications and identifies potential interactions and rationalisations. Ask your GP whether you qualify.

</Callout>
