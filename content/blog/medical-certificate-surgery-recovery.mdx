Surgery recovery documentation follows a clear process, but the details depend on why you had the surgery, what procedure was performed, and what your job involves. This guide covers the difference between personal sick leave and WorkCover, what initial documentation the hospital should provide, typical recovery timelines, and how to get follow-up certificates when your initial documentation expires.

## Initial Documentation: What the Hospital Provides

When you are discharged after surgery, the hospital or surgical team should provide:

- **Discharge summary:** A clinical document detailing the procedure performed, any complications, discharge medications, and post-operative instructions. Your employer may accept this as evidence of surgery.
- **Initial medical certificate:** Most surgical teams issue a [medical certificate](/medical-certificate) covering the expected initial recovery period.
- **Follow-up schedule:** Instructions on when to see your surgeon for review.
- **Activity restrictions:** Specific guidance on what you cannot do during recovery - lifting limits, driving restrictions, wound care, and return-to-normal-activity timelines.

If you were discharged without a medical certificate, contact the hospital's patient services or medical records department. They can usually issue one retrospectively based on the documented surgical episode.

## Personal Leave vs Workers' Compensation

This distinction determines which system manages your recovery and what documentation process applies.

**Personal/sick leave** applies when:

- The surgery addresses a condition unrelated to your work
- You are drawing on your National Employment Standards entitlement of 10 paid days per year
- You manage your own recovery with documentation from your treating doctors
- No WorkCover claim has been made

**Workers' compensation (WorkCover)** applies when:

- The surgery results directly from a work-related injury or illness
- A WorkCover claim has been made or should be made with your state's workers' compensation authority
- The employer's insurer is funding treatment including the surgery
- Return-to-work is managed under a formal WorkCover rehabilitation plan

Workers' compensation has its own certificate system. The specific document is called a Work Capacity Certificate, Certificate of Capacity, or similar depending on your state. If your surgery was work-related, your doctor needs to issue the appropriate WorkCover certificate - a standard sick leave certificate is not sufficient for the workers' compensation pathway.

<Callout variant="info">If you are unsure whether a surgery qualifies as work-related, speak with your treating doctor or contact your state's workers' compensation authority. Workers' compensation provides additional benefits including income replacement and rehabilitation support that personal leave does not. If you are eligible, a claim is worth making.</Callout>

## Typical Recovery Times by Surgery Type

Recovery is highly individual. The following are general guidance ranges - your surgeon's advice always takes precedence.

**Minor and day procedures:**

- Skin lesion excisions: 1-3 days for most roles
- Gastroscopy or colonoscopy: typically 1 day with escort required for sedation recovery
- Dental surgery (wisdom teeth, implants): 2-7 days depending on complexity

**Laparoscopic procedures:**

- Laparoscopic cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal): 1-2 weeks for desk work, 3-4 weeks for physical roles
- Laparoscopic appendectomy: 1-2 weeks desk work, 2-4 weeks physical roles
- Laparoscopic hernia repair: 2-3 weeks desk work, 4-6 weeks physical work
- Gynaecological laparoscopy: 1-2 weeks depending on procedure

**Abdominal and open surgery:**

- Open appendectomy: 3-6 weeks
- Open hernia repair: 4-8 weeks for physical roles
- Abdominal hysterectomy: 6-8 weeks for desk work, longer for physical roles
- Bowel surgery: 6-12 weeks depending on extent and complication

**Orthopaedic and joint surgery:**

- Knee arthroscopy (meniscus repair): 1-4 weeks desk work, 2-8 weeks physical work
- Knee replacement: 6-12 weeks before most work; longer for physically demanding roles
- Hip replacement: 6-12 weeks before most work
- Shoulder surgery: 2-16 weeks depending on procedure and job demands
- [Spinal surgery](/conditions/back-pain): weeks to months, highly variable by procedure and role

**Major or cardiac surgery:**

- Open cardiac surgery: 6-12 weeks minimum
- Major cancer surgery: varies widely based on procedure and adjuvant treatment requirements

These ranges assume uncomplicated recovery. Complications, infections, wound breakdown, or a physically demanding job requiring longer healing extend these timelines significantly.

## Getting a Follow-Up Certificate

Your initial hospital or surgeon certificate covers the expected recovery period. If recovery takes longer, you will need extension certificates.

**Your surgeon at follow-up appointments** is the most clinically appropriate source for ongoing recovery certificates. Your surgeon directly assesses your wound, recovery progress, and any complications.

**Your regular GP** takes over ongoing recovery management once your surgeon is no longer seeing you regularly. [GP reviews](/consult) are appropriate for monitoring recovery and issuing ongoing certificates.

**Telehealth consultation** is appropriate for straightforward planned surgery recovery with no complications, provided you can share your surgical documentation (discharge summary or surgeon's letter). The doctor needs to understand what procedure you had to assess appropriate recovery time.

For a telehealth follow-up certificate, have ready:

- Your discharge summary or surgeon's operative letter
- Your initial medical certificate showing the procedure and dates
- Current symptoms, wound status (photographs if there is a visible wound), and any restrictions still in place
- Your job description - specifically what physical demands your role involves

## Pre-Operative Leave

Surgery requiring general anaesthesia typically requires fasting from midnight the night before, and driving is restricted for 24 hours after sedation. Pre-operative leave is usually just the day of the procedure for day surgery. For procedures requiring hospital admission the night before, your surgeon or hospital will advise on timing.

Your surgeon can provide a pre-operative certificate covering the admission day if needed for your employer.

## Graduated Return to Work

Returning immediately to full duties after surgery is rarely appropriate or safe. A graduated return involves:

- Starting with reduced hours (half days, then building up over weeks)
- Restricting specific activities (no lifting over a set weight, no sustained standing, no driving)
- Progressively increasing workload as recovery allows
- Regular review by your treating doctor to adjust the plan

Your doctor can issue a certificate specifying your current capacity rather than simply certifying you are unfit for all work. A certificate stating "fit for sedentary duties only, no lifting over 5kg, not fit for full-time hours - review in two weeks" is far more useful than a blanket unfitness certificate. It allows your employer to plan meaningful work for you while respecting your recovery, and often enables an earlier partial return rather than a delayed full return.

## Restrictions and Modified Duties

Your certificate can and should specify restrictions rather than complete unfitness when you can perform some work. Common restrictions following surgery:

- No lifting or carrying over a specified weight
- No sustained standing or walking for extended periods
- No driving (particularly after procedures involving the leg, sedation recovery, or while taking opioid pain medication)
- No exposure to water, dust, or contaminants near a wound
- Sedentary work only
- Reduced hours with rest breaks

Ask your doctor to be specific about what you can and cannot do. Three specific restrictions are more useful than a general statement of unfitness.

## Employer Obligations Under Work Health and Safety Law

Under work health and safety legislation, employers have a duty to provide a safe workplace. During return from surgery, this includes:

- Accommodating a graduated return plan that your treating doctor has recommended
- Not requiring you to perform duties that your doctor has specifically restricted
- Considering temporary redeployment to lighter duties if your normal role cannot be modified
- Allowing reasonable time for recovery appointments related to the surgery

Employers who pressure employees to return before medical clearance, or who disregard documented medical restrictions, expose themselves to liability under WHS legislation. If you are injured because your employer did not honour medical restrictions, this may also generate a subsequent workers' compensation claim.

## When Your Certificate Expires But You Are Not Ready to Return

Do not return to work simply because your certificate has expired. Expired documentation means you need to see your doctor for an updated certificate - it does not mean you are automatically fit.

Contact your surgeon, GP, or telehealth service before your current certificate expires if you are not going to be ready to return. Proactive communication with your employer about the need for an extension is far better than going off a certificate and explaining after the fact.

If your employer is applying pressure to return before medical clearance, document the communications in writing and consider contacting the Fair Work Ombudsman if you believe your employment rights are being affected.
