Surgery - whether minor or major - requires recovery time. Your body needs time to heal, and returning to work too early can compromise your recovery or risk complications.

## Getting Clearance From Your Surgeon

Your surgeon or hospital will typically provide initial documentation covering your expected recovery period. This usually includes:

- Hospital discharge summary with expected recovery time
- Post-operative instructions and restrictions
- Initial medical certificate for your employer
- Follow-up appointment schedule

<Callout variant="info">

If you didn't receive a medical certificate at discharge, contact the hospital's medical records department. They can often provide one retrospectively.

</Callout>

## Typical Recovery Times

Recovery times vary greatly depending on the type of surgery and your overall health. Some general guidelines:

- Minor procedures (skin lesions, scope procedures): 1-3 days
- Laparoscopic surgery: 1-2 weeks
- Abdominal surgery: 2-6 weeks
- Joint replacement: 6-12 weeks
- Major surgery: 6 weeks or more

These are general estimates only. Your actual recovery depends on factors like your age, fitness, the specific procedure, and how your body heals.

## When You Need Additional Certificates

Sometimes recovery takes longer than initially anticipated. You may need additional certificates if:

- Your initial certificate is expiring but you're not ready to return
- You experience complications requiring extended recovery
- Your job has physical demands that require longer healing
- Your employer requests updated documentation

## Getting Follow-Up Certificates

For follow-up certificates after surgery, you have options:

- Your surgeon at your follow-up appointment
- Your regular GP who can assess your recovery
- Telehealth consultation if you have documentation of your surgery

<Callout variant="warning">

For telehealth certificates after surgery, you'll need to provide details of your procedure (discharge summary or surgeon's letter). The doctor needs to understand what surgery you had to assess appropriate recovery time.

</Callout>

## Returning to Work After Surgery

Before returning to work, consider:

- Have you been cleared by your surgeon or treating doctor?
- Can you safely perform your job duties?
- Do you need modified duties or a graduated return?
- Are there any ongoing restrictions (lifting, driving, standing)?

## Modified Duties and Graduated Return

Many people benefit from a graduated return to work. Your doctor can provide a certificate specifying:

- Reduced hours initially (e.g., half days)
- Restrictions on physical tasks (no lifting over X kg)
- Requirement for rest breaks
- Duration of modified duties before full return

Your employer has obligations under workplace health and safety laws to accommodate reasonable requests for modified duties during recovery.
