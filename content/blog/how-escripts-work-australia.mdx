<Callout variant="info">

An eScript is not just a text message. The message usually gives you a token that lets a pharmacy retrieve the electronic prescription through Australia's secure prescription delivery infrastructure.

</Callout>

Electronic prescriptions are now a normal part of Australian healthcare. They are useful after in-person appointments, telehealth consultations, hospital discharge, specialist review, and routine medicine management.

The important detail is that eScripts are a prescription delivery format, not a clinical shortcut. A valid prescription still depends on an appropriate prescriber, a safe prescribing decision, and a pharmacy dispensing process.

## The Two Main eScript Models

Australia uses two main ways to handle electronic prescriptions.

| Model | How it works | Best suited for |
|---|---|---|
| Token | A unique token is sent by SMS or email and shown to a pharmacy | One-off prescriptions or people who manage only a few medicines |
| Active Script List | Active prescriptions are stored in one consolidated list after registration and consent | People with multiple regular medicines or repeat prescriptions |

Both models are designed to let the pharmacy retrieve the prescription securely. The difference is whether the patient manages individual tokens or uses a consolidated list.

## The Token Pathway

The token pathway is the version most people recognise.

1. A prescriber creates an electronic prescription in conformant prescribing software.
2. A unique token is generated.
3. The token is sent to the patient by SMS or email.
4. The patient shows or shares the token with a pharmacy.
5. The pharmacy scans or enters the token.
6. The pharmacy retrieves the prescription through the prescription delivery system.
7. The pharmacist completes normal dispensing, counselling, identity, PBS, and safety checks.

The token may appear as a link, barcode, QR-style code, or token ID depending on the system. The important point is that the token is the access mechanism, not a plain-text copy of the whole prescription.

## The Active Script List Pathway

An Active Script List, or ASL, is a token management solution. Instead of keeping track of separate SMS or email tokens, the patient can register for a consolidated list of active prescriptions.

The Australian Digital Health Agency says patients register through an enabled pharmacy or prescriber. The process includes consent and an activation step.

ASL can help when:

- You take several regular medicines.
- You lose SMS tokens often.
- You have repeats across different prescribers.
- You prefer the pharmacy to retrieve active prescriptions from one list.
- You use a compatible app or a regular pharmacy workflow.

ASL is not the same as giving every pharmacy automatic access forever. Registration, identity, consent, and enabled systems matter.

## What Happens At The Pharmacy

The pharmacy still has a professional role. eScripts do not remove the pharmacist's checks.

The pharmacist may check:

- Your identity
- Medicine name, strength, form, and quantity
- Directions and number of repeats
- PBS or private prescription status
- Brand substitution options
- Allergies or interaction concerns if known
- Whether the prescription is still valid
- Whether the medicine has additional legal or clinical requirements
- Whether counselling is needed before supply

If something is unclear, the pharmacist may need to contact the prescriber or ask for more information. A digital token does not force the pharmacy to dispense if there is a safety or legal problem.

## Repeats And Expiry

Electronic repeats are linked to the prescription record rather than a loose piece of paper.

After a repeat is dispensed:

- The dispensing event is recorded.
- Remaining repeats are updated.
- The next supply can be managed electronically.
- The prescription still expires according to relevant medicine and jurisdiction rules.

Many prescriptions are valid for up to 12 months, but some medicines and circumstances have shorter validity or extra rules. Schedule 8 medicines, state and territory requirements, PBS authority conditions, hospital discharge prescriptions, staged supply, and certain controlled medicines can change what happens.

Do not assume every eScript token has the same expiry just because it is digital.

## What If You Lose The Token?

Losing the SMS does not necessarily mean the prescription is gone.

Options may include:

- Ask the prescriber to resend the token.
- Check whether your pharmacy can access it through an Active Script List if you are registered.
- Check whether your prescription app stored the token.
- Search SMS, email, deleted messages, or archived conversations.
- Ask the pharmacy or prescriber what recovery options are available for that specific prescription.

Do not screenshot and share tokens casually. A token can let another person request dispensing if the pharmacy accepts it and identity checks are satisfied.

## Privacy And Security

eScripts involve health information. The privacy question is not just "is SMS secure?" It is how the prescription, token, identity, consent, and pharmacy access are handled across the system.

Useful privacy boundaries:

- The token should not expose unnecessary clinical information in plain text.
- Share a token only with a pharmacy or trusted person collecting medicine for you.
- Be careful with shared phones, shared email accounts, workplace devices, and screenshots.
- Ask how ASL consent works before registering.
- Use pharmacy apps only if you understand their privacy settings.
- Keep phone numbers and email addresses current with your prescriber.

The Department of Health and the Australian Digital Health Agency publish privacy frameworks and guidance for electronic prescribing and Active Script List use.

## Common Problems And Fixes

| Problem | What to check |
|---|---|
| Token did not arrive | Confirm mobile/email details, spam folder, reception, and whether the prescriber sent token or paper |
| Pharmacy cannot scan token | Ask whether they can manually enter the token ID or whether the token is expired or already dispensed |
| Repeat not visible | Check whether the original supply was dispensed, whether the repeat token was issued, and whether ASL applies |
| Medicine unavailable | Ask pharmacy about stock, brand substitution, or contacting prescriber if a change is needed |
| Wrong details | Do not use it; contact prescriber for correction |
| Another person collecting | Check pharmacy identity and consent requirements before sending the token |

## eScripts And Telehealth

Electronic prescriptions fit naturally with telehealth because there is no paper handover. But the prescribing standard does not change just because the prescription is digital.

The prescriber still needs to decide whether:

- The medicine is clinically appropriate.
- The assessment is adequate.
- A real-time or in-person review is required.
- Monitoring is up to date.
- The medicine has dependence, misuse, or safety concerns.
- The prescription complies with relevant federal, state, and territory rules.

eScripts make delivery easier. They do not make unsafe prescribing safe.

## Paper Prescriptions Still Exist

Patients may still receive paper prescriptions. Some may be traditional paper scripts; some may be barcoded paper prescriptions.

The Australian Digital Health Agency notes that if a prescriber provides a barcoded paper prescription rather than an electronic prescription, the patient still needs to bring the paper copy to the pharmacy to be legally dispensed.

Paper may still be relevant when:

- The patient cannot reliably receive SMS or email.
- The prescriber or pharmacy system is not enabled.
- A medicine or local rule requires a particular process.
- The patient prefers paper and the prescriber can issue it.

## The Bottom Line

eScripts are a secure way to move prescriptions from prescriber to pharmacy without relying on paper. The token model works well for simple use; an Active Script List can reduce token management for people with multiple active medicines.

The prescription is still a clinical and legal document. The digital format helps delivery, but prescribers and pharmacists still need to apply normal safety, identity, privacy, PBS, and medicine-specific checks.
