Telehealth and in-person GP visits are not simply interchangeable options where you pick whichever is more convenient. Each is genuinely better suited to different situations. Using the wrong mode does not just affect convenience - it can affect the quality of care you receive. Here is a practical comparison of when each approach works best.

## The Core Difference

In-person care gives your doctor physical examination tools: stethoscope, otoscope, palpation, percussion, vital signs. These matter for certain diagnoses. Telehealth removes those tools but gains in access, speed, and convenience.

The question for any specific consultation is not "is telehealth as good as in-person?" in the abstract - it is "does this particular clinical situation require the physical examination tools that only in-person care provides?"

For many common presentations, the answer is no. A GP diagnosing a urinary tract infection in a healthy adult does not need to physically examine the patient - the history and symptoms are sufficient. A GP assessing whether a skin rash is cellulitis or contact dermatitis benefits from actually seeing it clearly, which can work via photos in telehealth. A GP assessing a possible ruptured appendix needs hands on the abdomen.

## Where Telehealth Is Better

### Speed and Accessibility

Telehealth wins decisively on access. Getting a same-day telehealth consultation typically takes less time than booking and attending a GP appointment. For acute conditions that need attention today - a UTI, a skin infection, a certificate needed this morning - this matters.

For people in rural and remote areas, telehealth is not a convenience but an access requirement. Many Australians live hours from the nearest GP clinic. Telehealth represents genuine healthcare access, not just convenience.

For people with mobility limitations, chronic illness, or caring responsibilities that make leaving home difficult, telehealth removes significant barriers to care.

### After-Hours Availability

Most GP clinics operate 9am-5pm weekdays. Illness does not follow business hours. Telehealth services available on evenings and weekends cover a gap that in-person clinics leave open.

### Mental Health Consultations

Telehealth has proven particularly effective for mental health. Many people find it easier to discuss mental health concerns remotely - they are in their own environment, there is a degree of distance that reduces the vulnerability of the conversation, and they do not have to manage the social aspects of a waiting room.

Evidence consistently shows that mental health outcomes for telehealth are comparable to in-person care for many presentations. For counselling, CBT, and initial assessments, the mode matters less than the quality of the clinician.

### Repeat Prescriptions and Ongoing Conditions

For stable chronic conditions - blood pressure, diabetes, cholesterol, thyroid disease, contraception - where the clinical situation is known and management is unchanged, telehealth removes the need for an unnecessary trip to a clinic. The relevant clinical information (medication history, most recent blood results, any new symptoms) can be communicated effectively in writing.

### Medical Certificates

There is no clinical reason a medical certificate requires an in-person consultation. The doctor needs to assess whether you are unfit for work, which they can do from your reported symptoms and history. Telehealth is the most practical way to get a certificate on the day you are sick - when leaving home is precisely what you cannot do.

## Where In-Person Care Is Better

### Physical Examination Requirements

Conditions where a hands-on examination changes the clinical picture:

- Abdominal pain requiring palpation (appendicitis, hernia, organomegaly)
- Chest symptoms requiring auscultation (pneumonia, cardiac murmur, pleural effusion)
- Ear infections in young children (requires otoscope)
- Musculoskeletal injuries requiring range-of-motion assessment (suspected fracture, joint instability, ligament tears)
- Skin lesions requiring dermoscopy (mole assessment, suspicious pigmented lesions)
- Testicular or groin pain (hernia, testicular torsion risk)
- Neurological symptoms requiring reflex and coordination examination

These are not conditions where telehealth provides inferior care - they are conditions where the key diagnostic information simply cannot be obtained without physical examination.

### Investigations and Procedures

Some care requires tools or procedures that cannot be done remotely:

- Blood tests and urinalysis (though telehealth can order these for collection elsewhere)
- ECG
- Imaging (X-ray, ultrasound, CT)
- Wound care, sutures, dressings
- Injections (vaccines, corticosteroid injections, contraceptive implants)
- Cervical screening (Pap smear)
- Ear syringing

### Complex New Presentations

First consultations for complex or unclear conditions benefit from in-person assessment. If you have never discussed a symptom with a doctor before, and you are unsure whether it is serious, a face-to-face consultation where the doctor can examine you is safer.

Telehealth works best for known conditions with a defined management plan. For genuinely new, unclear, or potentially serious presentations, in-person evaluation provides more clinical information.

### Paediatric Assessment (Young Children)

Young children present differently from adults, can deteriorate quickly, and often cannot communicate their symptoms clearly. Physical examination - observing breathing pattern, checking for ear infection, feeling an abdomen - provides information that parental description cannot fully replicate.

For infants under 6 months, any illness should generally be assessed in person. For older children with mild presentations (a rash you can photograph, a mild cold), telehealth is reasonable.

### Long-Term Relationship Care

Your regular GP knows your history, your family, your risk factors, and the context behind your health concerns. This longitudinal relationship has real clinical value - it means patterns get noticed, mental health changes get picked up over time, and complex interactions between your various health issues are tracked.

Telehealth is not designed to replace this. It fills gaps: when your GP is booked out, when it is after hours, when you have a straightforward issue that does not warrant taking a spot on your GP's schedule.

## Cost Comparison

| Consultation type | Typical cost |
|---|---|
| Bulk-billed GP visit | Free (with Medicare) |
| Private GP visit (out of pocket) | $80-120 |
| Private telehealth service | $20-50 |
| Medicare-rebated telehealth | Varies (partial rebate applies) |

Bulk billing rates at GP clinics have declined substantially. In many urban areas, getting a bulk-billed GP appointment requires waiting days or weeks. In reality, many Australians face the choice between a costly private GP visit and a reasonably priced telehealth consultation for routine presentations.

For straightforward requests - certificate, repeat prescription, common infection - the clinical quality difference between a telehealth consultation and a private GP visit is small to zero. The cost difference is substantial.

## The Practical Decision Framework

Ask yourself:

1. **Does this need a physical examination?** If yes, in-person. If no, telehealth is viable.
2. **Is this a new, complex, or potentially serious presentation?** If yes, in-person for the initial assessment.
3. **Is this a known, stable condition with established management?** Telehealth is appropriate.
4. **Is speed important?** Telehealth is usually faster.
5. **Is it after hours or a weekend?** Telehealth may be the only practical option.
6. **Am I too sick to leave home?** That is the clearest case for telehealth.

<Callout variant="info">

Neither mode is inherently superior. Telehealth is not "second-rate" medicine and in-person is not automatically more thorough. The right choice is the one that matches the clinical need. A telehealth doctor who cannot examine your abdomen but appropriately refers you to ED has provided excellent care.

</Callout>

## Integrating Both

The best approach for most people is not choosing between telehealth and an in-person GP - it is using both strategically.

Your regular GP manages your ongoing health, complex issues, and anything that benefits from the longitudinal relationship and physical examination. Telehealth handles the routine, the time-sensitive, and the after-hours - certificates, common infections, repeat prescriptions, mental health check-ins.

This is how most Australians are increasingly using both modes: not as competitors but as complementary components of a health system that is more accessible and more responsive than it was five years ago.
