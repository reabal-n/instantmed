Severe period pain - medically called dysmenorrhea - is a recognised medical condition that can prevent women from working safely and effectively. If you are experiencing debilitating cramping, nausea, or pain that makes concentration impossible, you are unfit for work in exactly the same way as someone with gastroenteritis or a migraine. Australian law recognises this. Here is what your entitlements are, what the certificate will actually say, and how to get documentation without leaving home on a day when that feels impossible.

## Period Pain Is a Medical Condition

Dysmenorrhea is not "just cramps." For the substantial proportion of women who experience moderate to severe period pain, symptoms include:

- Cramping pain in the lower abdomen, sometimes radiating to the back and thighs
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhoea
- Headache and dizziness
- Fatigue that makes concentration and physical activity difficult
- Fainting in severe cases

These are not trivial symptoms. Dysmenorrhea is classified in the International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11) and is the subject of international research into its underdiagnosis and undertreatment. The World Health Organization has called for improved recognition of menstrual health conditions globally.

## Primary vs Secondary Dysmenorrhea

Understanding this distinction matters for your long-term health, even if it does not change your immediate sick leave entitlement.

**Primary dysmenorrhea** is period pain without an underlying cause. It results from prostaglandins that trigger uterine contractions during menstruation. It typically starts within a year or two of the first period and tends to improve with age and after pregnancy.

**Secondary dysmenorrhea** is period pain caused by an underlying condition. It tends to start later in life, worsen over time, and often lasts longer than primary dysmenorrhea. Common causes include:

- Endometriosis: uterine-like tissue growing outside the uterus. It affects an estimated 1 in 9 Australian women and is frequently underdiagnosed, with an average delay of 7-8 years from symptom onset to diagnosis.
- Adenomyosis: uterine lining growing into the muscular wall of the uterus, causing heavy, painful periods.
- Uterine fibroids: non-cancerous growths in or around the uterus.
- Pelvic inflammatory disease: infection of the reproductive organs, often from an untreated STI.
- Copper IUD: can increase cramping, particularly in the first few months after insertion.

If your period pain is worsening over time, lasting longer than 2-3 days, or affecting you significantly beyond the first day or two, the underlying cause warrants investigation with your GP or gynaecologist.

## Your Fair Work Act Entitlements

Under the National Employment Standards (NES), full-time and part-time employees accrue 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year. The Fair Work Act 2009 covers illness or injury that prevents an employee from performing their role. Period pain that meets this threshold qualifies in exactly the same way as any other acute illness.

Your employer can request evidence when:

- Your absence is two or more consecutive days
- Your award, enterprise agreement, or workplace policy requires evidence for single-day absences
- You have a pattern of single-day absences that triggers a documentation requirement

For most workplaces, a single day off for period pain will not require documentation. For absences of two or more days, or if your workplace has stricter policies, a medical certificate provides the required evidence.

<Callout variant="info">Casual employees do not accrue paid sick leave but cannot be penalised for genuine illness. Casual employees have access to unpaid personal leave under the NES for periods of illness.</Callout>

## What Your Employer Can and Cannot Ask

### Employers can legitimately:

- Request a medical certificate for absences that meet their evidence threshold
- Ask for the expected duration of absence
- Ask when you expect to return to work

### Employers cannot:

- Demand to know your specific diagnosis - "medical condition" is sufficient
- Ask whether the absence is menstrual-related or related to your reproductive health
- Require more information than the certificate provides
- Take adverse action against you for taking valid personal leave

Your diagnosis is protected health information under the Privacy Act 1988. Your employer has no legal right to know whether your absence is due to period pain, endometriosis, or any other condition. The certificate provides what the law requires: confirmation from a registered practitioner that you were unfit for work.

## Getting a Medical Certificate via Telehealth

Period pain is particularly well-suited to telehealth assessment. Unlike conditions that require a physical examination, dysmenorrhea is assessed primarily through your symptom history: when symptoms started, their severity, how they are affecting your ability to function, and any relevant medical history.

The process:

- Complete the online consultation form. Include severity (what you cannot do, not just a number), duration, and how this compares to your usual experience.
- Note any relevant history: endometriosis diagnosis, fibroids, previous treatment for severe dysmenorrhea.
- An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case.
- If your symptoms are consistent with period pain preventing you from working, a certificate covering the appropriate period is issued.
- The certificate is emailed as a PDF, typically within an hour.

The benefit of telehealth on a day when severe cramping makes it difficult to get out of bed is real. You do not need to travel to a clinic, sit in a waiting room, or interact with people when you are in significant pain.

## What the Certificate Will Say

Your medical certificate will state:

- Your full legal name
- The consultation date
- That you were assessed and found unfit for work due to a medical condition
- The period covered (start date to end date)
- The doctor's name, AHPRA registration number, and contact details

It will not specify period pain, dysmenorrhea, or any menstrual-related diagnosis unless you explicitly request this. Most women prefer the standard "medical condition" phrasing, which is legally sufficient and discloses nothing about reproductive health.

If you have a formal endometriosis or adenomyosis diagnosis and you want the certificate to reflect this - for example, for an extended absence discussion with your employer - you can request this during your consultation.

## Symptoms That Warrant Investigation

A medical certificate deals with your immediate absence. But if any of the following apply, the underlying cause warrants investigation with your GP:

- Your pain is worsening with each cycle
- Pain lasts more than 2-3 days, or continues outside your period
- You have pain during sex (dyspareunia)
- You experience heavy or irregular bleeding
- Standard pain relief such as ibuprofen or naproxen no longer helps
- You are regularly missing work, social activities, or study due to pain
- You have been unable to fall pregnant despite trying

Endometriosis in particular is significantly underdiagnosed in Australia. If your period pain matches the pattern above, a GP referral to a gynaecologist is appropriate.

<Callout variant="tip">If you consult a doctor for a medical certificate and mention worsening pain or the symptoms above, ask whether a referral for further investigation is appropriate. Addressing an underlying cause like endometriosis is far more effective long-term than managing pain episode by episode.</Callout>

## Treatment Options for Severe Period Pain

For immediate relief:

- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen or naproxen are first-line treatment. Take them at the first sign of cramping, before pain becomes severe - they work best when started proactively.
- Heat packs on the lower abdomen provide genuine pain relief and are safe alongside medication.
- For severe pain unresponsive to OTC NSAIDs, prescription-strength options are available through your GP.

For longer-term management:

- Combined oral contraceptive pill: reduces prostaglandin production and often significantly reduces dysmenorrhea.
- Hormonal IUD (Mirena): many women experience significantly lighter periods and less pain.
- For confirmed endometriosis: specialist treatment including hormonal therapy or laparoscopic surgery.

## Menstrual Leave in Australian Workplaces

Australia does not currently have mandatory menstrual or reproductive health leave. Unlike some countries that have introduced specific provisions, Australian employment law does not separately categorise period-related absence.

Your existing 10-day personal leave entitlement covers period pain in the same way as any other illness. Some Australian employers have begun voluntarily introducing menstrual leave policies or additional personal leave as part of their wellbeing offerings. Check your enterprise agreement, workplace policy, or staff handbook to see if your employer offers this.

The absence of a specific menstrual leave category does not disadvantage you in practice: your personal leave entitlement applies fully, and the medical certificate process is identical to any other illness.
