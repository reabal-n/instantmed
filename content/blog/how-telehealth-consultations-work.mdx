Telehealth is now a permanent part of Australian healthcare delivery. Since the expansion of Medicare-funded telehealth following the COVID-19 pandemic, online consultations have become an expected option alongside in-person GP visits. According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), telehealth services accounted for approximately 37% of all GP Medicare-subsidised consultations in 2021-22.

Despite this, many Australians are uncertain about how telehealth actually works - particularly the newer asynchronous (form-based) model that does not involve a real-time call. This guide explains both models, what happens on the clinical side, and what you can expect as an outcome.

## The Two Models of Australian Telehealth

### Model 1: Asynchronous (Form-Based) Telehealth

You complete a structured online intake form at a time that suits you. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your submission later - typically within 1-4 hours during operating hours - and responds with an assessment. There is no real-time interaction.

**Process flow:**
1. You create an account on the telehealth platform
2. You complete the intake form with your symptoms, medical history, and what you are requesting
3. Your submission enters the doctor's review queue
4. The doctor reviews your case, possibly sends follow-up questions
5. The doctor makes a clinical decision: approve, decline, or request in-person care
6. If approved: the outcome is delivered digitally (eScript by SMS, certificate by email)

**Best suited for:** [Medical certificates](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate") for sick leave, [repeat prescriptions](/prescriptions "Request a repeat prescription") for established medications, specific conditions with well-defined remote assessment criteria (UTIs, some skin conditions), and any request where a written outcome is appropriate.

**Advantages:** Available 24/7 for submissions, no need to book an appointment, no scheduling coordination, can submit when too unwell to have a conversation, no video or reliable internet required.

### Model 2: Synchronous (Video or Phone) Telehealth

You book a time slot and speak with a doctor directly in real time. The interaction mirrors a standard GP consultation except that the doctor cannot physically examine you.

**Process flow:**
1. You book an appointment through the service's scheduling system
2. At the appointment time, you join via the video platform link or receive a phone call
3. The consultation proceeds as a dialogue - the doctor takes a history, you ask questions, the doctor provides advice
4. Outcomes are delivered during or immediately after the consultation

**Best suited for:** Complex presentations requiring dialogue, [mental health](/conditions/depression "Depression and mental health support") consultations, detailed discussions about medication changes, follow-up consultations, and any situation where back-and-forth communication is valuable.

**Advantages:** Real-time interaction, ability to show the doctor something visually on video, more natural clinical dialogue, suitable for complex or nuanced presentations.

## What the Doctor Does During Your Consultation

This is the core of what differentiates a legitimate telehealth service from an automated certificate or script generator. For every submission, a real doctor is doing real clinical work.

**For asynchronous consultations:**

The doctor reads your intake carefully, not just scanning for keywords. They are applying clinical reasoning:

- Is the described presentation internally consistent?
- Do the symptoms match what would be expected for the condition described?
- Is the requested outcome (certificate duration, medication) proportionate to the presentation?
- Are there any red flags suggesting more serious pathology that needs different management?
- Are there contraindications or interaction risks in the prescription request?
- Is there any information missing that would affect the decision?

If information is missing or unclear, the doctor sends a follow-up question. If the presentation is clinically consistent and the request is appropriate, the doctor approves. If there are concerns, the doctor may decline and recommend in-person care, or may ask for clarification before deciding.

This process is governed by the Medical Board of Australia's "Good Medical Practice: A Code of Conduct for Doctors in Australia." The standards applied are identical to those expected in in-person consultations. A telehealth doctor who approves a request that is not clinically appropriate - issuing certificates for conditions that do not warrant them, or prescribing medications that are contraindicated - faces the same professional consequences as an in-person GP making the same error.

**For synchronous consultations:**

The doctor takes a verbal history, just as in an in-person consultation. They ask structured questions about symptoms, timing, severity, associated features, and relevant history. They may ask you to show them something on video (a visible [rash](/conditions/skin-rash "Skin rash treatment"), a swollen area, a document). They apply the same clinical reasoning as for asynchronous review.

## What Outcomes Are Possible

A telehealth consultation can produce several different outcomes:

**Medical certificate:** A PDF document certifying you were unfit for work for a specified period, issued to your email. Includes the doctor's name and AHPRA registration number. Legally valid under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) when issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor.

**eScript (electronic prescription):** A QR code token sent by SMS to your phone, linked to the Prescription Exchange Service (PES) national infrastructure. Presentable at any pharmacy in Australia for dispensing. PBS pricing applies with a Medicare card.

**Pathology or imaging request:** A digital request sent to you or a specified collection service. You attend the collection centre or imaging facility in person. Results come back to the telehealth service and you are notified.

**Specialist referral:** A referral letter sent digitally, which you can present to a specialist. Equivalent to a referral from an in-person GP.

**Written clinical advice:** An assessment and management recommendations, such as advice on self-care, when to seek further care, or what over-the-counter treatment is appropriate.

**Decline with recommendation:** A clinical decision that the request is not appropriate for remote management, with a recommendation for in-person care. This is an appropriate outcome - not a service failure.

## The Role of Follow-Up Questions

In asynchronous telehealth, follow-up questions bridge the gap between the intake form and the clinical information needed for a confident decision.

When a doctor sends a follow-up question, it means:
- The initial intake was incomplete for the specific decision required
- An additional piece of information would change or confirm the clinical assessment
- The doctor wants to confirm something before proceeding

Common follow-up topics:
- "When did you last have blood tests for this medication, and what were the results?"
- "Can you describe the rash more specifically - is it blistered, weeping, or dry?"
- "How long have you been taking this medication, and was it originally prescribed for the same condition?"

Responding promptly and thoroughly to follow-up questions is the patient's part of the asynchronous consultation. Delayed responses extend the time to a decision.

## Costs and Medicare

**Asynchronous telehealth:** Generally private fees, not Medicare-rebatable. These services do not generate a real-time telehealth "attendance" that attracts an MBS item number. Flat fees typically range from $19.95 (medical certificate) to $79 (general consultation).

**Synchronous telehealth with Medicare-registered providers:** Real-time video and phone consultations with registered practitioners can attract Medicare rebates under specific MBS telehealth item numbers. The rebate partially offsets the consultation fee. Some services bulk bill (no gap); most charge a private fee with a partial rebate.

**PBS prescriptions:** The pharmacy co-payment for PBS medications is the same regardless of how the prescription was obtained. Services Australia sets the 2025-26 PBS co-payments at $31.60 for general patients and $7.70 for concession card holders.

## Integration with Your Regular GP

Telehealth works best as a complement to an ongoing GP relationship, not a replacement. The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care identifies continuity of care as a core safety principle in healthcare delivery.

Best practice after any telehealth consultation:
- Inform your regular GP of any prescriptions obtained via telehealth at your next appointment
- Share the consultation summary (available from the telehealth service) with your GP
- Continue attending regular GP check-ups for chronic conditions, annual health assessments, and any clinical situation requiring longitudinal management

Telehealth fills specific gaps: the same-day certificate when your GP is booked, the prescription running out over a long weekend, the 9pm question that does not warrant emergency care. Your GP provides the relational, longitudinal care that telehealth cannot replicate.
