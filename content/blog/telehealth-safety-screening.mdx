Telehealth safety screening is the step that decides whether remote care is suitable before an online outcome is given. It is not admin. It is part of the clinical assessment.

The screening process checks the problem, the person, the risk, the medicine context, and the limits of remote care. A safe result can be online advice, more questions, a prescription or document where clinically appropriate, a decline, local review, or urgent care.

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- Safety screening is how a doctor decides whether telehealth is suitable for a specific patient and problem.
- Detailed questions are often clinical history, not paperwork.
- Red flags, missing history, medicine risks, pregnancy or breastfeeding, immune risk, young age, severe symptoms, or examination needs can change the outcome.
- Referral or decline can be the safest answer when remote assessment is not enough.
- Honest answers make the review faster and safer than trying to steer the form toward a preferred result.
</KeyTakeaway>

## What safety screening means

Telehealth changes the way information reaches the doctor. In a clinic, a doctor may observe you, examine you, take vital signs, or notice cues during the consultation. Online, some of that signal has to come from structured questions, uploaded information, conversation, secure records, or follow-up messages.

The Medical Board of Australia supports responsible telehealth, but says it is not suitable for every consultation and should not be treated as a routine substitute for in-person care. The same guidance says telehealth care must be safe and, as far as possible, meet the same standard as in-person care.

That is the point of screening. It helps decide whether the doctor has enough reliable information to continue remotely.

Good screening asks:

- what the patient is asking for
- what symptoms are present and how they are changing
- whether any emergency or high-risk features are present
- whether a physical examination, vital signs, tests, imaging, or procedures are needed
- whether medicines, allergies, pregnancy, breastfeeding, or medical history change the risk
- whether the patient can be identified and contacted
- whether the patient understands the process, privacy limits, cost, and next steps

<EvidenceNote title="Current regulator signal" source="Ahpra">
Ahpra's October 2025 telehealth guidance update emphasises patient safety, practitioner accountability, necessary medical history, informed consent, confirming identity, and telling patients when telehealth may not meet their care needs.
</EvidenceNote>

## What gets checked before a doctor decides

Screening is not one question. It is a set of signals that either support remote review or point away from it.

| Screening signal | What the patient provides | What the doctor is checking | Possible outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identity and contact details | Legal name, date of birth, location, contact details | The record and outcome belong to the right person | Continue, clarify, or stop until identity is clear |
| Main problem | Symptoms, request type, timing, severity, photos where relevant | Whether the issue fits remote assessment | Continue, ask more, or redirect |
| Red flags | Severe, unusual, worsening, or dangerous symptoms | Whether urgent or in-person care is safer | Same-day local care or call 000 |
| Medical history | Chronic illness, immune risk, pregnancy or breastfeeding, recent care | Whether usual advice or medicine choices need to change | Continue, modify plan, or refer |
| Medicines and allergies | Current medicines, recent medicines, reactions | Interactions, contraindications, duplicate treatment, allergy severity | Continue, choose safer option, or decline |
| Examination need | What cannot be checked remotely | Whether vital signs, palpation, auscultation, tests, imaging, or procedures are needed | In-person review |
| Privacy and consent | Whether the patient understands and can use the channel privately | Whether the consultation can proceed safely and confidentially | Continue, change channel, or reschedule |
| Follow-up pathway | How the patient can receive advice, results, documents, or escalation | Whether the plan is clear if symptoms change | Online outcome, follow-up, or handover |

The riskier the request, the more important these signals become. A low-risk administrative document and a new medicine request should not be screened in the same way.

## Why detailed forms can be safer

A long form can feel frustrating when the patient already knows what they want. But in telehealth, the form may be the first clinical history.

Useful answers are specific:

- "fever for two days, highest 38.6, improving today"
- "rash started after the second dose, no breathing symptoms"
- "taking warfarin and recently started antibiotics"
- "possible pregnancy, not yet tested"
- "chest tightness with shortness of breath"

Those details change the decision. They can separate a low-risk online request from a problem that needs a different pathway.

Poor answers create risk:

- vague timing
- omitted medicines
- incomplete allergy details
- minimised symptoms
- uploaded photos without context
- pressure for a specific medicine or certificate outcome
- inconsistent answers across the form

A safe service should slow down when answers are incomplete or inconsistent. Faster is not better if the doctor does not have enough signal.

## The core screening pathway

Most telehealth screening leads to three broad routes, with clarification often sitting inside the online-care pathway before a final decision.

<DecisionBox title="What screening can decide">
### May fit telehealth
- The issue is low risk, the history is clear, red flags are absent, and the doctor has enough information to make a safe remote decision.
- The doctor may first ask for clarification, a better photo, missing medicine details, allergy details, pregnancy or breastfeeding status, or a clearer symptom timeline.
### Needs in-person care
- The problem may need examination, vital signs, pathology, imaging, a procedure, continuity from a usual GP, or same-day care.
### Urgent care
- Emergency symptoms should bypass routine telehealth and go through 000, emergency department, urgent care, or local emergency pathways.
</DecisionBox>

The important point is that these are all legitimate outcomes. A service that always approves the requested outcome is not safer than one that redirects when the risk profile changes.

## Red flags that should bypass routine telehealth

Some symptoms are not a good fit for routine online review. They may need emergency assessment, hands-on examination, monitoring, or immediate treatment.

Urgent red flags include:

- chest pain, pressure, or severe shortness of breath
- stroke signs such as face drooping, arm weakness, speech trouble, or sudden confusion
- collapse, fainting with ongoing symptoms, or severe weakness
- severe allergic reaction, swelling of the face or throat, or trouble breathing
- severe bleeding, head injury with concerning symptoms, or serious injury
- severe abdominal pain, stiff neck, or rapidly worsening pain
- fever with confusion, severe dehydration, or a very unwell appearance
- pregnancy symptoms that could indicate urgent risk
- symptoms in a baby, young child, frail older person, or high-risk patient where deterioration could be rapid

<CareBoundary title="Urgent symptoms need urgent care">
Routine telehealth should not be used to test whether an emergency is real. If symptoms sound life-threatening or rapidly worsening, call 000 or use local urgent care first.
</CareBoundary>

## Prescription and certificate checks need extra care

Prescriptions and medical certificates can look simple from the patient's side. Clinically, they still need screening.

For prescriptions, screening may check:

- whether the medicine matches the problem
- whether the dose and medicine class are appropriate for the patient
- allergies and previous reactions
- interactions with current medicines
- pregnancy or breastfeeding where relevant
- kidney, liver, heart, immune, or other relevant medical history
- recent use of the same or similar medicine
- monitoring needs, including blood tests or blood pressure checks
- whether telehealth is appropriate for the medicine and request

For certificates, screening may check:

- the symptom timeline
- the requested dates and purpose
- whether the request is routine or high stakes
- whether the history supports the claimed incapacity
- whether backdating, workplace dispute, insurance, court, or safety-critical work changes the required pathway

<PolicyNote title="Questionnaires are not the decision-maker" source="Medical Board of Australia">
The Medical Board says prescribing or providing healthcare without a real-time direct consultation is not good practice when it relies on asynchronous text, email, live chat, or online questionnaires and the practitioner has never spoken with the patient. A questionnaire can support the review, but the doctor remains responsible for whether the care is appropriate.
</PolicyNote>

## What happens when information is missing

Missing information should not be treated as a minor form problem. It can change safety.

A doctor may ask for:

- a clearer symptom timeline
- the name and dose of current medicines
- the exact allergy reaction
- whether the patient could be pregnant or breastfeeding
- a better photo with scale and lighting
- details from a recent GP, hospital, pathology, or pharmacy record
- confirmation of identity, location, or contact details
- whether symptoms are worsening

If the answer still does not give enough clinical confidence, the safer result may be referral or decline.

This matters because telehealth is not a vending process. It is a clinical review with limits. The doctor has to decide what can be done safely with the information available.

## How to prepare for safety screening

You can make the process smoother without trying to over-control it.

Before starting a telehealth request:

1. Write down when symptoms started and whether they are improving, stable, or worsening.
2. List current prescription medicines, non-prescription medicines, supplements, and recent medicines.
3. Note allergies and the reaction, not just the medicine name.
4. Include relevant medical history such as pregnancy, breastfeeding, immune suppression, kidney disease, heart disease, diabetes, or recent surgery.
5. Use clear photos only when they add useful context and the service asks for them.
6. Say if you have already seen another clinician or received treatment for the same issue.
7. Mention barriers to care, such as being remote, unable to travel, or needing communication support.
8. Use a private space where possible, and say if someone else can hear or is helping you.

Do not omit a detail because it might make approval less likely. The detail that changes the outcome may be the detail that protects you.

## When screening leads to referral or decline

Referral or decline can feel disappointing, especially when the patient expected a fast online outcome. But clinically, it can be the correct result.

Common reasons include:

- emergency symptoms
- a problem needing examination
- vital signs, pathology, imaging, or procedures needed
- new or unexplained severe symptoms
- repeated treatment failure
- high-risk medicine request
- pregnancy, young age, frailty, immune risk, or complex medical history
- inconsistent answers or insufficient information
- a document request that needs a different evidence standard

The best telehealth services make this clear before payment or before a patient invests too much time. They should also explain the next step in plain language.

## How to assess a telehealth service's screening quality

Before entering sensitive information, look for signs that the service treats screening as healthcare.

Safer signals include:

- clear service ownership, ABN, privacy policy, terms, and complaints contact
- clear practitioner accountability
- identity checks
- symptom and red-flag questions
- medicine, allergy, pregnancy, breastfeeding, and medical-history questions where relevant
- a way for the doctor to ask follow-up questions
- plain explanations for decline, referral, refund, or next steps
- secure handling of health information
- no pressure to pick a medicine or outcome before assessment

Warning signs include:

- approval promises before review
- no meaningful clinical questions
- no clear privacy or complaints pathway
- no ability to redirect to in-person or urgent care
- pressure tactics around payment
- unclear practitioner responsibility
- treating certificates, prescriptions, or referrals as products rather than clinical outcomes
