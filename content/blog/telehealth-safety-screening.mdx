Good telehealth is not "medicine without assessment." It is medicine conducted differently - using structured questionnaires, clinical history, and doctor judgment in place of physical examination tools. Safety screening is what makes this work. Here is what actually gets checked at each stage of a telehealth consultation, and why.

## Why Screening Matters More in Telehealth

In an in-person consultation, a GP can observe your appearance, take vital signs, perform a physical examination, and pick up nonverbal cues. None of that is available in a remote assessment. To compensate, responsible telehealth services use structured intake questionnaires designed to capture the clinical information a doctor would otherwise gather through examination.

This is not a workaround or a compromise. For conditions appropriate to telehealth, structured history-taking is often as diagnostically useful as a physical examination. The screening questions are not bureaucratic - they are the clinical assessment.

## What Gets Screened at Each Stage

### Stage 1: Is Telehealth Appropriate at All?

Before any clinical assessment begins, a responsible service screens for whether the request is suitable for remote management:

- Is this a condition that requires physical examination for diagnosis?
- Does the patient's description suggest a potentially serious or acute condition?
- Are there features suggesting an emergency (chest pain, breathing difficulty, severe pain, neurological symptoms)?
- Has the patient already sought in-person care, and if so, what was found?

For some presentations, the answer to "is telehealth appropriate?" is simply no, and the patient is directed to in-person care before the consultation proceeds further. This is correct practice, not a service failure.

### Stage 2: Medical History and Safety Flags

For consultations that proceed, the doctor reviews a structured medical history to identify any factors that change the assessment or treatment:

- **Known allergies**: Medication allergies directly affect what can safely be [prescribed](/prescriptions). A penicillin allergy, for example, rules out the entire penicillin antibiotic class and requires a different treatment choice.
- **Current medications**: Drug interactions can make an otherwise appropriate medication unsafe. A patient taking warfarin, for example, has interaction considerations for many antibiotics and pain medications.
- **Significant medical history**: Certain conditions affect prescribing decisions or indicate a need for in-person assessment. [Diabetes](/conditions/type-2-diabetes), immunosuppression, chronic kidney disease, and pregnancy all modify what is safe.
- **Recent treatment for the same condition**: If a patient has already had two courses of antibiotics for the same infection without resolution, something more complex may be occurring, and telehealth is not the right venue for further management.
- **Reproductive status**: Pregnancy and breastfeeding status significantly affect medication safety. This is asked for every prescription request.

### Stage 3: Symptom Assessment and Red Flag Screening

The doctor assesses the patient's current symptoms for features that indicate either clinical appropriateness or the need for referral:

- **Duration**: How long has this been occurring? Symptoms lasting beyond expected timeframes for common conditions may indicate complications or an incorrect working diagnosis.
- **Severity**: Is the severity proportionate to the stated cause? Disproportionate severity is a red flag.
- **Progression**: Is the condition improving, stable, or worsening? Rapid deterioration or failure to improve as expected changes the clinical picture.
- **Associated symptoms**: Certain combinations of symptoms are significant. A UTI with fever and [back pain](/conditions/back-pain) may indicate kidney involvement, which changes the appropriate treatment.
- **Specific red flags for the condition**: Each condition has its own list of features that indicate serious pathology. [Sinusitis](/conditions/sinus-headache) with visual changes, skin infection with rapidly expanding borders, and respiratory infection with confusion all require urgent in-person assessment regardless of the patient's preference for telehealth.

### Stage 4: Medication-Specific Checks for Prescriptions

For prescription requests, an additional layer of screening applies:

- **Is the medication appropriate for this condition?** First-line treatment guidelines are followed.
- **Is the dose appropriate for this patient?** Weight, renal function, and other factors may affect dosing.
- **Has the patient recently used the same medication?** Frequent use of the same antibiotic, for example, raises resistance concerns.
- **Are there contraindications?** Some medications have specific contraindications that the history may have revealed.
- **Is this medication appropriate for telehealth prescribing?** Some medications are restricted from telehealth prescribing by regulation or clinical guidelines.

## What Triggers a Referral

A referral - to a GP, urgent care clinic, emergency department, or specialist - occurs when the doctor's assessment indicates that telehealth is not the appropriate care setting for this patient and this presentation. Common triggers include:

- Any of the red flags described above
- Paediatric presentations in young children, where physical examination is important and deterioration can be rapid
- Symptoms that do not fit the stated working diagnosis
- Failure to respond to previous treatment for the same condition
- A combination of symptoms suggesting systemic illness rather than a localised problem
- Any situation where the doctor cannot be clinically confident without physical assessment
- Pregnancy with complications, or presentations that require obstetric assessment

<Callout variant="info">Receiving a referral is not a failure of the service. It means the screening identified something that requires a higher level of assessment. This is the system working as intended. A telehealth service that never refers anyone is a more concerning sign than one that refers appropriately.</Callout>

## How to Prepare for a Smooth Telehealth Consultation

The quality of your telehealth consultation is directly related to the completeness of the information you provide. Before completing your consultation form:

- **Know your symptoms precisely**: note when they started, how they have progressed, and their current severity. "Unwell for a few days" is less useful than "fever for 48 hours, peaked at 38.8 degrees, now resolving, with persistent cough."
- **Have your medication list ready**: include prescription medications, over-the-counter medications, vitamins, and supplements. Include doses if you know them.
- **Know your relevant history**: if you have been treated for the same condition before, know when and what you received.
- **Disclose allergies fully**: include the specific reaction you experienced, not just the substance you are allergic to. "Rash" and "anaphylaxis" have very different implications.
- **For skin conditions, take photos**: clear, well-lit photos of the affected area - including one close-up and one showing the extent of involvement - are significantly more useful than descriptions alone.

Answer every question honestly and completely. The screening exists to protect you. Omitting information to try to ensure a particular outcome (such as a specific prescription) does not work in your favour.

## Screening and the Limits of Telehealth

Safety screening exists precisely because telehealth has limits. The absence of physical examination means there are conditions that cannot be safely assessed remotely. Responsible telehealth services are explicit about these limits:

- Conditions requiring auscultation (chest infections with complex presentation, heart murmurs)
- Conditions requiring fundoscopy or slit lamp examination (eye conditions)
- Conditions requiring palpation (abdominal conditions, lymph nodes, joints)
- Conditions requiring vital signs for safe management (sepsis workup, cardiac assessment)

The purpose of screening is not to find a way to treat everything online. It is to identify which patients can be safely helped remotely and which need a different care pathway. That distinction is what makes telehealth medicine rather than just document delivery.
