A [medical certificate](/medical-certificate) for work is a formal document from a registered health practitioner confirming you are unfit for your duties due to illness or injury. It is the standard form of evidence required by Australian employers when you take personal sick leave. This guide covers when you need one, what it must contain, your rights under the Fair Work Act, and the fastest ways to get one.

## When Does Your Employer Need a Certificate?

Under the Fair Work Act 2009 and National Employment Standards (NES), your employer can request evidence of illness for any absence. Most do not do so for single-day absences, but there is no legal requirement to wait. When employers typically ask:

- **Two or more consecutive sick days**: The most common threshold. Most awards and enterprise agreements require evidence after a second consecutive day.
- **Any absence adjacent to a public holiday**: Many awards specifically allow certificate requests for single-day absences on Mondays, Fridays, or the day immediately before or after a public holiday.
- **Pattern of single-day absences**: If you have a recurring pattern (every Friday, or frequent Monday mornings), a policy requiring evidence for single days may kick in.
- **Your workplace policy or award requires it**: Some industries - construction, healthcare, mining, transport - have stricter thresholds. Check your specific award or enterprise agreement.
- **First-day requirement**: Fewer workplaces use this, but some awards and enterprise agreements require a certificate from day one.

If you are uncertain what applies to you, your HR team or the Fair Work Ombudsman website (fairwork.gov.au) lists all modern award requirements.

<Callout variant="info">Your employer must advise you that they require evidence before you are absent - or as soon as reasonably practicable. They cannot demand a certificate retrospectively without prior notice if their policy does not clearly state when evidence is required.</Callout>

## What a Medical Certificate Must Contain

For a certificate to be valid and satisfy employer requirements under the Fair Work Act, it should include:

- **Your full legal name**
- **Date of the consultation**
- **Statement of unfitness**: A clear statement that you were unfit for work due to illness or injury
- **Dates covered**: The period you are certified unfit (e.g., "unfit for work from 14 April to 16 April 2026")
- **Practitioner's details**: Name, AHPRA registration number, signature, and practice contact information

Crucially: the certificate does **not** need to specify your diagnosis. "Unfit for work due to a medical condition" is legally sufficient. Your employer has no right to your diagnosis, and a responsible doctor will not disclose it without your explicit consent.

## Who Can Issue a Medical Certificate

In Australia, medical certificates for work purposes can be issued by:

- **AHPRA-registered doctors (GPs and specialists)**: The most common source. Any doctor on the AHPRA register can certify incapacity.
- **Nurse practitioners**: Registered nurse practitioners are authorised to issue valid medical certificates and are increasingly available via telehealth and at some clinics.
- **Dentists**: For dental-related conditions (tooth extraction recovery, dental surgery) that prevent you from working, your dentist can issue a certificate.
- **Other AHPRA-registered practitioners**: For specific conditions, some other registered health practitioners (optometrists for eye conditions, physiotherapists in limited circumstances) may provide supporting evidence, though a standard medical certificate from a doctor or NP is the expected form.

## Your Rights Under the Fair Work Act

Full-time and part-time employees accrue **10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year** under the NES. This applies regardless of whether your employer is large or small, public or private. The entitlement:

- Accumulates year to year if unused (there is no "use it or lose it" - it rolls over)
- Covers both your own illness and caring for an immediate family or household member
- Is separate from annual leave and cannot be substituted by your employer without agreement
- Applies from your first day of employment (though some awards have qualifying periods for certain conditions)

Casual employees do not accrue paid personal leave but are entitled to 2 days of unpaid personal leave per occasion under the NES.

<Callout variant="info">An employer cannot deduct personal leave from your balance unless you have actually taken leave and been absent from work. Being present at work while unwell is not personal leave - it is working.</Callout>

## What Employers Can and Cannot Ask

### What they can do

- Request a medical certificate or statutory declaration as evidence
- Ask for the expected duration of your absence
- Ask when you expect to return to work
- Request a certificate from day one if their policy or your award requires it
- Request an independent medical examination (IME) for extended absences or return-to-work assessment (at their expense)
- Discuss patterns of absence with you as part of performance or attendance management

### What they cannot do

- Demand to know your specific diagnosis
- Contact your doctor without your written consent
- Require you to see a specific doctor for your initial certificate
- Reject a valid certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner simply because it was issued via telehealth
- Withhold sick leave pay for a legitimate absence supported by valid evidence
- Take adverse action against you for exercising your lawful right to take personal leave

## Single-Day Absences

For a single day off work, a certificate is not always required - but it depends on your workplace policy. Many employers only ask for evidence from the second consecutive day. However:

- Your employer may ask for evidence for any single-day absence if their policy permits
- Absences adjacent to public holidays, weekends, or annual leave blocks are more likely to attract requests
- If you are concerned about being questioned, getting a certificate proactively removes the ambiguity

Telehealth makes getting a same-day certificate practical. You do not need to travel to a clinic or spend hours in a waiting room on a day when you are genuinely unwell.

## Multi-Day and Extended Absences

For absences spanning several days, your certificate should cover the full period. If your illness extends beyond the period originally certificated, you will need a follow-up or extension certificate. Key points:

- The doctor sets the duration based on your condition - do not return before you are medically fit
- For absences extending beyond one week, your employer may request ongoing certification (every 5-7 days, or as specified in your award)
- For extended absences (weeks to months), your employer may request an IME or a return-to-work medical assessment before you come back
- Workers' compensation cases have separate certificate requirements (Certificate of Capacity or Work Capacity Certificate, depending on state)

## Industry-Specific Requirements

Some industries have stricter or different documentation requirements:

- **Food handling roles**: Public health regulations require exclusion for specific infectious illnesses ([gastroenteritis](/conditions/food-poisoning), hepatitis A). A clearance certificate confirming you are fit to return and no longer infectious may be required.
- **Healthcare and aged care**: Infection control requirements. Nurses, aged care workers, and others in clinical roles may need clearance after respiratory illness, gastroenteritis, or [skin infections](/conditions/skin-rash).
- **Transport and logistics**: Fatigue management obligations apply. Drivers who are unwell may have specific requirements under their award or Road Transport Award provisions.
- **Mining (FIFO/DIDO)**: Most mining companies require certificates from day one of absence. Fitness-for-duty assessments before returning to site are also common.
- **Construction**: Building and Construction General On-site Award and most enterprise agreements require evidence from day one for many situations.

## Return-to-Work Clearance Certificates

A medical certificate certifying unfitness is different from a return-to-work clearance certificate. For some roles, your employer may require medical clearance before you return:

- Safety-critical roles (operating heavy machinery, driving, working at heights)
- Roles requiring a specific licence (forklift, crane operation, high-risk work licences)
- Healthcare or food handling roles after infectious illness
- After extended absences (typically longer than 4-6 weeks) where fitness for duty needs confirmation
- Under a workers' compensation return-to-work plan

A return-to-work clearance is a separate document from your sick certificate. It states you are now fit to resume your duties, sometimes with specific restrictions. Your treating doctor or GP can provide this.

## Getting a Certificate the Same Day

If you wake up sick and need a certificate for work that day:

- **Telehealth**: Complete an [online consultation](/consult) from home. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case and, if clinically appropriate, issues a certificate delivered to your email. Typically completed within 1-2 hours during operating hours.
- **Your regular GP**: Call first thing for a same-day appointment. Early morning slots are often available.
- **Walk-in medical centre**: Accepts walk-in patients without appointments, usually at longer waits.
- **After-hours clinic**: Available outside standard GP hours for evenings and weekends.

Telehealth is often the most practical option when you are genuinely unwell - it removes the travel burden and waiting room exposure entirely.

## If Your Employer Disputes Your Certificate

A valid certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner should be accepted. If your employer raises concerns:

- Ask them to specify in writing what concern they have with the certificate
- Check whether the certificate is complete (all required fields present)
- Confirm the doctor's registration is current at ahpra.gov.au
- If they request an IME, this must be at their expense and you generally must comply with reasonable requests
- If they take action against you despite a valid certificate (docking pay, disciplining you), contact the Fair Work Ombudsman on 13 13 94 or via fairwork.gov.au

Patterns of absence can be legitimately managed by employers, but the management process must be separate from the certificate validity question. "You have taken a lot of sick leave" is a different matter from "this certificate is not valid."

## Statutory Declaration as an Alternative

Under the Fair Work Act, a statutory declaration is an acceptable alternative to a medical certificate when it was not reasonably practicable to obtain one. A stat dec is a written sworn statement made before an authorised witness, declaring that you were unwell during the relevant period.

When a stat dec may be appropriate:
- Brief illness (1-2 days) that resolved without a doctor visit
- Circumstances that made accessing a doctor genuinely impossible
- Your workplace policy or award explicitly accepts stat decs

Stat decs can be witnessed at no cost by a Justice of the Peace, pharmacist, or other authorised witness. They are not appropriate for extended absences where medical assessment would be expected.
