A medical certificate is a document from a registered health practitioner confirming you're unfit for work due to illness or injury. Most Australian employers will ask for one if you take [sick leave](/blog/sick-leave-rights-australia "Your sick leave rights"), especially for absences longer than a day or two.

## When Do You Need a Medical Certificate?

Your employer's policy determines when you need a certificate. Common requirements include:

- Absences of two or more consecutive days
- Any absence on a Monday, Friday, or adjacent to public holidays
- If you've had multiple short absences recently
- When your workplace policy specifically requires one
- For workers' compensation claims

<Callout variant="info">

Check your employment contract or workplace policy for specific requirements. If it's not clear, a quick question to HR can save confusion.

</Callout>

## What Should a Medical Certificate Include?

A valid medical certificate for work should contain:

- Your full name
- Date of the consultation
- Statement that you're unfit for work
- The dates you're covered for
- The practitioner's name, signature, and registration details
- Practice contact information

<Callout variant="tip">

Your certificate does NOT need to include your diagnosis. "Medical condition" is sufficient, and your employer cannot demand more detail.

</Callout>

## What Employers Can and Can't Ask

### Employers CAN:

- Request a medical certificate as evidence
- Ask when you expect to return
- Request a fitness for work clearance for safety-critical roles

### Employers CANNOT:

- Demand to know your specific diagnosis
- Require you to see a specific doctor
- Contact your doctor without your consent
- Reject a valid certificate from a registered practitioner

## How to Get a Medical Certificate

You have several options for obtaining a medical certificate:

- Your regular GP - ideal if they know your medical history
- A walk-in clinic - useful if your GP is unavailable
- Telehealth services - convenient when you're unwell at home
- Hospital emergency department - only for genuine emergencies

For straightforward illnesses like cold, flu, or gastro, [telehealth](/blog/what-is-telehealth "What is telehealth?") is often the most convenient option. You don't need to travel while unwell, and you can receive your certificate digitally.

## Same-Day Medical Certificates

If you wake up sick and need a certificate for work that day, your options include:

- Early morning GP appointments (if available)
- Walk-in clinics that open early
- Telehealth services operating from early morning
- After-hours medical services

<Callout variant="info">

Many telehealth services can issue certificates within an hour during business hours. Check the service's typical turnaround time.

</Callout>

## Medical Certificates for Multiple Days

When you need time off for several days, the doctor will assess your condition and recommend an appropriate duration. For common illnesses:

- Cold or flu: typically 2-5 days
- Gastro: usually 1-3 days
- Minor injuries: varies based on your job requirements
- Mental health: 1-3 days initially, with follow-up recommended for longer periods

## Returning to Work

Most employers simply require you to be well enough to perform your duties. However, some roles may require a [fitness for work certificate](/blog/return-to-work-after-illness "Returning to work after illness"), particularly:

- Safety-critical positions (operating machinery, driving)
- Food handling roles after gastro
- Healthcare workers after infectious illness
- After extended absences

## If Your Employer Questions Your Certificate

A valid certificate from a registered practitioner should be accepted. If your employer has concerns, they can:

- Request you attend an independent medical examination (at their expense)
- Discuss patterns of absence with you
- Review their sick leave policy with HR

They cannot simply reject a legitimate certificate or demand you provide more medical detail than the certificate contains.

<Callout variant="warning">

If you feel you're being treated unfairly regarding sick leave, the Fair Work Ombudsman provides free advice and can investigate complaints.

</Callout>
