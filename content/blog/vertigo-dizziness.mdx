Dizziness is one of the most common presenting complaints in Australian general practice. According to the AIHW, vestibular and balance disorders collectively affect a significant proportion of the Australian population, with prevalence increasing substantially with age. Falls related to balance problems are a major public health concern: the AIHW identifies falls as the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in Australia, with over 100,000 Australians hospitalised annually due to falls, predominantly older adults.

Understanding what type of dizziness you are experiencing is the first step, because the causes, assessment, and management differ substantially between them.

## The Three Types of Dizziness

The word "dizziness" covers at least three distinct experiences:

| Type | What it feels like | Where the signal comes from |
|---|---|---|
| Vertigo | The environment spinning or moving around you, or a sensation of self-rotation | Inner ear or, less commonly, brain |
| Presyncope (lightheadedness) | Feeling like you might faint; "going grey"; swimming sensation | Reduced cerebral blood flow |
| Disequilibrium | Off-balance, unsteady, difficulty walking without a spinning sensation | Multiple: proprioception, vision, cerebellum |

Accurately describing which type you experience helps your doctor narrow the differential diagnosis considerably.

## Vertigo: Inner Ear Causes

Most vertigo originates in the inner ear (peripheral vertigo). The three most common causes are:

### Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

BPPV is the most common cause of vertigo in Australian clinical practice, accounting for an estimated 20-30% of all vertigo presentations. It occurs when otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals normally embedded in the utricle) become dislodged and migrate into one of the semicircular canals, where they incorrectly generate movement signals.

**Characteristics:**
- Brief episodes of spinning triggered by specific head movements (rolling over in bed, tilting the head back, looking up)
- Typically lasts 30-60 seconds
- No hearing loss or tinnitus
- Most common in adults over 50

**Diagnosis**: The Dix-Hallpike test, performed by a doctor or physiotherapist, is the standard diagnostic manoeuvre. It reproduces the vertigo and elicits characteristic nystagmus (eye movements) that confirm the diagnosis.

**Treatment**: The Epley manoeuvre is the most effective treatment for posterior canal BPPV, the most common variant. It is a series of guided head movements that repositions the displaced crystals. The RACGP endorses the Epley manoeuvre as a first-line treatment, with resolution rates of 80-90% after one or two sessions. A doctor or vestibular physiotherapist can perform it, and many patients can learn a home version.

### Vestibular Neuritis and Labyrinthitis

These are inflammatory conditions of the inner ear, usually following a viral infection:

- **Vestibular neuritis**: affects the vestibular nerve only. Causes prolonged, severe vertigo lasting days to weeks, with no hearing loss.
- **Labyrinthitis**: affects both the vestibular nerve and cochlea. Causes vertigo plus hearing loss and tinnitus.

Both conditions are self-limiting. Initial severe vertigo is managed with antiemetics (prochlorperazine) and vestibular sedatives in the short term (typically no more than 3 days - longer use delays central compensation). Vestibular rehabilitation exercises, performed as a structured program by a physiotherapist, are the most effective intervention for accelerating recovery.

### Meniere's Disease

Meniere's disease involves abnormal pressure in the endolymph fluid of the inner ear:

- Recurrent discrete episodes of severe vertigo lasting 20 minutes to several hours
- Fluctuating hearing loss (usually unilateral)
- Tinnitus (ringing or roaring in the affected ear)
- Aural fullness or pressure

Meniere's disease requires specialist assessment (ENT or neurotologist) for diagnosis and management. Treatment options include dietary sodium restriction, diuretics, intratympanic steroid injections, and, for refractory cases, surgical or destructive procedures.

## Presyncope: Cardiovascular and Other Causes

Lightheadedness (presyncope) results from temporarily reduced blood flow to the brain:

| Cause | Mechanism | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Orthostatic hypotension | Blood pressure drops on standing | Standing up quickly, especially in older adults or after illness |
| Vasovagal syncope | Neural reflex causing bradycardia and hypotension | Prolonged standing, pain, heat, blood or needle phobia |
| Dehydration | Reduced circulating volume | Hot weather, illness, inadequate fluid intake |
| Cardiac arrhythmia | Irregular rhythm reduces cardiac output | Can occur at rest; palpitations often accompany |
| Medication effects | Antihypertensives, diuretics | Especially with dose changes or new medications |
| Anaemia | Reduced oxygen carrying capacity | Fatigue, pallor, exertional symptoms |

Orthostatic hypotension is particularly common in older adults. The RACGP Falls Prevention guidelines identify it as a modifiable risk factor for falls and recommend blood pressure measurement lying and standing at annual health assessments in older patients.

## Disequilibrium

Disequilibrium without vertigo or lightheadedness typically involves dysfunction in the systems that maintain balance - vision, proprioception (joint position sense), and cerebellar coordination. Causes include:

- Peripheral neuropathy affecting proprioception (common in diabetes)
- Medication side effects (sedatives, anticonvulsants, benzodiazepines)
- Cerebellar ataxia
- Bilateral vestibular hypofunction (from ototoxic drugs, autoimmune conditions)
- Age-related sensory and motor decline

## Managing Dizziness at Home

For mild or positional vertigo without red flags:

1. Sit or lie down immediately when vertigo strikes to reduce fall risk.
2. Stay hydrated - dehydration worsens all forms of dizziness.
3. Move slowly when changing positions, particularly from lying or sitting to standing. Pause on the edge of the bed for 30-60 seconds before standing.
4. Do not drive, operate machinery, work at heights, or make important decisions while experiencing active vertigo.
5. For BPPV, avoid the specific head position that triggers episodes until assessed.

> **Tip:** For confirmed BPPV, the Epley manoeuvre performed correctly can resolve symptoms rapidly. Ask your GP or a vestibular physiotherapist to teach you the technique -- most people can perform home versions independently after initial instruction.

## Medications for Symptom Relief

Several medications can reduce acute vertigo and nausea:

- **Prochlorperazine** (Stemetil): most commonly used vestibular sedative in Australia. Available by prescription. Short-term use only (maximum 3 days) to avoid blunting the brain's compensation process.
- **Antihistamines** (promethazine): used for motion sickness; some benefit for acute vertigo
- **Betahistine**: used for Meniere's disease to reduce episode frequency; not effective for other vertigo types

Vestibular sedatives should not be used long-term for BPPV - the Epley manoeuvre is far more effective.

## Red Flags: When Dizziness Needs Urgent Assessment

Most dizziness is benign. The following features suggest a potentially serious cause:

> **Emergency:** Call 000 immediately if dizziness is accompanied by: sudden severe headache, facial drooping or asymmetry, one-sided arm or leg weakness, speech difficulty or slurring, or double vision. These are symptoms of stroke. The Stroke Foundation FAST test (Face, Arms, Speech, Time) should be applied: if any feature is present, call 000 immediately.

Additional red flags requiring same-day or urgent medical assessment:
- New dizziness with unilateral hearing loss (possible acute cochlear or cerebrovascular event)
- Dizziness following a head injury
- Dizziness with chest pain or palpitations
- Progressively worsening balance over weeks without a clear diagnosis
- Dizziness with neck pain after trauma
- Fainting (syncope) without warning

## Falls Risk and Older Australians

For people over 65, recurrent dizziness and balance problems represent a significant falls risk. The AIHW reports that fall-related injuries are the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in this age group. If dizziness is contributing to unsteadiness in an older person, a comprehensive falls risk assessment is warranted. This typically includes:

- Medication review (polypharmacy and specific drug classes are major modifiable risk factors)
- Blood pressure assessment lying and standing
- Vision assessment
- Balance and gait assessment (Timed Up and Go test)
- Environmental assessment

Referral to a falls prevention program, vestibular physiotherapy, or specialist (neurologist, ENT) may be appropriate depending on findings.
