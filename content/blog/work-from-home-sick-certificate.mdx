Remote work has created a grey area that did not exist a decade ago. If you are unwell but your laptop is three steps away, are you expected to work? Do you need a certificate? Can your employer ask you to "just work from home" instead of taking leave? The Fair Work Act has clear answers to most of these questions - and they tend to favour the employee. Here is what you actually need to know.

<Callout variant="info">Direct answer: A medical certificate is required for work-from-home sick leave under the same rules as in-office sick leave. The certificate requirement depends on your workplace policy, your award, and the duration of your absence - not on where you were planning to work that day.</Callout>

## The Critical Distinction: Working vs Leave

This is the foundation of the whole issue. Remote work has blurred the line, but legally it is still sharp:

- **Working from home while unwell** means you are working. You complete tasks, you are reachable, you are delivering your normal duties (perhaps at reduced intensity). No certificate is required because you are not taking leave.
- **Taking sick leave** means you are not working. You are unwell, unfit for your duties, and exercising your entitlement under the National Employment Standards. Certificate rules apply.

Your employer cannot collapse these two categories. "Working from home" is not a lesser form of sick leave. It is working. If you are too unwell to work, you take leave - regardless of where your office is.

## Your Entitlements Under the Fair Work Act

Under the National Employment Standards (NES), permanent full-time employees are entitled to 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year. This entitlement:

- Accumulates year to year if unused
- Applies regardless of whether your normal work location is an office, a worksite, or home
- Cannot be forfeited or substituted by an employer without your agreement
- Covers both your own illness and caring for an immediate family or household member

Part-time employees receive a pro-rata entitlement based on their ordinary hours of work. Casual employees do not receive paid leave entitlements under the NES, though some enterprise agreements provide exceptions.

<Callout variant="info">Your sick leave entitlement exists independently of your work location. An employer who says "you can work from home, so you don't need to take sick leave" is misapplying the law. Fitness for work is about your ability to perform your duties - not about whether a commute is involved.</Callout>

## When You Definitely Need a Certificate

A medical certificate is required (or strongly advisable) in these situations, whether you work from home or not:

- You are taking sick leave for more than two consecutive days - most awards and enterprise agreements require evidence after two days
- You are absent on or around a public holiday - employers can request evidence for any single-day absence adjacent to a public holiday
- Your employer specifically requests evidence - they can ask for any absence, including single days
- You have a pattern of Monday and Friday absences - a pattern may trigger a policy requiring evidence regardless of duration
- Your role involves safety-critical duties - some roles have stricter requirements built into their award or enterprise agreement

Check your specific award or enterprise agreement. Some set lower thresholds than the standard two-day norm, particularly in healthcare, transport, or public sector roles.

## When You Probably Do Not Need One

- You are genuinely working from home (not on leave)
- Your absence is within your employer's no-evidence threshold and no request has been made
- Your award or enterprise agreement does not require evidence for the duration of absence
- Your manager has explicitly waived the requirement

Even when not required, getting a certificate is low-friction via telehealth and provides documentation that protects you if the absence is questioned later.

## Three Realistic Scenarios

### Scenario 1: You Have a Cold and Can Still Work

You wake up with a runny nose and mild fatigue. You are functional but do not want to infect colleagues. You work from home for the day, completing your normal tasks.

This is not sick leave. You are working. No certificate is required. Notify your manager if that is your normal practice, but there is no entitlement calculation involved.

### Scenario 2: You Have Flu and Cannot Work

You wake up with fever, body aches, and cannot concentrate. Working is not feasible regardless of where your desk is.

This is sick leave. You notify your employer as soon as reasonably practicable. Certificate requirements depend on the duration and your workplace policy. If it extends beyond two days, a certificate will almost certainly be required. Telehealth lets you get one without leaving home on a day when leaving home is the last thing you should be doing.

### Scenario 3: You Start the Day Working, Then Deteriorate

You begin working from home but by midday you are too unwell to continue. You stop working at noon and rest.

You can take sick leave for the afternoon. Whether you need a certificate for a partial day depends on your workplace policy. Some policies count any portion of a day as a sick day for evidence purposes; others only require evidence after a full day. Check your agreement.

## What Your Employer Can and Cannot Do

### Legitimate employer requests

- Ask you to notify them of absence as soon as reasonably practicable
- Request a medical certificate or statutory declaration as evidence
- Require you to follow reasonable notification procedures
- Ask about the expected duration of your absence (not the specific diagnosis)

### What they cannot do

- Require you to work from home as a condition of taking sick leave
- Refuse a valid medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor
- Demand your specific diagnosis (a certificate stating "unfit for work due to a medical condition" is legally sufficient)
- Treat sick leave absences as unauthorised if proper notice and evidence requirements are met
- Take adverse action against you for exercising your right to take sick leave

<Callout variant="warning">If your employer disciplines you, demotes you, reduces your hours, or otherwise treats you less favourably because you took legitimate sick leave, this may constitute adverse action under the Fair Work Act. Document communications and contact the Fair Work Ombudsman if needed.</Callout>

## The Productivity Trap

Here is a dynamic that plays out often in remote workplaces: you say you will "work from home" when you are sick, to avoid using leave or to seem committed. But you are too unwell to actually be productive. You spend the day half-working, barely contributing, while delaying your own recovery.

This is bad for you, bad for your work, and arguably misleading to your employer (who believes you are working at normal capacity). The honest, legally sound approach:

- Assess your actual fitness to work
- If you can work: work from home and note it as a normal working day
- If you cannot: take sick leave, notify appropriately, get documentation if needed

Sick leave exists to protect you. Using it appropriately is not a sign of weakness - it is how the system is designed to function.

## Industries and Awards with Different Rules

Some industries have award-specific requirements that override the general two-day threshold:

- **Construction** (Building and Construction General On-site Award): evidence may be required from day one for some absences
- **Healthcare** (various health sector awards): stricter requirements due to infection control obligations
- **Retail and hospitality**: evidence commonly required for absences adjacent to public holidays
- **Public sector**: state public service acts often impose additional evidence requirements beyond the NES

If you are covered by an enterprise agreement rather than a modern award, the agreement terms govern - provided they pass the Better Off Overall Test against the relevant award.

## Getting a Certificate Without Leaving Home

A common friction point: you are unwell, you need a certificate, but you also should not be driving to a clinic or sitting in a waiting room. [Telehealth medical certificates](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate online") solve this directly.

The process:

- Complete an online form describing your symptoms and the dates you need covered
- An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case and determines whether you are unfit for work
- If approved, your certificate is emailed as a PDF immediately
- The certificate is legally valid - it is issued by a registered medical practitioner and satisfies all employer and Fair Work requirements

Telehealth certificates are not "lesser" certificates. An employer cannot refuse a valid certificate from a registered doctor on the basis that it was issued remotely. If they do, the Fair Work Ombudsman is the correct escalation path.

## Communicating With Your Manager

Clear language prevents confusion and protects you:

- "I am unwell and not fit for work today. I am taking sick leave. I will provide a certificate if required." - Unambiguous. You are on leave.
- "I have a mild cold but can work. I will be working from home today." - Unambiguous. You are working.
- "I am not feeling great, I will try to work from home." - Ambiguous. Clarify whether this is leave or a working day.

Ambiguity creates disputes. Clarity costs nothing.
