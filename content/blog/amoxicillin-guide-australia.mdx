Amoxicillin is a penicillin-type antibiotic and the most frequently prescribed antibiotic in Australia, appearing consistently at the top of NPS MedicineWise dispensing data. It has been in clinical use since 1972, is PBS-listed for a range of bacterial infections, and remains effective against many common community-acquired bacteria. This guide covers what amoxicillin treats, dosing, side effects, important drug interactions, and the critical clinical distinction between bacterial infections (where antibiotics help) and viral infections (where they do not).

## What Amoxicillin Treats

Amoxicillin is active against many gram-positive and some gram-negative bacteria. The Australian Therapeutic Guidelines (eTG Complete, Antibiotic module) recommend it as first-line treatment for several common infections:

| Condition | eTG recommendation |
|---|---|
| Acute otitis media ([middle ear infection](/conditions/ear-infection)) | First-line if antibiotics are indicated (watchful waiting is appropriate for mild cases in children 2+ years) |
| Streptococcal pharyngitis (strep throat) | First-line where strep is confirmed or strongly suspected; 10-day course |
| Community-acquired pneumonia (mild [chest infection](/conditions/chest-infection)) | First-line for appropriate outpatient cases; combination therapy for moderate cases |
| Acute bacterial sinusitis | Reserved for moderate-severe cases persisting beyond 10 days; most sinusitis is viral |
| H. pylori eradication | As part of standard triple therapy (amoxicillin + clarithromycin + PPI) |
| Dental infections | First-line for appropriate dental infections when antibiotics are indicated |

## What Amoxicillin Does NOT Treat

Amoxicillin has no activity against viruses. This is not a limitation of the drug -- it is a fundamental property of all antibiotics. Viral infections include:

- The common cold (rhinovirus, coronavirus, and dozens of others)
- Influenza (influenza A and B viruses)
- COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2)
- Most [sore throats](/conditions/sore-throat) (approximately 70-80% are viral, per eTG)
- Most acute coughs and bronchitis (the large majority are viral even when productive)
- Glandular fever (Epstein-Barr virus)

According to the Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care's AURA (Antimicrobial Use and Resistance in Australia) surveillance program, antibiotic prescribing for respiratory tract infections remains above international benchmarks in some Australian settings. If your doctor assesses your infection as viral and recommends symptomatic management instead of antibiotics, that is evidence-based practice.

## Dosing

Standard adult dosing varies by indication according to eTG:

| Indication | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Mild infections (general) | 500mg every 8 hours | 5-7 days |
| Moderate infections | 500mg-1g every 8 hours | 5-7 days |
| Streptococcal pharyngitis | 500mg twice daily or 500mg three times daily | 10 days |
| H. pylori (triple therapy) | 1g twice daily (with clarithromycin + PPI) | 7-14 days (per protocol) |
| Dental infections | 500mg every 8 hours | 5 days |

**How to take it**: Amoxicillin is well-absorbed with or without food, unlike some older penicillins. Space doses as evenly as possible (e.g., 6am, 2pm, 10pm for a three-times-daily regimen). Completing the full course is important even if you feel better after 2-3 days.

## Amoxicillin vs Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin)

Amoxicillin-clavulanate combines amoxicillin with clavulanic acid, which inhibits bacterial beta-lactamase enzymes -- the mechanism many bacteria use to resist penicillins. The addition of clavulanate extends the spectrum to beta-lactamase-producing organisms.

Amoxicillin-clavulanate is indicated where beta-lactamase-producing bacteria are likely (e.g., complicated skin infections, animal bites, some respiratory infections when plain amoxicillin has failed). It has a higher rate of gastrointestinal side effects, particularly diarrhoea, compared to amoxicillin alone. The eTG recommends plain amoxicillin where its spectrum is sufficient; amoxicillin-clavulanate is not a first-line upgrade for routine infections.

## Side Effects

**Common (5-10% of patients):**
- Diarrhoea: the most frequent side effect. Amoxicillin disrupts the gut microbiome (intestinal flora), leading to loose stools. This is usually mild and self-limiting. Probiotic supplementation during and after the course may reduce diarrhoea frequency.
- Nausea: common but usually tolerable
- Non-allergic amoxicillin rash: a maculopapular (flat, blotchy) rash occurring in approximately 5-10% of patients, particularly those with concurrent glandular fever (Epstein-Barr virus infection). This rash is not a true allergic reaction -- it is a drug-virus interaction unique to aminopenicillins in the context of EBV infection. It does not indicate penicillin allergy.

**Uncommon but important:**
- Vaginal thrush (candidiasis): disruption of normal vaginal bacterial flora by antibiotics allows Candida to overgrow. Treat with over-the-counter antifungal (clotrimazole or fluconazole).
- C. difficile colitis: rare with amoxicillin but a known risk with all antibiotics. More common in older patients or those with prior antibiotic exposure. Symptoms: severe diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, fever after starting antibiotics.

> **Emergency**: If you develop difficulty breathing, facial or throat swelling, or widespread urticaria (hives) after taking amoxicillin, call 000 immediately. This may indicate anaphylaxis -- a life-threatening allergic reaction.

## Penicillin Allergy

Approximately 10-15% of Australians report a penicillin allergy, but research consistently shows that more than 90% of those labelled "penicillin allergic" do not have a true allergy when formally tested. The most common reason for the label is a childhood rash during an antibiotic course -- which, as described above, is often a non-allergic drug-virus reaction or an incidental viral rash unrelated to the antibiotic.

The "penicillin allergy" label matters because it leads to the use of broader-spectrum antibiotics as alternatives, which carry their own risks and contribute to antimicrobial resistance. The eTG and RACGP recommend that clinicians consider formal allergy assessment (penicillin skin testing, graded challenge) for patients with uncertain penicillin allergy histories.

If you have been told you are penicillin-allergic, discuss the history with your doctor. A rash 20 years ago during a childhood ear infection is not the same as anaphylaxis.

## Drug Interactions

**Allopurinol**: Concurrent use of amoxicillin and allopurinol (used for gout) significantly increases the risk of a maculopapular rash. This is not a contraindication but a relevant risk to be aware of.

**Methotrexate**: Amoxicillin can reduce the renal clearance of methotrexate, potentially increasing methotrexate toxicity. Avoid co-administration where possible; if necessary, monitor closely.

**Oral contraceptive pill**: The interaction between standard antibiotics (including amoxicillin) and the combined oral contraceptive pill has been debunked by current evidence. Non-enzyme-inducing antibiotics like amoxicillin do not reduce OCP efficacy through pharmacological interaction. The exception applies if severe diarrhoea or vomiting occurs, which can reduce pill absorption regardless of the cause.

**Warfarin**: Amoxicillin can increase INR in some patients on warfarin. If you take warfarin, notify your doctor; INR monitoring may be appropriate.

## Antibiotic Resistance

The AURA 2023 report from the Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care documents ongoing antibiotic resistance trends across Australian health settings. Resistance in community-acquired pathogens such as Escherichia coli (for UTIs) and Streptococcus pneumoniae is increasing. Approximately 30% of community-acquired E. coli in Australia now show amoxicillin resistance, which is why amoxicillin alone is now less commonly recommended for UTIs in areas with high resistance prevalence, with trimethoprim or nitrofurantoin often preferred.

The most effective individual actions to limit resistance contribution:
- Only take antibiotics for confirmed or strongly suspected bacterial infections, as directed by a doctor
- Complete the full prescribed course
- Never share antibiotics or use leftover antibiotics from a previous prescription
- Never pressure a prescriber for antibiotics when they advise against them

## PBS Status and Cost

Amoxicillin is PBS-listed for appropriate bacterial infections. PBS co-payments (Services Australia, 2025-26):
- General patients: up to $31.60 per prescription
- Concession card holders: up to $7.70 per prescription
- Without PBS (private prescription): approximately $8-$15 for a standard course at most pharmacies

Amoxicillin is available as capsules (250mg, 500mg), tablets, and oral suspension (liquid formulation for children or patients unable to swallow solid-dose forms).

## Getting Amoxicillin Prescribed

Amoxicillin is a Schedule 4 (prescription-only) medication under the Therapeutic Goods Administration's Poisons Standard. A valid prescription from an AHPRA-registered prescriber is required.

Telehealth can be clinically appropriate for some antibiotic prescribing -- for example, a woman with a documented history of recurrent, well-characterised uncomplicated UTIs where the clinical picture is clear from symptom history. For conditions where physical examination adds diagnostic value (examining the throat, ear canal, or skin), an in-person assessment produces a more accurate clinical decision.

A prescriber who declines to prescribe an antibiotic after clinical assessment is not being unhelpful -- they are practising according to evidence-based guidelines. Antibiotic stewardship protects both individual patients and population-level resistance patterns.
