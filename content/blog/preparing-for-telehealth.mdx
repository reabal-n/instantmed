Telehealth consultations produce better outcomes when you are prepared. Unlike an in-person appointment where the doctor can conduct an examination and draw on visual cues, a telehealth doctor depends heavily on the information you provide. The more clearly and completely you communicate your situation, the more accurately the doctor can assess it and the more useful their advice will be.

This applies to asynchronous services (form submission reviewed later) as much as to real-time video or phone consultations. Whether you are typing your symptoms into an intake form or speaking to a doctor live, the clinical quality of the output scales with the quality of the input.

## Before Your Consultation: Technical Preparation

Technical failures during a consultation are frustrating and waste clinical time. Five minutes of preparation prevents most of them.

**Internet connection:** For video consultations, a stable internet connection is essential. Check your connection before the appointment. Wired connections are more reliable than WiFi; move closer to your router if needed. Test by playing a YouTube video at HD quality - if it streams without buffering, your connection is adequate for video consultation.

**Device and camera:** Ensure your device is charged or plugged in. Test that your camera and microphone are working before the consultation begins. On a laptop, check that the camera is not physically blocked. On a phone, check that no app permissions are blocking microphone access.

**Environment:** Find a quiet, private space with good lighting. For video, position the camera so your face is clearly visible - avoid sitting with a bright window behind you, as this creates a silhouette effect. If you are showing a doctor a physical symptom (a rash, a wound, swelling), have the area accessible and positioned where the camera can see it with adequate light.

**Privacy:** Conduct the consultation in a space where you will not be overheard or interrupted, particularly if you are discussing sensitive health information. Close the door if possible.

**Backup plan:** Know the service's phone number or have an alternative consultation mode available in case video fails mid-consultation.

## Before Your Consultation: Medical Information

This is where most of the preparation value lies. Before a telehealth consultation, gather the following:

**Current medications list:** Write down every medication you take, including:
- Prescription medications (name, dose, frequency)
- Over-the-counter medications taken regularly
- Vitamins and supplements
- Herbal preparations

The NPS MedicineWise medication list tool (available at nps.org.au) provides a printable template. A current, accurate medication list is the single most useful document you can have ready for any medical consultation. It allows the doctor to check for interactions with any new medication they might recommend.

**Recent pathology results:** If you have had blood tests in the past 6-12 months, have access to the results. Most Australian pathology providers (Sonic, Australian Clinical Labs, Sullivan Nicolaides, etc.) now have patient portals where results are available digitally. Knowing your recent HbA1c, [thyroid function](/conditions/thyroid-issues) results, lipid panel, or other relevant tests is particularly valuable for chronic disease management consultations.

**Medical history summary:** For complex presentations, a brief summary of your relevant medical history is useful. Known diagnoses, past surgeries, known allergies (and reactions), and relevant family history. You do not need a comprehensive document - the key conditions and the dates they were diagnosed.

**Your regular GP's details:** If you have a regular GP, having their name and practice available means the telehealth doctor can include a referral back or suggest appropriate follow-up with someone who knows your history.

<Callout variant="tip">

For repeat prescriptions specifically, photograph the current medication packaging before the appointment. The image shows the exact product name, brand, dose, and manufacturer, which eliminates any ambiguity about what you are taking and what needs renewing.

</Callout>

## Describing Symptoms Clearly

Symptom description is the core clinical data in a telehealth consultation. Doctors are trained to take a history structured around: what, where, when, how bad, what makes it better or worse, and associated symptoms.

A useful framework for describing any symptom:

**What:** The nature of the symptom. Not just "pain" but: sharp, dull, aching, burning, cramping, pressure? Not just "unwell" but: nausea, fatigue, [headache](/conditions/sinus-headache), fever?

**Where:** The location, as specifically as possible. "Stomach" is vague; "lower right abdomen" is specific. For headaches: front, temple, back of head, behind one eye?

**When:** When it started, how it developed, whether it is constant or comes and goes, and if episodic, how long each episode lasts.

**Severity:** On a scale where 0 is no symptom and 10 is the worst imaginable, where does this symptom sit? Has the severity changed since it started?

**What makes it better or worse:** Activity, position, eating, time of day, medications taken?

**Associated symptoms:** What else is happening? A [sore throat](/conditions/sore-throat) with fever and fatigue has different clinical significance than a sore throat alone. Chest pain with shortness of breath has different significance than chest pain at rest that resolves with antacids.

**Functional impact:** How is this affecting your ability to do normal activities? This is particularly relevant for medical certificate requests.

## Questions to Ask

Prepare specific questions before a real-time consultation. Patients who prepare questions get more from their consultations, according to research on patient-doctor communication.

Write them down before the consultation. Common areas for questions:

- What is the likely cause of my symptoms?
- What are the warning signs that this is getting worse and I should seek urgent care?
- What is the expected timeline for recovery?
- Do I need any tests, and if so, where do I get them done?
- Should I follow up with my regular GP, and when?
- Are there any medications I should avoid given what you have prescribed?
- Is there anything I should not do while I am unwell?

Do not leave a telehealth consultation without understanding what the doctor recommends and what to do if your condition changes.

## During the Consultation

**For video/phone consultations:**
- State your main concern clearly at the start ("I have had a fever and cough for three days and need to know if this needs antibiotics, and whether I need a [medical certificate](/medical-certificate)")
- Answer questions directly but completely - add context where it is relevant
- Ask for clarification if the doctor uses terminology you do not understand
- Confirm the plan before the call ends: what the doctor recommends, whether any prescriptions or documents will be issued, and what the follow-up plan is

**For asynchronous form-based consultations:**
- Complete all fields accurately - do not skip sections that seem optional if they are relevant
- In open text fields, use the symptom description framework above
- If the platform allows it, note any specific questions you would like addressed in the response

## After the Consultation

**Follow the treatment plan:** If the doctor recommends a medication, fill and take it as directed. If they recommend avoiding certain activities, rest, or monitoring specific symptoms, follow that advice.

**Collect prescriptions promptly:** eScripts are time-sensitive in the sense that some medications are needed urgently. Take the eScript to a pharmacy when you have it - do not wait.

**Keep follow-up notes:** Particularly for new or changing conditions, note how you are responding to treatment. If you have a follow-up with your regular GP, bring a summary of what the telehealth doctor recommended.

**Inform your regular GP:** For any significant consultation - a new medication, a new diagnosis, an assessment that revealed something of clinical significance - share the consultation summary with your regular GP so it is part of your ongoing medical record. Good telehealth services provide a consultation summary you can forward.

According to the RACGP Position Statement on Telehealth, continuity of care is best maintained when telehealth consultations are integrated with an ongoing patient-GP relationship rather than treated as standalone events. Your telehealth service fills a gap; your GP provides the longitudinal care context.

## Preparing a Child for a Telehealth Consultation

For consultations about a child, you will typically be the patient representative rather than the patient. Additional preparation:

- Know the child's weight (useful for dose calculations)
- Have immunisation records available if the issue relates to vaccination or infectious disease
- Note recent illnesses and any medications the child has taken
- Be ready to describe how the child's behaviour has changed - appetite, sleep, activity level, responsiveness - not just physical symptoms
- Have the Medicare card (or DVA card) ready

For children under approximately 12 years, a phone or video consultation where the doctor can briefly observe the child is generally preferable to a purely text-based submission. If your telehealth service offers a video option, use it for paediatric consultations.

## Accessibility Considerations

If you have accessibility needs that affect how you communicate during a telehealth consultation:

- Inform the service in advance if possible, or note it in the intake form
- Most services can accommodate phone-only consultations for those unable to use video
- For hearing impairment, asynchronous text-based services may be the most accessible mode
- If English is not your primary language, some services offer interpreter support or allow a family member to assist with translation (with your explicit consent)
