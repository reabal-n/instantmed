Medical certificate duration is not determined by a fixed formula. It is a clinical judgment made by the reviewing doctor based on the nature of the illness, typical recovery timeframes for that condition, the patient's described symptom severity, and any relevant occupational context.

This guide clarifies when certificates are required, what determines their duration, and how the rules on extensions and retrospective certification work under Australian law.

## When Is a Medical Certificate Required?

The legal framework for sick leave evidence is set by the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).

Under section 107(1), an employer may require an employee to provide "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of their entitlement to personal or carer's leave. Section 107(3) specifies that a medical certificate satisfies this requirement.

Critically, the Fair Work Act does not specify a minimum absence threshold for when evidence can be requested. Employers can, in principle, request evidence for a single-day absence. However, the obligation to request evidence must come from the applicable industrial instrument (award, enterprise agreement) or workplace policy - and most do not require certificates for every single-day absence.

Common arrangements in modern awards and workplaces:

- **Evidence required after 2 or more consecutive sick days:** The most common arrangement across awards and enterprise agreements
- **Evidence required from day 1:** Required in some awards covering healthcare, food handling, aged care, and safety-critical industries
- **Evidence for single days adjacent to public holidays:** Many workplaces specify this separately, even if routine single-day absences do not require evidence
- **Pattern-triggered requirements:** Employers may require certificates for all future absences if there is a documented pattern of frequent single-day absences

If you are uncertain what applies to your situation, your award or enterprise agreement will specify the evidence requirements. The Fair Work Ombudsman's Pay and Conditions Tool (fairwork.gov.au) can identify your applicable award.

<Callout variant="info">

The day adjacent to a public holiday is the most common single-day scenario where employers require a certificate regardless of normal policy. If you call in sick on the Friday before a long weekend or the Tuesday after Easter, expect to be asked for documentation. A telehealth certificate can be obtained the same day without leaving home.

</Callout>

## What Determines Certificate Duration

When a doctor certifies an absence, they assess:

**The nature of the condition:** Different illnesses have different typical recovery trajectories. [Gastroenteritis](/conditions/food-poisoning) typically resolves in 1-3 days; influenza may require 5-7 days; an acute back injury may require 3-10 days depending on severity. The doctor's clinical experience with the condition informs the appropriate duration.

**Symptom severity and functional impact:** A mild cold in someone with a desk job may warrant a single day; the same cold in a nurse or teacher in regular close contact with others warrants more careful consideration. A high fever with significant prostration warrants more days than low-grade malaise.

**The patient's job requirements:** Physically demanding work (construction, nursing, manufacturing) typically justifies longer absence for musculoskeletal injuries than office work. Food handlers with gastroenteritis cannot return until 48 hours after last symptoms regardless of how they feel - this is a food safety regulation.

**Trajectory:** Is the patient improving, stable, or worsening? A patient on day 2 of a respiratory infection who is improving might be certified for 2-3 days; one who is worsening might be certified for longer with a note to review.

| Condition | Typical certificate duration | Notes |

| Back pain (acute mechanical) | 2-5 days | Longer for severe or physically demanding roles |

These are general guides. The reviewing doctor's clinical judgment for a specific patient takes precedence over any generic table.

## Single-Day Certificates

Single-day absence certification is the most common use case for same-day telehealth medical certificates. You complete an intake form describing your symptoms, the doctor reviews, and the certificate covers the one day you are unable to work. The certificate is delivered as a PDF by email.

Conditions that commonly warrant a single-day certificate:
- Severe [migraine](/conditions/migraine)
- Gastroenteritis (single episode, resolving)
- Acute period pain preventing work
- First day of a significant [cold or flu](/conditions/cold-and-flu)
- Mental health day where functional capacity is significantly compromised
- Significant acute injury or pain

A single-day certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor via telehealth is legally equivalent to one from your regular GP. Employers cannot require that it come from an in-person consultation, per Fair Work Ombudsman guidance.

## Certificate Extensions

If you are still unwell when your initial certificate expires, you need an extension or new certificate.

How to request an extension via telehealth:

- Submit a new request explaining that you are still unwell following an initial certified absence
- Describe your current symptoms and how they have changed (or not) since the initial certificate
- Note the original certification period and what the initial condition was
- Indicate when you expect to recover

The reviewing doctor assesses whether continued absence is clinically appropriate given the described progression. For conditions that are following a normal recovery trajectory, extensions are routine. For conditions where recovery is significantly prolonged beyond expected timeframes, the doctor may recommend in-person assessment with your GP to investigate whether something else is happening.

For extended absences - more than one to two weeks - regular [GP management](/consult) is appropriate. A GP with your ongoing medical history can coordinate specialist input, manage more complex conditions, and issue longer-term certificates with appropriate clinical context.

## Retrospective Certification

Retrospective (backdated) certification refers to issuing a certificate for a period of illness that has already passed. This is standard clinical practice and is appropriate when:

- The patient was genuinely too ill to seek medical care at the time
- The illness being certified is consistent with the time elapsed and symptoms described
- The certification period is reasonably short relative to the consultation date (days to a few weeks, not months)

A doctor may retrospectively certify a Monday and Tuesday absence when consulted on Wednesday, if the described illness - a gastroenteritis that resolved by Wednesday morning, for example - is clinically consistent with that timeline.

What doctors will not certify retrospectively:
- Extended periods (months) without strong clinical justification
- Absences that do not match any plausible medical timeline
- Illness the patient has described in a way that is internally inconsistent or implausible

It is important to distinguish retrospective certification (appropriate) from backdating (inappropriate - altering dates on an existing certificate to make it appear earlier than it was). Creating or altering certificates to reflect false dates is fraud.

## Extended and Long-Term Absence

For absences exceeding one to two weeks, different considerations apply:

**Regular GP as primary manager:** Extended illness is best managed by a treating practitioner who knows your history, can monitor your progress, order investigations, coordinate specialist referrals, and document the clinical course over time.

**Return-to-work planning:** Extended absences require planning for return. This may include a graduated return (reduced hours or modified duties), workplace modifications, or a formal return-to-work plan under workers' compensation arrangements.

**Workers' compensation certificates:** If the absence relates to a workplace injury, the Certificate of Capacity system in your state or territory (different from a standard sick leave certificate) governs the absence and return-to-work process.

**Long service leave and extended personal leave:** For very long absences, the specific provisions of your employment contract, award, and state legislation become relevant. AHPRA-registered GPs can issue certificates for any period that is clinically appropriate.

<Callout variant="tip">

If you find yourself frequently needing medical certificates for the same condition, speaking with your regular GP about better management of that condition is worthwhile. Addressing underlying causes - whether a chronic condition, workplace factors, or a mental health concern - is more effective than a pattern of short absences.

</Callout>
