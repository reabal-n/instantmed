One of the most common questions about medical certificates is how many days they can cover - and a related question that comes up just as often: do you even need a certificate for a single day off? Both answers depend on your condition and the applicable rules for your workplace.

## Do You Need a Medical Certificate for One Day Off?

The short answer is: it depends on your workplace policy.

Under the National Employment Standards, employers can request "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of illness when an employee takes personal leave. The Fair Work Act does not set a minimum absence threshold for when this evidence can be requested. This means employers can, in principle, ask for a certificate for a single-day absence.

However, most workplaces do not require certificates for single-day absences as a matter of routine policy. Common arrangements include:

- **No certificate for 1-2 days**: most private sector employers only require evidence for absences of 2 or more consecutive days, unless the absence falls adjacent to a public holiday
- **Certificate from day 1**: some workplaces (and some awards) require evidence for any absence, including single days. Healthcare settings, food handling, and shift-based industries often have stricter requirements.
- **Pattern-triggered requirements**: even where single-day certificates are not routinely required, a pattern of Monday or Friday absences can trigger an employer requesting evidence for future single-day absences.

Check your employment contract, your applicable modern award or enterprise agreement, and your workplace's leave policy. If you are unsure, a quick message to your manager asking whether a certificate is needed is the easiest way to find out - and there is no obligation to go to a clinic to get one. [Telehealth medical certificates](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate online") are issued for single-day absences and are available on the same day without leaving home.

<Callout variant="info">Adjacent to a public holiday is the most common scenario where employers require a certificate for a single-day absence. If you call in sick on the Friday before a long weekend, many workplaces will request documentation regardless of their standard policy. Plan accordingly.</Callout>

## What Determines Certificate Duration?

When deciding how many days to certify, doctors consider:

- The nature of your illness or injury
- Typical recovery times for that condition
- Your job requirements and physical demands
- Your overall health and any complicating factors
- Whether you've already been unwell for some time

## Typical Durations for Common Conditions

While every case is individual, here are typical ranges:

- Cold and flu: 1-5 days
- Gastroenteritis: 1-3 days (48 hours symptom-free for food handlers)
- Migraine: 1-2 days per episode
- Back pain (acute): 2-7 days
- Minor surgery: 3-14 days depending on procedure
- Mental health: 1-5 days initially, with review
- COVID-19: 5-7 days typically

<Callout variant="info">

These are general guides only. Your certificate may be shorter or longer based on your specific situation. The doctor will recommend what's clinically appropriate.

</Callout>

## Can Certificates Be Backdated?

Doctors can certify that you were unwell in the recent past if it's clinically reasonable. For example, if you were too sick to seek medical attention on Monday but see a doctor on Wednesday, they may backdate to cover Monday and Tuesday.

However, there are limits. Doctors generally won't:

- Backdate certificates for extended periods (weeks or months)
- Certify illness without reasonable evidence you were unwell
- Provide certificates for pre-planned absences disguised as illness
- Cover dates far in the past without strong clinical reasons

## What If You Need More Time?

If your initial certificate is expiring but you're still unwell, you can request an extension. The doctor will assess whether continued time off is appropriate.

For extension certificates, it helps to explain:

- Your current symptoms and how they're affecting your ability to work
- Whether you've improved, stayed the same, or worsened
- Any treatments you've tried
- Your original condition and when it started

## Workplace Requirements

Your employer's policies may influence certificate needs:

- Many workplaces only require certificates for absences over 2 days
- Some require certificates from day 1 - check your policy
- Awards or enterprise agreements may have specific requirements
- Excessive absences may trigger a requirement for certificates

<Callout variant="tip">

If you're frequently needing medical certificates, it may be worth speaking with your GP about underlying conditions that could be better managed, or workplace adjustments that might help.

</Callout>

## Extended Absences

For absences longer than 1-2 weeks, a single telehealth certificate may not be appropriate. Extended absences usually need:

- Assessment by your regular GP who knows your history
- Possible specialist input depending on the condition
- Regular reviews and updated certificates
- A graduated return-to-work plan in many cases

## Getting a Single-Day Certificate Without Leaving Home

If you are genuinely unwell for one day and your employer requires evidence, getting a certificate via telehealth is the practical solution. You complete an online form describing your symptoms, an AHPRA-registered doctor assesses your case, and the certificate is emailed as a PDF - typically within an hour. You do not need to drive to a clinic or sit in a waiting room on a day when you are too unwell to work.

Single-day certificates are a standard and common output of telehealth medical services. The process is the same as for longer certificates, and the resulting document is legally equivalent to one issued after an in-person consultation.

For [medical certificate backdating](/blog/medical-certificate-backdating "Medical certificate backdating rules"), where you were unwell recently but did not seek care at the time, the same telehealth assessment process applies - with the doctor making a clinical judgment about whether remote certification of a recent illness is appropriate.
