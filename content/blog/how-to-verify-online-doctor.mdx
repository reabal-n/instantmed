Online healthcare can be legitimate, useful, and safe. It can also be easy to imitate badly. A polished website, quick checkout, or professional-looking PDF does not prove that a real registered practitioner assessed you.

The safest approach is to verify four things: the practitioner, the service, the clinical process, and the document or prescription outcome.

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- Start with Ahpra's public Register of practitioners, not screenshots, badges, or old PDFs.
- Match the practitioner's registered name, profession, registration status, and any published limits.
- Registration is necessary, but it does not prove a specific telehealth decision was appropriate.
- A legitimate service should explain clinical review, privacy, fees, complaints, and when telehealth is not suitable.
- Be cautious with hidden clinicians, guaranteed outcomes, weak documents, and questionnaire-only prescribing that skips meaningful clinical review.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Start with the official register

Ahpra keeps the public Register of practitioners for registered health practitioners in Australia. For an online doctor, you are usually checking whether the person is registered under medical practice.

The register can show:

- the practitioner's registered name
- profession
- registration type
- registration status
- registration number
- any published conditions, undertakings, notations, cautions, reprimands, or other limits
- public statements or warnings where applicable

<EvidenceNote title="Best starting point" source="Ahpra">
Ahpra says that when a health practitioner's name appears on the Register of practitioners, you know they are allowed to practise. The register can also publish types of registration or conditions that limit what a practitioner can do.
</EvidenceNote>

Use the official register directly. A logo, testimonial, downloaded certificate, screenshot, or line on a website can be stale or altered.

## How to search the register

Use a simple process.

1. Go to the Ahpra Register of practitioners.
2. Search by the practitioner's registered name or registration number.
3. Select the relevant profession, such as medical practitioner.
4. Match the result against the name shown by the service or document.
5. Check registration status and registration type.
6. Read any published conditions, undertakings, notations, cautions, or reprimands.
7. If the name does not match, ask the service for the practitioner's registered name and number.

Names can be tricky. A practitioner may use a shortened name, middle name, married name, or a different public-facing name. Do not assume fraud from one failed search, but do not ignore a mismatch either.

## What registration proves and does not prove

Registration is a threshold check. It is not the whole safety check.

| Register result | What it tells you | What it does not prove |
|---|---|---|
| Registered medical practitioner | The person is registered to practise medicine within the published scope | That this specific online consultation was clinically appropriate |
| Specialist registration | The person has specialist registration in a listed specialty | That specialist care was required or provided for your request |
| Conditions or undertakings | There may be published limits or requirements on practice | That the practitioner is unsafe in every setting |
| No matching result | Your search did not find a matching registered practitioner | That fraud is proven; spelling, name use, or profession mismatch may need checking |
| Old certificate or screenshot | Someone once showed a registration-related document | Current registration status or current limits |

The practical standard is: register first, then check the process.

## Check the service, not just the doctor

A registered practitioner can still work inside a poor process. A safe telehealth service should make the healthcare pathway understandable.

Look for:

- a real business name, ABN, contact pathway, privacy policy, terms, and complaints process
- clear pricing before payment
- a description of who provides clinical care
- meaningful clinical questions before any outcome
- a way for the clinician to ask follow-up questions
- a willingness to decline or redirect care
- secure handling of forms, images, prescriptions, messages, and records
- plain information about refunds, declines, or next steps

Weak signals include vague phrases like "doctor approved" or "medical team reviewed" without explaining who assessed the request, what profession they belong to, and how follow-up works.

## Telehealth standards still apply

The Medical Board of Australia says telehealth is not suitable for every consultation and should not be treated as a routine substitute for in-person care. It expects telehealth care to be safe and, as far as possible, meet the same standard as in-person care.

Important telehealth expectations include:

- the practitioner should identify themselves
- the patient should be identified to the best of the practitioner's ability
- the patient should understand the process and consent
- privacy and confidentiality should be protected
- records, prescriptions, referrals, investigation requests, and images should be handled securely
- the practitioner should continuously assess whether telehealth remains appropriate
- in-person care should be arranged when remote care is not enough

<PolicyNote title="Registration is not the whole answer" source="Medical Board of Australia">
The Medical Board's telehealth guidance focuses on safe professional practice, not just whether the practitioner is registered. Identity, consent, privacy, records, clinical suitability, and escalation all matter.
</PolicyNote>

## Check documents like evidence, not receipts

If you receive a certificate, prescription-related document, referral, or letter, check it as evidence of care rather than proof by itself.

A credible clinical document should usually make clear:

- who issued it
- the practitioner's role and registration details where appropriate
- the patient name
- the date of consultation or issue
- the relevant date or period covered
- the purpose of the document
- contact or practice details
- signature or digital signing method

For routine sick leave certificates, diagnosis is often not needed and may be privacy-sensitive. What matters is whether the document supports the stated purpose and can be tied to a credible assessment.

Red flags include:

- no identifiable practitioner
- no issue date or impossible dates
- no service contact details
- claims that every request is approved
- a document issued instantly with no meaningful assessment
- visible alteration or inconsistent formatting
- a service that refuses to identify who made the clinical decision

## Prescribing red flags

Prescribing raises the risk level because the doctor must consider diagnosis, indication, contraindications, interactions, monitoring, pregnancy or breastfeeding where relevant, allergies, medicine scheduling, state and territory rules, and whether in-person care is needed.

Be cautious if a service:

- promises a prescription before assessment
- lets patients choose restricted medicines from a menu without clinical review
- avoids asking about medicines, allergies, pregnancy, medical history, or monitoring
- does not explain when prescribing will be declined
- hides the prescriber
- uses questionnaire-only prescribing for decisions that need real-time contact
- ignores controlled-medicine, monitoring, or jurisdiction requirements

The safe question is not "can this happen online?" It is "can this be assessed safely through this specific online process?"

<CareBoundary title="Do not use verification to delay urgent care">
Checking a doctor's registration is useful for routine telehealth decisions. It is not a triage tool for emergencies. Chest pain, severe breathing trouble, stroke signs, collapse, severe allergic reaction, severe bleeding, or rapidly worsening symptoms should go through urgent care first.
</CareBoundary>

## Privacy and data handling checks

Online health services handle sensitive health information. Before submitting private details, look for a privacy policy that explains health information, not only marketing data.

Check whether the service explains:

- how forms, images, messages, prescriptions, documents, and payments are handled
- who can access records
- whether third parties or overseas providers are involved
- how to contact the service about privacy concerns
- what happens if you need a correction or copy of your information
- whether uploads happen through a secure channel

Avoid sending medical images, certificates, prescriptions, or identity details through public comments, ordinary social media messages, workplace accounts, or unsecured channels when a safer option exists.

## When a hidden doctor is a warning sign

Some services allocate a clinician after intake. That can be normal. The warning sign is not "the doctor was not shown in the hero section." The warning sign is that no accountable practitioner is ever disclosed.

A service should be able to explain:

- who reviews the request
- what profession they belong to
- how you can identify the practitioner after the consultation where relevant
- how follow-up questions are handled
- how to raise a complaint
- how documents or prescriptions show practitioner accountability

If the service will not answer those questions, slow down before using the outcome.

## Where to report concerns

Different concerns go to different bodies. Keep screenshots, receipts, URLs, practitioner names, registration numbers, document copies, and message history where safe to do so.

| Concern | Possible pathway |
|---|---|
| Registered practitioner conduct | Ahpra or the relevant National Board |
| Health service complaint | State or territory health complaints organisation |
| Scam or suspicious contact | Scamwatch |
| Therapeutic goods or medicine advertising concern | TGA advertising report portal |
| Privacy concern | OAIC or the service's privacy contact |
| Immediate danger | Emergency services |

You do not need to prove the whole case before asking the relevant body for guidance. Provide the facts you have and avoid editing screenshots or documents.

## Where verification helps

<DecisionBox title="Where verification helps">
### May fit telehealth
- The practitioner is identifiable and registered, the service has a clear clinical process, the issue is suitable for remote review, and the document or prescription pathway is accountable.
- The service can ask follow-up questions and can redirect when the request is not suitable.
### Needs in-person care
- The issue needs examination, vital signs, pathology, imaging, procedures, continuity from a treating clinician, or a document standard remote review cannot support.
- The practitioner or service cannot provide enough identity, clinical, privacy, or complaints information for you to rely on the outcome.
### Urgent care
- Severe or rapidly worsening symptoms should bypass routine telehealth and verification checks.
- Call 000 or use local urgent care for emergency symptoms.
</DecisionBox>

## Quick verification checklist

Before relying on an online doctor service, ask:

1. Can I identify the practitioner where relevant?
2. Can I find the practitioner on Ahpra's public register?
3. Is the profession correct for the care being provided?
4. Are any published registration limits relevant?
5. Does the service explain how assessment works?
6. Can the clinician ask follow-up questions or redirect care?
7. Are fees clear before payment?
8. Is privacy explained properly?
9. Is there a complaints pathway?
10. Does the document contain enough detail to verify it?

If several answers are unclear, pause before submitting health information or relying on the document.
