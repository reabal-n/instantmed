Not having a Medicare card does not prevent you from accessing medical care in Australia, and it has no effect on the legal validity of a medical certificate. A certificate is valid because it is issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor - Medicare status is irrelevant to that equation. This guide covers who does not have Medicare, the options available for getting a certificate, what your health insurance may cover, and what employers can and cannot ask.

## Who Does Not Have Medicare in Australia

Medicare is available to Australian citizens, permanent residents, and certain temporary visa holders. A significant portion of people living and working in Australia at any given time are not Medicare eligible:

**International students (subclass 500)**: Most international students are not Medicare eligible. Exceptions include students from countries with bilateral health agreements (New Zealand, some EU countries, the UK).

**Working holiday makers (subclass 417, 462)**: Not Medicare eligible.

**Temporary Skill Shortage visa holders (subclass 482)**: Generally not Medicare eligible, though some employer-sponsored arrangements may include private health provisions.

**Graduate visa holders (subclass 485)**: Not Medicare eligible unless from a reciprocal agreement country.

**Visitor visas (subclass 600, eVisitors, ETAs)**: Not Medicare eligible except for visitors from reciprocal agreement countries.

**New migrants in the processing window**: Permanent residents are immediately Medicare eligible on arrival, but the card takes 1-4 weeks to arrive. Most GP practices will bulk-bill once you have a Medicare number, even before the physical card.

**People who have not yet enrolled**: Medicare is not automatic - you must enrol. Some people who are eligible have not yet enrolled, which creates a temporary gap.

## Your Medical Certificate Is Still Valid

The most important point: Medicare status does not affect a medical certificate's legal validity. The certificate is valid because it was issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor. That registration is what matters under the Fair Work Act, not whether you are enrolled in Medicare.

Your employer cannot:

- Reject a certificate because you do not have Medicare
- Ask whether you have Medicare
- Require you to see a bulk-billing GP as a condition of accepting your certificate

The certificate does not mention your Medicare status. It contains your name, the doctor's AHPRA details, the consultation date, and the unfitness period. Medicare is not referenced anywhere on a standard medical certificate.

## Options for Getting a Medical Certificate

### Private Telehealth Services

Many telehealth services operate as private providers - they charge a flat fee and do not require Medicare. You pay the same fee as everyone else, and the certificate you receive is identical in legal standing to one obtained by a Medicare patient.

This is often the most convenient option if you do not have Medicare:

- Flat fee regardless of Medicare status
- No need to find a clinic that accepts non-Medicare patients
- Certificate delivered digitally and can be emailed to an employer immediately
- Available 7 days a week including evenings

### Private GP Clinics

Any GP clinic can see you as a private patient. The cost varies: a standard consultation is typically $80 to $120, with higher fees at some practices. You pay upfront and, if you have private health insurance, may be able to claim some of this back.

Not all clinics prominently advertise their private fees. Ask when you book: "Do you see patients without Medicare, and what is the consultation fee?"

### University Health Services

If you are an international student, your university almost certainly has a campus health service that is accustomed to seeing international students. Many campus health services have reduced fees or direct billing arrangements with OSHC providers.

Your student services office can direct you to the campus health centre. Some universities have the health centre integrated with their OSHC provider, simplifying the billing process.

## Health Insurance Without Medicare

### Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC)

OSHC is mandatory for most international students. It is a condition of your student visa. Your OSHC policy covers:

- GP visits (you may pay upfront and claim back, or some providers have direct billing)
- Specialist consultations with a referral
- Hospital treatment in public hospitals
- Some emergency ambulance
- Limited pharmacy coverage for prescribed medications

OSHC does not provide PBS-equivalent drug subsidies. You pay the private price for prescription medications.

To claim: keep your receipt and submit a claim through your OSHC provider's app or portal. Most claims are processed within 2-5 business days. Some providers have a card you can use for direct billing at participating practices - ask your OSHC provider for a list.

### Overseas Visitors Health Cover (OVHC)

For working visa holders (482, 485, 417, 462), some employers or visa sponsors require OVHC. This covers:

- Hospital treatment
- Some GP visits (depends on the policy level)
- Emergency ambulance in most states

OVHC policies vary significantly in their GP coverage. Basic OVHC often covers only hospital treatment. Higher tiers include GP visits. Check your policy documents to understand what GP consultations cost after your policy applies.

### Travel Insurance

Visitors with standard travel insurance may have GP visit coverage, though most travel insurance is designed for unexpected illness rather than routine care. Check your policy for:

- Coverage of GP visits vs accident-only policies
- Whether telehealth is covered
- The claims process (pay upfront and claim back, or direct billing)
- The excess or deductible amount per claim

## Reciprocal Healthcare Agreements

Australia has bilateral health agreements with certain countries that give their citizens access to Medicare-equivalent services during their visit. This is not the same as full Medicare eligibility, but it covers medically necessary treatment.

Countries with reciprocal agreements currently include:

- United Kingdom and Ireland
- New Zealand
- Belgium, Finland, Italy, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Slovenia, and Sweden

If you are from one of these countries, you can enrol in Medicare on arrival for the duration of your stay. Take your passport and visa to a Services Australia centre. The agreement covers medically necessary treatment - not necessarily cosmetic or elective care.

Coverage varies slightly by country. The Services Australia website has country-specific information. In practice, this means UK and Irish visitors can often access bulk-billing services the same as Australian citizens.

## Prescription Medications Without Medicare

The PBS subsidy is tied to Medicare eligibility. Without Medicare, you pay the full private price for prescription medications. The difference can be significant:

- A PBS-listed antibiotic that costs $15 with Medicare may cost $40-50 privately
- Blood pressure medications subsidised at $31.60 per month may cost $80-150 privately
- Chronic disease medications can be substantially more expensive without PBS

If you are in Australia long-term and taking regular medications, this cost difference adds up. Options for reducing medication costs:

- Generic medications (bioequivalent but priced by the manufacturer, not the government) - ask your pharmacist
- Comparison shopping between pharmacies - private prices vary
- If you are eligible for Medicare but have not enrolled, enrolling is straightforward and free

## Getting Medicare if You Are Eligible

If you are an Australian citizen, permanent resident, or holder of a visa that confers Medicare eligibility and you have not yet enrolled:

1. Visit a Services Australia centre in person (bring your visa, passport, and any Medicare eligibility evidence)
2. Complete the enrollment form
3. Receive an interim Medicare number immediately - the physical card arrives within 2-3 weeks

For new permanent residents, the Medicare eligibility starts from the date of your permanent visa grant, not the date you enrol. If you incurred medical costs between your visa grant and enrollment, you may be able to claim retrospectively.

## What to Tell Your Employer

If you are providing a medical certificate and do not have Medicare, there is nothing you need to disclose. Your employer does not need to know your Medicare status, and as noted above, the certificate does not reflect it.

If your employer raises questions about your Medicare status in the context of accepting your certificate, the correct response is that Medicare status is irrelevant to the certificate's validity, which depends only on the issuing doctor's AHPRA registration.
