Australians use a half-dozen different terms for the same document. "Doctor's note," "sick note," "med cert," "doctor's certificate," "sick certificate" - they all mean a formal statement from a registered health practitioner confirming you are unfit for work. Here is what you actually need to know: what the document must contain, who can issue it, and when it is and is not required.

## The Terminology: All the Same Document

In Australian workplaces, the terms are used interchangeably:

- **Doctor's note**: Informal, most commonly used in everyday speech
- **Sick note**: Also informal, common in conversation
- **Medical certificate**: The formal term, used in the Fair Work Act and employment contracts
- **Doctor's certificate**: Slight variant, means the same
- **Med cert**: Common abbreviation in HR and healthcare
- **Fit note**: A UK term that has not caught on in Australia. If someone uses it, they mean a medical certificate
- **Statutory declaration**: This IS different - a sworn written statement, not a doctor's assessment. Used when a certificate was not reasonably obtainable

When your employer asks for a "doctor's note," they want a medical certificate. When your Fair Work award refers to "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person," a standard medical certificate is the default.

## What Australian Law Actually Requires

The Fair Work Act 2009 does not require a "medical certificate" specifically. Section 107 states that an employer can request "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of the need for leave. In practice, this means:

- A medical certificate from an AHPRA-registered practitioner (the standard)
- A statutory declaration (an acceptable alternative in many circumstances)
- Other evidence that is genuinely convincing (rare - typically for extraordinary circumstances)

The threshold for evidence depends on your specific award, enterprise agreement, or workplace policy. Many awards set the threshold at two or more consecutive days of absence. Some industries require evidence from day one.

<Callout variant="info">The evidence requirement is symmetrical - it protects both parties. Your employer has a legitimate interest in confirming genuine illness. You have the right to keep your diagnosis private. A certificate stating "unfit for work due to a medical condition" satisfies both sides of this.</Callout>

## What Must Be on the Certificate

A valid medical certificate for Australian workplace purposes must contain:

- Your **full legal name**
- The **date of the consultation**
- A statement that you were **unfit for work** (or unfit for your usual duties)
- The **dates covered** - the period you are certified as unfit
- The **practitioner's name**, AHPRA registration number, signature, and contact details
- Usually the practice name and address

It does **not** need to contain:
- Your diagnosis
- The name of your illness
- Details of any medication or treatment
- Any clinical information beyond the unfitness statement

If a certificate arrives missing one of the required fields (especially the AHPRA registration number), ask the issuing doctor to provide an updated version. Missing the registration number is the most common reason an employer might legitimately question a certificate's validity.

## Who Can Issue a Medical Certificate

For workplace purposes, a certificate must come from an AHPRA-registered health practitioner. This includes:

**Medical doctors (GPs and specialists)**: The most common source. Any doctor registered with AHPRA under the Medical Board of Australia can issue a certificate.

**Nurse practitioners**: Registered nurse practitioners hold AHPRA registration and are legally authorised to issue valid medical certificates. Nurse practitioners are available at many bulk-billing clinics and via telehealth services.

**Dentists**: For dental conditions that prevent you from working - wisdom tooth surgery, post-extraction recovery, dental abscess - your dentist can issue a certificate covering the appropriate period.

**Optometrists**: For eye conditions or injuries causing incapacity, an optometrist's certification may be accepted.

**Psychologists and other AHPRA practitioners**: For mental health conditions, a psychologist's documentation may accompany or supplement a GP's certificate. A psychologist's letter confirming treatment and impact on work capacity can support an extended absence application.

Pharmacists, physiotherapists (in most circumstances), naturopaths, and other non-AHPRA or limited-scope practitioners cannot issue standard medical certificates for work absence purposes.

## Online vs Paper Certificates

There is no legal requirement that a medical certificate be on paper. Electronic certificates issued by AHPRA-registered doctors via telehealth services are legally equivalent to paper ones. An employer cannot refuse a PDF certificate on the basis that it is digital.

What matters is:
- The issuing practitioner is AHPRA-registered (verifiable at ahpra.gov.au)
- The certificate contains the required information
- The certificate appears genuine (not altered, not from a suspicious source)

If your employer requires a paper copy, you can print a PDF certificate. If they require an original with a wet signature, you can ask your telehealth doctor to post one, though this is increasingly rare.

## Uses Beyond Work: Different Purposes, Different Requirements

A standard medical certificate covers sick leave at work. Other purposes may require different documentation:

**School absence**: A standard certificate (stating the child was unwell and the dates) is usually sufficient for school attendance records. State education authorities generally require a certificate for absences of 3 or more consecutive days.

**Centrelink**: For short-term incapacity affecting mutual obligations (JobSeeker, Youth Allowance), a standard certificate works. For formal exemptions, Centrelink may require their SU415 form. For Disability Support Pension, comprehensive reports from treating specialists are needed - a standard certificate is not sufficient.

**Courts (jury duty, court appearances)**: Courts accept standard certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors for jury duty exemptions or adjournment requests. The certificate should state you are unfit for jury service or attendance, with expected duration.

**Insurance claims**: Income protection and TPD claims typically require more detailed medical evidence than a standard sick leave certificate. Your insurer will specify what is needed - usually a treating doctor's report, medical records, and sometimes specialist assessment.

**Workers' compensation**: WorkCover uses jurisdiction-specific forms (Certificate of Capacity in NSW, Work Capacity Certificate in other states). A standard medical certificate is not sufficient for a workers' compensation claim.

**Travel insurance claims**: Usually requires a certificate confirming the medical reason for trip cancellation or curtailment. The certificate should state the condition and that travel was medically inadvisable.

**University extensions and special consideration**: Universities typically want a certificate covering the assessment dates, stating you were unfit for examination or study. Some universities have their own forms or specific wording requirements.

## Telehealth Certificates: Same Legal Standing

A medical certificate issued via telehealth by an AHPRA-registered doctor has exactly the same legal standing as one issued after an in-person consultation. There is no legal hierarchy of certificate validity based on how the consultation occurred.

The Fair Work Act refers to evidence from a "recognised medical practitioner." An AHPRA-registered doctor practicing via telehealth is a recognised medical practitioner. The delivery mode is not material to the certificate's validity.

If an employer refuses to accept a telehealth certificate, they are acting outside the law. The Fair Work Ombudsman has confirmed that telehealth consultations constitute legitimate medical consultations for the purposes of sick leave evidence.

## Statutory Declarations: The Alternative When You Couldn't See a Doctor

Under the Fair Work Act, a statutory declaration is acceptable evidence when it was not reasonably practicable to obtain a medical certificate. A stat dec is a formal written statement sworn before an authorised witness, declaring that you were unwell during the relevant period.

When this is appropriate:
- Brief illness (1-2 days) that resolved without a doctor visit
- Circumstances where accessing a doctor was genuinely not feasible
- Your workplace policy explicitly accepts stat decs

A stat dec is **not** appropriate:
- For extended absences where medical assessment would be expected
- As a routine substitute when medical care was available and accessible
- If your employer's policy specifically requires a certificate

Stat decs can be witnessed for free by a Justice of the Peace (available at many courthouses, council offices, and libraries), a pharmacist, a police officer, or other listed authorised witnesses. JP finder services are available through each state government website.

## When Your Employer Asks for More Than the Certificate Provides

Some employers ask for information beyond what a certificate contains - the specific illness, whether you saw a GP or specialist, or whether you are on medication. These requests exceed what the law allows:

- Your employer is not entitled to your diagnosis
- They cannot require you to provide treatment details
- They cannot ask which type of doctor you saw
- They cannot demand to speak with your doctor directly

If an employer is pressuring you for clinical information beyond what the certificate provides, you can politely decline and note that the certificate satisfies the legal evidence requirement. If the pressure continues, the Fair Work Ombudsman (fairwork.gov.au or 13 13 94) provides free advice.
