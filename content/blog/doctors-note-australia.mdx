In Australia, "doctor's note" is informal wording. For work leave, the practical test is usually whether the evidence is credible, relevant to the absence, and reasonable under the workplace policy, award, agreement, or process asking for it.

</Callout>

If you are searching for a doctor's note in Australia, you are probably dealing with one of three things: sick leave, carer's leave, or an organisation asking for proof that you could not attend something.

The wording gets messy. Employers say "doctor's note". Employees say "sick note". Clinics say "medical certificate". Pharmacies may say "absence-from-work certificate". Government and legal forms may refer to a statutory declaration.

Those terms overlap, but they are not identical. Knowing the difference helps you avoid two common mistakes: giving a document that does not match the purpose, or sharing more health information than the organisation actually needs.

## Short Answer

A doctor's note is usually an informal name for a medical certificate or doctor's certificate.

For ordinary workplace sick leave, the evidence usually needs to show that you were not fit for work, or that you needed to care for an immediate family or household member, for the relevant dates. It usually does not need to list your diagnosis.

Fair Work says employers can ask for evidence even for one day or less off work. Medical certificates and statutory declarations are examples of evidence, but there are no strict national rules saying every workplace must accept the same document in the same way. The evidence needs to satisfy a reasonable person, and the type of evidence requested must be reasonable in the circumstances.

That is why a one-day cold, a migraine, a longer absence, a workplace injury, an exam deferral, and an insurance claim can all need different paperwork.

## What People Mean By "Doctor's Note"

"Doctor's note" is not usually the formal Australian term. It is everyday language.

In practice, a person asking for a doctor's note may mean:

- A medical certificate for sick leave.
- A doctor's certificate confirming work capacity.
- A short letter from a doctor explaining restrictions or modified duties.
- Evidence that a child, partner, parent, or household member needed care.
- A document for study, travel, sport, court, insurance, or another administrative process.

Those uses are not interchangeable. A standard sick leave certificate may be enough for a routine absence from work. It may not be enough for workers' compensation, Centrelink forms, university special consideration, an insurance claim, or a return-to-work clearance after a safety-sensitive illness or injury.

When the request is unclear, ask for the exact requirement before chasing a document. The useful question is not "do you need a note?" It is "what evidence do you need, from whom, covering which dates, and for what purpose?"

## Doctor's Note, Sick Note, Medical Certificate, And Stat Dec

The names point to different levels of evidence.

A doctor's note is informal. It usually means evidence from a doctor that you were unfit for work, study, or another duty for a period.

A sick note is also informal. It usually means evidence for illness-related absence. It may come from a doctor, another registered health practitioner, a pharmacist in limited circumstances, or sometimes from your own statutory declaration depending on the policy.

A medical certificate is the more formal clinical document. It is issued by a registered health practitioner after an assessment and usually records the patient, assessment date, period covered, and work capacity.

An absence-from-work certificate is often the wording used for pharmacist-issued evidence. Pharmacists are registered health practitioners, but they are not medical practitioners. Professional pharmacy guidance treats these certificates as work absence evidence for personal or carer's leave within a pharmacist's scope, not as full medical certificates.

A statutory declaration, or stat dec, is your legal statement that certain facts are true. It is not a health assessment. It can be useful when a clinical certificate was not practical or when the leave policy allows it, but it does not replace clinical judgement.

## What Fair Work Says About Evidence

Fair Work's workplace leave guidance is built around notice and evidence.

An employer can ask for evidence that shows the employee took sick or carer's leave because they were not able to work due to illness or injury, or because they needed to care for or support an immediate family or household member affected by illness, injury, or an unexpected emergency.

Two details matter:

- Evidence can be requested for as little as one day or less off work.
- The evidence has to satisfy a reasonable person that the employee was genuinely entitled to the leave.

Fair Work gives medical certificates and statutory declarations as examples. It also says awards, registered agreements, contracts, and workplace policies can specify when evidence must be provided and what process applies.

This is why there is no single national script that answers every doctor's note dispute. The Fair Work baseline matters, but the workplace instrument and the facts also matter.

## Who Can Issue Work Absence Evidence?

The right issuer depends on the document and purpose.

### Doctors

GPs and specialists can issue medical certificates when they have assessed the person and formed a view about work capacity, dates, and any relevant restrictions.

For routine sick leave, a doctor's certificate usually focuses on whether the person was unfit for work or usual duties. It should not expose private clinical detail that is not needed for the purpose.

### Other registered health practitioners

Some AHPRA-registered practitioners can provide evidence within their professional scope. This may include dentists for dental issues, psychologists for mental health care, optometrists for eye problems, physiotherapists for certain injuries, nurse practitioners, and other registered practitioners.

Scope matters. Evidence from the right practitioner for the right problem is stronger than a generic letter from someone who cannot properly assess the issue.

### Pharmacists

Pharmacists may issue absence-from-work certificates in limited circumstances, usually for minor illness or carer's leave within their scope and professional guidance.

That does not make a pharmacist certificate identical to a doctor's medical certificate. Pharmacy guidance says patients should understand that a pharmacist is not a medical practitioner, cannot provide a medical certificate, and that a pharmacist certificate is generally for personal or carer's leave evidence rather than other purposes.

For a short, minor illness, a pharmacist certificate may be enough. For a long absence, complex illness, work injury, pregnancy-related request, return-to-work clearance, insurance claim, or disputed matter, a doctor or another suitable practitioner may be more appropriate.

### You, Through A Statutory Declaration

A statutory declaration is your statement of facts. It can say, for example, that you were unwell on a date, could not work, and could not reasonably get a medical appointment.

It is not a diagnosis. It does not prove clinical incapacity in the same way a practitioner assessment does. It can still be relevant because Fair Work lists statutory declarations as an example of evidence for sick or carer's leave.

## What A Good Doctor's Note Usually Includes

A useful work absence certificate is specific without being intrusive.

It usually includes:

- Your full name.
- Date of assessment.
- Date the document was issued.
- Dates or period covered.
- Whether you were unfit for work, unfit for usual duties, or fit for modified duties.
- Practitioner name and profession.
- Practitioner registration or identifying details where relevant.
- Practice or service contact details.
- Signature or secure digital equivalent.
- A verification pathway if the service provides one.

The document should make the practical point easy to understand: who was assessed, when they were assessed, what period is covered, and what work-capacity conclusion was reached.

If any of those details are missing, the document is easier to question.

## What It Usually Should Not Include

For ordinary sick leave, an employer usually needs work-capacity information, not your private diagnosis.

A routine certificate generally does not need:

- The specific illness.
- Medication list.
- Full clinical history.
- Counselling or mental health details.
- Test results.
- Private family details.
- Unrelated health conditions.

Fair Work also says it does not consider it reasonable for an employer to go to a medical appointment with an employee unless the employee requests this, or to contact the employee's doctor for further information.

There are exceptions where more detail may be requested through a different process: workers' compensation, insurance, long absence management, safety-sensitive roles, independent medical examination requests, return-to-work planning, government forms, and legal processes. Those are not ordinary sick leave paperwork.

## Can A Doctor's Note Be Issued Online?

Yes, in the right circumstances. The consultation method is not the whole issue.

An online doctor's note or online medical certificate is stronger when:

- The practitioner is registered and identifiable.
- The assessment is genuine.
- The patient identity and history are clear enough.
- The certificate dates match the information provided.
- The issue is suitable for remote assessment.
- The document has clear work-capacity wording.
- The certificate can be verified without revealing private medical details.

An online certificate is weaker when the service hides who issued it, promises approval before assessment, lacks dates or practitioner details, or appears to create documents without real clinical review.

Some absences need in-person care first. Severe symptoms, injuries needing examination, workplace injuries, safety-critical return-to-work issues, and high-stakes legal or compensation matters can require a different pathway.

## Can An Employer Check A Doctor's Note?

An employer can take reasonable steps to satisfy themselves that evidence is genuine. That does not mean they are entitled to your diagnosis.

Common checks include:

- Checking that the certificate has the employee name and relevant dates.
- Confirming the issuing practice or service exists.
- Asking the issuer to confirm whether the document was issued, without clinical details.
- Checking a practitioner's public registration on the Ahpra register.
- Using a certificate reference number or verification portal if one exists.

Ahpra's public Register of practitioners lists registered health practitioners in Australia, including medical practitioners, pharmacists, nurses, dentists, optometrists, psychologists, physiotherapists, and others. The register can also show limits or conditions on registration.

If a workplace wants clinical details, ask what policy or process requires them. For ordinary sick leave, the answer is often that capacity and dates are enough.

## How Many Days Can A Doctor's Note Cover?

There is no universal number of days that a medical certificate can cover. The period is a clinical judgement.

For routine illnesses, certificates often cover one to several days. For longer illnesses, surgery recovery, mental health, injury, or chronic conditions, the practitioner may certify a longer period or provide a review date.

Three points are worth knowing:

- One-day certificates are normal. Fair Work says evidence can be requested for one day or less.
- Longer certificates need a stronger clinical basis. The longer the period, the more important assessment quality and follow-up become.
- Backdating is limited. A practitioner may sometimes certify a past period if they are satisfied you were unfit at the time, but this depends on the facts and the clinical assessment. Our guide on [backdating a medical certificate](/blog/medical-certificate-backdating "When a certificate can cover a past absence") covers that in detail.

If you are still unwell when a certificate expires, a follow-up review is often cleaner than trying to stretch one document too far.

## When A Stat Dec May Be Enough

A statutory declaration can be useful when:

- The absence was short.
- You could not reasonably get a medical appointment.
- The issue had resolved by the time you returned to work.
- The policy allows a stat dec as evidence.
- The leave reason is straightforward and not disputed.

It may be less suitable when:

- The absence was prolonged.
- You need clinical restrictions or modified duties.
- There is a workplace injury.
- Pay, discipline, insurance, compensation, or legal consequences are involved.
- A specific form requires a treating practitioner.

A stat dec should be accurate and specific. It is a legal document, so guessing, exaggerating, or copying generic wording is a bad idea.

## Different Purpose, Different Document

Do not assume one doctor's note works everywhere.

For ordinary sick leave, a medical certificate, pharmacist absence certificate, or statutory declaration may be relevant depending on policy and circumstances.

For carer's leave, the evidence should support the need to provide care or support to an immediate family or household member.

For university extensions, the institution may need dates and study or exam capacity, and may have its own form.

For workers' compensation, the required document is often a jurisdiction-specific certificate of capacity or work capacity form.

For insurance, the insurer may need a treating practitioner report and records.

For Centrelink or government processes, a specific form may be required.

For court, tribunal, jury, or official summons issues, the authority may have its own evidence threshold.

If the process is higher-stakes than a routine sick day, check the exact form first.

## Common Reasons A Doctor's Note Is Questioned

Most disputes are about document quality, policy fit, or authenticity, not whether the person was actually unwell.

Common triggers include:

- Missing dates.
- Name mismatch.
- No clear statement about fitness for work.
- No identifiable practitioner or issuing service.
- A certificate issued for a period that does not match the absence.
- A pharmacist certificate used for a purpose outside its scope.
- A stat dec used where the policy asks for a practitioner certificate.
- A certificate that appears to be bought without assessment.
- A request involving workplace injury, insurance, or return-to-work clearance.

If a detail is wrong, ask the issuer whether a corrected document can be provided. Do not respond by adding private clinical detail unless the process genuinely requires it.

## What To Do If Work Questions It

Stay factual and document the conversation.

1. Ask for the exact concern in writing.
2. Check the certificate for name, dates, issue date, practitioner details, and work-capacity wording.
3. Check the workplace policy, award, enterprise agreement, or contract.
4. Ask the issuer to correct a clear administrative error if needed.
5. Offer a verification pathway if the certificate has one.
6. Keep diagnosis and treatment details private unless a proper process requires them.
7. Seek Fair Work, union, HR, or legal advice if pay, discipline, or dismissal is affected.

If the organisation is not your employer, such as a university, insurer, government agency, sporting body, or court, ask for its evidence rules directly. Fair Work guidance is useful for workplace leave, but it does not control every other setting.

## Quick Checklist Before You Submit Evidence

Before sending a doctor's note, medical certificate, sick note, or stat dec, check:

- Does it cover the right dates?
- Does it match the purpose?
- Does it identify the right person?
- Does it show who issued it?
- Does it state work capacity or the relevant leave reason?
- Does it avoid unnecessary diagnosis detail?
- Is it from the right type of practitioner for the issue?
- Does the policy allow this document type?
- Is there a way to verify it if questioned?

The best evidence is boring. It says enough, matches the rules, and does not invite a privacy argument.

## The Bottom Line

"Doctor's note" is usually everyday Australian wording for a medical certificate, but absence evidence is broader than that.

A doctor's certificate, pharmacist absence-from-work certificate, statutory declaration, or other practitioner evidence can all be relevant in different circumstances. The right document depends on the leave reason, the dates, the issuer's scope, and the policy asking for proof.

For routine sick leave, the document should prove capacity and dates without exposing your diagnosis. For anything higher-stakes, check the exact evidence requirement before assuming a standard note will be enough.
