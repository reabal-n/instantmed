<Callout variant="info">

In Australia, employers usually need reasonable evidence of illness or injury. The document's label matters less than whether it is credible, complete, relevant to the absence, and accepted under the workplace policy or agreement.

</Callout>

Australians use several names for absence evidence: doctor's note, sick note, med cert, medical certificate, doctor's certificate, absence-from-work certificate, and statutory declaration.

They overlap, but they are not always identical. The safest approach is to understand what each document actually is and what your workplace, university, insurer, or other organisation is asking for.

## The Terms

| Term | What it usually means in Australia |
|---|---|
| Doctor's note | Informal term for a medical certificate or doctor's letter |
| Sick note | Informal term for evidence of illness-related absence |
| Medical certificate | Formal clinical certificate from a registered practitioner |
| Med cert | Common shorthand for medical certificate |
| Absence-from-work certificate | Often used for pharmacist-issued work absence certificates |
| Statutory declaration | Your sworn statement, not a clinical assessment |
| Fit note | Mostly a UK term, not standard Australian wording |

If an employer says "doctor's note", they often mean a medical certificate. If they ask for "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person", a medical certificate or statutory declaration may be relevant depending on the situation.

## The Fair Work Evidence Standard

For most Australian employees, the Fair Work Act lets employers request evidence for sick and carer's leave. The evidence needs to satisfy a reasonable person that the leave was taken for a valid reason.

Common evidence includes:

- Medical certificate
- Certificate from another registered health practitioner where appropriate
- Pharmacist absence-from-work certificate for minor illness within scope
- Statutory declaration
- Other evidence that is convincing in the circumstances

Awards, enterprise agreements, contracts, and workplace policies can add process details. That is why a one-day cold, a long absence, a workplace injury, and a return-to-work restriction may need different evidence.

## What A Good Certificate Usually Includes

A useful work absence certificate usually includes:

- Your full name
- Date of assessment
- Dates or period covered
- Whether you were unfit for work or usual duties
- Practitioner name and profession
- Practitioner registration or identifying details where relevant
- Practice or service details
- Signature or secure digital equivalent

It usually does not need:

- Diagnosis
- Medication list
- Full clinical history
- Test results
- Private details unrelated to work capacity

If an employer questions the document, check whether the issue is a missing detail, date mismatch, policy requirement, or authenticity concern.

## How Many Days Can a Doctor's Note Cover?

There is no fixed legal limit on how many days a medical certificate can cover in Australia. The period is a clinical judgement: a practitioner certifies the time they reasonably believe you are unfit for work or study, based on their assessment.

A few practical points:

- **Single days count.** An employer can ask for evidence even for a one-day absence if their policy or agreement allows it, and a certificate for a single day is completely normal. There is no rule that certificates only apply to absences of two or more days.
- **Short certificates are most common.** Most certificates for common illnesses cover one to several days, reflecting the expected recovery time for something like a viral illness, a migraine, or gastro.
- **Longer or open-ended certificates** are used for more significant illness, injury, surgery recovery, or mental health, and may include a review date rather than a fixed end. Extended absences are often managed with follow-up assessments rather than one long certificate.
- **Backdating is limited.** A practitioner can sometimes provide a certificate covering a past period if they are satisfied you were unfit at the time, but this is a clinical decision, not a guarantee. Our guide on [backdating a medical certificate](/blog/medical-certificate-backdating "When a certificate can cover a past absence") covers the detail.

If you need a longer absence covered, a practitioner may prefer to certify a shorter period and review you again. That is normal practice and not a sign the certificate is weak.

## Who Can Provide Evidence?

The answer depends on the purpose and policy.

### Doctors

GPs and specialists can issue medical certificates when they have assessed the person and formed a view about work capacity.

### Nurse practitioners and some other registered practitioners

Some AHPRA-registered practitioners may provide evidence within their professional scope. Whether a workplace accepts it may depend on the absence type and policy wording.

### Pharmacists

Community pharmacists may issue absence-from-work certificates for minor ailments or carer's leave circumstances within professional guidelines. These are not the same as a detailed medical certificate from a doctor and may not fit complex, prolonged, or disputed absences.

### Dentists, optometrists, psychologists, and allied health practitioners

Evidence from these practitioners may be relevant when the condition is within their scope, such as dental surgery, eye conditions, or mental health care. Some organisations may ask for a medical practitioner for specific processes, so check the requirement.

### Statutory declarations

A statutory declaration can be useful when a medical certificate was not practical or when the absence was brief and resolved. It is a legal statement, not a health assessment.

## Online, Digital, And Paper Certificates

There is no magic in paper.

Digital certificates, including a telehealth medical certificate, can be acceptable when they are issued after a genuine assessment by an appropriately registered practitioner and include the necessary information. A PDF can be printed if a workplace wants a paper copy.

What matters more:

- Practitioner identity
- Registration or professional status
- Assessment date
- Covered dates
- Work-capacity statement
- Document integrity

Be cautious with services that guarantee certificates before assessment, hide the practitioner, or produce documents without real clinical review.

## Can an Employer Check That a Doctor's Note Is Genuine?

Yes, and reputable services make this straightforward. Employers are entitled to satisfy themselves that evidence is genuine, and the rise of fake certificates has made some workplaces more careful about checking.

How verification usually works:

- **Contacting the issuing practice.** An employer can contact the practice or service named on the certificate to confirm it was issued, without the practitioner disclosing your diagnosis or clinical details.
- **Checking practitioner registration.** The practitioner's name and registration number can be checked on the public Ahpra register, which confirms they are registered to practise.
- **Online verification tools.** Some telehealth services issue certificates with a unique reference number or QR code that an employer can check directly against the service's records, confirming a certificate is authentic without revealing any private health information.

What an employer generally cannot do is demand your diagnosis, contact your doctor for clinical details without your consent, or reject a genuine certificate simply because it was issued online. If a certificate is issued after a real assessment by a registered practitioner and contains the right details, the consultation method does not, on its own, make it invalid.

If you are concerned a workplace might question an online certificate, choosing a service that displays the practitioner's registration and offers a way to verify the document gives your evidence a stronger footing.

## Diagnosis Privacy

For ordinary sick leave, employers usually need work-capacity information, not your diagnosis.

Reasonable evidence might say:

- Unfit for work due to a medical condition
- Unfit for usual duties
- Fit for modified duties from a certain date
- Expected review date

The specific illness, medication, counselling content, or test results are generally private. More detail may be needed for workers' compensation, insurance, long absence management, safety-sensitive roles, or return-to-work planning, but those are different processes.

## Different Purpose, Different Document

A standard work certificate may not be enough for every setting.

| Purpose | Often requires |
|---|---|
| Sick leave | Medical certificate, absence certificate, or statutory declaration depending on policy |
| Carer's leave | Evidence of need to care for a family or household member |
| University extension | Evidence covering study or exam capacity and dates |
| Centrelink or government process | Specific forms or treating-practitioner reports may be required |
| Workers' compensation | Jurisdiction-specific certificate of capacity or work capacity form |
| Insurance claim | Detailed treating-practitioner report and records |
| Court or official summons | Evidence matched to attendance capacity and authority requirements |

If the stakes are higher than a routine sick day, check the exact form before booking an assessment.

## Common Reasons a Doctor's Note Is Questioned

Most disputes are not about whether you were genuinely unwell, but about the document itself. The usual triggers are:

- **Missing or incomplete details**, such as no clear dates, no work-capacity statement, or no identifiable practitioner.
- **A date mismatch** between the certificate, the absence, and when you notified your employer.
- **Vague wording** that does not clearly state you were unfit for work or for your usual duties.
- **Authenticity concerns**, often directed at very cheap or instant services that appear to issue documents without a genuine assessment.
- **A policy that specifies a particular practitioner**, for example a workplace that requires a medical certificate from a doctor where a pharmacist certificate was provided.

Most of these are fixable. If a detail is missing or wrong, ask the issuer for a corrected document rather than starting over, and keep the conversation focused on the evidence rather than your private health information.

## When Evidence Is Questioned

If a workplace questions your doctor's note or certificate:

1. Ask for the specific concern in writing.
2. Check the document for name, dates, practitioner details, and work-capacity wording.
3. Ask the issuer for a corrected document if a detail is missing.
4. Keep the discussion focused on evidence, not diagnosis.
5. Check your award, enterprise agreement, and workplace policy.
6. Seek Fair Work, union, HR, or legal advice if pay or discipline is affected.

## The Bottom Line

"Doctor's note" is usually everyday language for a medical certificate, but Australian absence evidence is broader than that. A pharmacist certificate, statutory declaration, dentist certificate, psychologist letter, or formal doctor certificate may all be relevant in different settings.

The right document is the one that matches the absence, the practitioner scope, and the policy asking for evidence.
