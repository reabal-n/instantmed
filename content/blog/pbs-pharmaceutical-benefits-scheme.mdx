Most Australians know the PBS exists but are fuzzy on how it actually works. This matters when you are at the pharmacy counter, when deciding whether to see a doctor, or when comparing the cost of different prescriptions. Here is a complete, practical guide to what you pay, how the subsidies work, and what happens when a medication falls outside the PBS.

## What Is the PBS?

The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) is an Australian Government program that subsidises the cost of approved medications. Without the PBS, most common prescription medications would cost hundreds or thousands of dollars per course. With it, you pay a co-payment that is the same regardless of the actual cost of the drug.

The government negotiates prices directly with pharmaceutical companies and covers the difference between the negotiated price and your co-payment. For some medications, the subsidy is enormous: a course of cancer medication that costs $5,000-10,000 per month might cost you $31.60 at the pharmacy.

The PBS currently lists over 900 medications covering thousands of individual products and formulations.

## Co-Payment Amounts for 2026

The amount you pay depends on whether you hold a concession card.

| Patient type | Co-payment per script | Who qualifies |
|---|---|---|
| General patient | Up to $31.60 | All Medicare-eligible patients without a concession card |
| Concession card holder | Up to $7.70 | Pensioner Concession Card, Health Care Card, Commonwealth Seniors Health Card |
| Safety Net (concession) | Free | After spending $262.80 in the calendar year |
| Safety Net (general) | Concession rate ($7.70) | After spending $1,563.50 in the calendar year |

Co-payment amounts are indexed and updated on 1 January each year. If you fill a prescription and are charged more than the applicable co-payment, ask your pharmacist to check the price.

<Callout variant="info">You only ever pay up to the co-payment amount for PBS-listed medications. If the actual cost of the medication is less than the co-payment (which occasionally happens for short courses of generics), you pay the lower actual price.</Callout>

## Who Qualifies for PBS Pricing

PBS pricing is available to:

- Australian citizens
- Permanent residents
- Certain temporary visa holders (check Services Australia for current eligibility)
- Visitors from countries with reciprocal healthcare agreements: United Kingdom, Ireland, New Zealand, Italy, Belgium, Finland, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Slovenia, and Sweden

You will need your Medicare card at the pharmacy. Concession card holders should also present their concession card to receive the reduced rate. If you do not have your Medicare card on you, most pharmacies can look up your details, though they may charge you the full price and issue a refund or adjustment later.

## Concession Card Holders: Which Cards Count

Three card types qualify for the concessional co-payment rate:

**Pensioner Concession Card (PCC)**: Issued to age pensioners, disability support pension recipients, and certain other Centrelink payment recipients.

**Health Care Card (HCC)**: Issued to JobSeeker recipients, people receiving Family Tax Benefit Part A above certain levels, and some other income-tested groups. Also available as a Low Income Health Care Card for Australians on low incomes who are not receiving Centrelink payments.

**Commonwealth Seniors Health Card (CSHC)**: For older Australians who are not receiving the age pension but meet income tests.

Dependants listed on a concession card also qualify for the concessional rate.

## The PBS Safety Net

The Safety Net protects heavy PBS users from excessive costs. Once your out-of-pocket PBS spending reaches the annual threshold, your costs drop substantially for the rest of that calendar year.

**Concession Safety Net**: After spending $262.80 in a calendar year, PBS medications become free for the rest of that year.

**General Safety Net**: After spending $1,563.50 in a calendar year, PBS medications reduce to the concession rate ($7.70) for the rest of that year.

### How to Track Your Safety Net Spending

Your PBS spending is automatically tracked through Medicare (via your Medicare card). To access your Safety Net status:

- Check via MyGov / Medicare online
- Ask your pharmacist to look up your Safety Net status
- Get a PBS Safety Net card from any pharmacy - this is a physical record you can give to your pharmacist each time you fill a script

Family units can combine their spending toward the Safety Net. A Medicare Safety Net family registration is required - do this online through MyGov or by calling Services Australia.

## Authority Prescriptions

Some PBS medications require prior approval before PBS pricing applies. This is called an Authority prescription. Authority requirements exist for:

- Expensive medications where evidence of appropriate clinical indication is required
- Medications with specific PBS-listed indications (the drug is on PBS for one condition but not others)
- Medications that can only be initiated by certain specialist types

Your doctor manages the Authority process before issuing the prescription. From your perspective at the pharmacy, an Authority prescription works identically to a standard PBS script - you pay the same co-payment. The Authority number appears on the prescription and tells the pharmacist that approval has been obtained.

If your doctor writes a prescription for a medication that requires Authority but does not obtain it, you will be charged the full private price at the pharmacy. This should be rare if your doctor is using prescribing software, which typically flags Authority requirements.

## Non-PBS Medications

Not every medication is on the PBS. For non-PBS medications, you pay the full private price, which varies by manufacturer and product. Common categories of non-PBS medications include:

- Medications not yet assessed by the Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee (PBAC)
- Medications assessed but not approved for PBS listing (due to cost-effectiveness or insufficient evidence)
- Medications listed on the PBS only for specific indications, prescribed for a different indication
- Some oral contraceptives (partial PBS coverage - some formulations are listed, others are not)
- Many vitamins and supplements
- Certain skincare and dermatological preparations

For non-PBS medications, generics can reduce your cost significantly - the active ingredient is bioequivalent but the price is set by the manufacturer rather than the government. Ask your pharmacist whether a cheaper generic version is available.

<Callout variant="tip">If you are regularly paying full price for a medication you believe should be PBS-listed, ask your GP whether an alternative in the same drug class IS on the PBS. In many therapeutic areas, PBS-listed alternatives with equivalent effectiveness exist.</Callout>

## PBS and Telehealth Prescriptions

PBS pricing applies to the medication, not to how the prescription was obtained. A prescription issued via telehealth - including an electronic prescription (eScript) from an online doctor - attracts exactly the same PBS subsidy as one from an in-person GP.

The process at the pharmacy is identical:

1. You receive your eScript as an SMS token or via a prescription exchange service
2. You show this to the pharmacist (or send the token via your pharmacy's app)
3. The pharmacist processes it like any other PBS prescription
4. You pay the applicable co-payment

Some people assume telehealth prescriptions are more expensive, or that they will not qualify for PBS pricing. This is incorrect. The subsidy is linked to the medication and your Medicare eligibility, not the consultation channel.

## Getting a Prescription

To access PBS pricing, you need a valid prescription from an AHPRA-registered doctor. For medications you take regularly, options include:

- GP consultation (in-person or telehealth) for a new prescription
- Repeat prescription request for ongoing medications you are already established on
- Pharmacist-dispensed emergency supply in limited circumstances (typically for short-term continuity)

For most common medications prescribed online - blood pressure medications, [high cholesterol](/conditions/high-cholesterol) treatments, [type 2 diabetes](/conditions/type-2-diabetes) medications, contraceptives, and others - a telehealth repeat prescription is appropriate and results in an eScript delivered directly to your phone.

## How to Check If a Medication Is PBS-Listed

The PBS website (pbs.gov.au) has a complete searchable database of all listed medications. You can search by medication name, active ingredient, or brand name. The listing shows the approved indications, any Authority requirements, and the co-payment tier.

Your pharmacist can also check PBS listing and pricing for any medication during your visit.
