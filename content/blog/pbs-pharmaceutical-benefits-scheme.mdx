The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) is the Australian Government system that subsidises many prescription medicines. It does not make every medicine free, and it does not cover every use of every medicine. It sets a subsidised price pathway for eligible people, listed medicines, approved indications, and sometimes specific clinical criteria.

The important 2026 numbers are simple: from 1 January 2026, most PBS medicines cost up to $25 for general patients or up to $7.70 for concession card holders. The Safety Net then reduces costs further once a person or family reaches the annual threshold.

## What the PBS Actually Does

The PBS Schedule lists medicines available at a government-subsidised price. The Department of Health, Disability and Ageing manages the scheme and Services Australia administers PBS payments.

For a PBS medicine, the patient pays a contribution and the government pays the remaining eligible subsidised cost. The PBS website says the Schedule includes:

- Medicines listed on the PBS
- Conditions of use for PBS prescribing
- Consumer information for prescribed medicines
- What a patient can expect to pay

A medicine being available in Australia is not enough. It must be PBS-listed, prescribed for the listed indication, and supplied under the PBS rules.

## Who Is Eligible

PBS eligibility generally applies to Australian residents who hold a current Medicare card. The PBS also applies to some overseas visitors from countries with Reciprocal Health Care Agreements.

As listed by the PBS, reciprocal agreement countries include:

- United Kingdom
- Ireland
- New Zealand
- Malta
- Italy
- Sweden
- The Netherlands
- Finland
- Norway
- Belgium
- Slovenia

At the pharmacy, eligible patients usually need a Medicare card. Veterans may also need a DVA card. Concession pricing requires an eligible concession card.

## Concession Cards That Count

The PBS lists these concession cards for concessional PBS benefits:

- Pensioner Concession Card
- Commonwealth Seniors Health Card
- Health Care Card
- DVA White, Gold, or Orange Card

State or territory Seniors Cards are not PBS concession cards by themselves. If a person does not have one of the eligible cards, general benefits apply.

## What You Pay in 2026

From 1 January 2026:

- **General patient:** up to $25.00 for most PBS medicines
- **Concession card holder:** up to $7.70
- **General patient after Safety Net:** $7.70
- **Concession holder after Safety Net:** $0 for eligible PBS prescriptions, except applicable premiums

These amounts are current for 2026 and can change with government policy or annual updates. Always check pbs.gov.au, Services Australia, or a pharmacist for the current amount.

If the actual price of a PBS medicine is below the patient co-payment, the patient may pay the lower price rather than the maximum cap. Pharmacy prices can vary for some under-co-payment medicines.

## The PBS Safety Net

The PBS Safety Net helps people and families who spend a lot on medicines in a calendar year.

From 1 January 2026:

- **General patient threshold:** $1,748.20
- **Concession threshold:** $277.20
- **After reaching the general threshold:** further eligible PBS medicines are charged at the concessional rate
- **After reaching the concession threshold:** further eligible PBS medicines are supplied at $0, except applicable premiums

The Safety Net can apply to an individual or family unit. PBS guidance says patients need to maintain records of PBS, RPBS, and eligible non-PBS public outpatient medicine expenditure on a prescription record form or equivalent pharmacy record. Pharmacists can help patients track spending and issue Safety Net cards when the threshold is reached.

Brand premiums, therapeutic group premiums, and special patient contributions do not count towards the Safety Net threshold.

## Why You May Pay More Than the Co-Payment

The co-payment is not always the whole bill. Some extra amounts can apply:

- **Brand premium:** extra cost for a more expensive brand of a PBS medicine when a lower-cost brand is subsidised.
- **Therapeutic group premium:** extra cost where medicines in a therapeutic group are subsidised up to the lowest-priced option.
- **Special patient contribution:** extra amount where the government and supplier have not agreed on full subsidy terms.
- **Delivery or after-hours fees:** service fees that are separate from the PBS subsidy.
- **Under-co-payment medicine variation:** medicines below the cap can vary in price between pharmacies.

If the pharmacy price looks wrong, ask for a breakdown: co-payment, premium, dispensing-related fee, private price, or non-PBS cost.

## PBS-Listed vs Private Prescription

There are three common categories people confuse:

- **Prescription-only:** the medicine legally requires a prescription.
- **PBS-listed:** the medicine is subsidised for certain patients and indications.
- **Private prescription:** the medicine is not being supplied under the PBS for that script, so the patient pays the private price.

A private prescription may happen because the medicine is not listed on the PBS, is prescribed outside the listed indication, exceeds PBS quantity or repeat rules, or does not meet an Authority requirement.

## Restrictions and Authority Prescriptions

PBS restrictions are part of how the scheme keeps medicines clinically appropriate and financially sustainable. The PBS describes three broad restriction categories:

- **Unrestricted benefits:** no PBS restriction on therapeutic use.
- **Restricted benefits:** subsidised only for specific therapeutic uses.
- **Authority required benefits:** approval or authority coding is required because the medicine has specific criteria.

For Authority medicines, the prescriber may need to contact Services Australia or the Department of Veterans' Affairs, or use an electronic streamlined authority process where permitted. At the pharmacy, the Authority number or code tells the pharmacist that PBS criteria have been addressed.

If an Authority step is missing, the medicine may price as private even if it appears on the PBS.

## Why Some Medicines Are Not PBS-Listed

The PBS does not list every medicine. The Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee considers clinical effectiveness, safety, comparative benefit, cost-effectiveness, and community need. A medicine may be unavailable on the PBS because:

- It has not yet been assessed.
- It was assessed but not recommended.
- It is listed only for another condition.
- It is listed only after specific prior treatments.
- It is listed only for certain age groups, specialist settings, or clinical criteria.
- There is a private product or brand outside the subsidised listing.

This is why two people taking the same active ingredient can sometimes pay different prices: the PBS indication, authority status, dose, quantity, brand, and eligibility all matter.

## Repeats, Quantities, and 60-Day Prescriptions

PBS listings can specify maximum quantities and repeat numbers. Some medicines have no repeats, some have several, and some long-term stable medicines may be eligible for 60-day prescribing. The prescriber still needs to decide whether the quantity and repeat pattern are clinically appropriate.

If a repeat has expired, been used, or is no longer clinically appropriate, a new prescription is needed. If the medicine has changed dose or indication, the PBS listing may also need to be checked again.

## How to Check a Medicine

To check a medicine:

1. Search the active ingredient or brand name on the PBS Medicine Search.
2. Confirm the form and strength.
3. Check whether it is unrestricted, restricted, authority required, or streamlined authority.
4. Check the listed indication and criteria.
5. Check quantity, repeats, and whether 60-day supply applies.
6. Ask the pharmacist whether any premium or private cost applies.

The pharmacist is often the best person to explain the final counter price because they can see the exact item, brand, subsidy, premium, and patient eligibility at dispensing.
