The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) is the Australian Government system that subsidises many prescription medicines. It can lower what you pay at the pharmacy, but it does not make every medicine free and it does not cover every use of every medicine.

The important 2026 numbers are simple. From 1 January 2026, most PBS medicines cost up to $25 for general patients or up to $7.70 for concession card holders. The Safety Net then reduces costs further once a person or family reaches the annual threshold. As at 1 July 2026, the PBS lists the general Safety Net threshold as $1,748.20 and the concessional threshold as $277.20.

The catch is that the PBS has rules. The medicine must be PBS-listed, prescribed for the listed use, supplied under PBS conditions, and matched to the patient's eligibility. If one of those steps does not fit, the script may become private even when the medicine is prescription-only.

## The Short Version

Here is the practical map.

- **Prescription-only:** a doctor, nurse practitioner, or other authorised prescriber must prescribe it. It is not automatically subsidised.
- **PBS-listed:** the medicine appears on the PBS Schedule for certain uses. It does not mean every patient or every indication qualifies.
- **Private prescription:** the medicine is supplied outside PBS subsidy rules. It does not mean the medicine is necessarily unusual or unsafe.
- **Authority prescription:** PBS subsidy needs extra criteria, approval, or a streamlined authority code. It does not mean the medicine cannot be prescribed.
- **Safety Net:** annual protection after enough eligible PBS spending. It does not mean all pharmacy costs count.

If you remember one thing, make it this: the prescription and the PBS subsidy are related, but they are not the same thing.

## What the PBS Actually Does

The PBS Schedule lists medicines available at a government-subsidised price. The Department of Health, Disability and Ageing manages the scheme and Services Australia administers PBS payments.

For a PBS medicine, the patient pays a contribution and the government pays the remaining eligible subsidised cost. The Schedule also records practical details such as:

- The listed brands, forms, and strengths
- Conditions of use for PBS prescribing
- Quantity and repeat limits
- Restriction or authority requirements
- Patient contribution and price information
- Consumer medicine information where available

A medicine being available in Australia is not enough. It must be PBS-listed, prescribed for the listed indication, and supplied under PBS rules. This is why the same active ingredient can sometimes be subsidised in one circumstance and private in another.

## Prescription-Only vs PBS-Listed

People often use these terms as if they mean the same thing. They do not.

**Prescription-only** is an access rule. It means the medicine cannot be supplied without a valid prescription.

**PBS-listed** is a subsidy rule. It means the medicine is listed for subsidy when the PBS criteria are met.

**Private** means the script is not being supplied under PBS subsidy rules. The patient pays the private pharmacy price.

That difference matters for common situations:

- A medicine may be prescription-only but not listed on the PBS.
- A medicine may be PBS-listed only for certain diagnoses.
- A medicine may be PBS-listed only after another treatment has been tried.
- A medicine may need an authority code before subsidy applies.
- A medicine may be prescribed at a dose, quantity, or purpose outside the PBS listing.

The prescriber decides whether the medicine is clinically appropriate. The PBS rules decide whether the government subsidises that particular supply.

## Who Is Eligible For PBS Prices

PBS eligibility generally applies to Australian residents who hold a current Medicare card. The PBS also applies to some overseas visitors from countries with Reciprocal Health Care Agreements.

The PBS lists reciprocal agreement countries including:

- United Kingdom
- Ireland
- New Zealand
- Malta
- Italy
- Sweden
- The Netherlands
- Finland
- Norway
- Belgium
- Slovenia

At the pharmacy, eligible patients usually need a Medicare card. Veterans may also need a DVA card. Concession pricing requires an eligible concession card.

If you do not have Medicare eligibility for PBS purposes, the medicine may still be prescribed when clinically appropriate, but the PBS subsidy may not apply.

## Concession Cards That Count For PBS

The PBS lists these concession cards for concessional PBS benefits:

- Pensioner Concession Card
- Commonwealth Seniors Health Card
- Health Care Card
- DVA White, Gold, or Orange Card

State or territory Seniors Cards are not PBS concession cards by themselves. If a person does not have one of the eligible cards, general benefits apply.

Bring the card to the pharmacy or keep it correctly recorded with your regular pharmacy. The prescriber may write the script, but the pharmacist still needs to supply it under the correct eligibility details.

## What You Pay in 2026

From 1 January 2026:

- **General patient:** up to $25.00 for most PBS medicines
- **Concession card holder:** up to $7.70
- **General patient after Safety Net:** $7.70
- **Concession holder after Safety Net:** $0 for eligible PBS prescriptions, except applicable premiums

These amounts are current for 2026 and can change with government policy or annual updates. Always check pbs.gov.au, Services Australia, or a pharmacist for the current amount.

If the actual price of a PBS medicine is below the patient co-payment, the patient may pay the lower price rather than the maximum cap. Pharmacy prices can vary for some under-co-payment medicines.

This means "up to $25" is not the same as "always exactly $25." Some PBS medicines cost less than the cap. Some pharmacy charges or premiums can still change the final counter price.

## Why The Counter Price Can Be Different

The co-payment is not always the whole bill. Some extra amounts can apply:

- **Brand premium:** extra cost for a more expensive brand of a PBS medicine when a lower-cost brand is subsidised.
- **Therapeutic group premium:** extra cost where medicines in a therapeutic group are subsidised up to the lowest-priced option.
- **Special patient contribution:** extra amount where the government and supplier have not agreed on full subsidy terms.
- **Optional pharmacy charge:** a discretionary charge may apply in limited under-co-payment situations, within PBS rules.
- **Delivery or after-hours fees:** service fees that are separate from the PBS subsidy.
- **Private price:** the script is not supplied under PBS subsidy rules.

If the pharmacy price looks wrong, ask for the breakdown. The useful question is not just "is this PBS?" It is: which part is the PBS co-payment, which part is a premium or fee, and is the script being supplied as PBS or private?

## The PBS Safety Net

The PBS Safety Net helps people and families who spend a lot on eligible medicines in a calendar year.

As at 1 July 2026, the PBS lists:

- **General patient threshold:** $1,748.20
- **Concession threshold:** $277.20
- **After reaching the general threshold:** further eligible PBS medicines are charged at the concessional rate
- **After reaching the concession threshold:** further eligible PBS medicines are supplied at $0, except applicable premiums

The Safety Net can apply to an individual or family unit. Services Australia explains that a family can combine eligible PBS medicine costs, which can include a patient, partner, and dependent children. Pharmacists can help patients track spending and issue Safety Net cards when the threshold is reached.

Not every amount paid at the pharmacy counts. Brand premiums, therapeutic group premiums, special patient contributions, delivery fees, and after-hours fees may sit outside the Safety Net calculation.

## What Counts Toward The Safety Net

The detail matters because people can overestimate how close they are to the threshold.

Commonly relevant points:

- Eligible PBS and RPBS prescriptions can count.
- Some eligible non-PBS public hospital outpatient medicine spending may count when recorded correctly.
- Family members usually need to be linked on the same record for family Safety Net tracking.
- Pharmacy records are easier than paper forms if you usually use one pharmacy.
- If you use multiple pharmacies, ask how to keep the record complete.

The pharmacist is often the best person to reconcile the record because they can see the supply history and the item-level price details.

## Authority Prescriptions

PBS restrictions are part of how the scheme keeps medicines clinically appropriate and financially sustainable. Restrictions can apply because a medicine is expensive, works only for certain conditions, needs prior treatment trials, or has safety considerations.

The PBS uses several restriction types:

- **Unrestricted benefits:** no PBS restriction on therapeutic use.
- **Restricted benefits:** subsidised only for specific therapeutic uses.
- **Authority required benefits:** approval or authority coding is needed because specific criteria apply.
- **Authority required (streamlined):** an authority code can be used when the prescriber confirms the listed criteria are met.

For some authority medicines, the prescriber may need to contact Services Australia or the Department of Veterans' Affairs. For streamlined authority items, the prescriber may record the relevant code on the prescription.

If the authority step is missing, the medicine may be priced privately even if it appears on the PBS.

## Why A Script Becomes Private

A private prescription is not automatically a bad prescription. It just means the PBS subsidy is not being used for that supply.

Common reasons include:

- The medicine is not PBS-listed.
- The medicine is PBS-listed, but not for that diagnosis or indication.
- The patient is not eligible for PBS subsidy.
- Quantity, repeats, dose, or form do not fit the listing.
- The script is outside authority criteria.
- The prescriber intentionally uses a private script because PBS criteria are not met.
- The pharmacy cannot process it as PBS because information is missing.

Private price can vary by pharmacy, brand, and supply details. If cost matters, ask the prescriber and pharmacist early, before assuming the PBS cap applies.

## Online Prescriptions And PBS

The consultation method does not decide PBS eligibility by itself. A prescription issued after telehealth can still be supplied under PBS rules if the medicine, patient, indication, authority details, and pharmacy supply all fit.

The reverse is also true. A prescription written after an in-person appointment can still be private if PBS criteria are not met.

For patients, the clean way to think about it is:

1. The doctor decides whether a medicine is clinically appropriate.
2. The prescription records the medicine, dose, quantity, repeats, and any authority details.
3. The pharmacist checks supply and PBS processing rules.
4. The final price depends on PBS status, eligibility, Safety Net status, premiums, and any private-price issues.

If you are requesting a repeat prescription online, the service fee and the pharmacy price are separate. The review fee pays for the clinical request process. The medicine price is handled at the pharmacy when the eScript is dispensed.

## Why Some Medicines Are Not PBS-Listed

The PBS does not list every medicine. The Pharmaceutical Benefits Advisory Committee considers clinical effectiveness, safety, comparative benefit, cost-effectiveness, and community need. A medicine may be unavailable on the PBS because:

- It has not yet been assessed.
- It was assessed but not recommended.
- It is listed only for another condition.
- It is listed only after specific prior treatments.
- It is listed only for certain age groups, specialist settings, or clinical criteria.
- There is a private product or brand outside the subsidised listing.

This is why two people taking the same active ingredient can sometimes pay different prices: the PBS indication, authority status, dose, quantity, brand, and eligibility all matter.

## Repeats, Quantities, and 60-Day Prescriptions

PBS listings can specify maximum quantities and repeat numbers. Some medicines have no repeats, some have several, and some long-term stable medicines may be eligible for 60-day prescribing. The prescriber still needs to decide whether the quantity and repeat pattern are clinically appropriate.

If a repeat has expired, been used, or is no longer clinically appropriate, a new prescription is needed. If the medicine has changed dose or indication, the PBS listing may also need to be checked again.

For long-term medicines, a larger quantity or repeat pattern is not just an administrative preference. It depends on clinical stability, monitoring needs, medicine safety, and PBS listing rules.

## Common Pharmacy Scenarios

These examples are not individual medical advice, but they show how PBS rules play out.

- **"This medicine is $25."** The general patient PBS co-payment may apply. Ask whether there is a cheaper brand or any premium.
- **"This medicine is $7.70."** The concession PBS co-payment may apply. Ask whether your concession card has been recorded correctly.
- **"This one is private."** PBS criteria may not fit, or information may be missing. Ask whether it is private because of indication, authority, quantity, or eligibility.
- **"You need an authority."** PBS subsidy requires extra criteria or a code. Ask whether the prescriber has included the authority details.
- **"It is cheaper than $25."** The medicine may be under the general cap. Ask whether the amount counts toward the Safety Net.
- **"There is a brand premium."** The selected brand may cost more than the benchmark. Ask whether there is a substitutable brand without the premium.

The pharmacist can usually answer these faster than anyone else because they are looking at the exact PBS item, brand, patient record, and dispensing system.

## How To Check A Medicine

To check a medicine:

1. Search the active ingredient or brand name on the PBS Medicine Search.
2. Confirm the form and strength.
3. Check whether it is unrestricted, restricted, authority required, or streamlined authority.
4. Check the listed indication and criteria.
5. Check quantity, repeats, and whether 60-day supply applies.
6. Ask the pharmacist whether any premium or private cost applies.

The pharmacist is often the best person to explain the final counter price because they can see the exact item, brand, subsidy, premium, and patient eligibility at dispensing.

## When To Ask Before Paying

Ask before paying if:

- The price is much higher than expected.
- You thought you had reached the Safety Net.
- The pharmacy says the script is private.
- The medicine needs an authority.
- You changed pharmacies and your Safety Net record may be incomplete.
- The brand has a premium and you are open to substitution.
- You are using the medicine for a different indication from the PBS listing.
- Your Medicare or concession card details may be out of date.

Most price surprises are easier to fix before supply than after. Once a medicine has been dispensed, the pharmacist may have less room to change how it was processed.

## What This Means For Patients

The PBS is useful, but it is not a blanket price promise. It is a structured subsidy system with eligibility, listing, authority, and supply rules.

For most patients, the practical steps are:

- Know whether you are general or concessional.
- Keep Medicare and concession details current.
- Ask whether the script is PBS or private.
- Ask whether any premium or private price applies.
- Track Safety Net spending, especially if you use multiple pharmacies.
- Check the PBS listing when cost or authority status matters.
- Talk to a doctor or pharmacist before changing or stopping a medicine because of price.

If a medicine is unaffordable, say so. There may be a different brand, a PBS-listed alternative, a different quantity, or a safer plan. Do not ration prescription medicine without medical advice.
