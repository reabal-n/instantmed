When your doctor prescribes medication for an ongoing condition, the prescription usually includes a number of repeats. These let you return to the pharmacy to refill the medication without seeing a doctor each time. Understanding how repeats work - and what to do when they run out - saves time and ensures you are never without medication you rely on.

## How Repeat Prescriptions Work

A prescription is the original dispense. A repeat is a subsequent fill of the same medication, at the same dose, without a new clinical assessment. The number of repeats authorised depends on the medication type, your condition, and your doctor's clinical judgment.

The workflow is simple:

1. Your doctor writes a prescription, authorising a certain number of repeats
2. You fill the prescription at a pharmacy (original dispense)
3. You return to the pharmacy when you need a refill (using a repeat)
4. The pharmacist uses the repeat without needing a new prescription from your doctor
5. Repeat until the authorised repeats are exhausted or the 12-month prescription period expires

For eScripts (electronic prescriptions), each repeat generates a new token delivered to your phone. For paper scripts, the pharmacist retains the original and issues a repeat docket for each subsequent fill.

## How Many Repeats Are Allowed?

The number of repeats depends on the medication type and clinical indication:

| Medication category | Typical repeats | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Chronic medications (BP, cholesterol, diabetes, thyroid) | 3-5 repeats | Often sufficient for 6-12 months supply |
| Contraceptives (pill, patch, ring) | 3-5 repeats | |
| Mental health medications (antidepressants, anxiolytics) | 1-5 repeats | Varies by medication and clinical picture |
| Dermatology (topical corticosteroids, antibiotics) | 1-3 repeats | |
| Short-course antibiotics | Usually 0 | Not repeated unless specific indication |
| Authority medications | Specified by Authority approval | May be restricted |
| Schedule 8 medications | Strictly controlled | State-specific rules apply |

These are general patterns. Your doctor determines the number of repeats appropriate for your situation. If you are on a stable chronic medication and your doctor has only authorised 1 repeat, asking for more repeats at your next consultation is reasonable.

## Prescription Validity: The 12-Month Rule

Standard PBS and private prescriptions are valid for 12 months from the date written. Any repeats must be used within this 12-month window.

What this means:

- A prescription written on 1 April 2026 expires on 31 March 2027
- If you have 3 repeats remaining on 1 April 2027, those repeats cannot be dispensed
- You need a new prescription from a doctor to continue your medication

**Why prescriptions expire**: A 12-month validity ensures that medications are reviewed periodically. Your dose may need adjusting, your condition may have changed, or new clinical evidence may affect the appropriate treatment. The 12-month rule builds in a minimum review cycle.

<Callout variant="tip">

Plan ahead. Check the issue date on your current script when you start a new medication. If you have 5 repeats but only 6 months remain before expiry, you will need a new prescription before using all your repeats. Setting a calendar reminder about 2 months before expiry avoids last-minute requests.

</Callout>

## PBS vs Private Prescriptions

Most common medications in Australia are listed on the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme. PBS prescriptions are subsidised:

- General patients: up to $31.60 per script (2025-26)
- Concession card holders: up to $7.70 per script

Repeat prescriptions on PBS-listed medications attract the same co-payment as the original dispense. The subsidy does not reduce with subsequent fills.

Private prescriptions (for medications not on the PBS, or for PBS medications prescribed for non-PBS-listed indications) are priced at the medication's market cost. Generics are generally cheaper than brand-name medications even at private prices - ask your pharmacist.

## Authority Prescriptions and Repeats

Some medications require prior approval ("Authority") from Services Australia before PBS pricing applies. Your doctor obtains the Authority by calling the Authority line or submitting an online application, providing clinical justification.

For repeat prescriptions of Authority medications:

- Each new prescription (when repeats expire) requires a new Authority
- Your doctor handles this as part of writing the script
- The Authority number appears on the prescription
- From your perspective at the pharmacy, it works identically to a standard PBS script

If your doctor does not obtain Authority for a medication that requires it, you will be charged the full private price at the pharmacy.

## When Repeats Run Out

When your authorised repeats are exhausted, you need a new prescription. Options:

**See your regular GP**: The traditional approach. Useful if your condition needs a clinical review alongside the prescription renewal. Book in advance if your GP has long waiting times for routine appointments.

**Telehealth repeat prescription**: For stable, ongoing medications, an online doctor review can produce a new prescription within hours. The doctor reviews your medication history, current symptoms, and any relevant information, and if appropriate issues a new prescription with repeats.

**Pharmacist emergency supply**: In limited circumstances, a pharmacist can supply a short bridging amount of a medication to ensure continuity while you arrange a new prescription. This is at the pharmacist's discretion and is not available for all medications.

## Online Repeat Prescriptions: What Is and Is Not Eligible

Telehealth repeat prescriptions work well for stable, established medications where:

- You have been taking the medication for at least 3-6 months
- Your condition is well-controlled and stable
- You are not experiencing new symptoms or side effects
- The medication is not a controlled substance requiring in-person monitoring

Medications commonly renewed via telehealth:

- Antihypertensives (blood pressure medications)
- Statins and other lipid-lowering agents
- Metformin and other diabetes medications
- Thyroid medications (thyroxine)
- Combined oral contraceptives and other contraceptives
- Antidepressants and some anxiolytics (for established patients)
- Inhaled corticosteroids and long-acting beta-agonists (stable asthma)

Telehealth is **not** appropriate for:

- First-time prescriptions for new conditions (requires a full clinical assessment)
- Schedule 8 controlled drugs
- Medications where monitoring parameters need to be checked (e.g., blood tests required before continuing)
- Conditions that are unstable or deteriorating
- Medications with serious safety concerns where in-person review is clinically indicated

A telehealth doctor may ask you to get blood tests before renewing some medications (thyroid function for thyroxine, lipid panel for statins). This is appropriate clinical practice - telehealth does not bypass the need for monitoring, it facilitates the consultation around it.

<Callout variant="info">

When requesting a repeat prescription online, provide the name of the medication, the dose, and how long you have been taking it. Mentioning your most recent relevant blood results (if applicable) and the name of your regular GP helps the reviewing doctor make an informed decision. The more clinical context you provide, the smoother the review.

</Callout>

## Managing Multiple Regular Medications

If you are on several ongoing medications, keeping track of which repeats run out when is a practical challenge. Some strategies:

**Active Script List**: The national digital prescription system allows pharmacies to access all your current prescriptions from a central database. No tokens to manage. Ask your pharmacy to enrol you.

**Request aligned expiry dates**: When seeing your GP, ask them to issue prescriptions for all your ongoing medications at the same time so they expire together. This reduces the frequency of separate repeat requests.

**Set reminders**: When you pick up a refill that has only 1-2 repeats left, note the date and set a reminder to request a renewal 3-4 weeks before you run out.

**Pharmacy management**: Many pharmacies offer free reminder services - they text or call you when a regular medication is due. Ask your pharmacist if this service is available.

Running out of medication unexpectedly is stressful and occasionally risky (for medications where stopping abruptly is not advisable). Building repeat requests into your routine - before the last tablet, not after - avoids the problem.
