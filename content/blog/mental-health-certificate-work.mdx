In Australia, a mental health-related illness can be a reason to use personal leave when you are not fit for work. A medical certificate for work usually does not need to name your diagnosis. It needs to support the practical point your employer is assessing: whether you were unfit for work for the relevant period.

The careful answer is that evidence rules still matter. You need to notify your employer as soon as possible, provide reasonable evidence if asked, and follow any award, enterprise agreement, contract, or workplace policy that applies. If you are in crisis, feel unsafe, or are having thoughts of self-harm, urgent support comes before any certificate.

<KeyTakeaway title="Short answer">
- Mental health-related illness can support sick leave when you are not fit for work.
- Employers can ask for reasonable evidence, including for short absences, but the evidence request must fit the circumstances.
- A certificate usually confirms assessment, dates, and fitness for work. It does not usually need to disclose your diagnosis.
- Telehealth may fit straightforward work evidence, but urgent safety concerns, severe symptoms, or ongoing treatment needs require a different care path.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Mental health-related illness can support sick leave

Fair Work frames sick and carer's leave as leave for personal illness or injury, caring responsibilities, or family emergencies. It is also called personal leave. The category is not limited to visible physical illness.

That matters because "mental health day" is not usually a separate employment entitlement. In most workplaces, the leave type is personal or sick leave. The reason is that your mental health, stress, anxiety, low mood, sleep loss, panic symptoms, or burnout-related symptoms may affect your ability to work safely or effectively.

This does not mean every stressful day automatically needs a certificate. It means the practical question is work capacity. If symptoms mean you cannot do your usual work, sick leave evidence may be appropriate.

## What workplace evidence needs to show

Fair Work says employees need to tell their employer they are taking sick or carer's leave as soon as possible, and say how long they expect to be away if they can. Employers can ask for evidence that shows the employee was not able to work because of illness or injury. Medical certificates and statutory declarations are examples of acceptable evidence.

| Workplace question | Practical answer | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Do I need to notify my employer? | Yes, as soon as possible. It can be after leave starts if that is the first practical time. | Notice is separate from medical evidence. |
| Can evidence be requested for one day? | Yes, Fair Work says evidence can be requested for as little as one day or less. | Do not assume one day is always evidence-free. |
| What evidence can be used? | A medical certificate or statutory declaration can be acceptable. | The evidence needs to satisfy a reasonable person. |
| Does the certificate need a diagnosis? | Usually no. Fitness for work and dates are usually the relevant points. | Diagnosis disclosure raises privacy and stigma concerns. |
| Can my employer contact my doctor? | Fair Work says it does not consider that reasonable unless the employee requests it. | Extra medical detail should not become routine. |

<PolicyNote title="Policy context">
Workplace policies can vary. Awards, registered agreements, contracts, and employer policies may set notice and evidence rules, but the evidence requested still needs to be reasonable in the circumstances.
</PolicyNote>

## What a mental health certificate usually says

A work certificate usually keeps the clinical details minimal. It commonly confirms that a doctor assessed you, that you were unfit for work or usual duties, and the date or period covered. It should not turn into a mental health history.

That distinction protects both sides. Your employer gets evidence for leave administration. You do not have to disclose a diagnosis, treatment details, medication, therapy attendance, or the personal circumstances behind the absence unless you choose to share them or a separate process genuinely requires more.

Privacy can become more complex if you are asking for longer-term workplace adjustments, workers compensation, fitness-for-duty assessment, or a formal return-to-work plan. Those processes may involve more information, but that is different from a routine certificate for a short absence.

## What the doctor is assessing

For a mental health-related certificate, the clinical question is not whether your situation is "bad enough" in a moral sense. The doctor is assessing whether your current symptoms and circumstances make your usual work unsafe, unreasonable, or clinically inappropriate for the period requested.

Common assessment points include:

- symptoms such as panic, low mood, insomnia, exhaustion, intrusive thoughts, poor concentration, or emotional distress
- the effect on work tasks, including judgement, memory, communication, driving, machinery, public contact, or high-consequence decisions
- duration, including whether a short rest period is reasonable or whether longer review is needed
- safety, including self-harm thoughts, psychosis, severe agitation, substance risk, or inability to care for yourself
- support, including whether you need a GP review, psychologist, EAP, crisis service, or emergency care

## Telehealth may fit some certificates, but not every case

Telehealth can be suitable for some work evidence because the assessment is often based on history, current symptoms, functional impact, risk screening, and the period of incapacity. It can be useful when leaving home would make symptoms worse.

The Medical Board and Ahpra also make the boundary clear: telehealth is only appropriate when it is safe, clinically appropriate, and suitable for the patient. The professional standard does not drop because the assessment is remote.

<DecisionBox title="Where telehealth fits">
### May fit telehealth
- Short, straightforward work evidence where the doctor can assess symptoms, work capacity, risk, and dates remotely.
- Follow-up evidence where the situation is stable and remote review gives enough information.
### Needs in-person care
- Severe, worsening, unclear, or complex symptoms where examination, observation, medication review, or ongoing treatment planning is needed.
- Fitness-for-duty, workers compensation, or formal return-to-work situations that need more detailed assessment.
### Urgent care
- Immediate danger, self-harm thoughts with intent, psychosis, severe agitation, confusion, or inability to stay safe.
</DecisionBox>

## Safety comes before a certificate

A mental health certificate is workplace evidence. It is not crisis care, therapy, a Mental Health Treatment Plan, or a substitute for a regular GP when symptoms are ongoing.

If you are worried you may harm yourself or someone else, or someone is in immediate danger, call 000 or go to an emergency department. Healthdirect lists Lifeline on 13 11 14 and Beyond Blue on 1300 22 4636 as national crisis support options.

<CareBoundary title="Work paperwork can wait">
If safety is the issue, seek urgent help first. A certificate can be sorted out later, after the immediate risk has been addressed.
</CareBoundary>

## Burnout, stress and diagnosed conditions are different questions

People often use "stress leave", "mental health day", "burnout", "anxiety", and "depression" to describe overlapping experiences. They are not all the same clinically, but for short sick leave evidence the immediate question is often narrower: are you fit for work right now?

Burnout may describe work-related exhaustion and reduced functioning. Anxiety or depression may be diagnosed mental health conditions. Acute grief, panic, insomnia, or a major stressor may also affect capacity. A certificate does not have to label all of this. It can simply support that you were not fit for your usual duties for a defined period.

Where symptoms keep recurring, last longer, or affect your ability to function outside work, it is worth moving beyond one-off certificates. A GP can assess broader causes, check risk, consider treatment options, and discuss whether a Mental Health Treatment Plan or referral is appropriate.

## Returning to work after a mental health absence

Returning to work is not always an all-or-nothing decision. Depending on your symptoms, role, and workplace, a doctor may consider a short absence, a staged return, modified duties, reduced exposure to a trigger, or a review date.

Possible return options include:

- a short certificate covering the period you were unfit for work
- modified hours or duties where your role can safely adjust
- a review date if capacity may change quickly
- referral to your usual GP or a mental health professional when symptoms are ongoing
- urgent escalation if safety concerns appear

Employers also have obligations under disability discrimination law where a mental health condition meets the relevant definition of disability. Reasonable adjustments are fact-specific, and not every short absence needs a formal adjustment process. But if mental health is affecting work repeatedly, it is better to discuss support early than to rely on repeated same-day evidence.

## Longer-term support after the certificate

A certificate can help with a work absence. It should not be the whole plan if mental health is repeatedly affecting your life.

<EvidenceNote title="Treatment support">
The Australian Government's Better Access initiative can help eligible patients access Medicare benefits for mental health treatment services after assessment and referral. A GP can explain whether a Mental Health Treatment Plan or another care path fits your situation.
</EvidenceNote>

Other support routes can include:

- your usual GP for assessment, risk review, and care planning
- a psychologist, counsellor, or other mental health professional
- an Employee Assistance Program if your workplace has one
- local public mental health services for urgent or complex needs
- Lifeline, Beyond Blue, or emergency care when safety is the issue
