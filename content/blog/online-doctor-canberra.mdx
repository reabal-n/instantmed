Canberra has a GP access problem that is well-documented and persistent. The ACT consistently records the lowest GP bulk-billing rate in Australia - approximately 50-55% according to Services Australia, compared to a national average of around 78% and some states approaching 90%. For a city of approximately 470,000 people (ABS 2021 Census), many of whom work in structured government roles with leave documentation requirements, this creates real practical difficulties.

The Capital Health Network has identified primary care access as a critical priority for the ACT, and the Australian Medical Association ACT branch has consistently raised GP workforce shortages as a structural issue. Wait times for new patient appointments at bulk-billing or low-gap practices extend into weeks for many Canberrans, particularly in growth areas where the population has expanded faster than the healthcare workforce.

Telehealth fills specific, well-defined gaps: same-day [medical certificates](/medical-certificate "Get a medical certificate"), [repeat prescriptions](/prescriptions "Request a repeat prescription") for established medications, and non-urgent consultations that do not require physical examination.

## The ACT's GP Landscape

The ACT's healthcare cost structure is distinctive. Canberra operates with high costs for commercial leases, staff wages, and overhead - meaning GP practices face higher costs than in most other cities. This makes bulk billing economically difficult to sustain at scale.

What this means practically for Canberra residents:
- Finding a GP practice that bulk bills and has capacity for new patients is genuinely difficult
- Gap fees of $50-90 above the Medicare rebate are common at private practices
- Same-day appointments for acute illness are rare at most practices
- After-hours options are limited compared to Sydney, Melbourne, or Brisbane

This is the context in which telehealth is most valuable. For a medical certificate, a repeat prescription for a stable chronic condition, or a non-urgent health question, paying a fixed-price telehealth fee and receiving the outcome on the same day is often more practical than waiting weeks for a GP appointment at a similar or higher cost.

## APS and Commonwealth Workplace Requirements

Canberra is Australia's public service capital. The Australian Public Service employs over 100,000 people in the ACT, and Commonwealth agencies operate under the Public Service Act 1999 and agency-specific enterprise agreements, most of which are negotiated under the Fair Work Act 2009 framework.

Medical certificate requirements across APS agencies typically follow this pattern:

| Absence type | Certificate requirement |
|---|---|
| Single day, once-off | Many agencies do not require a certificate, or require one only if a pattern is identified |
| 3+ consecutive days | Certificate required in most APS enterprise agreements |
| Adjacent to public holidays or weekends | Many agencies require a certificate for absences immediately before or after public holidays |
| Absences on performance management plan | Specific certificate requirements often apply regardless of absence length |

Certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors meet the evidentiary standard under all APS and Commonwealth enterprise agreements. There is no provision in APS enterprise agreements that requires in-person consultations - the requirement is for evidence from a "registered health practitioner," which a telehealth doctor is.

**Parliamentary staff and ministerial advisers**: Parliamentary staff have separate employment arrangements under the Members of Parliament (Staff) Act 1984 and individual contracts. Leave requirements vary by office. Telehealth certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors are generally accepted.

**Defence personnel at Russell Offices, Brindabella Park, and HQJOC**: ADF members follow Defence leave policies under the Defence Act 1903 and Service-specific regulations rather than the APS framework. Medical certificates for ADF members should come from a registered medical practitioner, and telehealth certificates are accepted.

**Contractors**: Employees engaged through labour hire or contracting firms follow their direct employer's policies. Most accept AHPRA-registered doctor certificates from any consultation type.

## University Students in Canberra

The ACT has three universities: the Australian National University (ANU), the University of Canberra (UC), and the Australian Catholic University (ACU) Canberra campus, collectively enrolling over 50,000 students. Many are interstate and overseas students who do not have a local GP.

Both ANU and UC accept medical certificates from AHPRA-registered doctors for academic consideration and academic adjustment applications. The ANU Registrar's office has confirmed that certificates from registered practitioners - including telehealth doctors - are acceptable documentation for exam-related applications, supplementary assessment, and extensions.

Key points for ACT students:

- Obtain documentation contemporaneously - a certificate dated close to the affected assessment carries more weight than one obtained weeks later
- Check whether your faculty requires the university's specific form or whether a standard medical certificate is sufficient
- ANU and UC both have Student Health services - these are often the fastest in-person option when unwell during exam periods
- For ongoing health issues affecting study, register with your university's disability or accessibility service for proactive accommodations

## Suburb Coverage Across Canberra

Canberra's town-centre planning model means healthcare services cluster around district centres, leaving residents between centres with limited local options:

**Inner North (Civic, Braddon, Turner, Lyneham, Watson)**: Best GP coverage in the ACT, but very high demand. Waits for bulk-billing practices are typically the longest due to competition for limited capacity.

**Belconnen**: Reasonable coverage around Westfield Belconnen and the Bruce precinct, but bulk-billing options are limited. Belconnen Community Health Centre provides some public services.

**Woden/Weston Creek**: Moderate GP access. Calvary Hospital provides emergency care. Older suburbs with reasonably established practices.

**Tuggeranong**: GP supply relative to population is tighter than the ACT average. Calvary Wakefield Hospital in Greenway provides emergency services.

**Gungahlin**: The ACT's fastest-growing region since 2010. GP services have expanded but have not kept pace with population growth. Residents in newer suburbs like Taylor, Jacka, Throsby, and Moncrieff have limited walking-distance options.

**Molonglo Valley (Denman Prospect, Wright, Coombs)**: Very limited local medical services. The nearest GPs are typically in Woden, Weston Creek, or the inner south. Telehealth is particularly practical for Molonglo Valley residents avoiding a 15-20 minute drive for a certificate or prescription.

**Inner South (Kingston, Griffith, Narrabundah, Manuka)**: Good access, with several established practices around the Manuka precinct.

## Parliamentary and Political Calendar Considerations

During parliamentary sitting weeks (approximately 20 weeks per year), Canberra's effective working population increases significantly with MPs, senators, ministerial advisers, and related staff. This cohort typically has intensive and unpredictable schedules that make attending a GP difficult.

Telehealth is well-suited to this reality. A medical certificate request can be submitted on a phone during a break between committee hearings or while travelling between offices. The outcome - a PDF certificate - arrives by email and can be forwarded to a Chief of Staff or HR team without requiring any additional steps.

## When In-Person Care Is Necessary

Telehealth is not appropriate for all situations. Visit a clinic or emergency department for:

- Conditions requiring physical examination: [ear infections](/conditions/ear-infection "Ear infection treatment"), abdominal pain, respiratory assessment requiring auscultation
- Injuries requiring imaging or wound management
- Any presentation where clinical examination is needed for safe management

**Canberra emergency resources:**
- Canberra Hospital (TCH), Yamba Drive, Garran: ACT's main tertiary public hospital with a 24/7 emergency department
- Calvary Public Hospital Bruce: 24/7 emergency department in the north
- Calvary Wakefield Hospital Greenway (private): emergency services in Tuggeranong
- **Call 000** for life-threatening emergencies - cardiac symptoms, stroke (FAST: Face, Arms, Speech, Time), severe injury

## Pharmacy Access

Canberra has pharmacy coverage across all district centres and major shopping precincts. All Australian pharmacies accept eScripts - the QR code on your phone. Capital Chemist (a Canberra-founded and operated chain), Chemist Warehouse, Priceline, and independent pharmacies all participate in the national Prescription Exchange Service.

PBS pricing is the same regardless of how the prescription was obtained. General patients pay up to $31.60 per PBS item; concession card holders pay up to $7.70 (Services Australia, 2025-26).

## How the Service Works

1. Complete the online intake form (5-10 minutes) describing your symptoms and what you need.
2. An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case - this is a genuine clinical assessment, not an automated process.
3. If appropriate, the outcome is delivered digitally: PDF certificate by email, or eScript by SMS.
4. If in-person assessment is needed, the doctor will tell you and advise the appropriate level of care.

Typical review times: 30-90 minutes during business hours, 1-4 hours in evenings. The service operates 8am to 10pm AEST daily.

## What Telehealth Cannot Replace

Telehealth complements but does not replace an ongoing GP relationship. Canberra residents should have a regular GP for:
- Chronic disease management plans (CDM plans under MBS items 721/723)
- Preventive health assessments
- Complex prescribing requiring monitoring
- [Mental health](/conditions/depression "Depression and mental health") care plans (Better Access under Medicare)
- Specialist referrals requiring clinical history and examination

For one-off needs - a certificate, a repeat script, an acute symptom question - telehealth is efficient. For ongoing care of complex conditions, your regular GP provides the longitudinal relationship and continuity that telehealth cannot replicate.
