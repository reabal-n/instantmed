Health problems don't follow business hours. A fever that develops on Saturday night, a cough that worsens at 9pm, or a child who suddenly looks more unwell can make the usual GP pathway feel too slow. Understanding which after-hours option is appropriate can save unnecessary waiting and, in genuine emergencies, get you to the right care quickly.

## Urgency Assessment: The Critical First Step

Before choosing an after-hours care option, assess how urgent your situation is. The first question is not "what is open?" It is "could this become dangerous if I wait?" Healthdirect advises calling triple zero (000) or attending an emergency department for serious illness or injury; minor illness or injury can often be directed through healthdirect, a GP, a medical centre, an urgent care clinic, or a pharmacist.

| Urgency level | Examples | Appropriate action |
|---|---|---|
| Life-threatening emergency | Chest pain or tightness, difficulty breathing, stroke signs (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty), severe allergic reaction with throat swelling, unconsciousness, heavy uncontrolled bleeding, overdose, serious injury | Call 000 immediately. Do not attempt telehealth first. |
| Urgent (same day, not emergency) | High fever not responding to paracetamol/ibuprofen, severe pain, worsening infection, dehydration with inability to keep fluids down, significant mental health crisis | After-hours GP clinic, or ED if no clinic is accessible. Call HealthDirect (1800 022 222) if unsure. |
| Appropriate for telehealth | Cold and flu symptoms, minor infections, medical certificates, prescription refills, UTI symptoms, skin concerns, medication advice, non-severe anxiety or mental health support | Telehealth service |
| Can wait for GP | Routine check-ups, ongoing condition management, non-acute new symptoms, preventive health | Book with your regular GP |

> **Emergency**: For any life-threatening emergency, call 000 immediately. Do not delay to use telehealth. For mental health crisis, call Lifeline on 13 11 14 (24/7) or present to your nearest hospital emergency department.

## After-Hours Care Options in Australia

### Telehealth

For non-urgent presentations, telehealth can be a practical after-hours option. It works best when the problem can be assessed mainly through history, medication records, photos, or a focused conversation. It is a poor fit when diagnosis depends on a physical examination, vital signs, imaging, wound care, or observation.

**What telehealth can deliver after hours:**
- Medical certificates for work or university absence where documentation is clinically appropriate
- Repeat prescriptions for stable medications (eScript sent by SMS, fillable at any pharmacy)
- Assessment of minor infections (UTIs, skin infections, minor respiratory illness)
- Mental health support and referrals for non-crisis presentations
- General health advice and medication management

**What telehealth cannot do after hours (or at any time):**
- Emergency care (call 000)
- Prescribe Schedule 8 controlled medications
- Assess conditions requiring physical examination

### Medicare Urgent Care Clinics

Medicare Urgent Care Clinics are designed for urgent, non-life-threatening illnesses and injuries that cannot wait for a routine GP appointment but do not require hospital admission. The Department of Health lists examples such as minor infections, minor fractures or sprains, UTIs, minor cuts, insect bites, rashes, minor eye or ear problems, respiratory illness, gastroenteritis, and mild burns.

These clinics are not for routine preventive care, chronic disease management, cervical screening, or life-threatening symptoms. For chest pain, breathing difficulty, severe bleeding, poisoning, seizures, unconsciousness, or serious neurological symptoms, call 000 or go to an emergency department.

### After-Hours Home Visit Services

Home visit services vary by city, state, provider, and availability. They may be suitable when:

- You are too unwell to safely travel to a clinic
- You need physical examination that telehealth cannot provide
- The presentation is urgent but not life-threatening
- You are caring for a child or elderly person who cannot easily travel

Check local availability, fees, Medicare billing rules, and expected wait time before relying on a home visit. If symptoms are deteriorating or time-critical, use emergency care instead of waiting at home.

### HealthDirect (1800 022 222)

HealthDirect is a free, 24-hour telephone health advisory service funded by state, territory, and federal governments and staffed by registered nurses. It is available anywhere in Australia.

Call HealthDirect when you are uncertain whether your situation requires emergency care, urgent clinic attendance, or can be safely managed at home or via telehealth. The nurse follows a structured triage protocol and advises on the most appropriate care pathway. This is particularly useful for parents assessing a sick child after hours -- the nurses are experienced in paediatric triage.

HealthDirect does not provide prescriptions or medical certificates -- it is an advisory and triage service only.

### After-Hours GP Clinics

Most major cities and large regional centres have after-hours GP clinics (also called medical centres with extended hours, super-clinics, or urgent care centres). These operate on weeknights (typically until 10pm) and during weekends and public holidays.

After-hours GP clinics are appropriate when:
- You need physical examination (ear inspection, throat culture, wound assessment)
- Your condition is too urgent to wait until your regular GP opens
- Telehealth assessment is insufficient for your presentation

Wait times at after-hours clinics vary but are typically shorter than ED wait times for non-emergency presentations.

### Hospital Emergency Departments (ED)

Emergency departments are staffed and equipped for genuine emergencies -- they are not a walk-in GP service. Attending an ED for a non-urgent presentation means:
- Triage wait times of 2-6 hours or more for non-urgent presentations (Category 4-5 on the Australasian Triage Scale)
- Higher cost to the public health system (ED presentations cost significantly more than GP visits)
- Exposure to genuinely sick patients in a shared waiting environment

Use the ED for:
- Life-threatening emergencies
- Conditions where rapid deterioration is possible and monitoring is needed
- Presentations that after-hours GPs or telehealth have directed you to attend
- Any situation where you are significantly concerned about the severity

## What Telehealth Covers Well After Hours

Several common after-hours presentations are well-suited to telehealth assessment:

**Medical certificates**: For non-urgent illness where absence evidence is clinically appropriate, telehealth may support documentation without using an emergency department as an administrative workaround.

**Urinary tract infections (UTIs)**: Some uncomplicated UTI symptoms can be assessed remotely, especially when the history is clear and there are no red flags. Fever, flank pain, vomiting, pregnancy, male UTI symptoms, or recurrent complicated infections need a lower threshold for in-person review.

**Repeat prescriptions**: If you have run out of an ongoing medication on a weekend, a telehealth doctor can issue a repeat prescription for stable, established medications. This avoids a gap in your treatment until your regular GP opens.

**Minor respiratory illness**: [Cold and flu](/conditions/cold-and-flu) symptoms -- congestion, cough, [sore throat](/conditions/sore-throat) without severe difficulty swallowing -- are appropriate for telehealth assessment. The doctor can confirm whether antibiotics are or are not indicated, and provide symptomatic management advice.

**Skin conditions**: [Rashes](/conditions/skin-rash), insect bites, mild skin infections, and known skin conditions (such as eczema or psoriasis) can often be assessed via photo submission after hours.

**Mental health support**: Non-crisis [anxiety](/conditions/panic-attacks), stress, sleep disturbance, and mood symptoms can be assessed via telehealth. If the presentation involves thoughts of self-harm, call Lifeline (13 11 14) or attend an ED -- telehealth is not the right resource for acute mental health crisis.

## After-Hours Pharmacies and eScripts

Finding a pharmacy after hours can be an additional challenge. eScripts (electronic prescriptions via QR code SMS) can be filled at any pharmacy that participates in the national Prescription Exchange Service -- which includes all Australian pharmacies.

To find a 24-hour or late-trading pharmacy, check local pharmacy websites, call ahead before travelling, or use healthdirect's Service Finder. Major hospital precincts and larger shopping areas are more likely to have extended-hours pharmacies, but opening hours and medicine stock can still vary.

## After-Hours Care for Children

Children's presentations after hours can be particularly stressful for parents. Key guidance:

- For children under 12 months with a fever, seek same-day medical assessment
- For children 1-5 years, a fever above 39.5°C that is not responding to paracetamol or ibuprofen warrants after-hours assessment (in-person or home visit, not telehealth alone)
- For older children with mild illness, HealthDirect (1800 022 222) can help assess urgency
- Any child with difficulty breathing, persistent vomiting, drowsiness, a non-blanching rash, or severe pain should be seen in person immediately

Telehealth can support parents with advice about symptom management for mild illness, but physical examination is often important for children, and a lower threshold for in-person assessment is appropriate.

## After-Hours Mental Health Support

If you are experiencing a mental health crisis after hours:

- **Lifeline**: 13 11 14 (24/7 crisis support, phone and online chat)
- **Beyond Blue**: 1300 22 4636 (24/7 phone, online chat)
- **SANE Australia**: 1800 187 263 (mental health information and support, weekday hours)
- **Kids Helpline** (for people under 25): 1800 55 1800 (24/7)

If you are in immediate danger or have thoughts of self-harm, call 000 or attend your nearest hospital emergency department. These services do not require a referral.

Telehealth is appropriate for non-crisis mental health presentations -- anxiety, stress, mild-to-moderate mood symptoms. For acute mental health crisis, the services above or an ED are the appropriate care pathway.
