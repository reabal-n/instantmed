## The Major Players at a Glance

Australia's telehealth market has matured significantly since the pandemic-era expansion of Medicare telehealth items. According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), approximately 37% of GP consultations were conducted via telehealth in 2021-22 -- a figure sustained after the Australian Government permanently incorporated telehealth into the Medicare Benefits Schedule (MBS).

The providers operating in this space have diverged into distinct models: some focus on video consultations, others on asynchronous (form-based) care. Some are generalists, others specialise in specific conditions. Here is how the key services compare.

## InstantConsult

InstantConsult is probably the closest to a traditional GP experience delivered online. Video consultations mean you speak with a doctor in real time. For patients who value that face-to-face interaction -- even through a screen -- this is a genuine advantage. Doctors can ask follow-up questions on the spot, pick up on visual cues, and adjust their approach mid-consultation.

The trade-off is scheduling. You need to be available for the video call at an appointed time. If you are filling out a form at 10pm because you woke up sick and need a medical certificate before work tomorrow morning, InstantConsult requires you to wait for an available slot. Pricing is competitive, generally $35-$55 per consultation. They are bulk-billing eligible for some consultations via Medicare.

Best for: patients who want a real-time video interaction and prefer a closer approximation of a clinic appointment.

## Rosemary Health

Rosemary has built a solid niche in specialised prescribing pathways -- particularly weight management, hair loss, skin conditions, and erectile dysfunction. Their subscription model creates genuine continuity: a doctor monitors your progress over time and adjusts treatment as needed. For ongoing management of a specific condition, that continuity has real clinical value.

The subscription model is not suited to everyone, though. If you need a one-off repeat script or a medical certificate, paying for a subscription is the wrong tool. Their specialised clinical pathways are well-constructed -- they have invested in the workflows -- but the model assumes you want ongoing care rather than episodic access.

Best for: patients seeking ongoing treatment for specific conditions (weight, hair, skin). Less suitable for one-off requests or general medical certificates.

## Eucalyptus (Pilot, Kin, Software, Juniper)

Eucalyptus is the most heavily funded player in Australian telehealth. Their multi-brand model -- Pilot for men's health, Kin for women's health, Software for skin, Juniper for weight management -- allows each brand to speak directly to its target audience without trying to be everything to everyone. The user experience across their platforms is polished, and the clinical pathways are well-designed.

Like Rosemary, Eucalyptus is subscription-focused. This works well for long-term treatment plans, less so for acute needs. They do not offer general medical certificates or broad-scope consultations -- each brand stays in its lane. If you need a med cert for tomorrow and also want to start a treatment pathway, you are using two different services.

Best for: patients wanting a premium, well-designed experience for specific ongoing treatments (men's health, women's health, weight, skin).

## Youly

Youly focuses exclusively on women's health -- contraception, UTI treatment, and related services. There is real value in a service that understands its audience deeply rather than serving everyone superficially. Women dealing with recurrent UTIs or needing contraception refills have straightforward, well-characterised needs that Youly addresses efficiently.

The scope is deliberately narrow: if you are not seeking women's health services specifically, Youly is not the right service. Within their area of focus, they execute well.

Best for: women seeking contraception management, UTI treatment, or related women's health prescriptions.

## HotDoc

HotDoc is a different model entirely -- primarily a booking platform that connects patients with their existing GP practices. Many GPs offer telehealth through HotDoc, which means you can see your regular GP via video without a new account or service. The continuity of care is genuinely valuable: your GP knows your history, your medications, and your clinical context.

The limitation is availability. Your specific GP may not have telehealth slots when you need them. HotDoc is not a standalone telehealth provider -- it is a bridge to your existing GP practice. For bulk billing, if your GP offers it, HotDoc is the most cost-effective route.

Best for: patients who want to see their regular GP via telehealth and whose GP practice is on the platform.

## Quality and Safety: What the Regulatory Framework Requires

Every provider listed here uses AHPRA-registered doctors bound by the Medical Board of Australia's Good Medical Practice code -- the same professional obligations as any GP in a clinic. The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) and state/territory Poisons Standards govern what can be prescribed. The Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles protect your health information regardless of which platform you use.

The delivery model differs across providers; the regulatory framework does not. A doctor reviewing your request through any reputable telehealth service has exactly the same professional obligations as one you see face-to-face. Before using any telehealth service, verify that their doctors are AHPRA-registered (searchable at ahpra.gov.au) and that the service clearly states the qualifications of its practitioners.

Responsible telehealth services -- including all those listed here -- will decline requests that require in-person assessment. If a service never declines or never directs you to see a GP in person, that is worth noting.
