Telehealth offers particular advantages for elderly patients: no need to arrange transport, no waiting rooms with other sick people, no exposure to respiratory illness at a clinic. For older Australians with limited mobility, chronic conditions, or living in regional areas, telehealth access can meaningfully improve continuity of care.

Setting it up requires some initial investment of time - choosing the right service, testing the technology, and understanding the relevant Medicare entitlements. This guide covers the practical and clinical aspects.

## Why Telehealth Matters for Older Australians

According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), Australians aged 65 and over account for approximately 15% of the population but use around 50% of all hospital bed days, reflecting the disproportionately high healthcare need in this age group. Regular contact with healthcare providers for chronic condition management, medication reviews, and preventive care is central to keeping older people well and out of hospital.

Barriers to accessing that care are well documented. AIHW data on healthcare access identifies transport as the most common access barrier reported by older Australians in outer regional, remote, and very remote areas. Even in metropolitan areas, older Australians report difficulty with transport and parking, reluctance to burden family with drives to appointments, and discomfort with waiting rooms, particularly after COVID-19.

Telehealth addresses these barriers directly. Since the permanent expansion of Medicare-funded telehealth in 2022, it has become a standard part of Australian general practice - not an emergency workaround, but an established care mode suitable for many of the consultations older Australians need most frequently.

## Medicare Entitlements for Seniors

Elderly Australians are generally well-positioned to use telehealth from a Medicare perspective.

**Standard Medicare rebates:** All eligible Medicare card holders receive the same Medicare rebates for telehealth consultations as for in-person consultations, where the service attracts an MBS telehealth item. There is no age-based variation in rebates.

**Pensioner Concession Card (PCC) and Commonwealth Seniors Health Card (CSHC):** These cards attract PBS co-payment reductions. Under the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (Services Australia 2025-26), concession card holders pay up to $7.70 per PBS item at the pharmacy, compared to $31.60 for general patients. For an elderly person on four or five regular medications, this difference is significant.

**PBS Safety Net:** The Safety Net reduces costs further once cumulative PBS expenditure reaches the annual threshold. For concessional patients, the threshold is $262.80 per calendar year (Services Australia 2025-26); after that, PBS medications are free for the rest of the year.

**Chronic Disease Management plans:** MBS item 721 (GP Management Plan) and item 723 (Team Care Arrangements) allow GPs to develop structured care plans for patients with chronic conditions and to coordinate referrals to up to five allied health services. These items can be conducted via telehealth, and the allied health sessions (MBS items 10950-10970) are also available via telehealth for eligible patients.

## Department of Veterans' Affairs (DVA) Telehealth Access

Veterans and war widows/widowers receiving DVA support may have additional telehealth entitlements. Under DVA arrangements, Gold Card holders receive full coverage of all clinically necessary medical treatment regardless of cost. DVA has telehealth-specific guidance for practitioners, and Gold Card holders can access telehealth consultations through any registered DVA provider.

Veterans with specific White Card entitlements for accepted conditions can also access telehealth for treatment of those conditions. If you are setting up telehealth for a DVA-entitled parent, confirm with the service whether they accept DVA billing before the first consultation.

## Choosing the Right Device and Service

The best device is one the person already uses comfortably. For elderly users new to telehealth:

**Tablet:** Usually the best choice for video consultations. Large screen is easier to see, the interface is simpler than a laptop, and touch controls are intuitive. An iPad or Android tablet with a front-facing camera is ideal.

**Smartphone:** Works well if they already use one. The screen is smaller but sufficient. Most telehealth apps work on any modern smartphone.

**Landline or mobile for phone-only:** Many telehealth services offer phone consultations for patients who cannot manage video. For repeat prescriptions, medication queries, and straightforward certificate requests, phone-only works as well as video.

**Computer:** If they are already comfortable, this works. Webcam and microphone need to be functional.

If buying a new device, tablets offer the best accessibility combination: large screen, simple interface, reliable cameras, and many accessibility features (large text, voice control, high contrast) available in the settings. iPads in particular have straightforward video call functionality that does not require installing additional software for many services.

## Setting Up: A Practical Sequence

Do the setup before it is needed - not when your parent is unwell.

**1. Choose a service suited to their needs**

Look for services that:
- Offer phone consultations as an alternative to video
- Have clear, simple intake forms (not long multi-step questionnaires)
- Have accessible customer support if something goes wrong
- Are web-based rather than app-only (apps require installation and updates)

**2. Create the account**

- Use their name and details - not yours
- Set up with an email address they can access, or that you manage on their behalf with their knowledge
- Record login details in a safe, accessible place
- Add their Medicare card number and concession card details if applicable

**3. Test the connection before the first real consultation**

- Log in together and navigate to the consultation page
- Test the camera and microphone
- Make sure the internet connection is stable enough for video
- Do a test video call between your own devices to check video quality

**4. Prepare their medication list**

Elderly patients typically take multiple medications. Before any telehealth consultation, prepare a current medication list including:
- All prescription medications and doses
- Over-the-counter medications taken regularly
- Vitamins and supplements
- Any recent changes made by other doctors

This takes 10-15 minutes to prepare once and can be updated as medications change. It is more useful to a reviewing doctor than any other single piece of information.

## Privacy and Consent

Under the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles, patients have the right to control their own health information regardless of age. An elderly parent's health records belong to them, not to their adult children.

Family involvement in consultations is common and often clinically beneficial, but it requires the patient's informed consent. When joining a telehealth consultation as a family member:

- Tell the doctor at the start that a family member is present and confirm your parent consents
- Do not answer questions directed to the patient unless they cannot communicate adequately
- For ongoing involvement in healthcare decisions, discuss formal legal arrangements

**Power of Attorney for health matters:** In most Australian states, a person can appoint an enduring power of attorney that covers health and personal decisions. This becomes relevant when a person loses capacity to make healthcare decisions themselves. Until capacity is lost, the patient retains all decision-making rights regardless of family preference.

If your parent has an existing enduring power of attorney that covers health decisions, inform the telehealth service and keep a copy accessible.

## Chronic Condition Management via Telehealth

Many elderly patients have one or more chronic conditions requiring regular review: type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, osteoporosis, thyroid conditions. Regular medication renewal, monitoring, and care coordination are central to managing these well.

Telehealth is well suited to:

- Reviewing stable chronic conditions and renewing medications
- Discussing recent pathology results with a GP
- Coordinating referrals to allied health under a CDM plan
- Monitoring mental health, particularly depression and anxiety common in older age
- Reviewing vaccination status and arranging flu or COVID-19 vaccines (though the vaccine itself requires in-person attendance)

The RACGP (Royal Australian College of General Practitioners) has published guidance on telehealth for chronic disease management, confirming that for stable patients where no physical examination finding would change management, telehealth is clinically appropriate.

## When In-Person Care Is Still Necessary

Telehealth does not replace all in-person care. For elderly patients, these situations typically require physical attendance:

- Falls assessment and balance evaluation
- Wound assessment and management
- Cognitive assessment (dementia screening tools require physical presence)
- Immunisations and injections
- Physical examination required to assess a new or changing symptom (abdominal pain, chest pain, new respiratory symptoms)
- Ear, eye, or other examination requiring equipment
- Any situation where the doctor needs to examine to make a clinical decision

A responsible telehealth service will recommend in-person review when the clinical situation warrants it. Setting up telehealth for an elderly parent is about adding access to routine and straightforward care - not replacing the GP relationship.

## Managing eScripts for Seniors

Electronic prescriptions (eScripts) are delivered as QR code tokens via SMS or email. For elderly patients less familiar with digital systems:

- The pharmacist can scan the QR code directly from a phone screen - no printing needed
- If the SMS is saved in messages, it is easy to find at the pharmacy
- Multiple repeats generate a token for each repeat - help your parent understand how this works
- Expired tokens (12 months from issue) can be renewed by requesting a new prescription

Many elderly patients find the pharmacy pharmacy step straightforward once they have done it once. Walking through the first eScript redemption together removes any uncertainty.
