When a child wakes up sick, two problems land in your lap at once: caring for the child, and dealing with the paperwork. The school wants to know why they are absent. Your employer may want documentation for carer's leave. And dragging a miserable, contagious child to a GP waiting room - where they sit among other sick people and probably make everyone worse - is nobody's idea of a good morning.

This guide covers your workplace entitlements, school and childcare rules, **how long to keep your child home for each common illness**, and when telehealth can get you the documentation you need without leaving home.

## Carer's Leave Under the Fair Work Act

Under the National Employment Standards, permanent and part-time employees are entitled to 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year. This is a combined entitlement - the same leave pool covers both your own sick days and days when you need to care for a family member. Carer's leave accumulates year to year and does not expire.

You can take carer's leave when an immediate family member or household member needs your care or support due to illness, injury, or an unexpected emergency. Children - including stepchildren, adopted children, and foster children - are covered. So are grandchildren if they are part of your household. Casual employees are entitled to 2 days of unpaid carer's leave per occasion.

<Callout variant="info">

Carer's leave comes from the same 10-day personal leave entitlement as your own sick leave. If you take 3 days caring for a sick child, you have 7 days remaining for the year, plus any accumulated balance from previous years.

</Callout>

## What Your Employer Can Ask For

Your employer can request evidence that supports your carer's leave. Under the Fair Work Act, that means a medical certificate or a statutory declaration. They can ask for it for any period of absence, including a single day - though many workplaces only require evidence for absences of two or more days.

A carer's leave certificate is slightly different from a standard sick certificate. It documents that your child was unwell and required care, not that you were personally unfit for work. A parent or guardian completes the request on behalf of the child, describing the child's symptoms and the care required.

## How Long Should Your Child Stay Home?

This is the question most parents actually need answered, and it is not a matter of opinion. Australia's exclusion periods come from the NHMRC's *Staying Healthy* guidelines, which set how long a child should stay away from school or childcare to avoid spreading an infection. The exclusion is about being **contagious**, not about feeling better - a child can feel fine but still need to stay home.

| Illness | Stay home until... |
|---|---|
| Common cold / runny nose | No fixed exclusion - keep home if unwell or feverish |
| Influenza (flu) | They are well again |
| COVID-19 | Acute symptoms have gone (follow current health advice) |
| Gastro (vomiting / diarrhoea) | 24 hours after the last vomit or loose motion (many childcare centres require 48 hours) |
| Conjunctivitis (pink eye) | The discharge from the eyes has stopped |
| Hand, foot and mouth | All blisters have dried |
| Chickenpox | All blisters have crusted/dried - usually about 5 days after the rash appears |
| Measles | 4 days after the rash appears |
| Whooping cough | 5 days after starting antibiotics (or 21 days from when the cough began) |
| Impetigo (school sores) | Antibiotic treatment has started and sores are covered with a watertight dressing |
| Slapped cheek (parvovirus B19) | No exclusion needed - not contagious once the rash appears |
| Head lice | Treatment has started (they can usually return the next day) |

<Callout variant="info">

These are minimum exclusion periods. Your school or childcare centre can set stricter rules, and a doctor may advise a longer period based on your child's specific illness. When in doubt, ask the centre and your doctor.

</Callout>

## School Absence Rules by State

School attendance is compulsory in Australia, and each state has slightly different rules about documentation for absences:

- **NSW** - parents must explain all absences within 7 days. A medical certificate is expected for absences of 3 or more consecutive days, and may be requested for frequent short absences. Unexplained absences can be referred to the Home School Liaison Officer.
- **Victoria** - parents must notify the school of any absence on the day it occurs. Certificates are expected for extended absences and may be requested for frequent ones. Schools must follow up unexplained absences.
- **Queensland** - parents must provide a reasonable excuse for any absence. Certificates are expected for absences over 3 days and for any absence during exam periods.
- **South Australia** - all absences must be explained. Certificates are requested for absences over 3 days, with specific provisions for chronic conditions needing ongoing documentation.
- **Western Australia** - certificates are expected for extended absences, with schools given significant discretion over the evidence they require.

## Childcare and Preschool: Stricter Rules and Clearance Certificates

Childcare centres and preschools often have stricter requirements than schools, because infections spread fast among young children who share toys and touch everything. Many require a child to be excluded for the full periods in the table above, and some require a **clearance certificate** - written confirmation that the child is well enough to return - particularly after gastroenteritis, conjunctivitis, hand-foot-and-mouth, or any illness that caused a long absence.

A telehealth consultation can provide both the initial certificate documenting the illness and, where clinically appropriate, the clearance certificate for return - without two separate clinic visits in one week. Always check your centre's specific policy, because requirements vary between providers.

## Getting a Certificate for Your Child

A parent or guardian can request a medical certificate on behalf of a child of any age. For a telehealth service, the parent completes the form, provides the child's details (name and date of birth), and describes the symptoms and timeline. The doctor reviews the information and decides whether a certificate is appropriate.

For children under 2, most services recommend in-person assessment rather than a remote certificate - young children can deteriorate quickly and physical examination matters. For school-aged children (5-17) with common, clearly described illnesses - gastro, cold, [flu](/conditions/cold-and-flu "Cold and flu"), hand-foot-and-mouth, [conjunctivitis](/conditions/pink-eye "Pink eye") - telehealth documentation is generally straightforward.

## When Your Child Needs In-Person Care

Telehealth is appropriate for documenting common childhood illnesses you can describe clearly. It is not a substitute for clinical assessment when something might be seriously wrong.

<Callout variant="emergency">

Take your child to a doctor in person or call **000** if they have: difficulty or rapid breathing; a rash that does not fade when pressed (possible meningococcal); high fever in a baby under 3 months; persistent vomiting or inability to keep fluids down for more than 6 hours; unusual drowsiness or difficulty waking; a seizure; or any symptom that worries you as a parent.

</Callout>

- **Telehealth is suitable for:** common cold and cough, mild gastroenteritis, conjunctivitis, hand-foot-and-mouth disease, mild ear pain, and general unwellness where the child is alert and drinking fluids.
- **In-person assessment is better for:** any child under 2, high or persistent fever, rashes you cannot identify, breathing difficulty, severe pain, signs of dehydration, or symptoms lasting more than a few days without improvement.
