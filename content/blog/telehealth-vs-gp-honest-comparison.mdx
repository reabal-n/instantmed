Let us get this out of the way upfront: InstantMed is not trying to replace your GP. We do not want to, and frankly, we could not even if we tried. Your GP is a cornerstone of the Australian healthcare system -- someone who knows your history, manages your ongoing care, holds your chronic disease management plans, and can physically examine you when needed. What we are trying to do is handle the straightforward stuff so you do not have to take a half-day off work to get a medical certificate for the cold you obviously have.

This is an honest comparison. We will tell you when telehealth is genuinely better, when the GP is genuinely better, and when the answer is "it depends." We will use real numbers, not marketing spin.

## When Telehealth Wins

There are situations where dragging yourself to a GP clinic is unnecessary, inconvenient, or borderline absurd.

**You already know what you need, and so does the doctor.** Medical certificates for straightforward illness, repeat prescriptions for stable medications, and UTI prescriptions for women with a clear, well-characterised history are all situations where the clinical outcome is the same whether you attend in person or submit a form online.

**Time is the real cost.** The AIHW reports that the average time from booking to completion of a GP visit (including travel, wait, and consultation) is typically 60-90 minutes. If you earn $40 per hour, that is $40-$60 in lost productivity, on top of any gap payment. For a straightforward interaction, the telehealth model captures the clinical value at a fraction of the time cost.

**You are sick and getting to a clinic makes it worse.** Being genuinely unwell and needing to drive, navigate parking, and sit in a waiting room next to other sick people serves no one. For a medical certificate review, there is no clinical reason to do this.

**You cannot get an appointment.** According to the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) Health of the Nation report, a growing proportion of Australians are unable to get a same-day GP appointment. GP workforce shortages, particularly in outer metropolitan and regional areas, mean wait times for non-urgent appointments regularly extend to one to two weeks. Telehealth is not a second choice when the GP is unavailable -- it is the appropriate tool for the occasion.

**After-hours needs.** Most GP clinics close between 5pm and 6pm on weekdays and are not open on weekends. InstantMed operates 8am to 10pm AEST, seven days a week. After-hours GP clinics exist but typically have longer wait times and higher gap payments than standard GP visits.

## When the GP Wins

There are situations where no amount of technology can replace a doctor who can see you, touch you, listen to your chest, and look in your ears.

**Physical examination is required.** Telehealth cannot auscultate lungs, palpate an abdomen, inspect an ear canal, take a blood pressure, or properly assess a rash with depth and texture. If your presentation needs hands-on clinical assessment, see a GP. Attempting to manage these presentations remotely produces lower-quality clinical decisions, and a responsible telehealth provider will decline the request.

**Longitudinal, complex care.** Your GP's value is not a single consultation -- it is the accumulation of years of knowing your health history, your family background, your medications, your lifestyle, and your preferences. For managing [type 2 diabetes](/conditions/type-2-diabetes "Type 2 diabetes"), cardiovascular disease, mental health conditions, and other ongoing issues, that longitudinal relationship has genuine clinical value that telehealth does not replicate.

**Starting new medications.** Beginning a new medication class typically requires baseline assessments (blood tests, blood pressure, ECG), patient education about side effects and monitoring, and a clinical relationship that can track response over time. Telehealth is appropriate for continuing stable, established prescriptions -- not for initiating complex new treatments.

**Children.** Paediatric presentations are clinically different. Children's symptoms change quickly, physical examination is more diagnostically valuable than in adults, and the clinical history depends on a parent's interpretation. A conservative approach is appropriate.

**Mental health care plans, CDM plans, health assessments.** These structured Medicare items require in-person consultations to be properly initiated. They are designed around the GP-patient relationship and cannot be meaningfully replaced.

**Controlled substances.** Schedule 8 medications (certain opioids, benzodiazepines, stimulants for ADHD) cannot be prescribed via asynchronous telehealth. Full stop.

The hidden cost most comparisons omit is time. A GP visit including travel, waiting room, and appointment typically runs 60-90 minutes for a straightforward interaction. If you earn $40 per hour, that is $40-60 in time cost before any gap payment. For a 5-minute clinical decision about a repeat prescription, that time overhead is disproportionate.

## The GP Workforce Reality

Here is the structural context that shapes this comparison. The RACGP Health of the Nation 2023 report documents a GP workforce in significant strain. According to the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) and AIHW data, the number of GP FTE (full-time equivalent) per 100,000 population declined in many major cities between 2015 and 2023. In outer metropolitan growth corridors and regional areas, the situation is more severe.

Bulk-billing rates -- the clearest indicator of GP accessibility -- declined nationally from approximately 90% in 2021-22 (partly sustained by pandemic-era incentives) to below 80% in 2023-24 as those incentives lapsed, according to AIHW Medicare data. For patients in inner-city and outer suburban areas, the assumption of a bulk-billing GP is increasingly inaccurate.

This is not a criticism of GPs. They are managing increasing demand, complex chronic disease loads, and chronic underfunding of primary care. The structural problem is at the system level, not the practitioner level. Telehealth can handle some of the low-complexity volume -- freeing GP time for patients who genuinely need a GP.

## Quality and Safety: Same Rules, Different Setting

A question we hear regularly: "Is an online doctor as good as a real doctor?"

The answer is that an online doctor is a real doctor. AHPRA-registered, with the same medical qualifications, the same prescribing authority, and the same professional obligations under the Medical Board of Australia's Good Medical Practice code. The Medical Board's standards apply equally to in-person and telehealth practice.

The delivery method does matter. A GP who can examine you has more clinical information available than a doctor reviewing a form. For straightforward situations, that additional information would not change the clinical outcome. For complex situations, it could materially alter the decision. Responsible telehealth providers know this boundary and decline requests that require in-person assessment.

> **Important**: Any telehealth service that claims to handle everything a GP can is overpromising. If a provider never declines requests or suggests in-person follow-up, question their clinical governance.

## The Best Approach: Use Both

The smartest approach is not choosing between telehealth and a GP -- it is using both for what each does best. Keep your regular GP for ongoing care, annual reviews, complex conditions, and anything that needs physical examination or a Mental Health Care Plan. Use telehealth for straightforward, acute needs that do not require hands-on assessment.

| Situation | Best option |
|---|---|
| Annual health check-up or chronic disease review | GP |
| Ongoing management of diabetes, cardiovascular disease | GP |
| Sick with a [cold](/conditions/cold-and-flu "Cold and flu"), need a certificate for work | Telehealth |
| [Repeat script](/prescriptions "Repeat prescription") for a stable, established medication | Telehealth |
| New symptom that concerns you | GP |
| Rash, lump, abdominal pain, ear pain | GP (physical examination needed) |
| Cannot get a same-day GP appointment for a non-urgent issue | Telehealth |
| After-hours need that is not an emergency | Telehealth or after-hours clinic |
| Medical emergency | Call 000 immediately |

## What We Would Tell a Friend

If a friend asked "should I use InstantMed or see my GP?", we would ask one question: what do you need?

If the answer is a medical certificate for a day off sick, or a repeat of a medication they have been stable on for years, we would say use telehealth. If the answer involves anything requiring examination, ongoing management, or clinical complexity, we would say see your GP.

That is not a sales pitch. It is the practical answer -- and it is the same answer the clinical evidence supports.
