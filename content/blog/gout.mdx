Gout is an inflammatory arthritis caused by the deposition of monosodium urate crystals in joints and soft tissues, occurring when blood uric acid levels (serum urate) exceed the saturation threshold. According to Arthritis Australia and AIHW data, gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in Australian adults, affecting approximately 1 in 25 Australians. It is substantially more prevalent in men (approximately 3:1 male to female ratio), in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, and in Maori and Pacific Islander populations due to a combination of genetic and dietary factors.

Gout is a treatable condition with an excellent long-term prognosis if urate levels are managed. However, it is frequently undertreated: many patients receive treatment only for acute attacks without addressing the underlying hyperuricaemia, leading to recurrent attacks and progressive joint damage.

## Why Uric Acid Matters

Uric acid is the final metabolic product of purine catabolism in humans. Unlike most other mammals, humans lack the enzyme uricase, which would break down uric acid further. When serum urate exceeds approximately 408 micromol/L (6.8 mg/dL), it is at or above the saturation point for monosodium urate at physiological temperature. In this supersaturated environment, urate can crystallise and deposit in:

- **Joint spaces**: triggering the acute inflammatory response responsible for gout attacks
- **Periarticular tissues**: forming tophi (visible or palpable urate crystal deposits)
- **Kidneys**: contributing to urate nephropathy and nephrolithiasis (kidney stones)

Importantly, approximately two-thirds of uric acid is produced endogenously from purine nucleotide turnover - dietary purines account for only about one-third. This is why dietary modification alone rarely normalises serum urate in patients with established gout, and why urate-lowering medication is often required.

## Risk Factors

| Risk factor | How it raises uric acid | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Age and male sex | Oestrogen promotes renal urate excretion; testosterone does not | Post-menopausal women lose oestrogen-related protection |
| High-purine diet | Purines metabolised to uric acid | Red meat, organ meats, shellfish highest risk |
| Alcohol (especially beer) | Beer contains purines; alcohol impairs renal urate excretion | Even moderate intake worsens gout |
| Sugary drinks and fructose | Fructose metabolism generates uric acid | Soft drinks, fruit juice, high-fructose corn syrup |
| Obesity | Increases uric acid production; reduces renal clearance | Weight loss is one of the most effective interventions |
| Chronic kidney disease | Reduced renal urate excretion | Very common association |
| Medications | Thiazide and loop diuretics, low-dose aspirin, cyclosporin, some chemotherapy agents | Medication review is important in all gout patients |
| Genetic factors | Transporters SLC22A12 and SLC2A9 affect renal urate handling | Family history significantly raises risk |

## Recognising an Acute Gout Attack

Acute gout is typically unmistakable:

- **Sudden onset**, often waking the patient at night
- **Severe pain**: described as crushing, burning, or unbearable. The intensity is characteristic - many patients describe it as the worst pain they have experienced.
- **Monoarthritis**: typically one joint at a time
- **Podagra**: the first metatarsophalangeal joint (base of the big toe) is affected in over 50% of initial attacks and over 90% of patients at some point
- **Local inflammation**: the joint and surrounding tissue become red, hot, swollen, and exquisitely tender. Even the touch of bedsheets or clothing is painful.
- **Systemic features**: low-grade fever and malaise may accompany a severe attack

Other common sites: ankle, knee, midfoot, wrist. Polyarticular attacks become more common in recurrent gout.

An acute attack typically peaks within 12-24 hours and resolves within 1-2 weeks even without treatment.

## Treating an Acute Attack

Early treatment significantly shortens the duration and severity of an attack. The three first-line options, all with strong evidence from clinical trials:

### NSAIDs

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as indomethacin, naproxen, or celecoxib are highly effective when started early. Indomethacin was historically used but is now replaced in many guidelines by naproxen or celecoxib due to a better gastrointestinal safety profile. NSAIDs should be taken at the maximum recommended dose for 5-7 days. Contraindicated in patients with significant renal impairment, active peptic ulcer disease, or NSAID hypersensitivity.

### Colchicine

Colchicine is highly effective for acute gout, particularly if started within the first 12-24 hours. The low-dose regimen (1mg immediately, then 0.5mg one hour later) is as effective as high-dose regimens with substantially fewer gastrointestinal side effects. It is particularly useful when NSAIDs are contraindicated. Dose adjustment required in renal impairment.

### Corticosteroids

Oral prednisolone (30-35mg daily for 3-5 days) or intra-articular corticosteroid injection are effective alternatives when NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated or not tolerated. Intra-articular injection provides rapid, targeted relief for accessible joints.

> **Warning:** A hot, swollen joint with fever and systemic unwellness requires urgent medical assessment to exclude septic arthritis (joint infection), which is a medical emergency. This distinction cannot always be made clinically - joint aspiration may be required.

## Home Management During an Attack

1. Rest the affected joint completely. Do not bear weight on a gouty foot or toe.
2. Apply ice wrapped in a cloth for 20 minutes several times per day to reduce swelling and pain.
3. Begin NSAID or colchicine treatment as soon as the attack starts - early treatment is far more effective.
4. Stay well hydrated (2+ litres of water daily) to support renal urate excretion.
5. Elevate the affected limb to reduce swelling.

Do not start urate-lowering therapy (allopurinol) during an acute attack, as fluctuating urate levels can prolong or worsen the attack. Wait until the attack has fully resolved.

## Long-Term Management: Urate-Lowering Therapy

This is where long-term gout management is most often inadequate. RACGP and eTG guidelines recommend urate-lowering therapy (ULT) for:

- Two or more acute attacks per year
- Any attack with tophi
- Gout with coexisting chronic kidney disease stage 2 or above
- Gout with urate nephrolithiasis

The target serum urate on ULT is below 360 micromol/L (6 mg/dL) for most patients, or below 300 micromol/L (5 mg/dL) for patients with tophi. This target maintains urate below the saturation threshold, causing existing crystals to slowly dissolve and preventing new deposition.

**Allopurinol** is the first-line ULT in Australia and is PBS-listed for gout. It works by inhibiting xanthine oxidase, the enzyme responsible for the final step of uric acid synthesis. Allopurinol is typically started at a low dose (50-100mg daily), with gradual dose escalation every 4 weeks to achieve the serum urate target.

Key points about allopurinol:
- Start low and titrate slowly to minimise the risk of precipitating an acute attack
- Continue any anti-inflammatory prophylaxis (low-dose colchicine or NSAID) for the first 6 months of ULT
- Dose must be adjusted for renal impairment
- Rare but serious hypersensitivity reactions can occur; Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk is higher in HLA-B*5801 carriers (more common in Han Chinese, Korean, and Thai populations - consider genetic testing before starting)
- Patients must understand ULT is lifelong and that stopping allows urate to re-accumulate

**Febuxostat** is an alternative xanthine oxidase inhibitor for patients who cannot tolerate allopurinol or who do not achieve target urate on maximum allopurinol. Not PBS-listed, requiring private prescription.

## Dietary and Lifestyle Modification

Diet alone rarely achieves the urate reduction needed to prevent attacks, but it is an important adjunct to ULT and can reduce attack frequency:

| Reduce or avoid | Rationale | Impact on urate |
|---|---|---|
| Beer and spirits | High purine content + impaired renal excretion | High |
| Red meat and offal (liver, kidney) | Highest purine content of animal proteins | Moderate-high |
| Seafood (anchovies, sardines, shellfish, mussels) | High purine content | Moderate |
| Sugary drinks and fruit juice | Fructose metabolism generates urate | Moderate |
| High-dose fructose sweeteners | As above | Moderate |

| Increase | Rationale | Impact on urate |
|---|---|---|
| Water (2+ litres daily) | Supports renal urate excretion | Helpful |
| Low-fat dairy (milk, yoghurt) | Active component reduces serum urate | Modest reduction |
| Cherries and cherry juice | Anti-inflammatory and may reduce attack frequency | Modest |
| Vegetables (including high-purine types: asparagus, spinach) | Vegetable purines do not raise urate risk | Neutral to positive |
| Weight loss | Reduces urate production and improves renal clearance | Significant if overweight |

Vegetable sources of purines - despite their purine content - do not appear to increase gout risk. Dietary restriction of vegetables is not recommended. The major dietary contributors to hyperuricaemia are alcohol, fructose, and animal-source purines.

## Gout, Work Capacity, and Medical Certificates

Severe acute gout can make it impossible to walk or perform work duties, particularly in occupations involving prolonged standing, heavy lifting, or physical activity. A medical certificate is appropriate when an acute attack prevents normal work. AHPRA-registered doctors can issue medical certificates via telehealth once the clinical picture is clearly described.
