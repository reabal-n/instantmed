Telehealth can be bulk billed in Australia, but it is not automatic.

A bulk-billed telehealth consultation usually needs four things to line up: you are eligible for Medicare, the service fits a current MBS telehealth item, the telehealth eligibility rules are met, and the clinic chooses to bulk bill.

If one of those pieces is missing, the consultation may still be valid telehealth, but you may pay a gap fee or a private fee.

<Callout variant="info">

Direct answer: telehealth may be bulk billed when Medicare rules and the provider's billing model allow it. A private online fee does not automatically mean the care is illegitimate. It means the service is not being billed to Medicare as a bulk-billed item.

</Callout>

This guide explains the billing terms, the current Medicare telehealth rules, where MyMedicare fits, and the questions to ask before you book.

## Short Answer: Sometimes, Not Automatically

"Bulk billed telehealth" means the provider bills Medicare directly and accepts the Medicare benefit as full payment for that medical service.

For the patient, the consultation cost is $0 for that service.

That does not mean every online appointment is free.

Telehealth is a delivery format. Bulk billing is a billing arrangement. Medicare rebates are the funding rules that may apply to a particular service.

Those three ideas are often mixed together.

A telehealth consultation can be:

- Bulk billed, where you pay nothing for the medical service
- Rebate eligible with a gap, where Medicare pays a benefit and you pay the difference
- Fully private, where you pay the listed fee and there is no Medicare claim

The practical question is not only "is it telehealth?" The practical question is "what will I pay after any Medicare benefit?"

## Bulk Billing, Rebates, And Private Fees

Bulk billing is the cleanest billing outcome for a patient.

Services Australia says bulk billing means you do not pay for the medical service. The health professional bills Medicare and accepts the Medicare benefit as full payment.

If a provider does not bulk bill, you may need to pay the full cost or the difference between what the provider charges and what Medicare covers.

That difference is often called a gap or out-of-pocket cost.

A rebate is not the same as bulk billing.

A rebate means Medicare may pay an amount for an eligible service. The provider can still charge more than the Medicare benefit, leaving you with a gap.

A private fee is different again.

Private telehealth services usually publish a flat fee and do not claim a Medicare rebate for that consultation. That can be simpler to understand, but it means the consultation fee is paid out of pocket.

## How Medicare-Funded Telehealth Works

The Australian Government says ongoing MBS-funded telehealth services are available nationally.

Telehealth can include phone or video consultations with health professionals such as GPs, specialists, allied health providers, mental health professionals, and nurse practitioners.

For GP telehealth, the Department of Health says patients generally need to have had a face-to-face consultation with their GP, or another GP in the same practice, in the 12 months before the telehealth service.

There are limited exceptions.

The MBS calls this the eligible telehealth practitioner requirement.

The exact item matters. Different MBS telehealth items have different requirements, lengths, professions, and fees.

That is why a clinic should be able to tell you whether the appointment is bulk billed, rebate eligible with a gap, or private before you proceed.

<Callout variant="tip">

Do not rely on an old rebate amount from a blog post. MBS fees and item rules change. Check the clinic's current fee information and the relevant MBS item if the exact rebate matters.

</Callout>

## The Eligible Telehealth Practitioner Requirement

For many GP and medical practitioner telehealth items, the patient must receive the service from an eligible telehealth practitioner.

In plain English, that usually means the practice has an existing relationship with the patient.

The MBS note describes several ways this can work.

One common pathway is that the medical practitioner is located at a medical or nurse practitioner practice, and the patient has had a face-to-face service arranged by that practice in the previous 12 months.

The service may be performed by another practitioner located at that same practice.

A previous phone or video consultation does not count as the face-to-face service for this requirement.

This is why first-time online GP consults are often not bulk billed under ordinary MBS telehealth rules.

The doctor may still be able to provide care. The billing may just be private rather than Medicare-funded.

## Where MyMedicare Fits

MyMedicare is a voluntary patient registration model.

The Department says it is free to register and is designed to formalise the relationship between a patient, their general practice, GP, and care team.

For patients, MyMedicare can support access to longer MBS-funded telehealth consultations with the registered practice.

It does not mean every telehealth appointment is automatically bulk billed.

The provider still needs to offer the service, the MBS item still needs to fit, and the clinic still decides its billing model.

MyMedicare also has its own eligibility rules.

The Department says a patient generally needs a Medicare or DVA Veteran Card and recorded face-to-face visits with the same practice in the previous 24 months, with different visit counts for remote and other locations.

People facing hardship can be exempt from all MyMedicare eligibility requirements.

## Exemptions And Special Telehealth Items

Some people and some service categories are treated differently.

The MBS eligible telehealth practitioner requirement does not apply to every situation.

Listed exemptions include:

- A person under 12 months old
- A person experiencing homelessness
- A person living in a natural-disaster affected area
- A person receiving the service from a practitioner at an Aboriginal Medical Service or Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Service
- A person isolating or in quarantine under a COVID-related state or territory public health order

The MBS note also lists specific service groups where the requirement does not apply, including urgent after-hours services in unsociable hours, specific mental health treatment items, blood-borne virus, sexual or reproductive health telehealth items, and preparing or reviewing a GP chronic condition management plan.

This is not a reason to guess.

It is a reason to ask the provider which MBS item they are using, whether an exemption applies, and whether the consultation will be bulk billed.

## Bulk Billing Is Still The Provider's Choice

Even when a Medicare rebate is available, a provider does not have to bulk bill.

Services Australia says you can be bulk billed if you are enrolled in Medicare and your health professional chooses to bulk bill.

That second part matters.

The same telehealth appointment type can be bulk billed at one clinic and mixed billed at another.

Some clinics bulk bill children, concession card holders, aged-care residents, or registered patients, but charge a gap for other patients.

Some clinics bulk bill short consults but charge gaps for longer consults.

Some clinics do not bulk bill at all.

The safest wording to look for is not "Medicare available" or "rebates may apply." Ask whether the appointment is bulk billed, and what you will pay if it is not.

## Bulk Billing Incentives And 2025-26 Changes

Bulk billing incentives are additional payments to providers when they bulk bill eligible services.

They are designed to make bulk billing more viable for clinics. They do not create an automatic patient entitlement to every service being bulk billed.

The Department says MyMedicare practices can access longer MBS-funded telephone calls and a triple bulk billing incentive for longer MBS telehealth consultations.

It also says the Bulk Billing Practice Incentive Program started from 1 November 2025 to support practices and GPs to provide bulk-billed care.

These changes can affect clinic behaviour.

They still do not remove the need to check the actual billing model at the time of booking.

## Gap Fees Explained

A gap fee is the part you pay when a provider charges more than the Medicare benefit.

For example, if an eligible service attracts a Medicare benefit and the clinic charges above that amount, the patient may pay the difference.

The exact gap depends on the item, the provider's fee, and whether Medicare pays the benefit to the patient or the clinic.

Bulk billing removes the gap for that medical service because the provider accepts the Medicare benefit as full payment.

Mixed billing keeps Medicare involved but may still leave an out-of-pocket cost.

Fully private billing does not rely on a Medicare rebate for that consultation.

For patients, the best comparison is the final out-of-pocket cost, not the headline phrase.

## Why Private Telehealth Exists

Private telehealth exists because some patients need a lawful, clinically reviewed service when Medicare-funded access is not available or convenient.

That can happen when:

- The patient does not have a regular GP
- The patient has not attended the practice face to face in the relevant period
- The usual clinic does not offer telehealth
- The clinic offers telehealth but does not bulk bill it
- The service type does not fit a Medicare-funded item
- The patient wants a published private fee rather than a rebate-and-gap model

Private billing does not prove the service is better. It also does not prove the service is unsafe.

The real checks are clinical suitability, practitioner registration, privacy, clear fees, records, and a pathway to urgent or in-person care when remote review is not enough.

For a broader safety check, see [what telehealth means in Australia](/blog/what-is-telehealth "What telehealth means in Australia").

## PBS Medicines Are A Separate Question

Consultation billing and medicine pricing are related, but they are not the same thing.

The PBS can lower the cost of some prescription medicines for eligible patients.

Services Australia says PBS medicine generally requires a doctor's prescription, and a pharmacist can tell you if a medicine is cheaper under the PBS.

The PBS also has rules.

Pharmaceutical benefits can only be prescribed by approved PBS prescribers. Some medicines have unrestricted listings, some have restricted uses, and some require authority approval.

That means a private telehealth consultation does not automatically make a medicine full-price.

It also means a bulk-billed consultation does not automatically make every medicine PBS-subsidised.

In 2026, before the PBS Safety Net is reached, Services Australia says each PBS medicine can cost up to $25.00 for general patients and $7.70 for concession card holders. Brand premiums, non-PBS medicines, and eligibility criteria can change the pharmacy cost.

The better question is: is this medicine PBS-listed for my situation, and has the prescription been written in a way that meets PBS rules?

## Private Health Insurance And Telehealth

Private health insurance usually does not cover ordinary GP consultations outside hospital.

PrivateHealth.gov.au explains that Medicare covers some or all of the cost of seeing a GP or specialist outside hospital. It also says that if you are not an admitted hospital patient, your fees may only be claimable with Medicare.

Extras policies can cover some non-hospital services such as dental, optical, physiotherapy, pharmacy, or other therapies, depending on the policy.

That is different from saying ordinary GP telehealth is covered.

Some insurers may offer telehealth partnerships, member programs, or specific extras benefits. Check your policy before assuming the consultation fee is claimable.

## Questions To Ask Before Booking

Before booking telehealth, ask the provider:

- Is this appointment bulk billed?
- If not, is it Medicare rebate eligible?
- What will I pay after any rebate?
- Which practitioner or practice is providing the service?
- Does the eligible telehealth practitioner requirement apply?
- Can MyMedicare registration help for this service?
- Are there any exemption rules that apply?
- Is the consultation by phone, video, form review, or a mix?
- Will I need in-person care if remote review is not enough?
- If a prescription is issued, is the medicine PBS-listed for my situation?

Good billing information should be plain before you pay.

If the answer is "rebates may apply", ask what that means for your specific appointment.

## When Billing Is Not The Main Issue

Cost matters, but safety comes first.

Telehealth is not suitable for every problem.

If there is chest pain, severe breathing trouble, stroke symptoms, collapse, severe bleeding, severe allergic reaction, or another emergency symptom, call 000 or use urgent local care.

Healthdirect's virtual care guidance also separates urgent but not life-threatening issues from life-threatening symptoms.

Billing should never be the reason to delay emergency care.

For non-urgent problems, the billing model is one factor alongside clinical fit, continuity, privacy, records, and follow-up.

## Quick Decision Guide

Use bulk-billed telehealth when:

- You have a regular GP or registered practice
- The clinic offers telehealth and bulk bills that service
- The MBS item and eligibility rules fit
- The problem is appropriate for remote care
- You can wait for the clinic's available appointment

Expect a rebate-with-gap model when:

- Medicare applies, but the clinic does not bulk bill
- The provider charges above the Medicare benefit
- The clinic offers mixed billing by patient group, consult length, or appointment type

Expect private billing when:

- Medicare telehealth eligibility is not met
- You are using a private online service outside ordinary MBS billing
- The service type does not attract a Medicare rebate
- You prefer a published private fee and understand there is no Medicare claim

The right choice depends on access, urgency, continuity, and cost.

For ongoing care, your regular GP or registered practice is often the best first option. For one-off administrative or straightforward needs, a private online model may be more practical if it is clinically appropriate and the fee is clear.
