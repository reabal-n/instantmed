An eScript is a digital prescription. Instead of a printed piece of paper, you receive a QR code - delivered via SMS or email - that any pharmacy in Australia can scan to dispense your medication. The technology is mandated for most new [prescriptions](/prescriptions) in Australia, and the PBS subsidy applies in exactly the same way as with paper scripts. Here is a complete breakdown of how the system works.

## The Two eScript Models

There are two ways eScripts work, and most patients encounter both:

### Token-Based eScripts

The most common model. When your doctor issues a prescription, the system generates a unique QR code (the "token"). This token is sent to you via:

- SMS to your mobile number
- Email to your address
- Both

The token is a long alphanumeric code embedded in the QR code. You show it to the pharmacist - either by displaying it on your phone screen or by printing it - and they scan it. The token can only be used once per dispense. If the prescription has repeats, a new token is generated for each remaining repeat after you fill the current one.

### Active Script List (ASL)

The ASL is a centralised secure database that stores all your current prescriptions. Instead of managing tokens for each script, the pharmacist can look up your ASL using your Medicare card or IHI (Individual Healthcare Identifier) number.

To use the ASL, you need to:

1. Register with a prescription exchange service (most pharmacies can do this, or you can do it online)
2. Ask your doctor to add future prescriptions to your ASL
3. At the pharmacy, tell them you have an ASL and present your Medicare card

The ASL is particularly useful if you manage multiple regular medications - it eliminates the risk of losing tokens and makes refills straightforward.

## The Prescription Exchange Infrastructure

Behind every eScript is a national prescription exchange service (PES). These are secure, TGA-regulated systems that transmit prescription data between prescribers, pharmacies, and patients. The main PES providers in Australia are MediSecure and eRx Script Exchange.

When a doctor writes an eScript:

1. The prescription data is uploaded to the PES
2. A token is generated and sent to you
3. When you present the token at a pharmacy, the pharmacist's dispensing system queries the PES
4. The PES confirms the prescription is valid, unused, and not expired
5. The pharmacist dispenses the medication and marks the token as used in the PES

The whole system is designed so that the same prescription cannot be dispensed twice and cannot be altered.

## Using an eScript at the Pharmacy

The experience at the pharmacy is nearly identical to presenting a paper script:

1. Show the pharmacist your QR code token (SMS, email, or screenshot)
2. The pharmacist scans the code with their dispensing system
3. You present your Medicare card and concession card (if applicable)
4. The pharmacist dispenses the medication and collects the co-payment
5. If repeats remain, a new token is generated and sent to you

If you have an Active Script List instead of a token, tell the pharmacist your preference and present your Medicare card. They access your prescriptions directly from the ASL.

<Callout variant="tip">

Screenshot the QR code when you receive it. Your SMS or email may not be available if you have no mobile signal in the pharmacy. A screenshot saved to your photo library is reliable.

</Callout>

## PBS Pricing With eScripts

The PBS co-payment applies to eScripts exactly as it does to paper prescriptions. The subsidy is tied to your Medicare eligibility and the medication's PBS listing - not the format of the prescription.

| Patient type | Co-payment per script (2025-26) |
|---|---|
| General patient | Up to $31.60 |
| Concession card holder | Up to $7.70 |
| Safety Net (concession) | Free |
| Safety Net (general) | $7.70 |

Your PBS Safety Net spending is tracked through Medicare and applies to eScripts in the same way as paper scripts.

If you do not have a Medicare card (international students, visitors without a reciprocal agreement), you pay the full private price for the medication. The eScript format does not affect this.

## Repeats: How They Work With eScripts

When a doctor prescribes medication with repeats:

- The original script token is used for the first dispense
- After dispensing, the pharmacist's system generates a new token for repeat 1
- This new token is sent to your phone via the PES
- Each subsequent dispense works the same way

If your prescription has 5 repeats, you end up managing 5 separate tokens over the life of the prescription (in addition to the original dispense). The Active Script List simplifies this - all repeats are stored centrally and accessible at any pharmacy.

**Repeat expiry**: All repeats must be used within 12 months of the prescription date. Repeats remaining on an expired prescription cannot be dispensed.

## Getting an eScript From a Telehealth Service

When you receive a prescription from a telehealth doctor, the delivery process is:

1. The doctor issues the eScript through an approved prescribing platform
2. The prescription is uploaded to the PES
3. A token is sent to your mobile number or email
4. You receive an SMS or email containing the QR code
5. You take the QR code (on your phone or printed) to any pharmacy

The timeframe from approval to token in your hand is typically a few minutes. For asynchronous telehealth services, the token arrives when the doctor has reviewed and approved your request - usually within 1-4 hours during operating hours.

<Callout variant="info">

An eScript from a [telehealth doctor](/consult) is indistinguishable from one issued by an in-person GP at the pharmacy counter. The pharmacist sees the medication, dose, prescriber details, and PBS information. How the consultation occurred is not visible to them and is not relevant to the dispense.

</Callout>

## Private Prescriptions vs PBS Prescriptions

Not all medications are PBS-listed. If your medication is not on the PBS:

- You receive a private eScript rather than a PBS eScript
- The format (token or ASL) is identical
- At the pharmacy, you pay the full private price of the medication
- Private prices vary by manufacturer and pharmacy - asking for a generic may reduce the cost

Some medications are PBS-listed only for specific indications. If you are prescribed the medication for an indication not covered by the PBS, you receive a private prescription.

## Paper Prescriptions Still Exist

While eScripts are now the default for most new prescriptions in Australia, paper prescriptions are not obsolete. Some circumstances where paper is still used:

- Some Schedule 8 medications in certain states (controlled drugs with special prescribing forms)
- Prescribers not yet fully integrated with PES infrastructure
- Patient preference in some cases (though this is increasingly rare)
- Some prescriptions for non-PBS medications

If you receive a paper prescription from a telehealth doctor by post (for example, if your employer requires an original signed document), it has the same legal standing as an eScript.

## Common Questions and Misconceptions

**Do I have to register for anything to receive an eScript?** No. If you have a mobile number or email, you receive the token automatically. The ASL requires registration, but basic eScript access does not.

**Can a pharmacist see other prescriptions I have?** Not from a single eScript token. If you are enrolled in the ASL, the pharmacist can see all prescriptions in your ASL profile - that is the purpose of the system.

**What if my phone is broken?** Access your eScript from email on another device, or contact the prescribing service to have the token resent or cancelled and reissued.

**Can I forward the eScript to someone else?** Technically you can forward the token, but the prescription must be dispensed in the patient's name and presented with their Medicare card. Forwarding a token to another person to dispense in your name is not appropriate practice.

**Is there a cost for the eScript itself?** No. The eScript infrastructure is a national system and there is no separate charge for the digital format.
