Food poisoning is sudden, unpleasant, and entirely outside your control. Whether it was last night's takeaway or last Sunday's barbecue, if you are spending your morning in the bathroom you are not fit for work - and Australian law recognises this. Here is what your entitlements are, what rules apply if you work with food, and how to get a medical certificate without dragging yourself to a clinic.

## Your Sick Leave Entitlements Under the Fair Work Act

Under the National Employment Standards (NES), full-time and part-time employees accrue 10 days of paid personal/carer's leave per year. Food poisoning qualifies as personal illness under the Fair Work Act 2009 in the same way as any other acute illness.

Your employer can request "evidence that would satisfy a reasonable person" of your illness. For a single day off with food poisoning, many workplaces do not require documentation. For absences of two or more days, a medical certificate is standard practice.

The certificate does not need to disclose the cause of your illness. It states you were assessed by a doctor and found unfit for work for a specified period. "Medical condition" is sufficient - you are not required to tell your employer you had food poisoning.

<Callout variant="info">Casual employees do not accrue paid sick leave but cannot be penalised for being absent due to genuine illness. Casual employees may access unpaid personal leave under the NES for periods of illness.</Callout>

## The 48-Hour Rule for Food Handlers

If you work in food service, childcare, aged care, or healthcare, different rules apply. Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) guidelines, reinforced by state food safety legislation, require that food handlers must not return to work until at least 48 hours after their last episode of vomiting or diarrhoea.

This rule exists because you remain infectious and capable of contaminating food or spreading illness even after you feel better. The 48-hour exclusion period reflects the time needed for the infectious agents that cause gastroenteritis to clear from your system to safe levels.

The 48-hour rule applies regardless of whether your illness was food poisoning or viral gastroenteritis - the food safety outcome is the same. The relevant provisions:

- Food Standards Code Standard 3.2.2, Clause 15 requires that food handlers not handle food while ill with symptoms that could contaminate food
- State food safety legislation mirrors and enforces this standard
- Employers in food, childcare, aged care, and healthcare who allow symptomatic staff to return early face significant compliance and liability risk

<Callout variant="warning">If you work with food, in childcare, aged care, or healthcare, the 48-hour rule is not optional. Returning before you are clear of symptoms puts vulnerable people at risk and may breach your employment obligations and food safety law.</Callout>

## Food Poisoning vs Viral Gastroenteritis

From a sick leave perspective, the distinction barely matters - both make you genuinely unfit for work. But understanding the difference helps set expectations about duration and transmission.

Food poisoning occurs when you consume food contaminated with bacteria, viruses, or toxins. Symptoms typically appear within 1-24 hours of the contaminated meal, though some types take longer.

Viral gastroenteritis spreads person-to-person. Symptoms overlap significantly with food poisoning: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, stomach cramps, and sometimes fever.

Common causes in Australia and their typical onset times:

- Staphylococcal toxin: symptoms within 1-6 hours, classically from improperly stored cooked meats or dairy
- Salmonella: symptoms 6-72 hours after contaminated chicken, eggs, or produce
- Campylobacter: symptoms 2-5 days, the most common bacterial food poisoning in Australia, usually from poultry
- Norovirus: symptoms 12-48 hours, highly contagious person-to-person
- E. coli: variable onset of 1-10 days depending on strain

You do not need to know the exact pathogen for your certificate. Describe your symptoms and the doctor will make the clinical assessment.

## Typical Recovery Time and Certificate Duration

Most uncomplicated food poisoning resolves within 1-3 days. The acute phase - active vomiting and diarrhoea - typically lasts 12-48 hours. Fatigue and reduced appetite can persist for a day or two after.

Typical certificate durations by role:

- Office and desk workers: 1-2 days for mild cases, 2-3 days for moderate
- Physical roles: may need an extra day to regain adequate strength
- Food handlers: at minimum 2 days to clear the 48-hour exclusion, often 3 days if symptoms persisted beyond the first day

If your recovery takes longer than expected - fever not settling, symptoms worsening, or still unable to keep fluids down after 48 hours - a follow-up consultation is appropriate.

## Getting a Medical Certificate for Food Poisoning

When you are actively vomiting or managing diarrhoea, travelling to a clinic is both unpleasant and inadvisable from an infection control perspective. Telehealth is the practical option.

The process:

- Complete the online consultation form. Describe your symptoms: when they started, what you ate before symptoms began, the severity and frequency of vomiting and diarrhoea, any associated fever, and whether family members who ate the same food are also unwell.
- Note your occupation, particularly if you handle food, work in childcare, aged care, or healthcare.
- An AHPRA-registered doctor reviews your case.
- If your presentation is consistent with food poisoning or gastroenteritis and you are clearly unfit for work, a certificate is issued.
- You receive a PDF by email, typically within an hour during operating hours.

What to tell the doctor to help your assessment:

- Exact symptoms and when they started
- Whether symptoms are improving, staying the same, or worsening
- Any fever and its level (above 38.5 degrees is clinically significant)
- Whether others who ate the same food are also unwell
- Whether you have any relevant medical history (diabetes, immunosuppression, pregnancy)

## What Your Certificate Will and Will Not Say

Your medical certificate will state that you were assessed on a particular date and found unfit for work for a specified period due to a medical condition. It will not say "food poisoning," describe your symptoms, or specify that your illness was gastrointestinal in nature unless you specifically request this.

The certificate will include:

- Your full legal name
- The consultation date
- The period you are unfit for work (start and end dates)
- The doctor's full name, AHPRA registration number, and contact details
- The doctor's signature (digital signatures are legally valid in Australia)

If your employer or a food safety authority needs confirmation of the 48-hour rule applying to your absence duration, mention this to the doctor during your consultation. The certificate can note that you were unfit for duties involving food handling for the relevant period.

## When to Seek Urgent Care

Most food poisoning is unpleasant but manageable at home. Seek in-person or emergency care when:

- Blood appears in vomit or stool
- Fever is above 38.5 degrees and not settling
- Signs of significant dehydration: dark urine, confusion, dizziness, or no urination for 8 or more hours
- Symptoms show no improvement after 3 days
- You are pregnant - gastroenteritis in pregnancy requires prompt assessment
- The person affected is elderly, immunocompromised, or very young

<Callout variant="emergency">If you experience blood in vomit or stool, severe dehydration with confusion or no urination for 8 or more hours, or rapidly worsening symptoms, seek emergency care or call 000. These are not situations for telehealth.</Callout>

## Recovery: What Actually Helps

During the acute phase:

- Sip small amounts of water, clear broth, or oral rehydration solution frequently. Large amounts may trigger vomiting.
- Rest completely while symptoms are active.
- Avoid solid food until vomiting has settled for several hours.
- When starting to eat, stick to bland foods (plain toast, rice, banana, crackers) before returning to a normal diet.
- Avoid dairy, alcohol, caffeine, and fatty foods until fully recovered.

Oral rehydration solution (ORS) is more effective than water alone for replacing electrolytes lost through vomiting and diarrhoea. Pharmacy sachets (Hydralyte, Gastrolyte) are inexpensive and worth having at home.

Over-the-counter loperamide (Imodium) reduces diarrhoea frequency but is generally not recommended for bacterial food poisoning as it can prolong the time the pathogen stays in your system. Ask a pharmacist if you are considering this.

## Returning to Work

For most workers, return when you feel genuinely well enough to perform your role - symptoms resolved and energy adequate.

For food handlers, childcare, aged care, and healthcare workers: 48 hours from your last episode of vomiting or diarrhoea. If your last symptom was Tuesday morning, Thursday morning is the earliest you can return.

If your employer requests a return-to-work clearance certificate, a telehealth or in-person doctor can confirm you are fit to resume normal duties once you have been symptom-free for the required period.
