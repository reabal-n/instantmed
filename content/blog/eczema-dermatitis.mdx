According to the Eczema Association of Australasia, eczema affects approximately 1 in 3 Australians at some point in their lives, with around 10% of the population experiencing active eczema at any given time. The AIHW identifies atopic dermatitis as one of the most common chronic conditions in Australian children, affecting up to 30% of children and typically persisting into adolescence or adulthood in a significant proportion.

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterised by skin barrier dysfunction, immune dysregulation, and heightened sensitivity to environmental triggers. It is not simply dry skin, and it is not contagious. With the right approach, most patients achieve sustained control - but understanding what drives the condition is essential for effective management.

## The Skin Barrier: Why It Matters

Contemporary understanding of eczema centres on the skin barrier hypothesis, developed following the discovery that loss-of-function mutations in the gene encoding filaggrin - a key structural protein in the skin barrier - are a major genetic risk factor for atopic dermatitis. Filaggrin deficiency impairs the skin's ability to retain water and form an effective physical barrier, allowing allergens, irritants, and microorganisms to penetrate the skin and trigger immune activation.

This explains several clinical features:
- Why moisturising is the foundation of management (it supports and augments the impaired barrier)
- Why eczema is associated with other atopic conditions (hay fever, asthma, food allergies) - all share underlying immune dysregulation
- Why early emollient therapy in high-risk infants may reduce eczema development (currently under active research)

## Recognising Eczema

Classic symptoms:
- Dry, sensitive skin that feels rough or tight
- Intense itching, often worse at night and disrupting sleep
- Red, inflamed patches (may appear brown or grey on darker skin tones - eczema is frequently missed or misdiagnosed in people with darker skin)
- Thickened, lichenified skin in chronic areas (from repeated scratching)
- Most commonly: flexural areas (inner elbows, behind knees), hands, face, neck, around the eyes

In infants and young children, eczema typically affects the face (cheeks), scalp, and extensor surfaces. The flexural pattern becomes more prominent from toddler age onward.

Weeping, crusting, and increased redness of eczema patches may indicate secondary infection with Staphylococcus aureus, a very common complication that requires antibiotic treatment alongside topical therapy.

## Identifying and Avoiding Triggers

Triggers do not cause eczema, but they precipitate flares. Identifying individual triggers through observation and modification is a core component of management:

| Trigger category | Common examples | Reduction strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Skin irritants | Soaps, detergents, fragrances, shampoos | Switch to fragrance-free, pH-balanced cleansers; use gloves when washing up |
| Environmental allergens | Dust mites, pet dander, mould, pollen | Encase mattress and pillows in dust-mite covers; HEPA air filtration |
| Extremes of temperature | Cold dry air, hot showers, excessive sweating | Lukewarm showers (not hot); humidifier in winter; cool-down after exercise |
| Stress | Work pressure, anxiety, life events | Stress management; maintain skincare routine during high-stress periods |
| Clothing | Wool, synthetic fabrics next to skin | Cotton or bamboo fabrics; avoid fabric conditioner |
| Food (in subset of patients) | More relevant in young children with severe eczema than adults | Elimination under dietitian guidance; food allergy testing if suspected |
| Skin infections | Staphylococcus aureus, herpes simplex | Prompt treatment; antiseptic bath additives in some cases |

Food allergies are more significant triggers in young children with eczema than in adults. In adults, food-triggered eczema flares are much less common and dietary restriction should only be undertaken if a clear temporal relationship is established, ideally under the guidance of an allergist or dietitian.

## The Foundation: Emollient Therapy

Emollients (moisturisers) are the cornerstone of eczema management. Evidence consistently shows that regular, generous moisturising reduces flare frequency, reduces the need for topical corticosteroids, and improves quality of life.

ASCIA and RACGP guidelines recommend:

- Apply at least twice daily, every day - not just during flares
- Apply within 3 minutes of getting out of the bath or shower, while skin is still slightly damp
- Use a thick cream or ointment formulation rather than a light lotion - the heavier the formulation, the better the barrier protection
- Use fragrance-free, preservative-free products
- Apply generously - a typical adult with eczema may use 250-500g of emollient per week

Product selection matters: patients should trial different formulations to find one they will actually use consistently. An effective emollient that is used regularly is better than a theoretically superior one that the patient finds uncomfortable and uses infrequently.

## Treating Flares: Topical Corticosteroids

Topical corticosteroids (TCS) are the most established and effective treatment for active eczema flares. The Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) lists a range of TCS formulations at different potency levels.

Understanding potency and appropriate use:

| Potency | Examples | Appropriate site |
|---|---|---|
| Mild (Group I) | Hydrocortisone 1% | Face, eyelids, flexures, infants |
| Moderate (Group II) | Betamethasone valerate 0.05% | Trunk, limbs |
| Potent (Group III) | Betamethasone valerate 0.1%, mometasone furoate | Thick skin, palms, soles |
| Very potent (Group IV) | Clobetasol propionate | Short-term, specialist guidance |

Key prescribing principles:
- Use the lowest effective potency for the site being treated
- Apply once or twice daily until the flare resolves
- Do not apply to unaffected skin
- Limit continuous use on the face and flexures to short courses
- "Weekend therapy" (applying on 2 days per week to previously affected areas) reduces relapse frequency

Common concerns about TCS relate to skin thinning (atrophy). This is primarily a risk with prolonged use of potent preparations on sensitive areas. Short courses as directed are safe, and the undertreatment of eczema flares carries its own risks (infection, sleep disruption, quality of life impact).

## Calcineurin Inhibitors

Topical calcineurin inhibitors - tacrolimus (Protopic) and pimecrolimus (Elidel) - are steroid-free anti-inflammatory treatments approved by the TGA for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis. They are particularly useful for:

- Sensitive areas where TCS use is limited (face, eyelids, neck, genitalia)
- Patients where long-term TCS use is a concern
- Maintenance therapy to reduce flare frequency

Both are available in Australia via prescription (Schedule 4). A transient burning sensation on application is common when first used and typically resolves with continued use.

## Newer Biological Treatments

For moderate-to-severe eczema not adequately controlled with topical therapy, systemic treatments are available:

**Dupilumab (Dupixent)**: A biologic (monoclonal antibody) targeting the IL-4/IL-13 pathway - key inflammatory mediators in atopic dermatitis. PBS-listed for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis in adults and adolescents (requires specialist initiation). Highly effective, with significant improvements in itch, skin clearance, and quality of life in clinical trials.

**Baricitinib (Olumiant) and upadacitinib (Rinvoq)**: Oral JAK inhibitors PBS-listed for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis in adults. Provide rapid itch relief and skin clearance. Require specialist initiation and monitoring.

These treatments require specialist referral (dermatologist or clinical immunologist).

## When to See a Doctor

See your GP if:
- Skin is not controlled after 2 weeks of consistent emollient use
- You need corticosteroid cream more than 2-3 times per week for most of the year
- Sleep is regularly disrupted by itching
- Skin is weeping, crusting, or showing signs of infection
- Eczema is affecting daily life, work, or mental health
- You want to discuss prescription treatments (calcineurin inhibitors, biologics)

Seek prompt care for:
- Widespread weeping or crusting eczema (possible eczema herpeticum - viral superinfection)
- Signs of bacterial infection (increasing redness, warmth, swelling, fever)
- Sudden severe widespread eczema flare with systemic unwellness

> **Warning:** Eczema herpeticum - herpes simplex virus infection of eczematous skin - is a medical emergency. Symptoms include rapidly spreading, painful, punched-out vesicles and ulcers, often with fever. Seek urgent medical care immediately.

## Telehealth for Eczema

Established eczema in adults, not responding to over-the-counter treatment, can often be managed via telehealth. A doctor can review your current treatment, prescribe appropriate topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors, and advise on management adjustments. In-person assessment is preferable for initial diagnosis, suspected infection, severe flares, or when specialist referral is being considered.
