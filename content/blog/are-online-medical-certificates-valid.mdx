When online medical certificates first became mainstream during COVID-19, many employers and employees were uncertain about their legal standing. That uncertainty has largely resolved. Telehealth medical certificates occupy the same legal position as certificates obtained at a GP clinic, provided they are issued by an AHPRA-registered doctor following a genuine clinical assessment.

This article lays out the legal framework, what employers can and cannot require, how to identify legitimate telehealth certificate services, and the specific situations where in-person assessment remains necessary.

## The Legal Basis

The primary framework for medical certificates in employment is the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth). Section 107 governs evidence for personal and carer's leave:

Under section 107(1), an employer may require an employee to provide evidence that "would satisfy a reasonable person" of their entitlement to personal leave.

Section 107(3) specifies that a medical certificate satisfies this requirement when it is issued by a "medical practitioner" - defined elsewhere in the Act with reference to a person "registered or licensed" to practise under a law of a state or territory relating to medical practitioners.

AHPRA registration is the national registration system for medical practitioners in Australia, enacted under the Health Practitioner Regulation National Law applied in all states and territories. An AHPRA-registered doctor is a registered medical practitioner for the purposes of the Fair Work Act.

**The Act does not specify how the consultation must be conducted.** It does not require that it be in person. It does not specify a minimum consultation duration. It does not restrict telehealth. The only requirement is that the issuing person be a registered medical practitioner.

<Callout variant="info">

The Fair Work Ombudsman has published guidance confirming that telehealth medical certificates are accepted as valid evidence under the Fair Work Act 2009. Employers who reject certificates solely because they were obtained online are likely acting contrary to this guidance and may be taking adverse action for a legitimate absence.

</Callout>

## What Employers Can Require

Employers have some legitimate rights around medical certificates:

**Requiring a certificate for absences of certain length:** Awards and enterprise agreements commonly specify that a certificate is required for absences of two or more consecutive days, or in some circumstances for a single day. The thresholds vary by award. The National Employment Standards (NES) do not specify a minimum certificate threshold, leaving this to be set by applicable industrial instruments or workplace policies.

**Requiring the certificate promptly:** Employers can require that a certificate be provided within a reasonable time, typically when the employee returns to work or sometimes during the absence for longer periods.

**Requiring that the certificate cover the dates of absence:** The certificate must be relevant to the period of absence claimed. A certificate that covers different dates than the absence claimed is not compliant evidence.

What employers cannot do:

- Refuse a certificate solely because it was obtained via telehealth
- Require that the certificate come from a specific doctor or practice
- Demand to know the patient's diagnosis (the certificate documents absence from work, not the specific medical condition)
- Take adverse action (dismissal, demotion, reduced hours) against an employee for a legitimate personal leave absence supported by valid evidence
- Require an independent medical examination as a routine condition for accepting every sick leave absence (though IME requests may be appropriate in specific circumstances after extended absences)

## Award-Specific Requirements

Many awards and enterprise agreements have specific language about medical certificates. Common variations include:

**"Medical practitioner" vs "health practitioner":** Some instruments specifically require a certificate from a "medical practitioner" (doctor), while others accept certificates from any registered health practitioner (including nurses in certain roles, pharmacist practitioners, or nurse practitioners). An AHPRA-registered doctor satisfies both formulations.

**"Statutory declaration" as an alternative:** Some awards accept a statutory declaration (sworn statement before a JP or other authorised witness) as an alternative to a medical certificate for shorter absences. Check your specific award or enterprise agreement for this option.

**Specific certificate requirements for safety-critical roles:** Workers in industries with safety-critical roles - aviation, heavy vehicle operation, certain mining and construction roles - may face additional certificate requirements under industry-specific regulations. These are separate from the Fair Work Act framework.

The Fair Work Ombudsman's Pay and Conditions Tool (available at fairwork.gov.au) can identify your applicable award and its specific evidence requirements.

## What Makes a Certificate Valid

A valid medical certificate issued via telehealth must:

**Be issued by an AHPRA-registered medical practitioner:** The certificate must include the doctor's name and AHPRA registration number. This allows the employer (if they choose) to verify registration at ahpra.gov.au.

**Be issued following a genuine clinical assessment:** The doctor must have reviewed the patient's presentation and formed a clinical opinion that the patient was unfit for work for the specified period. Certificates that are automatically generated without medical review - or that are issued by overseas practitioners not registered in Australia - are not valid.

**Cover the relevant period:** The certificate must accurately reflect the period of illness certified.

**Be accurate:** The information the patient provided to obtain the certificate must have been truthful. A certificate obtained by providing false information to a doctor is fraudulent and potentially a criminal matter.

<Callout variant="warning">

Providing false information to obtain a medical certificate - fabricating symptoms, claiming illness for days you were not unwell, or exaggerating the impact of symptoms - is dishonest and potentially unlawful. It also causes real harm: it devalues the medical certificate system and places doctors in the position of certifying false claims. A doctor who discovers they were deceived has no obligation to honour the certificate and may have reporting obligations in some circumstances.

</Callout>

## Identifying Legitimate Telehealth Certificate Services

The legitimacy of a telehealth certificate depends on the legitimacy of the service issuing it. Indicators of a legitimate service:

**AHPRA-registered practitioners:** The service should disclose that its doctors are AHPRA-registered. Some services display registration numbers; at minimum, the certificate itself should contain the issuing doctor's name and AHPRA number.

**Genuine clinical review:** The service should require clinical information from the patient (symptoms, onset, medical history) and involve a doctor reviewing that information before issuing a certificate. Services that issue certificates instantly upon payment without collecting clinical information are not conducting a genuine medical assessment.

**LegitScript certification:** LegitScript is an international certification body for online healthcare services. LegitScript-certified services have been independently verified as compliant with applicable regulatory standards for their jurisdiction. Certification does not guarantee any specific outcome for a consultation but indicates the service operates within regulatory boundaries.

**Privacy policy and terms:** Legitimate services have accessible privacy policies complying with the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and Australian Privacy Principles, and terms of service that accurately describe the clinical process.

**Transparency about limitations:** A legitimate telehealth service will decline requests it cannot appropriately handle and will advise patients when in-person care is needed. Services that never decline any request are not exercising clinical judgment.

Red flags include: no identification of doctors, instant certificate generation with no clinical intake, overseas-based or unverifiable practitioners, and no refund policy for clinically declined requests.

## Certificates for Purposes Beyond Employment

**Centrelink and government benefits:** Centrelink requires medical evidence for some payments. A certificate or medical report from an AHPRA-registered doctor satisfies Centrelink's evidence requirements. Some Centrelink forms (SU415 for sickness allowance, for example) require specific information that may not be contained in a standard sick leave certificate - check the specific form requirements.

**Workers' compensation:** Workers' compensation certificates (Certificate of Capacity in most states) have specific forms and requirements that differ from standard sick leave certificates. These are specific to the workers' compensation system in each state and territory and require completion by a treating practitioner with knowledge of the specific injury or illness.

**Travel insurance:** Travel insurance medical claims typically require documentation from a treating practitioner. A telehealth certificate from an AHPRA-registered doctor is generally accepted, but check your specific policy's requirements.

**University and education:** Universities and other educational institutions have their own policies on medical documentation for special consideration, extensions, or deferred examinations. Most accept certificates from registered medical practitioners obtained via telehealth, though some institutions specify their own forms or processes. Check the specific institution's requirements.

## When In-Person Assessment Is Still Required

Online certificates are valid, but not every clinical situation can be appropriately assessed via telehealth. A legitimate telehealth service will decline to issue a certificate (and recommend in-person care) when:

- The symptoms described suggest a condition that requires physical examination to assess
- The presentation is consistent with a medical emergency
- The described symptoms are inconsistent or raise clinical concerns requiring investigation
- The requested absence duration significantly exceeds what the described symptoms would support
- The patient's description raises questions the doctor cannot resolve without examination

In these situations, "the telehealth doctor refused" is not a sign the certificate system has failed. It is the clinical governance system working correctly. See your GP or, if the situation is urgent, a medical clinic or emergency department.
