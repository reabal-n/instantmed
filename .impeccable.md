## Design Context

### Users

**Primary — anxious patient on their phone, probably morning:**
Australian adults (18+) who have called in sick and need a valid medical certificate today. Overwhelmingly mobile. Often unwell, groggy, or stressed. Decision context: lying in bed or on the couch, having just texted their manager. The competing option is physically going to a GP clinic — they'd rather not. Key anxieties: "Will my employer actually accept this?", "Is this legitimate medicine or a loophole?", "What if the doctor says no and I've wasted money?"

**Secondary — patients managing chronic conditions:**
Repeat prescription users. Less anxious, more routine-minded. Slightly older. Want speed and zero friction.

**Specialty — men seeking discreet treatment (ED, hair loss):**
High privacy concern. Do not want to discuss this in a clinic. Expect discretion, clinical seriousness, no bro-marketing vibes.

**Portal users — doctors and admin:**
AHPRA-registered GPs reviewing cases. Time-pressured, clinical mindset. No decorative motion. `transition-colors` only in portal surfaces.

### Brand Personality

**Three words: calm, authoritative, crisp.**

Voice model: an experienced GP explaining their service over coffee. Dry wit. Never stiff, never slangy. Not a startup, not a pharmacy chain, not a wellness app.

Brand essence (from the design system): "Clarity emerging." Morning light. Good judgment. If it feels impressive but loud — kill it. If it resembles crypto or AI SaaS — kill it. If it feels like wellness marketing — kill it.

**Emotional goals:**
- Patient feels calm confidence: "I can sort this in 2 minutes and get on with my day"
- Patient feels legitimacy: "This is proper medicine, not a workaround"
- Patient feels relief: "I don't have to leave the house or talk to anyone"
- Never: urgency-as-pressure, clinical sterility, fintech aggression

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme:** Light mode default. Dark mode ("Quiet Night Sky") supported site-wide. Patients use this in the morning at home — light is correct.

**Palette:** Warm ivory backgrounds (`#F8F7F4`). Pure white card surfaces. Sky-toned shadows (`shadow-primary/[0.06]`) only — never black. Morning spectrum (`--morning-sky`, `--morning-peach`, `--morning-ivory`) on hero/marketing surfaces only.

**Typography:** Source Sans 3 exclusively. No serif. No Inter. No decorative fonts. Negative tracking on headings. 16px body minimum on all patient flows — non-negotiable.

**Geometry:** Squircle-preferred. `rounded-xl` to `rounded-2xl` on all surfaces. `rounded-full` for pills and avatars only. No sharp corners anywhere.

**Elevation:** Solid depth pattern (dub.co). Real white cards with real sky-toned shadows on warm backgrounds. No `backdrop-blur` on content surfaces — ever.

**Motion:** Subtle and purposeful. Anxious patients on health platforms do not benefit from heavy animation. Entrance is `fadeUp`, duration caps at 250ms. No elastic. No parallax. Always respect `useReducedMotion()`. Doctor/admin portal: no decorative motion whatsoever.

**Anti-references:** Crypto/DeFi dashboards (dark, neon, aggressive), wellness marketing (Headspace/Calm softness), generic AI SaaS (purple-gradient-on-dark, glass cards), pharmacy chain sterility (clinical white, cold).

**References:** dub.co (elevation/card pattern), linear (quality bar, precision), vercel (trust through design clarity), stripe (information density done right), loop.co (fluid, premium feel).

### Design Principles

1. **Calm first, then conversion.** Every element should reduce anxiety before it sells. Urgency tactics (countdown timers, "limited spots") are off-brand and off-strategy here.
2. **Solid depth, never glass.** Real weight signals real trust. Frosted glass reads "startup" in health context.
3. **Every word earns its place.** Anxious patients scan, not read. Copy that fails the `/clarify` test ships as friction.
4. **Morning Canvas always.** Use tokens, never arbitraries. Sky-toned shadows, warm ivory, Source Sans 3. Non-negotiable.
5. **Motion serves clarity.** Animation reveals state transitions and hierarchy. It never decorates. If it doesn't help a patient understand what just happened, cut it.
